Malaria

About Malaria Prevention & Treatment

About Malaria

Malaria is a serious tropical disease spread by mosquitoes. If it isn't diagnosed and treated quickly, it can be fatal. A single mosquito bite is all it takes for someone to become infected.

Symptoms of Malaria

Symptoms are similar to those of flu and usually appear 6 to 30 days after the mosquito bite, but it can sometimes take up to a year for symptoms to start.

The initial symptoms of malaria include:

  • A high temperature (fever)
  • Headaches
  • Sweats
  • Chills
  • Muscle aches or pains
  • Vomiting and/or diarrhoea

Emergency: Phone 111 or go to A&E if you or your child develop symptoms of malaria during or after a visit to an area where the disease is found, even if it has been several weeks, months or a year after you return from travelling.

What Causes Malaria?

Malaria is caused by the Plasmodium parasite. The parasite is spread to humans through the bites of infected mosquitoes.

There are 5 different types of Plasmodium parasite that cause malaria in humans:

  • Plasmodium falciparum - Mainly found in Africa, most common and responsible for most malaria deaths
  • Plasmodium vivax - Mainly found in Asia and South America, causes milder symptoms but can stay in the liver for years
  • Plasmodium ovale - Fairly uncommon, usually found in West Africa
  • Plasmodium malariae - Quite rare, usually only found in Africa
  • Plasmodium knowlesi - Very rare, found in parts of southeast Asia

How Malaria Spreads

The Plasmodium parasite is spread by 'night-biting' mosquitoes that most commonly bite between sunset and sunrise. When a mosquito bites a person already infected with malaria, it becomes infected and spreads the parasite to the next person it bites.

Important: Malaria can't be spread directly from person to person, though in very rare cases it can be spread through blood transfusions and sharing needles.

Complications of Malaria

Malaria is a serious illness that can be fatal if not diagnosed and treated quickly. Severe complications can occur within hours or days of the first symptoms.

Complications include:

  • Severe anaemia
  • Cerebral malaria (affecting the brain)
  • Liver failure and jaundice
  • Kidney failure
  • Pulmonary oedema (fluid in lungs)
  • Shock and dehydration

Malaria in Pregnancy

If you get malaria while pregnant, you and your baby have an increased risk of serious complications including premature birth, low birth weight, stillbirth, miscarriage, and death of the mother.

Warning: Pregnant women are advised to avoid travelling to regions with a risk of malaria. Visit your GP or travel health advisor for specific advice if you're pregnant and considering travel to a high-risk area.

Preventing and Treating Malaria

If you travel to an area that has malaria, you are at risk of the infection. It's very important that you take precautions to prevent the disease and get treatment immediately if symptoms do develop.

Preventing Malaria - The ABCD Approach

  • Awareness of risk - find out whether you're at risk of getting malaria
  • Bite prevention - use insect repellent, cover your skin, and use mosquito nets
  • Check whether you need to take antimalarial prevention tablets
  • Diagnosis - seek immediate medical advice if you have malaria symptoms

Preventing Mosquito Bites

Avoiding mosquito bites is one of the best ways to prevent malaria, particularly during early evening and at night when mosquitoes bite.

Protection Tips:

  • Wear light, loose-fitting clothing that covers arms and legs
  • Apply insect repellent containing 50% DEET to exposed skin
  • Reapply insect repellent frequently
  • Sleep under an insecticide-treated mosquito net
  • Use air conditioning in rooms when available

Antimalarial Tablets

Antimalarial tablets can help prevent you from developing a malaria infection. You must start taking them before travel and continue taking them while in the risk area and for a period after you leave.

Main types of antimalarials include:

  • Atovaquone plus proguanil (Maloff protect or Malarone)
  • Doxycycline (Vibramycin-D)
  • Mefloquine (Lariam)

Important: Visit a travel health clinic to get the right antimalarial tablets for your destination and personal health circumstances. Complete the full course of tablets as directed.

Treating Malaria

If malaria is diagnosed and treated quickly, you should fully recover. Treatment should be started as soon as possible in a hospital setting.

Treatment depends on:

  • The type of malaria you have
  • The severity of your symptoms
  • Whether you took preventative antimalarial tablets
  • Your age and whether you're pregnant

Treatment for malaria can leave you feeling very tired and weak for several weeks after recovery.

Remember: There's currently no vaccine available that offers protection against malaria. Prevention through avoiding bites and taking antimalarial medication is crucial.
