About Croup

Croup is a childhood condition that affects the windpipe (trachea), the airways to the lungs (the bronchi) and the voice box (larynx).

Children with croup have a distinctive barking cough and will make a harsh sound, known as stridor, when they breathe in.

They may also have a hoarse voice and find it difficult to breathe because their airway is blocked.

Take your child to A&E or phone 999 if your child:

  • Has severe breathing difficulties
  • Has an increased breathing rate or 'silent chest'
  • Has a worsening cough or rasping sound
  • Shows distress and agitation
  • Has dark, blue-tinged or pale skin
  • Skin around ribs appears pulled in and tight
  • Has abnormal drowsiness and sleepiness
  • Has an inability to drink fluids

Who is affected by croup?

Croup usually affects young children aged between six months and three years, with most cases occurring in one-year-olds.

The condition is more common during the late autumn and early winter months and tends to affect more boys than girls.

Symptoms of Croup

A child can get croup at any time of the year, although it's more likely to occur during late autumn or early winter.

Typical symptoms of croup include:

  • A bark-like cough
  • A hoarse or croaky voice
  • Difficulty breathing
  • A harsh grating sound when breathing in, called stridor

Stridor is often most noticeable when the child cries or coughs. Symptoms tend to be worse at night.

Cold-like symptoms that may precede croup:

  • Sore throat
  • Runny nose
  • Cough
  • High temperature (fever)

Note: Although croup symptoms usually only last for a few days, they can occasionally last up to two weeks.

Causes of Croup

Croup usually develops as the result of a viral infection. The infection causes the larynx (voice box) to become swollen and the trachea (windpipe) to become blocked.

Parainfluenza virus

The parainfluenza virus is the most common cause of croup. Four strains of the virus are responsible for most croup cases.

Other viruses that can cause croup:

  • Influenza A and B (flu viruses)
  • The measles virus
  • The rhinovirus (common cold virus)
  • Enteroviruses
  • Respiratory syncytial virus (RSV)

Less common causes:

  • Inhaling a small object
  • Epiglottitis
  • Allergic reaction
  • Inhalation of irritants
  • Acid reflux

Diagnosing Croup

A GP can diagnose croup by studying your child's symptoms, particularly the sound of their cough. They may also check your child's temperature for a fever and ask about recent cold or viral infections.

Diagnostic methods:

  • Symptom assessment (barking cough, stridor)
  • Temperature check
  • Pulse oximetry test to measure oxygen levels

Important: Do not try to check your child's throat yourself, as this could trigger a spasm of the airway and make breathing more difficult.

Ruling out other conditions

Croup can usually be diagnosed by symptoms, but your GP may want to rule out other conditions with similar symptoms, such as airway abnormalities, inhaled objects, or epiglottitis.

Treating Croup

Treatment of croup depends on how severe the symptoms are. Most cases are mild and can be managed at home.

Home treatment for mild croup:

  • Comfort your child and keep them calm
  • Encourage plenty of fluids
  • Use children's paracetamol for pain and fever
  • Sit your child upright to help with breathing
  • Your GP may prescribe corticosteroids to reduce swelling

What to avoid:

  • Do not use cough medicines or decongestants
  • Avoid steam treatment - it's not proven to help
  • Do not give aspirin to children under 16

Hospital treatment for severe croup:

  • Adrenaline through a nebuliser
  • Oxygen therapy
  • Corticosteroid medication
  • In rare cases, intubation may be necessary

Seek immediate medical help if:

Your child is struggling to breathe, shows severe distress, has blue-tinged skin, or cannot drink fluids.

Complications of Croup

Complications that develop as a result of croup are rare.

Possible complications include:

  • Airway obstruction - Can lead to severe breathing difficulty
  • Dehydration - Due to difficulty drinking fluids
  • Secondary infections - Such as pneumonia or bacterial tracheitis
  • Middle ear infection

Remember: Most cases of croup clear up within 48 hours and it is extremely rare for a child to die from croup.

Prevention

Croup is spread like the common cold, so good hygiene is the main defence. Regular hand washing and routine childhood vaccinations can help prevent some infections that cause croup.
