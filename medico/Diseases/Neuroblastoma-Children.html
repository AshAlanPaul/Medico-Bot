Neuroblastoma in Children

Most children who have neuroblastoma are younger than 5 years old. It's the most common solid tumour in children that occurs outside of the brain and makes up 8% of the total number of children's cancers.

More children than ever are surviving childhood cancer. There are new and better drugs and treatments, and we can now also work to reduce the after-effects of having had cancer in the past.

What is Neuroblastoma?

Neuroblastoma develops from the cells left behind from a baby's development in the womb. The cells that it develops from are called neuroblasts.

  • 'Neuro' means nerve
  • 'Blast' means cells in early development
  • 'Oma' means a group of cells, or a tumour

Neuroblastoma can occur anywhere in the body, typically in the adrenal glands or nerve tissue alongside the spinal cord.

Important: Neuroblastoma is not infectious and cannot be passed on to other people. The exact cause is unknown.

Symptoms of Neuroblastoma

The symptoms vary depending on where the neuroblastoma tumour is located in the body.

Common Symptoms by Location

Abdominal Tumours

  • Swollen abdomen
  • Constipation
  • Difficulty passing urine

Chest Tumours

  • Breathlessness
  • Difficulty swallowing

Neck Tumours

  • Visible lump
  • Breathing and swallowing difficulties

Other Symptoms

  • Small blue-colored lumps on skin
  • Weakness in legs or unsteady walking
  • High blood pressure
  • Jerky eye and muscle movements
  • Tiredness, pale appearance, loss of appetite
  • Weight loss and bone pain

If your child shows any of these symptoms, consult your healthcare provider for proper evaluation and diagnosis.

Diagnosing Neuroblastoma

A variety of tests and investigations are needed to diagnose neuroblastoma and determine its extent.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Biopsy - Sample of tumour tissue examined under microscope
  • Blood and bone marrow tests - Check for cancer spread
  • Imaging scans - X-rays, CT or MRI scans to locate tumour
  • MIBG scan - Special nuclear medicine scan using radioactive substance
  • Urine tests - Measure VMA and HVA levels (tumour markers)

Tumour Biology

Doctors examine chromosomes and biological markers in tumour cells, including MYCN status. MYCN amplification indicates a more aggressive neuroblastoma requiring intensive treatment.

Early and accurate diagnosis is crucial for determining the most effective treatment approach for neuroblastoma.

Staging Neuroblastoma

Staging describes the size of the cancer and whether it has spread, helping doctors determine the best treatment approach.

Traditional Staging System

  • Stage 1 - Contained in one area, completely removable
  • Stage 2A - Localized but not completely removable
  • Stage 2B - Localized with spread to nearby lymph nodes
  • Stage 3 - Spread to surrounding organs
  • Stage 4 - Spread to distant areas
  • Stage 4S - Special neuroblastoma in children under 1 year

International Neuroblastoma Risk Group (INRG) System

  • Stage L1 - Localized, no risk factors, surgically removable
  • Stage L2 - Localized with risk factors, not safely removable
  • Stage M - Spread to other body parts
  • Stage MS - Spread to skin, liver, or bone marrow in children under 18 months

Good prognosis: Children under 18 months often have excellent outcomes, and Stage 4S disease frequently resolves with minimal or no treatment.

Treatment for Neuroblastoma

Treatment depends on the child's age, tumour size and position, tumour biology, and whether the cancer has spread.

Treatment Options

Surgery

For localized tumours, surgery to remove the tumour is usually the first treatment. Chemotherapy may be used first to shrink larger tumours.

Chemotherapy

Used for tumours that have spread or are high-risk. Given through a central venous line to minimize injection distress.

High-Dose Chemotherapy with Stem Cell Rescue

For widespread or high-risk neuroblastoma. Stem cells are collected before high-dose chemotherapy and returned afterward to rebuild bone marrow.

Radiotherapy

Uses high-energy rays to destroy cancer cells. May be external beam or internal using radioactive MIBG.

Monoclonal Antibody Treatment

Experimental treatment using antibodies to target cancer cells specifically. Currently available mainly through clinical trials.

Treatment Side Effects

  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Weight loss
  • Hair loss
  • Increased infection risk
  • Bruising and bleeding
  • Tiredness

Late effects: Some children may develop long-term side effects including heart, kidney, or hearing problems, fertility issues, or increased risk of secondary cancers.

Support available: Many organizations provide support for families dealing with childhood cancer. Clinical trials offer access to new treatments and contribute to medical knowledge.
