Anxiety

Anxiety is a feeling of unease, like worry or fear, that can be mild or severe.

Everyone experiences feelings of anxiety at some point in their life. It's normal to feel worried about exams, job interviews, or other stressful situations. However, when anxiety becomes constant and affects daily life, it may be a disorder that needs treatment.

Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)

GAD is a long-term condition that causes you to feel anxious about a wide range of issues, rather than one specific event.

  • People with GAD feel anxious most days
  • They often struggle to remember the last time they felt relaxed
  • As soon as one anxious thought is resolved, another may appear

Anxiety is the main symptom of several conditions including:

  • Panic disorder
  • Phobias
  • Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)
  • Social anxiety disorder (social phobia)

Symptoms of Generalised Anxiety Disorder

GAD can cause changes in behaviour, thinking patterns, and physical symptoms:

Psychological Symptoms

  • Restlessness and feeling constantly "on edge"
  • A sense of dread
  • Difficulty concentrating
  • Irritability
  • Difficulty falling or staying asleep (insomnia)

Physical Symptoms

  • Strong, fast or irregular heartbeat (palpitations)
  • Dizziness and tiredness
  • Muscle aches and tension
  • Trembling or shaking
  • Dry mouth and excessive sweating
  • Shortness of breath
  • Stomach ache and feeling sick
  • Headaches and pins and needles

These symptoms may cause withdrawal from social contact and make work difficult and stressful, which can further increase anxiety and affect self-esteem.

Causes of Generalised Anxiety Disorder

The exact cause of GAD is not fully understood, but several factors likely play a role:

  • Brain activity: Overactivity in areas of the brain involved in emotions and behaviour
  • Chemical imbalance: Imbalance of serotonin and noradrenaline, which control mood
  • Genetics: You're 5 times more likely to develop GAD if a close relative has it
  • Traumatic experiences: History of domestic violence, child abuse or bullying
  • Health conditions: Painful long-term conditions like arthritis
  • Substance misuse: History of drug or alcohol misuse

Many people develop GAD for no apparent reason. Unlike specific phobias where triggers are clear, GAD may not always have obvious causes.

Treating Generalised Anxiety Disorder

Several effective treatments are available for GAD:

Professional Treatments

  • Talking therapies: Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) is particularly effective
  • Medication: Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) - a type of antidepressant

Referral to Specialists

If initial treatments aren't helping, your GP may refer you to a mental health specialist. Community mental health teams include:

  • Psychiatrists and psychiatric nurses
  • Clinical psychologists
  • Occupational therapists and social workers

With treatment, many people are able to control their anxiety levels. However, some treatments may need to be continued long-term, and there may be periods where symptoms worsen.

Self-Help for Anxiety

There are many things you can do yourself to help reduce anxiety:

  • Self-help courses: Based on Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)
  • Regular exercise: Physical activity can significantly reduce anxiety
  • Lifestyle changes: Stop smoking, reduce alcohol, and cut down on caffeine
  • Digital tools: Programmes like Daylight (CBT-based app)

Support Groups

Support groups can provide valuable advice and connect you with others who have similar experiences:

  • Anxiety UK
  • Mind
  • Rethink Mental Illness

Ask your GP about local support groups or search for mental health information and support services in your area. Many groups offer face-to-face meetings, phone support, and written guidance.
