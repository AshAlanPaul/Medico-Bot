About Laryngeal Cancer

Laryngeal cancer is a type of cancer that affects the larynx (voice box).

The larynx is part of the throat found at the entrance of the windpipe (trachea). It plays an important role in helping you breathe and speak.

The condition is more common in people over the age of 60. It's about 4 times more common in men than women.

Symptoms of Laryngeal Cancer

The main symptoms of laryngeal cancer include:

  • A change in your voice, such as sounding hoarse
  • Pain when swallowing or difficulty swallowing
  • A lump or swelling in your neck
  • A long-lasting cough
  • A persistent sore throat or earache
  • In severe cases, difficulty breathing

Some people may also experience bad breath, breathlessness, a high-pitched wheezing noise when breathing, unexplained weight loss, or fatigue (extreme tiredness).

When to see a doctor: You should speak to your GP if you have had any of the main symptoms for more than 3 weeks.

Causes of Laryngeal Cancer

Laryngeal cancer is caused by changes in the cells of the larynx, although it's not clear exactly why this happens.

What Can Increase Your Risk?

Alcohol and Tobacco

Alcohol and tobacco are the 2 main things that can increase your risk of developing laryngeal cancer. They're thought to contain chemicals that can damage the cells of the larynx.

People who smoke more than 25 cigarettes a day, or people who have smoked for more than 40 years, have been found to be about 40 times more likely to develop laryngeal cancer than people who don't smoke.

Family History

People who have a first-degree relative (parent, sibling or child) diagnosed with head or neck cancer are thought to be twice as likely to develop laryngeal cancer.

Diet

There's evidence to suggest that a diet high in red meat, processed food and fried food might increase your risk of developing laryngeal cancer.

Human Papilloma Virus (HPV)

HPV is known to cause changes in the cells of the cervix, which can lead to cervical cancer. It's thought the virus may have a similar effect on the cells of the throat.

Major Risk Factors: Smoking and heavy alcohol consumption are the most significant risk factors for developing laryngeal cancer.

Diagnosing Laryngeal Cancer

If you have symptoms of laryngeal cancer, your GP will ask about your symptoms and recent medical history. They may examine the inside and outside of your throat for abnormalities.

Nasendoscopy

A nasendoscopy is a procedure used to get a clear view of your larynx. During the procedure, a small, flexible tube with a light and video camera is inserted into your nostril and passed down the back of your throat.

Laryngoscopy

If a nasendoscopy doesn't provide a good view or a problem is spotted, you may need a laryngoscopy. This involves using a longer endoscope inserted through the mouth to examine your larynx in greater detail.

Biopsy

During these procedures, your doctor may remove a sample of cells from your larynx to examine for signs of cancer. This is known as a biopsy.

Further Testing

If cancer is confirmed, further tests may be needed to assess how widespread it is, including:

  • CT scan
  • MRI scan
  • PET-CT scan
  • Ultrasound scan

Early Detection: The National Institute for Health and Care Excellence recommends that anyone aged 45 or over with persistent unexplained hoarseness or an unexplained lump in their neck should have an appointment with a specialist within 2 weeks.

Treating Laryngeal Cancer

The treatment for laryngeal cancer largely depends on the size of the cancer. The main treatments are radiotherapy, surgery and chemotherapy.

Radiotherapy

Radiotherapy uses controlled doses of high-energy radiation to destroy cancerous cells. It can be used as a treatment on its own for early-stage laryngeal cancer, or after surgery to stop cancerous cells returning.

Surgery

There are 3 types of surgery that can be used to treat laryngeal cancer:

  • Endoscopic resection - for early-stage cancer
  • Partial laryngectomy - removing the affected part of the larynx
  • Total laryngectomy - removing the entire larynx for advanced cancer

Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy uses powerful cancer-killing medicines to damage the DNA of cancerous cells and stop them reproducing. It may be used to shrink a tumor before surgery or radiotherapy.

Cetuximab

Cetuximab is a type of biological therapy that targets and disrupts the processes that cancerous cells use to grow and reproduce. It can be used when chemotherapy is not suitable.

Treatment Team: Most hospitals use multidisciplinary teams of specialists that work together to decide the best treatment approach for each individual case.

Recovering from Laryngeal Cancer

If you've had some or all of your larynx removed (laryngectomy), it's likely that you'll need to spend 1 or 2 days in an intensive care unit until you've recovered.

Your Emotions

The emotional impact of living with a laryngectomy can be significant. Many people report experiencing a rollercoaster effect of emotions.

Looking After Your Stoma

If you've had all of your larynx removed, the surgeon will create a permanent hole in your throat through which you will breathe (a stoma). It's important to clean your stoma daily to prevent infection.

Speaking After Surgery

If your larynx has been completely removed, you'll need additional treatment to help restore your voice. Options include:

  • Voice prosthesis
  • Oesophageal speech
  • Electrolarynx

Support: The National Association of Laryngectomee Clubs is a useful resource for people trying to come to terms with living with a laryngectomy.

Preventing Laryngeal Cancer

It's thought that most laryngeal cancers can be prevented by adopting a healthy lifestyle.

Avoiding Tobacco Products

Using tobacco products increases your chances of developing several different types of cancer, including laryngeal cancer. Stopping will have both short- and long-term health benefits.

Cutting Down on Alcohol

Staying within the recommended guidelines for alcohol consumption will reduce your risk of developing laryngeal and liver cancers.

Healthy Diet

A diet that contains plenty of fresh fruits and vegetables, particularly tomatoes, citrus fruit, olive oil and fish oil, may reduce your risk of getting laryngeal cancer.

Prevention is Key: Avoiding tobacco products, cutting down on alcohol, and having a healthy diet are particularly important in reducing your chances of developing laryngeal cancer.
