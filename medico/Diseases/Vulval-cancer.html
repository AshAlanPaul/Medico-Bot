Vulval Cancer Information

About Vulval Cancer Causes Diagnosis Treatment

About Vulval Cancer

Cancer of the vulva is a rare type of cancer that affects women and anyone with a vulva. The vulva is the external genitals including the lips surrounding the vagina, clitoris, and Bartholin's glands.

Symptoms of Vulval Cancer

  • A persistent itch in the vulva
  • Pain, soreness or tenderness in the vulva
  • Raised and thickened patches of skin (red, white or dark)
  • A lump or wart-like growth on the vulva
  • Bleeding from the vulva or blood-stained vaginal discharge
  • An open sore in the vulva
  • Burning pain when passing urine
  • A mole on the vulva that changes shape or color

Important: Speak to your GP if you notice any changes in the usual appearance of your vulva. While it's highly unlikely to be cancer, these changes should be investigated.

Causes of Vulval Cancer

Cancer occurs when cells divide and multiply too rapidly, producing a tumor. While the exact cause is unknown, certain factors increase your risk:

Risk Factors

  • Increasing age - Most cases occur in women over 65
  • Vulval intraepithelial neoplasia (VIN) - Pre-cancerous cell changes
  • HPV infection - Persistent human papilloma virus infection
  • Skin conditions - Such as lichen sclerosus
  • Smoking - Weakens immune system and increases HPV vulnerability

Prevention: You can reduce your risk by practicing safer sex, attending cervical screening, stopping smoking, and getting the HPV vaccination.

Diagnosing Vulval Cancer

If you notice changes in your vulva, your GP will examine you and may refer you to a gynaecologist for further testing.

Diagnostic Procedures

  • Physical examination of the vulva
  • Biopsy - removing tissue sample for analysis
  • Colposcopy - detailed examination with a microscope
  • Imaging tests - CT scans, MRI scans, X-rays
  • Additional examinations of bladder and rectum if needed

Cancer Staging

  • Stage 1 - Cancer confined to the vulva
  • Stage 2 - Spread to nearby areas like lower vagina or anus
  • Stage 3 - Spread to nearby lymph nodes
  • Stage 4 - Spread to other parts of the body

Note: Stage 1 and 2 vulval cancers are generally early-stage with good treatment success rates.

Treating Vulval Cancer

Treatment depends on the cancer stage, your general health, and personal preferences. A multidisciplinary team will create your treatment plan.

Treatment Options

  • Surgery - Main treatment to remove cancerous tissue
  • Radiotherapy - Using radiation to destroy cancer cells
  • Chemotherapy - Medication to kill cancer cells

Types of Surgery

  • Radical wide local excision - Removing cancerous tissue with healthy margin
  • Radical partial vulvectomy - Removing larger section of vulva
  • Radical vulvectomy - Removing entire vulva
  • Lymph node removal - Assessing and removing affected lymph nodes

Important: Treatment can have significant emotional and physical impacts. Support is available through cancer charities and support groups to help cope with the effects of treatment.
