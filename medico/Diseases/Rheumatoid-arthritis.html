About Rheumatoid Arthritis

Rheumatoid arthritis is a long-term condition that causes pain, swelling and stiffness in the joints.

The hands, feet and wrists are commonly affected, but it can also cause problems in other parts of the body.

There may be periods where your symptoms become worse, known as a flare-up or flare. A flare can be difficult to predict, but with treatment it is possible to decrease the number of flares and minimize or prevent long-term damage to the joints.

Important: You should see your GP if you think you have symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis. Early diagnosis and treatment can help stop the condition getting worse and reduce the risk of further problems such as joint damage.

Who is Affected

Rheumatoid arthritis affects around 400,000 people in the UK.

It can affect adults at any age, but most commonly starts between the ages of 40 and 50. About three times as many women as men are affected.

Symptoms of Rheumatoid Arthritis

Rheumatoid arthritis mainly affects the joints, although it can cause problems in other parts of the body too.

The symptoms of rheumatoid arthritis often develop gradually over several weeks, but some cases can progress quickly over a number of days.

Joint Symptoms

Rheumatoid arthritis typically affects the joints symmetrically (both sides of the body at the same time). The main symptoms include:

  • Pain – usually a throbbing and aching pain, often worse in the mornings
  • Stiffness – particularly in the morning or after inactivity
  • Swelling, warmth and redness – in affected joints
  • Rheumatoid nodules – firm swellings that can develop under the skin

Additional Symptoms

Some people with rheumatoid arthritis experience more general symptoms, such as:

  • Tiredness and lack of energy
  • High temperature (fever)
  • Sweating
  • Poor appetite
  • Weight loss

Medical Attention: If you experience joint pain and stiffness, especially if it's worse in the morning and affects multiple joints, see your GP for proper diagnosis.

Causes of Rheumatoid Arthritis

Rheumatoid arthritis is an autoimmune condition, which means it is caused by the body's immune system attacking itself.

Normally, your immune system makes antibodies that attack bacteria and viruses, helping fight infection. But if you have rheumatoid arthritis, your immune system mistakenly sends antibodies to the lining of your joints, where they attack the tissue surrounding the joint.

Possible Risk Factors

There are a number of things that may increase your risk of developing rheumatoid arthritis:

  • Genes – rheumatoid arthritis can run in families
  • Hormones – more common in women, possibly due to estrogen
  • Smoking – smokers are at increased risk
  • Age – most common between ages 40-50

It's not yet known exactly what triggers the immune system to attack the joints.

Diagnosing Rheumatoid Arthritis

Rheumatoid arthritis can be difficult to diagnose because many conditions cause joint stiffness and inflammation and there is no definitive test for the condition.

Blood Tests

Several blood tests can help diagnose rheumatoid arthritis:

  • Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) – measures inflammation
  • C-reactive protein (CRP) – another inflammation marker
  • Full blood count – checks for anemia
  • Rheumatoid factor and anti-CCP antibodies – specific markers for RA

Joint Imaging

Scans that may be carried out include:

  • X-rays
  • Ultrasound scans
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scans

Diagnosis Process: Your GP will carry out a physical examination, ask about your symptoms, and may arrange blood tests before referring you to a rheumatologist (specialist) for confirmation of diagnosis.

Treating Rheumatoid Arthritis

There is no cure for rheumatoid arthritis, but early diagnosis and appropriate treatment enables many people to have periods between flares and lead full lives.

Disease-modifying Anti-rheumatic Drugs (DMARDs)

DMARDs are the main treatment for rheumatoid arthritis. They work by blocking the effects of chemicals released when the immune system attacks the joints.

Common DMARDs include methotrexate, leflunomide, hydroxychloroquine and sulfasalazine.

Biological Treatments

Biological treatments are newer medications that are usually taken in combination with DMARDs. They include etanercept, infliximab, adalimumab and others.

Pain Relief

Additional medications for pain relief include:

  • Painkillers (paracetamol, co-codamol)
  • Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)
  • Corticosteroids (for short-term relief)

Supportive Treatments

Other treatments that can help include:

  • Physiotherapy
  • Occupational therapy
  • Podiatry
  • Surgery (in severe cases)

Treatment Success: With modern treatments, most people with rheumatoid arthritis can control their symptoms and prevent joint damage, allowing them to continue regular activities and employment.

Living with Rheumatoid Arthritis

Taking control of rheumatoid arthritis will help you cope with its impact on your lifestyle.

Self-management

Self-management courses can teach techniques for living positively with arthritis, including relaxation, goal-setting, and positive thinking.

Emotional Wellbeing

The unpredictable nature of rheumatoid arthritis can be challenging. It's common to experience frustration, fear, or depression. Speak to your healthcare team if you're struggling emotionally.

Support and Relationships

Many people find it helpful to talk to others in a similar position. Patient organizations have local support groups and helplines.

Work and Finances

If rheumatoid arthritis affects your ability to work, you may be entitled to financial support such as Statutory Sick Pay, Employment and Support Allowance, or Personal Independence Payment.

Complications of Rheumatoid Arthritis

Having rheumatoid arthritis can put you at a higher risk of developing other conditions, particularly if it isn't well controlled.

Common Complications

  • Carpal tunnel syndrome – compression of nerves in the wrist
  • Widespread inflammation – affecting lungs, heart, eyes, or blood vessels
  • Joint damage – permanent damage to bones, cartilage, and tendons
  • Cardiovascular disease – increased risk of heart attacks and strokes
  • Cervical myelopathy – spinal cord compression in the neck

Important: Ensuring that rheumatoid arthritis is well controlled through proper treatment helps reduce your risk of these complications. Regular check-ups with your healthcare team are essential.

Thanks to early treatment, serious complications from rheumatoid arthritis are becoming less common.
