About Womb Cancer

Cancer of the womb (uterus) is a common cancer that affects the female reproductive system. It's also called uterine cancer and endometrial cancer.

Abnormal vaginal bleeding is the most common symptom of womb cancer.

Important: If you have been through the menopause, any vaginal bleeding is considered abnormal. If you have not yet been through the menopause, unusual bleeding may include bleeding between your periods.

You should speak to your GP as soon as possible if you experience any unusual vaginal bleeding. While it's unlikely to be caused by womb cancer, it's best to be sure.

Types of womb cancer

The vast majority of womb cancers begin in the cells that make up the lining of the womb (called the endometrium), which is why cancer of the womb is often called endometrial cancer.

In rare cases, womb cancer can start in the muscles surrounding the womb. This type of cancer is called uterine sarcoma and may be treated differently from endometrial cancer.

Womb cancer is separate from other cancers of the female reproductive system, such as ovarian cancer and cervical cancer.

Symptoms of Womb Cancer

The most common symptom of womb cancer is abnormal bleeding from the vagina, although most people with abnormal bleeding don't have cancer.

Bleeding may start as light bleeding accompanied by a watery discharge, which may get heavier over time.

Symptoms include:

  • Post-menopausal: Any vaginal bleeding after menopause
  • Pre-menopausal: Periods that are heavier than usual or bleeding between periods
  • Pain in the lower abdomen
  • Pain during sex

Advanced stage symptoms:

  • Pain in the back, legs, or pelvis
  • Loss of appetite
  • Tiredness
  • Nausea

When to seek medical advice: If you have postmenopausal vaginal bleeding, or notice a change in the normal pattern of your period, visit your GP immediately.

Causes of Womb Cancer

It's not known exactly what causes womb cancer, but certain things can increase your risk of developing it.

Risk factors include:

  • Age: Risk increases with age (most cases occur in women aged 40-74)
  • Oestrogen levels: High levels of unopposed oestrogen
  • Obesity: Being overweight or obese significantly increases risk
  • Reproductive history: Not having children
  • Medical conditions: Diabetes, polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS)
  • Medications: Tamoxifen treatment, oestrogen-only HRT
  • Endometrial hyperplasia: Thickening of the womb lining

Prevention: Maintaining a healthy weight and using certain types of contraception may help reduce your risk of developing womb cancer.

Diagnosing Womb Cancer

Your GP will probably carry out a physical examination of your pelvic area and may refer you to a specialist for further tests.

Diagnostic tests include:

  • Transvaginal ultrasound (TVU): Uses a small probe to check the thickness of the womb lining
  • Biopsy: Taking a sample of cells from the womb lining
  • Hysteroscopy: Using a thin telescope to look inside the womb
  • Blood tests: To check for tumour markers and general health

Further tests if cancer is confirmed:

  • Chest X-ray
  • MRI scan
  • CT scan
  • Additional blood tests

Treating Womb Cancer

Treatment depends on the stage of the cancer and your individual circumstances.

Cancer stages:

  • Stage 1: Cancer contained inside the womb
  • Stage 2: Cancer spread to the cervix
  • Stage 3: Cancer spread outside the womb to nearby tissues
  • Stage 4: Cancer spread to other organs

Treatment options:

  • Surgery: Hysterectomy (removal of the womb)
  • Radiotherapy: Using radiation to kill cancer cells
  • Chemotherapy: Using drugs to kill cancer cells
  • Hormone therapy: For cancers affected by hormones

For younger women: If you haven't been through menopause and wish to have children, hormone therapy may be an option instead of hysterectomy in specific circumstances.

Living with Womb Cancer

Recovery from womb cancer treatment takes time and support.

Recovery and follow-up:

  • Recovery from hysterectomy takes 6-12 weeks
  • Regular check-ups after treatment completion
  • Managing fatigue and other treatment side effects

Relationships and emotional support:

  • Open communication with family and friends
  • Potential impact on sex life and relationships
  • Access to counselling and support groups
  • Financial support options

Support is available: Many organizations provide support for people living with womb cancer, including counseling, financial advice, and peer support groups.
