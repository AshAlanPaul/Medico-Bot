What is Depression?

Everyone has spells of feeling down, but depression is more than just spending a few days feeling sad or unhappy. Depression can make you feel persistently sad and down for weeks or months at a time.

While some people believe that depression is trivial or not a genuine health problem, it's actually a real condition that affects around one in 10 people over the course of their lives. It impacts people of all genders and ages – including children.

Do I have depression?

Depression has a range of different symptoms, and it can affect everybody differently. The symptoms include feeling very tearful, feeling hopelessness and sadness, and losing interest in things you enjoyed before.

Important: Physical symptoms happen with depression too – these can include feeling tired all the time, getting poor sleep, losing your sex drive, losing your appetite, and feeling aches and pains.

Emergency: Severe symptoms of depression can make people feel suicidal – as if life is no longer worth living. If you're experiencing these thoughts, seek immediate help.

Symptoms and Causes of Depression

Symptoms of depression can be very different from person to person. However, as a general rule, if you are depressed you feel hopeless, sad and lacking interest in things that used to make you feel happy.

Levels of Depression

  • Mild depression – has some impact on daily life
  • Moderate depression – has a significant impact on your daily life
  • Severe depression – makes it nearly impossible to get through daily life

Psychological Symptoms

  • Continuous sadness or low mood
  • Losing interest in things
  • Losing motivation
  • Feeling tearful, guilty, or anxious
  • Low self-esteem
  • Thinking about suicide or self-harm

Physical Symptoms

  • Speaking or moving slower than usual
  • Unexplained aches and pains
  • Changes in appetite or weight
  • Disturbed sleep patterns
  • Loss of energy and constant fatigue

Common Causes

Depression doesn't have one single cause – it can have a range of triggers:

  • Stressful life events (bereavement, divorce, job loss)
  • Family history of depression
  • Long-term health conditions
  • Personality traits and early life experiences
  • Alcohol and drug misuse

Diagnosing and Treating Depression

There's no physical test for depression. Diagnosis is based on your symptoms and how they affect your daily life.

When to see a doctor: If you experience depression symptoms most of the day, every day, for more than two weeks, you should visit your GP.

Talking Therapies

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) – helps change negative thought patterns
  • Interpersonal Therapy (IPT) – focuses on relationship issues
  • Counselling – provides support during life crises

Medication

Antidepressants can help relieve symptoms of depression:

  • SSRIs (Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors)
  • TCAs (Tricyclic Antidepressants)
  • SNRIs (Serotonin-Noradrenaline Reuptake Inhibitors)

Note: Antidepressants aren't addictive, but you should never stop taking them suddenly. Always consult your doctor before making any changes to your medication.

Living with Depression

There are many strategies that can help you manage depression in daily life.

Self-Care Strategies

  • Maintain a regular routine
  • Stay connected with friends and family
  • Engage in physical activity
  • Practice relaxation techniques
  • Avoid alcohol and recreational drugs

Work and Depression

If work is contributing to your depression, consider:

  • Speaking to your employer about adjustments
  • Exploring flexible working arrangements
  • Taking time off if needed

Suicide Warning Signs: If someone talks about death or suicide, makes final arrangements, or shows a sudden lifting of mood after being depressed, seek immediate help.

Psychotic Depression

Some people with severe depression also experience symptoms of psychosis, including hallucinations and delusions.

Symptoms of Psychosis

  • Delusions – false beliefs not based on reality
  • Hallucinations – hearing, seeing, or feeling things that aren't there
  • Psychomotor agitation – inability to sit still

Emergency: If you're worried about someone showing signs of psychotic depression, contact their GP or take them to A&E immediately.

Depression Self-Help Guide

Learning healthy thought patterns and coping skills is an important part of managing depression.

Self-help strategies: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) techniques can be highly effective in helping people with depression develop better coping mechanisms.

Immediate Help Resources

  • Samaritans: 116 123 (24/7)
  • Your GP: First point of contact for treatment
  • NHS 111: For urgent medical advice
  • A&E: For immediate danger to life
