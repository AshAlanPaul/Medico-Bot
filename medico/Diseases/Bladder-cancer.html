About Bladder Cancer

Bladder cancer is where a growth of abnormal tissue, known as a tumour, develops in the bladder lining. In some cases, the tumour spreads into the surrounding muscles.

The most common symptom of bladder cancer is blood in your urine, which is usually painless.

Important: If you notice blood in your urine, even if it comes and goes, you should speak to your GP so the cause can be investigated.

Types of Bladder Cancer

  • Non-muscle-invasive bladder cancer - Cancerous cells contained inside the lining of the bladder (most common type)
  • Muscle-invasive bladder cancer - Cancerous cells spread beyond the lining into surrounding muscles
  • Metastatic bladder cancer - Cancer has spread to other parts of the body

Who is Affected?

Bladder cancer is more common in older adults, with more than half of all new cases diagnosed in people aged 75 and over. It's also more common in men than in women.

Symptoms of Bladder Cancer

Blood in your urine is the most common symptom of bladder cancer. The medical name for this is haematuria and it's usually painless.

Common Symptoms

  • Blood in urine (may appear as streaks or turn urine brown)
  • Need to urinate more frequently
  • Sudden urges to urinate
  • Burning sensation when passing urine

Advanced Stage Symptoms

If bladder cancer reaches an advanced stage and begins to spread, symptoms can include:

  • Pelvic pain
  • Bone pain
  • Unintentional weight loss
  • Swelling of the legs

Note: Having blood in your urine doesn't mean you definitely have bladder cancer. There are other, more common causes including urinary tract infections, kidney stones, and enlarged prostate.

Causes of Bladder Cancer

Bladder cancer is caused by changes to the cells of the bladder. It's often linked with exposure to certain chemicals, but the cause isn't always known.

Main Risk Factors

  • Smoking - The single biggest risk factor, accounting for over a third of cases
  • Chemical exposure - Industrial chemicals used in manufacturing
  • Occupational risks - Jobs involving dyes, textiles, rubbers, paints, and plastics
  • Previous treatments - Radiotherapy or chemotherapy for other cancers
  • Chronic irritation - Long-term urinary tract infections or bladder stones

Important: People who smoke may be up to four times more likely to develop bladder cancer than non-smokers.

Diagnosing Bladder Cancer

If you have symptoms of bladder cancer, such as blood in your urine, you should speak to your GP who may refer you to a hospital for further tests.

Diagnostic Procedures

  • Cystoscopy - Examination of the bladder using a thin tube with a camera
  • Imaging scans - CT scans or MRI scans for detailed pictures
  • TURBT - Surgical procedure to remove tissue for testing
  • Urine tests - Checking for blood, bacteria, or abnormal cells

Staging and Grading

Once diagnosed, bladder cancer is classified by stage (how far it has spread) and grade (how likely it is to spread). The TNM system is commonly used:

  • T - How far into the bladder the tumour has grown
  • N - Whether cancer has spread to nearby lymph nodes
  • M - Whether cancer has spread to other parts of the body

Treating Bladder Cancer

Treatment options depend on how advanced the cancer is and whether it's non-muscle-invasive or muscle-invasive.

Non-Muscle-Invasive Bladder Cancer

  • TURBT - Surgical removal of tumours from the bladder lining
  • Chemotherapy - Medication placed directly into the bladder
  • BCG treatment - Immunotherapy to reduce recurrence risk

Muscle-Invasive Bladder Cancer

  • Cystectomy - Surgical removal of the bladder
  • Radiotherapy - With or without chemotherapy
  • Chemotherapy - To shrink tumours before other treatments

Treatment Team: Your care will be managed by a multidisciplinary team including urologists, oncologists, pathologists, radiologists, and clinical nurse specialists.

Advanced Bladder Cancer

For locally advanced or metastatic bladder cancer, treatment focuses on:

  • Chemotherapy to control cancer growth
  • Treatments to relieve symptoms
  • Palliative care for comfort and quality of life

Complications of Bladder Cancer

A diagnosis of bladder cancer, and some treatments for the condition, can have a significant impact on your life.

Emotional Impact

The emotional impact of living with bladder cancer can be significant. Many people experience depression and should seek support from healthcare professionals.

Urinary Diversion

If the bladder is removed, alternative ways of passing urine include:

  • Urostomy - Using an external bag to collect urine
  • Continent urinary diversion - Internal pouch emptied with a catheter
  • Bladder reconstruction - Creating a new bladder from bowel tissue

Sexual Problems

Both surgery and radiotherapy can cause:

  • Erectile dysfunction in men
  • Narrowing of the vagina in women
  • Changes in sexual function and intimacy

Support Available: Specialist nurses and support services can provide advice and treatment options for managing these complications.

Preventing Bladder Cancer

While it's not always possible to prevent bladder cancer, you can reduce your risk by addressing known risk factors.

Key Prevention Strategies

  • Stop smoking - The most effective way to reduce risk
  • Workplace safety - Follow safety protocols when working with chemicals
  • Healthy diet - High in fruits and vegetables, low in fat
  • Stay hydrated - Drink plenty of fluids

Healthy Lifestyle: A diet rich in fruits and vegetables and low in saturated fats can help prevent bladder cancer and other serious health conditions.

Diet Recommendations

Focus on:

  • Fresh fruits and vegetables (5 portions daily)
  • Whole grains
  • Foods high in unsaturated fats (oily fish, avocados, nuts)
  • Limited salt intake (no more than 6g daily)
  • Avoidance of saturated fats found in processed foods
