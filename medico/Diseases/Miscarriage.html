About Miscarriage

A miscarriage is the loss of your baby before 24 weeks of pregnancy. Early miscarriages happen in the first 12 weeks, while late miscarriages occur between 12 and 24 weeks.

Most of the time there's no clear reason why it happens, but it's very unlikely to be caused by anything you did or didn't do.

About 1 out of 5 pregnancies miscarry. Since many miscarriages aren't recorded, the actual figure might be higher.

Emergency: Always get medical help if:

  • You're bleeding from your vagina
  • You've got strong, cramping pain
  • Your waters break
  • Your baby's movements have changed, or you haven't felt them move for a while

Contact your midwife, local maternity unit, GP, or NHS 24 111 service immediately.

Causes and Symptoms

What Causes a Miscarriage?

Doctors think most miscarriages are caused when the chromosomes controlling the development of a baby aren't right. Babies with too many or not enough chromosomes won't develop properly.

Miscarriages can also be caused by:

  • Issues with your placenta
  • Cervical weakness - when your cervix (neck of your womb) starts to open

Early Miscarriage Symptoms

You might be having an early miscarriage if:

  • You're bleeding from your vagina
  • You have cramps in your lower abdomen - these can feel like bad period pains
  • There's fluid or tissue coming from your vagina
  • Your breasts are no longer tender and any morning sickness has passed

Note: Having some light bleeding is fairly common in the first 12 weeks of pregnancy and doesn't necessarily mean you're having a miscarriage. However, you should always contact your GP or midwife for advice.

Early vs Late Miscarriage

Early Miscarriages (First 12 Weeks)

Most women who miscarry do so in the first 12 weeks of their pregnancy. Many women have a miscarriage before they even know they're pregnant.

What happens during an early miscarriage:

  • You'll probably be asked to go to the early pregnancy unit at your local hospital
  • You'll have tests and usually an ultrasound scan
  • Your body usually completes the miscarriage naturally

Late Miscarriages (12-24 Weeks)

For many parents who lose their baby after a late miscarriage, the word 'miscarriage' doesn't properly express the impact of their loss.

Symptoms of a late miscarriage:

  • Bleeding from your vagina - this can be heavy with blood clots
  • Strong, cramping pains

What happens during a late miscarriage:

  • You may be asked to go to the maternity ward
  • You'll have tests and usually an ultrasound scan
  • You're likely to go through labour in hospital

Support Available: This is an understandably difficult time. There are many organisations that can support you, your baby's father and your family.

After a Miscarriage

Immediate Aftercare

Depending on your circumstances and stage of pregnancy, your midwife or doctor may ask if you'd like to see or hold your baby. This is a personal decision only you can make.

You'll probably have some bleeding for a week or two. If symptoms continue, it may mean that some pregnancy tissue is still in your womb, and you may need medical treatment.

Time Off Work

Many women want to take time off work after having a miscarriage:

  • Before 24 weeks: Entitled to sick leave and any sick pay you'd normally qualify for
  • After 24 weeks: Entitled to maternity leave and any maternity pay you qualify for

Repeated Miscarriages

Most women go on to have successful pregnancies after a miscarriage:

  • 1 or 2 miscarriages: You're not more likely than anyone else to have another one
  • 3 or more miscarriages: Your GP can refer you to a specialist to see whether there's a specific cause

Support Resources: Remember you're not alone. There are many support organizations available to help you through this difficult time.
