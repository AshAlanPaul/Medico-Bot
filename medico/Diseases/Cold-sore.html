Cold Sores Information

About Cold Sores Symptoms Causes Treatment Prevention

About Cold Sores

Cold sores are small blisters that develop on the lips or around the mouth. They're caused by the herpes simplex virus and usually clear up without treatment within 7 to 10 days.

You may not have any symptoms when you first become infected with the herpes simplex virus. An outbreak of cold sores may happen some time later.

Cold sores often start with a tingling, itching or burning sensation around your mouth. Small fluid-filled sores then appear, usually on the edges of your lower lip.

When to Get Professional Advice

Cold sores aren't usually serious and can be treated by a pharmacist. You only need to visit your pharmacist if you're unsure whether it's a cold sore or if it's severe and spreading further than just the lip.

See your pharmacist if a cold sore hasn't healed after 7 to 10 days.

Symptoms of Cold Sores

Primary Infection Symptoms

In children, symptoms of the primary infection may include:

  • Swollen and irritated gums with small, painful sores in and around the mouth
  • Sore throat and swollen glands
  • Producing more saliva than normal
  • High temperature (fever) of 38C (100.4F) or above
  • Dehydration
  • Feeling sick (nausea)
  • Headaches

Recurrent Infections (Cold Sores)

Recurrent infections usually last for less time and are less severe than the primary infection:

  • Starts with tingling, itching or burning sensation
  • Small fluid-filled sores develop on lip edges
  • Sores may ooze before crusting over within 48 hours
  • Most disappear within 7 to 10 days without treatment
  • Usually heal without scarring

Causes of Cold Sores

Cold sores are usually caused by the herpes simplex virus type 1 (HSV-1).

The virus passes through the skin and travels up the nerves, where it lies inactive (dormant) until it's triggered at a later date.

Cold Sore Triggers

  • Having another infection, such as a respiratory tract infection
  • Having a high temperature (fever)
  • Emotional upset or psychological stress
  • Tiredness and fatigue
  • An injury to the affected area
  • Menstruation (periods)
  • Strong sunlight

In many cases there's no obvious trigger for an outbreak.

Treating Cold Sores

Cold sores usually clear up without treatment within 7 to 10 days. Antiviral tablets or cream can be used to ease your symptoms and speed up the healing time.

General Advice

  • Drink plenty of fluids to avoid dehydration
  • Avoid acidic or salty foods and eat cool, soft foods
  • If brushing teeth is painful, use an antiseptic mouthwash
  • Dab creams on to sores rather than rubbing them in
  • Wash hands before and after applying cold sore creams

Treatment Options

Antiviral creams: Such as aciclovir or penciclovir may speed up healing if applied as soon as the first signs appear.

Cold sore patches: Contain hydrocolloid gel and are placed over the cold sore while it heals.

Antiviral tablets: Generally more effective than creams but usually only prescribed for more severe cases.

Pain relief: Ibuprofen or paracetamol can help manage pain.

Important: Antiviral creams are only effective if you apply them as soon as the first signs of a cold sore appear. Using them after this initial period is unlikely to have much effect.

Preventing Infection

It's not possible to prevent infection with the herpes simplex virus or prevent outbreaks of cold sores, but you can take steps to minimise the spread of infection.

Cold sores are at their most contagious when they burst, but remain contagious until they're completely healed.

Prevention Tips

  • Avoid touching cold sores unless applying cream
  • Always wash hands before and after applying cream
  • Do not share cold sore creams or medication with others
  • Do not share items that come into contact with the affected area
  • Avoid kissing and oral sex until cold sores have completely healed
  • Be particularly careful around newborn babies, pregnant women and people with low immunity
  • Use sun block lip balm (SPF 15 or higher) if sunlight triggers your cold sores

Complications: In rare cases, cold sores can cause complications. People with weak immune systems are particularly at risk. If you develop severe symptoms or complications, seek medical advice.
