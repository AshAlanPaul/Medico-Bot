About Pleurisy

Pleurisy is inflammation of the sheet-like layers that cover the lungs (the pleura).

What is Pleurisy?

The pleura are two thin sheets of tissue that separate the lungs and ribcage. One is attached to the ribcage and the other is attached to the lungs.

Between the pleural sheets is a thin layer of liquid that lubricates the pleura, helping to reduce friction when you breathe in and out.

Note: When there's inflammation, the surfaces of the two layers can become rough and the fluid can become sticky. This causes the layers to rub together, resulting in pain and discomfort.

Symptoms of Pleurisy

The most common symptom of pleurisy is a sharp chest pain when breathing deeply.

Common Symptoms

  • Sharp chest pain when breathing deeply
  • Pain that may be felt in the shoulder
  • Pain that worsens when coughing, sneezing, or moving
  • Pain relief when taking shallow breaths
  • Shortness of breath
  • Dry cough
  • Fever (if caused by infection)
  • Rapid, shallow breathing

Causes of Pleurisy

Pleurisy is usually caused by another condition, such as an infection, but sometimes no cause can be identified.

Infections (Most Common Cause)

Viral infections are usually responsible for pleurisy:

  • Influenza (flu) virus
  • Epstein-Barr virus (causes glandular fever)
  • Cytomegalovirus (CMV)
  • Parainfluenza virus

Bacterial infections can also cause pleurisy:

  • Streptococcal bacteria (associated with pneumonia)
  • Staphylococcal bacteria
  • MRSA (in hospital patients)

Other Causes

  • Pulmonary embolism (blood clot in the lungs)
  • Lung cancer
  • Autoimmune conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, lupus)
  • Rib injuries or fractures
  • Sickle cell anemia
  • Chemotherapy or radiotherapy
  • HIV or AIDS
  • Mesothelioma (cancer caused by asbestos)

Risk factors: People aged 65 years and over are most at risk because they're more likely to develop chest infections.

Diagnosing Pleurisy

Pleurisy can usually be diagnosed by studying your symptoms. Your GP can listen to your chest to check for the distinctive dry, crunching sound that suggests pleurisy.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Blood tests - to check for infection or autoimmune conditions
  • Chest X-rays - to examine the lungs and pleural space
  • Ultrasound scan - to visualize the pleura and any fluid buildup
  • CT scan - for detailed images of the chest
  • Biopsy - small sample of pleural or lung tissue for testing
  • Thoracentesis - removal of pleural fluid for analysis

Treating Pleurisy

Treatment for pleurisy usually involves relieving pain and treating the underlying cause of the condition.

Positive outlook: If treated promptly, pleurisy often resolves without any lasting damage to the lungs.

Pain Management

  • NSAIDs - such as ibuprofen to reduce inflammation and pain
  • Other painkillers - paracetamol or codeine if NSAIDs are unsuitable
  • Positioning - lying on the painful side may help reduce discomfort
  • Breathing exercises - to maintain lung function

Treating Underlying Causes

  • Antibiotics - for bacterial infections
  • Antiviral medication - if appropriate for viral infections
  • Treatment for specific conditions - such as autoimmune disorders or cancer
  • Hospital care - for severe cases or people in poor health

Pleural Effusion Treatment

In some cases, pleurisy causes a build-up of excess fluid around the lungs called pleural effusion.

  • Thoracentesis - drainage of excess fluid with a needle
  • Chest tube insertion - for larger fluid accumulations
  • Pleurodesis - procedure to prevent fluid from reaccumulating
  • Surgery - in rare, persistent cases

Recovery: Most people recover from pleurisy within a few days to two weeks, depending on the underlying cause and treatment effectiveness.
