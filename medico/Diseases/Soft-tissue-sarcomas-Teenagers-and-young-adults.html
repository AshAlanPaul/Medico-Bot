Introduction to Soft Tissue Sarcoma

Soft tissue sarcomas are cancers that develop from cells in the soft, supporting tissues of the body. These cancers can affect teenagers and young adults, with the most common types being:

  • Rhabdomyosarcomas - develop from muscle tissue
  • Synovial sarcomas - develop near joints
  • Soft tissue Ewing's sarcoma - treated similarly to bone Ewing's sarcoma
  • Fibrosarcomas - develop from fibrous tissue

Signs and Symptoms

Symptoms depend on where the sarcoma is located:

  • Arms or legs: Lump or swelling (usually painless)
  • Abdomen: Painful, swollen tummy, constipation
  • Bladder: Lower abdominal pain, difficulty urinating, blood in urine
  • Chest: Breathlessness, cough, chest pain
  • Head or neck: Lump, nasal blockage or discharge, eye swelling

Important: Most people with these symptoms won't have a sarcoma. If you're concerned, speak to your GP who can examine you and refer you to a specialist if needed.

Causes

The exact cause of sarcomas in teenagers and young adults is unknown. It's important to remember that nothing you've done has caused the cancer.

Understanding Soft Tissue

The soft tissues of the body connect, surround and support other body tissue and organs. Soft tissue includes:

  • Muscle
  • Cartilage
  • Fat
  • Fibrous tissue
  • Nerves
  • Blood vessels

Types of Soft Tissue Sarcoma

Rhabdomyosarcomas

Develop from muscle and surrounding tissue. Two main types affect younger people:

  • Alveolar rhabdomyosarcomas: More common in teenagers, found in limbs or torso
  • Embryonal rhabdomyosarcomas: More common in children, found around eyes, throat, bladder

Synovial Sarcomas

Usually develop near joints (knee, elbow) but can occur anywhere. Appear as hard lumps.

Fibrosarcomas

Start in fibrocytes (tissue that joins body parts together). Usually found in arms, legs, or torso.

Note: Some rare genetic conditions can increase sarcoma risk. Previous radiotherapy treatment in childhood may also increase risk.

Tests and Diagnosis

If you have symptoms that could indicate a soft tissue sarcoma, your GP will examine you and may arrange initial tests like X-rays. If needed, you'll be referred to a specialist.

Hospital Tests

  • X-rays - Basic imaging to examine bones and tissues
  • Bone scans - Detailed imaging of bones
  • Ultrasound scans - Uses sound waves to create images
  • MRI, CT or PET scans - Advanced imaging techniques
  • Biopsies - Tissue sample examination
  • Bone marrow tests - Check bone marrow health

Additional Tests

If sarcoma is confirmed, further tests check overall health:

  • Blood tests
  • Heart function tests (echocardiogram, ECG)
  • Kidney function tests

Support: Waiting for test results can be stressful. Talking to family, friends, and your medical team can help during this time.

Treatment Options

The three main treatments for soft tissue sarcomas are surgery, chemotherapy, and radiotherapy. Most people have a combination of treatments tailored to their specific situation.

Chemotherapy

Anti-cancer drugs used to:

  • Shrink tumors before surgery
  • Reduce risk of cancer returning after surgery
  • Treat cancer cells that have spread

Common Chemotherapy Drugs

  • Ifosfamide
  • Vincristine
  • Actinomycin D
  • Doxorubicin
  • Cyclophosphamide
  • Cisplatin

Surgery Options

Wide Local Excision

Removes cancer and surrounding tissue to ensure complete removal.

Limb-Sparing Surgery

Removes tumor while preserving the limb, sometimes using metal implants or artificial joints.

Amputation

Only considered when no other options exist to remove the cancer completely.

Radiotherapy

Uses high-energy X-rays to destroy cancer cells. Given before or after surgery, or when surgery isn't possible.

Side Effects: All treatments have potential side effects. Your medical team will help manage these and provide support throughout treatment.

Living with Soft Tissue Sarcoma

Life after sarcoma treatment involves adaptation and recovery. The experience varies depending on the treatment received.

After Limb-Sparing Surgery

The affected limb might not work as well as before. Physiotherapy is crucial for recovery and regaining function.

Joint Replacements

Most normal activities are possible, but high-impact sports might need to be avoided to protect the new joint.

Life After Amputation

Modern prosthetics (artificial limbs) allow for most activities including sports and swimming. Different prosthetics can be used for different activities.

Emotional Support

It's normal to experience various emotions including anger, sadness, or depression. Support is available through:

  • Family and friends
  • Professional counsellors
  • Support groups with other young people
  • Your medical team

Remember: Recovery takes time, and it's okay to ask for help. Many people go on to live full, active lives after sarcoma treatment.
