Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS)

Ankylosing spondylitis (AS) is a long-term (chronic) condition in which the spine and other areas of the body become inflamed.

Main Symptoms

  • Back pain and stiffness that improves with exercise and isn't relieved by rest
  • Pain and swelling in other parts of the body, such as hips, knees and ribs
  • Fatigue (extreme tiredness)

Important: Symptoms develop gradually over months or years and may come and go over time. In some people, the condition gets better with time, but for others it can slowly worsen.

When to Seek Medical Advice

See your GP if you have persistent symptoms of AS. They should refer you to a rheumatologist for further tests and treatment.

Symptoms of AS

Main Symptoms

Back Pain and Stiffness

  • Pain improves with exercise but doesn't improve with rest
  • Pain and stiffness worse in morning and at night
  • May wake you up regularly during the night
  • Pain in the area around buttocks

Arthritis

Inflammation of joints in other parts of the body:

  • Pain on moving affected joints
  • Tenderness when examined
  • Swelling and warmth in affected areas

Enthesitis

Painful inflammation where bone joins to tendons or ligaments:

  • Top of shin bone
  • Behind heel (Achilles tendon)
  • Under heel
  • Where ribs join to breast bone

Fatigue

Extreme tiredness and lack of energy are common symptoms.

Causes of AS

In AS, several parts of the lower spine become inflamed. Over time, this can damage the spine and lead to bone fusion (ankylosis).

HLA-B27 Gene

  • More than 90% of people with AS carry the HLA-B27 gene
  • Having the gene doesn't guarantee you'll develop AS
  • About 8% of general population has the gene but most don't have AS
  • The gene may make you more vulnerable, triggered by environmental factors

Inheritance

  • AS can run in families
  • If you have AS and HLA-B27 gene: child's risk less than 20%
  • If you have AS without HLA-B27: child's risk less than 10%
  • Close relative with AS increases your risk 3x

Diagnosing AS

AS can be difficult to diagnose as it develops slowly and there's no definitive test.

Blood Tests

Check for signs of inflammation in the body. If positive, referral to rheumatologist.

Imaging Tests

  • X-rays – Show damage to sacroiliac joints and new bone formation
  • MRI scan – Highlights early changes and inflammation
  • Ultrasound scan – Detects inflammation in tendons and ligaments

Genetic Testing

Blood test for HLA-B27 gene - contributes to diagnosis but not definitive.

Diagnosis Confirmation

Confirmed if X-ray shows sacroiliitis plus at least one of:

  • 3+ months of lower back pain improving with exercise
  • Limited movement in lower back
  • Limited chest expansion for age and sex

Treating AS

No cure exists, but treatment can relieve symptoms and delay spine fusion.

Physiotherapy and Exercise

  • Group exercise programmes
  • Individual exercise programmes
  • Massage (soft tissues only)
  • Hydrotherapy (water exercises)

Medications

Painkillers

  • NSAIDs – Ibuprofen, naproxen, diclofenac
  • Paracetamol – Alternative if NSAIDs unsuitable
  • Codeine – Stronger pain relief when needed

Anti-TNF Medication

For severe cases not controlled by other treatments:

  • Adalimumab, etanercept, golimumab
  • Given by injection
  • Reduces inflammation by blocking TNF
  • Requires careful monitoring due to infection risks

Other Treatments

  • Corticosteroids – For severe inflammation
  • DMARDs – For joint inflammation outside spine

Surgery

Rarely needed but may include:

  • Joint replacement for severely damaged joints
  • Corrective surgery for badly bent spine (very rare)

Complications & Outlook

Outlook

  • Highly variable between individuals
  • 70-90% remain fully independent or minimally disabled
  • Some may become severely disabled due to bone fusion
  • Modern treatments don't significantly affect life expectancy

Potential Complications

  • Osteoporosis (weakening of bones)
  • Spinal fractures
  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Severe spinal curvature
  • Joint damage in hips, knees

Regular follow-up: As symptoms develop slowly and fluctuate, regular check-ups with your rheumatologist or GP are essential for monitoring and adjusting treatment.
