About Indigestion

Indigestion can be pain or discomfort in your upper abdomen (dyspepsia) or burning pain behind the breastbone (heartburn).

Dyspepsia and heartburn may occur together or on their own. Symptoms usually appear soon after eating or drinking.

Common associated symptoms include:

  • feeling full or bloated
  • feeling sick (nausea)
  • belching
  • bringing up (regurgitating) fluid or food into the gullet (oesophagus)

Indigestion is a common problem that affects many people, but in most cases it's mild and only occurs occasionally.

Why it happens

Indigestion may be caused by stomach acid coming into contact with the sensitive, protective lining of the digestive system (mucosa). The stomach acid breaks down the lining, leading to irritation and inflammation, which can be painful.

The majority of people with indigestion don't have inflammation in their digestive system. Therefore, their symptoms are thought to be caused by increased sensitivity of the mucosa (to acidity or stretching).

In most cases indigestion is related to eating, although it can be triggered by other factors such as smoking, drinking, alcohol, pregnancy, stress or taking certain medications.

When to get professional advice: Most people will not need to seek medical advice for their indigestion. However, see your GP if you have recurring indigestion and are 55 years old or over, have lost weight without meaning to, or have difficulty swallowing.

Symptoms of Indigestion

The main symptom of indigestion is pain or a feeling of discomfort in your upper abdomen (dyspepsia). People often experience the associated feeling of burning behind the breastbone (heartburn), but this may occur on its own.

These symptoms usually come on soon after eating or drinking, although there can sometimes be a delay between eating a meal and experiencing indigestion.

Heartburn is caused by acid that passes from your stomach into your gullet (oesophagus).

If you have indigestion, you may also have symptoms such as:

  • feeling uncomfortably full or heavy
  • belching or flatulence (passing wind)
  • bringing food or fluid back up from your stomach (reflux)
  • bloating
  • feeling sick (nausea)
  • vomiting

Causes of Indigestion

Indigestion has a number of different causes, but it's rarely due to a serious, underlying condition.

It's normal for your stomach to produce acid, but sometimes this acid can irritate the lining of your stomach, the top part of your bowel (duodenum) or your gullet (oesophagus).

This irritation can be painful and often causes a burning sensation. Indigestion may also be due to the lining of your digestive system being overly sensitive to acid, or the 'stretching' caused by eating.

Common Causes

  • Medications: Some medicines can affect your digestive tract and cause indigestion
  • Obesity: Increased pressure inside your stomach can lead to acid reflux
  • Stress or anxiety: Can contribute to symptoms of indigestion
  • Hiatus hernia: When part of your stomach pushes up into your diaphragm
  • Helicobacter pylori infection: A common bacterial infection
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD): One of the main causes of recurring indigestion
  • Stomach ulcers: Open sores that develop on the stomach lining

Important: Do not take NSAIDs, such as aspirin and ibuprofen, if you have stomach problems or have had stomach ulcers in the past. Never stop taking prescribed medication without consulting your healthcare professional.

Diagnosing Indigestion

For most people, indigestion (dyspepsia) is mild and infrequent, and does not require treatment from a healthcare professional.

However, if you have indigestion regularly, or if it causes you severe pain or discomfort, see your pharmacist.

They will ask about your indigestion symptoms, as well as:

  • any other symptoms you have - which may indicate an underlying health condition
  • any medication you are taking - as some medications can cause indigestion
  • your lifestyle - as some lifestyle factors can cause indigestion

Further Investigations

Depending on your symptoms, your healthcare provider may recommend:

  • Endoscopy: To examine the inside of your digestive system
  • H. pylori testing: Through stool, breath, or blood tests
  • Liver function tests: To rule out liver-related issues
  • Abdominal ultrasound: To examine abdominal organs

Treating Indigestion

Treatment for indigestion (dyspepsia) will vary, depending on what is causing it and how severe your symptoms are.

Diet and Lifestyle Changes

For occasional indigestion, simple changes may help:

  • Healthy weight: Being overweight puts more pressure on your stomach
  • Stop smoking: Chemicals in cigarette smoke can contribute to indigestion
  • Diet adjustments: Avoid rich, spicy, fatty foods and caffeine
  • Bedtime habits: Avoid eating 3-4 hours before bed
  • Stress management: Practice relaxation techniques

Medications

For immediate relief, your pharmacist may recommend:

  • Antacids: Neutralize stomach acid
  • Alginates: Form a protective barrier in the stomach
  • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs): Reduce acid production
  • H2-receptor antagonists: Lower stomach acidity

H. pylori Treatment

If caused by H. pylori infection, treatment typically involves:

  • Two different antibiotics
  • A proton pump inhibitor (PPI)
  • 7-day course of treatment

Medical advice: Always consult with a healthcare professional before starting any treatment. Some medications can interact with other drugs you may be taking.

Complications of Indigestion

In most cases, indigestion (dyspepsia) is mild and only occurs occasionally. However, severe indigestion can cause complications.

Oesophageal Stricture

Long-term acid reflux can cause your oesophagus to become scarred and narrowed. Symptoms include:

  • Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia)
  • Food that becomes lodged in your throat
  • Chest pain

Pyloric Stenosis

Occurs when the passage between your stomach and small intestine becomes scarred and narrowed, causing vomiting and preventing proper digestion.

Barrett's Oesophagus

Repeated episodes of GORD can lead to changes in the cells lining your lower oesophagus. This is a pre-cancerous condition that requires monitoring.

Prevention: Managing indigestion symptoms early and making appropriate lifestyle changes can help prevent these complications from developing.
