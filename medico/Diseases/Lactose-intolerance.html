About Lactose Intolerance

Lactose intolerance is a common digestive problem where the body is unable to digest lactose, a type of sugar mainly found in milk and dairy products.

Symptoms of lactose intolerance usually develop within a few hours of consuming food or drink that contains lactose.

Common Symptoms

  • Flatulence (wind)
  • Diarrhoea
  • Bloated stomach
  • Stomach cramps and pains
  • Stomach rumbling
  • Feeling sick

The severity of your symptoms and when they appear depends on the amount of lactose you've consumed.

Important: The symptoms of lactose intolerance can be similar to several other conditions, so it's important to see your GP for a diagnosis before removing milk and dairy products from your diet.

Causes of Lactose Intolerance

Lactose intolerance is usually the result of your body not producing enough lactase, an enzyme normally produced in your small intestine that's used to digest lactose.

Types of Lactase Deficiency

Primary Lactase Deficiency

This is the most common cause of lactose intolerance worldwide, caused by an inherited genetic fault that runs in families.

Secondary Lactase Deficiency

This is a shortage of lactase caused by a problem in your small intestine. It can occur at any age and may be the result of:

  • Gastroenteritis
  • Coeliac disease
  • Crohn's disease
  • Ulcerative colitis
  • Chemotherapy
  • Long courses of antibiotics

Congenital Lactase Deficiency

A rare condition that runs in families and is found in newborn babies, caused by an inherited genetic fault.

Developmental Lactase Deficiency

Some babies born prematurely have temporary lactose intolerance because their small intestine wasn't fully developed.

Diagnosing Lactose Intolerance

It's important to visit your GP if you think you or your child may have lactose intolerance, as the symptoms can be similar to other conditions.

Diagnostic Tests

Hydrogen Breath Test

A simple way of determining if you may be lactose intolerant by measuring hydrogen levels in your breath after consuming lactose.

Lactose Tolerance Test

Measures blood sugar levels after consuming lactose to see if your body is able to break it down properly.

Milk Tolerance Test

Similar to the lactose tolerance test but uses regular milk instead of lactose solution.

Small Bowel Biopsy

Rarely used, but may be carried out to see if symptoms are being caused by another condition like coeliac disease.

Tip: Before seeing your GP, keep a diary of what you eat and drink, and what symptoms you experience. This can help identify patterns.

Treating Lactose Intolerance

There's no cure for lactose intolerance, but most people are able to control their symptoms by making changes to their diet.

Dietary Changes

Cutting down on or avoiding sources of lactose and replacing them with lactose-free alternatives is usually enough to control symptoms.

Foods to Avoid or Limit

  • Milk (cow's, goat's, sheep's)
  • Butter, ice cream, and cheese
  • Some processed foods containing hidden lactose
  • Certain medications containing lactose

Lactose-Free Alternatives

  • Soya milks, yoghurts and cheeses
  • Milks made from rice, oats, almonds, coconut
  • Foods labeled 'dairy-free' or 'suitable for vegans'

Calcium Sources

Since dairy products are rich in calcium, it's important to include alternative sources in your diet:

  • Green leafy vegetables (spinach, kale, broccoli)
  • Soya beans and tofu
  • Nuts
  • Fish with edible bones (sardines, salmon)

Lactase Substitutes

Available as liquid drops, tablets or capsules that contain lactase substitutes to help digest lactose more easily.

Complications of Lactose Intolerance

Milk and dairy products contain calcium, protein and vitamins essential for healthy bones and overall health.

Potential Complications

  • Osteopenia - very low bone-mineral density
  • Osteoporosis - thin and weak bones with increased fracture risk
  • Malnutrition - when food doesn't provide essential nutrients

Professional Help: If you're concerned that dietary restrictions are putting you at risk of complications, consult a dietitian who can advise you on your diet and whether you require food supplements.

Your GP should be able to refer you to an NHS dietitian free of charge, or you can contact a private dietitian through the British Dietetic Association.
