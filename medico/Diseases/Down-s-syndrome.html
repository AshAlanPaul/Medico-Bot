About Down's Syndrome

Down's syndrome is a genetic condition where a baby is born with an extra copy of chromosome 21 in some or all of their cells. This is also known as Trisomy 21.

The additional genetic material affects development and causes the characteristic features associated with Down's syndrome.

Key Facts

  • It's the most common chromosomal condition
  • Occurs in about 1 in every 1,000 babies born
  • Can affect people of all ethnic and social backgrounds
  • The risk increases with maternal age, but can occur at any age
  • It's a lifelong condition but not a disease

Important: People with Down's syndrome can lead active, healthy and independent lives into their 60s, 70s and beyond with proper support and opportunities.

Living with Down's Syndrome

There are increasing opportunities and support in education, work and housing for people with Down's syndrome, enabling them to live fulfilling lives as part of their families and communities.

Education and Development

  • Most people have mild to moderate learning disabilities
  • Some may have more complex needs
  • Many children attend mainstream schools with additional support
  • Early intervention programs can significantly help development

Independence and Lifestyle

With proper support, people with Down's syndrome can:

  • Live independently or with minimal support
  • Have meaningful employment
  • Form relationships and social connections
  • Participate in community activities
  • Develop hobbies and interests

Positive outlook: Evidence suggests most families of people with Down's syndrome feel satisfied with their lives and report positive experiences.

Congenital Condition Register

Babies with Down's syndrome are included in the secure congenital and rare condition (CARDRISS) register maintained by Public Health Scotland. This helps:

  • Track prevalence of the condition
  • Plan and improve health and care services
  • Support research and service development

Health Considerations

While many children with Down's syndrome have similar health conditions to all children, some health aspects require special attention.

Common Health Considerations

  • Heart issues: Around half of children may have heart conditions, though fewer than 1 in 5 need surgery
  • Hearing and vision: Increased likelihood of reduced hearing or vision
  • Muscle tone: May experience hypotonia (low muscle tone)
  • Digestion: Potential for digestive issues
  • Thyroid function: Higher risk of thyroid problems

Positive Health Aspects

Interestingly, people with Down's syndrome have:

  • Lower risk of developing some cancers
  • Reduced risk of certain types of heart disease
  • Lower incidence of dental cavities

Health monitoring: Regular health checks are essential to identify and manage any health conditions early. This includes cardiac, hearing, vision, and thyroid screenings.

Life Expectancy

Down's syndrome is not a life-limiting condition. With modern healthcare and support:

  • Life expectancy has increased significantly
  • Many people live into their 60s and 70s
  • Quality of life continues to improve with better support systems

Screening for Down's Syndrome

During pregnancy, screening tests are offered to assess the likelihood of the baby having Down's syndrome.

Screening Availability

  • Available to all pregnant women regardless of age
  • Offered as part of routine prenatal care
  • Completely voluntary - you can choose whether to have the tests

Screening Methods

The main screening approaches include:

  • Combined test: Blood test and nuchal translucency (NT) scan
  • Non-invasive prenatal testing (NIPT): Blood test analyzing fetal DNA
  • Diagnostic tests: Chorionic villus sampling (CVS) or amniocentesis for confirmation

Important note: Screening tests assess probability, not certainty. Only diagnostic tests can provide definitive results, and these carry a small risk of miscarriage.

Support & Resources

Numerous organizations and resources are available to support individuals with Down's syndrome and their families.

Support Organizations

  • Down's Syndrome Scotland: Provides comprehensive information and support services
  • Local health services: Offer developmental support and healthcare
  • Educational support: Schools provide additional learning support
  • Community groups: Connect families for mutual support

Types of Support Available

  • Early intervention programs for infants and toddlers
  • Educational support and inclusion programs
  • Transition support for moving into adulthood
  • Employment assistance and workplace support
  • Housing and independent living support
  • Social and recreational opportunities

Remember: Every person with Down's syndrome is unique with their own strengths, talents, and personality. With proper support and opportunities, they can achieve their full potential and lead fulfilling lives.

Looking Forward

Society's understanding and acceptance of Down's syndrome continues to improve, with:

  • Better educational inclusion
  • Increased employment opportunities
  • Greater social acceptance and awareness
  • Ongoing research into supporting development and health
