About Congenital Heart Disease

Congenital heart disease (CHD) is a general term used to refer to a series of birth defects that affect the heart.

CHD is the most common type of birth defect, with 1 in 180 babies being born with the condition.

Outlook

The outlook for congenital heart disease varies depending on the type and severity of the heart defect.

Research into the causes and improved treatments have meant that 80% of children with congenital heart disease will survive into adulthood.

Adult care: Congenital heart disease in adults is sometimes known as 'grown-up congenital heart disease' (GUCH) or Adult Congenital Heart Disease (ACHD), requiring specialized lifelong care.

Types of Congenital Heart Disease

Cyanotic Heart Disease

Cyanotic heart disease is where problems with the heart mean there isn't enough oxygen present in the blood.

Babies born with cyanotic heart disease generally have a blue-colored tinge to areas such as their fingers, toes and lips because of a lack of oxygen.

Acyanotic Heart Disease

Acyanotic heart disease is where the blood contains enough oxygen but it's pumped abnormally around the body.

Babies born with acyanotic heart disease may not have any apparent symptoms but, over time, the condition can cause health problems including increased blood pressure and strain on the heart.

There are over 30 different types of heart defects, with cyanotic and acyanotic being the two main categories.

Symptoms of Congenital Heart Disease

Cyanotic Heart Disease Symptoms

  • Blue colored tinge to lips, fingers and toes (cyanosis)
  • Palpitations
  • Fainting
  • Fatigue
  • Chest pains
  • Breathing problems

Acyanotic Heart Disease Symptoms

  • Severe tiredness
  • Palpitations
  • Chest pain
  • Shortness of breath (particularly during activities)

Common Symptoms in Both Types

  • Poor appetite and feeding difficulties
  • Sweating, particularly when feeding
  • Delayed growth
  • Children are usually underweight or small for their age

Causes & Risk Factors

Congenital heart disease develops when abnormalities in the heart's structure prevent it from working as it should.

Common Types of Defects

  • Ventricular Septal Defect (VSD) - Hole between left and right ventricle
  • Atrial Septal Defect (ASD) - Hole between left and right atria
  • Pulmonary Stenosis - Narrow pulmonary valve
  • Aortic Stenosis - Narrow aortic valve
  • Patent Ductus Ateriosus (PDA) - Failure of duct to close after birth
  • Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF) - Combination of four heart defects
  • Transposition of Great Arteries (TGA) - Arteries attached to wrong sides

Risk Factors

  • Maternal diabetes - Five times higher risk
  • Rubella infection during early pregnancy
  • Maternal alcohol abuse during pregnancy
  • Genetic conditions such as Down's syndrome, Turner syndrome, Noonan syndrome

In many cases of congenital heart disease, there is no clear reason why a baby is born with a defective heart.

Diagnosing Congenital Heart Disease

Prenatal Diagnosis

Many cases are now diagnosed before birth using foetal echocardiography during routine antenatal examinations between 18th-20th week of pregnancy.

Postnatal Diagnosis

Cyanotic heart disease can usually be diagnosed quickly due to distinctive blue skin color. Acyanotic heart disease may not become apparent for several months or years.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Echocardiogram - Ultrasound of the heart
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG) - Measures heart's electrical activity
  • Chest X-ray - Checks for lung abnormalities and heart size
  • Pulse oximetry - Measures oxygen levels in blood
  • Cardiac catheterisation - Detailed study of blood flow through heart

Treating Congenital Heart Disease

Surgical Techniques

  • Catheter procedures - Minimally invasive repair using catheters
  • Open heart surgery - Direct surgical repair of heart defects
  • Heart transplant - For severely damaged hearts

Treatment by Condition

Septal defects: Small defects may close naturally; medium defects may require catheter closure; large defects need open heart surgery.

Stenosis: Mild cases may require monitoring; serious cases may need balloon angioplasty or valve replacement.

PDA: Often treated with medication shortly after birth; if unresponsive, catheter closure may be needed.

Adult Care

About 80% of children with CHD survive into adulthood, requiring specialized adult congenital heart disease clinics for ongoing care and monitoring.

Routine medical procedures for adults with CHD, such as giving birth or having general anaesthetic, need close supervision by specialist staff.

Complications of Congenital Heart Disease

Developmental Delays

Many children with CHD experience delays in development and may take longer to reach milestones like walking or talking.

Some children also have associated learning difficulties requiring specialized educational and psychological assistance.

Endocarditis

Both children and adults with CHD have increased risk of endocarditis - an infection of the heart lining that can be life-threatening.

Symptoms include fever, chills, night sweats, muscular aches, chest pain, weakness and fatigue.

Prevention: Excellent oral hygiene is crucial to prevent gum disease leading to endocarditis. Avoid cosmetic procedures involving skin piercing like tattoos.
