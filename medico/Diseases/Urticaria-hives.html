About Hives

Hives (urticaria) is a skin condition characterized by a distinctive rash that can appear anywhere on the body.

Characteristics of Hives Rash

The rash may:

  • Be raised above the skin surface
  • Be very itchy and uncomfortable
  • Appear on one part of the body or spread over large areas
  • Range in size from a few millimeters to the size of a hand
  • Change in appearance within 24 hours
  • Come and go quickly, with individual welts lasting less than 24 hours

Note: Hives are usually harmless and temporary, but they can be uncomfortable and concerning when they appear.

Symptoms of Hives

The main symptom of hives is the appearance of welts or wheals on the skin that are typically:

  • Red or skin-colored raised bumps
  • Itchy - sometimes intensely so
  • Variable in size - from small spots to large patches
  • Blanchable - turn white when pressed
  • Migratory - they may disappear and reappear in different areas

Duration: Individual hives typically last less than 24 hours, but new ones may continue to appear.

Diagnosing Hives

Hives can usually be diagnosed by examining the distinctive red rash. A healthcare professional may ask questions to identify potential triggers.

Diagnostic Process

  • Physical examination of the rash
  • Medical history to identify potential triggers
  • Duration assessment - acute (less than 6 weeks) vs chronic (more than 6 weeks)
  • Trigger identification through careful questioning

Chronic hives: If symptoms persist, additional tests may be needed to determine the underlying cause.

Treating Hives

Most hives rashes don't need extensive treatment. The symptoms are usually mild and often get better within a few days.

Mild Cases

  • Antihistamines - available over-the-counter from pharmacies
  • Avoiding triggers - identify and avoid what causes your hives
  • Cool compresses - to relieve itching and inflammation
  • Loose clothing - to prevent irritation

Severe Cases

For more severe hives, medical treatment may include:

  • Prescription antihistamines
  • Oral corticosteroids for short-term use
  • Menthol cream to relieve itchiness
  • Referral to a dermatologist if hives persist

Causes of Hives

Hives occur when histamine and other chemicals are released from under the skin's surface, causing tissues to swell.

Common Triggers

  • Foods - certain foods can trigger allergic reactions
  • Insect bites or stings
  • Temperature changes - cold water, wind, or heat from exercise
  • Emotional stress
  • Infections - like colds or other viral illnesses
  • Medications - NSAIDs, antibiotics, and other drugs
  • Environmental factors - pollen, dust mites, or chemicals
  • Physical pressure - tight clothing or scratching
  • Alcohol or caffeine

Identification tip: Try to identify and avoid your specific triggers to prevent future outbreaks of hives.

Complications of Hives

While most cases of hives are temporary and harmless, they can sometimes lead to complications:

  • Angioedema - deeper swelling of tissues, often around eyes and lips
  • Psychological impact - stress and anxiety due to persistent symptoms
  • Sleep disturbance - from nighttime itching
  • Anaphylaxis - a severe, life-threatening allergic reaction
  • Chronic hives - persistent symptoms lasting more than 6 weeks

Important: If you experience difficulty breathing, swelling of the face or throat, or feel faint, seek emergency medical attention immediately as these could be signs of anaphylaxis.
