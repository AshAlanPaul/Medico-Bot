High Cholesterol

Cholesterol is a fatty substance known as a lipid. It's vital for the normal functioning of the body. Cell membranes, hormones and vitamin D are created by your body using cholesterol.

Sources of Cholesterol

There are 2 main sources of the cholesterol in your blood:

  • Cholesterol in the food you eat
  • Cholesterol produced by your liver

Having an excessively high level of lipids in your blood (hyperlipidemia) can affect your health. High cholesterol itself doesn't usually cause symptoms but increases your risk of serious health conditions.

Types of Cholesterol

Cholesterol is carried in your blood by proteins. When the two combine, they're called lipoproteins.

HDL (Good Cholesterol)

High-density lipoprotein (HDL) carries cholesterol away from the cells and back to the liver, where it's either broken down or passed out of the body as waste. Higher levels of HDL are better.

LDL (Bad Cholesterol)

Low-density lipoprotein (LDL) carries cholesterol to cells that need it. If there's too much cholesterol, it can build up in artery walls, leading to arterial disease.

Why Lower Cholesterol?

Evidence strongly indicates that high cholesterol can increase the risk of:

  • Narrowing of the arteries (atherosclerosis)
  • Heart attack
  • Stroke
  • Transient ischaemic attack (TIA) - "mini stroke"
  • Peripheral arterial disease (PAD)

Important: Cholesterol can build up in artery walls, restricting blood flow to your heart, brain and the rest of your body. It also increases the risk of blood clots.

Causes of High Cholesterol

Many different factors can contribute to high blood cholesterol, including lifestyle factors and underlying medical conditions.

Lifestyle Factors

  • Unhealthy diet - particularly high in saturated fat
  • Lack of exercise - increases LDL levels
  • Obesity - associated with higher LDL and triglycerides
  • Excessive alcohol - can increase cholesterol and triglyceride levels
  • Smoking - chemicals in cigarettes prevent HDL from transporting cholesterol effectively

Underlying Conditions

People with these conditions often have high cholesterol:

  • High blood pressure (hypertension)
  • Diabetes
  • Kidney disease
  • Liver disease
  • Underactive thyroid gland (hypothyroidism)

Fixed Risk Factors

Some risk factors cannot be changed:

  • Family history of early heart disease or stroke
  • Age - risk increases as you get older
  • Ethnic background - higher risk in some ethnic groups
  • Sex - males generally have higher risk

Familial Hypercholesterolaemia

This is an inherited condition affecting about 1 in 500 people in the UK. It causes high cholesterol from birth and significantly increases the risk of early heart disease.

Genetic testing: If you have a family history of very high cholesterol or early heart disease, speak to your GP about genetic testing for familial hypercholesterolaemia.

Diagnosing High Cholesterol

Blood cholesterol levels are measured with a simple blood test that checks HDL, LDL, and triglyceride levels.

The Cholesterol Test

A blood sample is taken, usually after fasting for 10-12 hours. Your GP or practice nurse can perform this test.

Who Should Be Tested?

Your GP may recommend testing if you:

  • Have been diagnosed with heart disease, stroke, or peripheral arterial disease
  • Are over 40 years old
  • Have a family history of early cardiovascular disease
  • Are overweight or obese
  • Have high blood pressure or diabetes
  • Have other relevant medical conditions

Recommended Cholesterol Levels

Blood cholesterol is measured in millimoles per litre (mmol/L):

Total Cholesterol

  • 5mmol/L or less for healthy adults
  • 4mmol/L or less for those at high risk

LDL (Bad Cholesterol)

  • 3mmol/L or less for healthy adults
  • 2mmol/L or less for those at high risk

HDL (Good Cholesterol)

An ideal level is above 1mmol/L. Higher levels are better.

Comprehensive assessment: Your doctor will consider your cholesterol levels alongside other risk factors like age, family history, blood pressure, and smoking status to determine your overall cardiovascular risk.

Treating High Cholesterol

Treatment typically begins with lifestyle changes. If these aren't sufficient, medication may be recommended.

Dietary Changes

Reduce intake of foods high in saturated fat:

  • Fatty cuts of meat and meat products
  • Butter, ghee, and lard
  • Cream and full-fat dairy products
  • Hard cheeses
  • Cakes and biscuits
  • Foods containing coconut or palm oil

Medication Options

Statins

These block an enzyme in your liver that helps make cholesterol. They're the most commonly prescribed cholesterol-lowering medication and are taken for life.

Common statins: Atorvastatin, Simvastatin, Rosuvastatin

Ezetimibe

This medication blocks cholesterol absorption from your intestines. It can be used alone or with statins, and has fewer side effects.

Aspirin

Low-dose aspirin may be prescribed for some people to help prevent blood clots, particularly those with established heart disease or high risk.

Omega-3 Fatty Acids

Found in oily fish like mackerel, salmon, and tuna, omega-3s can help lower triglyceride levels. Aim for at least two portions of oily fish per week.

Treatment approach: Your doctor will recommend the most suitable treatment based on your cholesterol levels, overall cardiovascular risk, and any other health conditions you may have.

Preventing High Cholesterol

You can lower your risk of high cholesterol through healthy lifestyle choices.

Healthy Diet

Replace saturated fats with unsaturated fats:

Foods to Limit

  • Meat pies and sausages
  • Butter and lard
  • Full-fat cream and cheese
  • Cakes and biscuits
  • Foods with coconut or palm oil

Healthy Alternatives

  • Oily fish (mackerel, salmon, tuna)
  • Avocados
  • Nuts and seeds
  • Olive oil and rapeseed oil
  • Wholegrain foods and plenty of fruits and vegetables

Stop Smoking

Smoking damages blood vessels and reduces HDL's ability to transport cholesterol. Quitting smoking significantly improves your cholesterol profile and overall heart health.

Regular Exercise

Aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week:

  • Walking
  • Swimming
  • Cycling
  • Other aerobic activities

Exercise benefits: Regular physical activity increases HDL levels, helps maintain a healthy weight, and improves blood pressure - all of which contribute to better cholesterol management.

Alcohol Moderation

Limit alcohol consumption, as excessive drinking can raise cholesterol and triglyceride levels.

Long-term commitment: Preventing high cholesterol requires ongoing healthy lifestyle choices. Regular check-ups with your GP can help monitor your progress and catch any issues early.
