About Asthma

Asthma is a common long-term condition that can cause coughing, wheezing, chest tightness and breathlessness.

The severity of these symptoms varies from person to person. Asthma can be controlled well in most people most of the time, although some people may have more persistent problems.

Occasionally, asthma symptoms can get gradually or suddenly worse. This is known as an 'asthma attack', although doctors sometimes use the term 'exacerbation'.

Severe attacks may require hospital treatment and can be life threatening, although this is unusual.

What causes asthma?

Asthma is caused by inflammation of the small tubes, called bronchi, which carry air in and out of the lungs. If you have asthma, the bronchi will be inflamed and more sensitive than normal.

Common asthma triggers include:

  • House dust mites
  • Animal fur
  • Pollen
  • Cigarette smoke
  • Exercise
  • Viral infections

Who is affected?

In the UK, around 5.4 million people are currently receiving treatment for asthma.

That's the equivalent of 1 in every 12 adults and 1 in every 11 children. Asthma in adults is more common in women than men.

How asthma is treated

While there is no cure for asthma, there are a number of treatments that can help control the condition.

Treatment is based on two important goals:

  • Relieving symptoms
  • Preventing future symptoms and attacks

Important: You should have a personal asthma action plan agreed with your doctor or nurse that includes information about your medicines and what to do if symptoms worsen.

Symptoms of Asthma

The symptoms of asthma can range from mild to severe. Most people will only experience occasional symptoms, although a few people will have problems most of the time.

The main symptoms of asthma are:

  • Wheezing (a whistling sound when you breathe)
  • Shortness of breath
  • A tight chest – which may feel like a band is tightening around it
  • Coughing

These symptoms are often worse at night and early in the morning, particularly if the condition is not well controlled.

Asthma Attacks

When asthma symptoms get significantly worse, it is known as an asthma attack or 'acute asthma exacerbation'.

Signs of a severe asthma attack can include:

  • Your reliever inhaler not helping symptoms as much as usual
  • Wheezing, coughing and chest tightness becoming severe and constant
  • Being too breathless to eat, speak or sleep
  • Breathing faster
  • A rapid heartbeat
  • Feeling drowsy, exhausted or dizzy
  • Your lips or fingers turning blue

Emergency: Phone 999 to seek immediate help if you or someone else has symptoms of a severe asthma attack.

Causes of Asthma

It's not clear exactly what causes asthma, although it is likely to be a combination of factors.

Who is at risk?

Factors that can increase your chances of developing asthma include:

  • A family history of asthma or other allergic conditions
  • Having another atopic condition
  • Having bronchiolitis as a child
  • Childhood exposure to tobacco smoke
  • Being born prematurely
  • Having a low birth weight

Asthma Triggers

Asthma symptoms can have a range of triggers, such as:

  • Respiratory tract infections
  • Allergens – including pollen, dust mites, animal fur
  • Airborne irritants – including cigarette smoke and pollution
  • Medicines – particularly NSAIDs and beta-blockers
  • Emotions – including stress
  • Weather conditions
  • Exercise

Occupational Asthma

In some cases, asthma is associated with substances you may be exposed to at work. Common causes include:

  • Isocyanates (chemicals often found in spray paint)
  • Flour and grain dust
  • Colophony (found in solder fumes)
  • Latex
  • Animals
  • Wood dust

Diagnosing Asthma

If you have typical asthma symptoms, your GP will often be able to make a diagnosis.

Spirometry

A breathing test called spirometry will often be carried out to assess how well your lungs work. This involves taking a deep breath and exhaling as fast as you can through a mouthpiece.

Peak Expiratory Flow Test

A small hand-held device known as a peak flow meter can be used to measure how fast you can blow air out of your lungs in one breath.

Other Tests

Some people may need more specialised tests, including:

  • Airways responsiveness testing
  • Testing airway inflammation
  • Allergy tests

Note: In children, asthma can be difficult to diagnose because many other conditions can cause similar symptoms.

Treating Asthma

Reliever Inhalers

Reliever inhalers – usually blue – are taken to relieve asthma symptoms quickly.

They work by relaxing the muscles surrounding the narrowed airways, allowing them to open wider.

Preventer Inhalers

Preventer inhalers – usually brown, red or orange – work over time to reduce inflammation and sensitivity of the airways.

They must be used regularly to keep asthma under control.

Other Treatments

Additional treatments may include:

  • Long-acting reliever inhalers
  • Leukotriene receptor antagonists
  • Theophyllines
  • Oral steroids (for severe cases)
  • Omalizumab (for severe allergic asthma)

Asthma Attacks

A personal asthma action plan will help you recognise the initial symptoms of an asthma attack and know how to respond.

Emergency Action: If you have an asthma attack, use your reliever inhaler immediately and seek medical help if symptoms don't improve.

Living with Asthma

With the right treatment and management, asthma shouldn't restrict your daily life in any way.

Sleeping

Asthma symptoms are often worse at night. Effectively controlling asthma with treatment will reduce symptoms and improve sleep.

Exercise

Most people with asthma can exercise normally. Important tips include:

  • Always have your reliever inhaler with you
  • Use your reliever inhaler immediately before warming up
  • Warm up and cool down thoroughly
  • Stop and use your inhaler if symptoms occur

Know Your Triggers

It's important to identify possible asthma triggers and avoid them when possible.

Complications

Poorly controlled asthma can lead to:

  • Fatigue and underperformance
  • Psychological problems
  • Respiratory complications
  • In severe cases, life-threatening situations

Asthma and Pregnancy

It's important to ensure asthma is well controlled during pregnancy, as poorly controlled asthma can lead to complications.

Remember: With proper management, most people with asthma can lead active, normal lives without restrictions.
