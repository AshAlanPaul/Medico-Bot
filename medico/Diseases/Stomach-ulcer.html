About Stomach Ulcers

Stomach ulcers (gastric ulcers) are open sores that develop on the lining of the stomach. Ulcers can also occur in part of the intestine just beyond the stomach. These are called duodenal ulcers.

Stomach and duodenal ulcers are sometimes called peptic ulcers. This information applies to both.

Emergency: These could be a sign of a serious complication and require immediate medical attention.

Stomach Ulcer Symptoms

The most common symptom of a stomach ulcer is a burning or gnawing pain that develops in your abdomen (tummy).

However, some stomach ulcers aren't painful and are only noticed when a complication develops, such as bleeding from the ulcer.

Abdominal Pain Characteristics

The pain caused by a stomach ulcer can:

  • Travel from the middle of your tummy up to your neck, down to your belly button, or through to your back
  • Last from a few minutes to a few hours
  • Often start within a few hours of eating
  • Cause you to wake up during the night

Less Common Symptoms

Less common symptoms of a stomach ulcer include:

  • Indigestion
  • Heartburn
  • Loss of appetite
  • Feeling and being sick
  • Weight loss

Note: Taking antacids may temporarily relieve the pain, but it will keep coming back if the ulcer isn't treated.

Stomach Ulcer Causes

Stomach ulcers are usually caused by Helicobacter pylori (H. pylori) bacteria or non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs).

These can break down the stomach's defence against the acid it produces to digest food. The stomach lining then becomes damaged causing an ulcer to form.

H. pylori Bacteria

H. pylori bacteria live in the stomach lining. The bacteria can irritate the stomach lining and make it more vulnerable to damage from stomach acid.

Infections are common. It's possible to be infected without realising it because the infection doesn't usually cause symptoms.

NSAIDs (Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs)

NSAIDs are medicines widely used to treat pain, fever and inflammation. Commonly used NSAIDs include:

  • Ibuprofen
  • Aspirin
  • Naproxen
  • Diclofenac

Important: Many people take NSAIDs without side effects, but there's always a risk they could cause stomach ulcers, particularly if taken for a long time or at high doses.

Lifestyle Factors

There's little evidence that stomach ulcers are caused by spicy foods, stress, or alcohol. However, these can make symptoms worse.

Smoking can increase your risk of developing stomach ulcers and make treatment less effective.

Stomach Ulcer Diagnosis

If your GP thinks you have an ulcer, they will:

  • Ask if you're taking NSAIDs
  • Test you for an H. pylori infection

In some cases, you may be referred for a gastroscopy to look for an ulcer inside your stomach.

Testing for H. pylori Infection

If your GP thinks your symptoms might be caused by an H. pylori infection, you'll be offered:

  • Urea breath test
  • Stool antigen test
  • Blood test

Gastroscopy

A gastroscopy involves passing a thin, flexible tube with a camera into your stomach and duodenum. This procedure:

  • Is done as an outpatient in hospital
  • May involve a mild sedative or throat spray
  • Can confirm or rule out an ulcer
  • May include taking a small tissue sample

Stomach Ulcer Treatment

If you have a stomach ulcer, your treatment will depend on what caused it. With treatment, most ulcers heal in a month or two.

Treating H. pylori Infection

If your stomach ulcer's caused by H. pylori bacteria, you'll be given:

  • A course of antibiotics
  • A proton pump inhibitor (PPI) medication

Treating Ulcers Caused by NSAIDs

If your stomach ulcer's caused by taking NSAIDs:

  • You'll be given a course of PPI medication
  • Your use of NSAIDs will be reviewed
  • You may be advised to use alternative painkillers like paracetamol

Common Medications

The most commonly used treatments include:

  • Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) - omeprazole, pantoprazole, lansoprazole
  • H2-receptor antagonists - ranitidine
  • Antacids and alginates - for immediate symptom relief

Recovery: Most stomach ulcers heal completely with proper treatment. Follow your doctor's advice and attend follow-up appointments.

Stomach Ulcer Complications

Complications of stomach ulcers are rare, but can be very serious and potentially life-threatening.

Internal Bleeding

Internal bleeding is the most common complication. It can occur when an ulcer develops at the site of a blood vessel.

Symptoms of severe bleeding include:

  • Vomiting blood (bright red or dark brown like coffee grounds)
  • Passing dark, sticky, tar-like stools

Perforation

A rarer complication where the stomach lining splits open, allowing bacteria to escape and cause peritonitis (infection of the abdominal lining).

Gastric Outlet Obstruction

When an inflamed or scarred stomach ulcer blocks the normal passage of food through your digestive system.

Symptoms include:

  • Vomiting large amounts of undigested food
  • Constant feeling of bloating or fullness
  • Feeling full after eating very little
  • Unexplained weight loss

Seek Immediate Medical Help If You Experience:

  • Sudden, severe abdominal pain
  • Vomiting blood
  • Black, tarry stools
  • Fainting or severe dizziness
