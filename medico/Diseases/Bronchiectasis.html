About Bronchiectasis Symptoms Causes Diagnosis Treatment Complications

About Bronchiectasis

Bronchiectasis is a long-term condition where the airways of the lungs become abnormally widened, leading to a build-up of excess mucus that can make the lungs more vulnerable to infection.

The most common symptoms of bronchiectasis include:

  • A persistent cough that usually brings up phlegm (sputum)
  • Breathlessness

The severity of symptoms can vary widely. Some people have only a few symptoms that don't appear often, while others have wide-ranging daily symptoms.

Important: The symptoms tend to get worse if you develop an infection in your lungs.

How the Lungs are Affected

The lungs are full of tiny branching airways, known as bronchi. In bronchiectasis, one or more of the bronchi are abnormally widened. This means more mucus than usual gathers there, which makes the bronchi more vulnerable to infection.

Over time, this cycle of infection and inflammation can cause gradually worsening damage to the lungs.

Outlook

The outlook for people with bronchiectasis is highly variable and often depends on the underlying cause. Most people with the condition have a normal life expectancy, but for people with very severe symptoms, bronchiectasis can be fatal if the lungs stop working properly.

Symptoms of Bronchiectasis

The most common symptom of bronchiectasis is a persistent cough that brings up a large amount of phlegm on a daily basis.

The phlegm can be clear, pale yellow or yellow-greenish in colour. Other people may only occasionally cough up small amounts of phlegm, or none at all.

Other Symptoms

  • Shortness of breath
  • Wheezing
  • Coughing up blood or blood-stained phlegm
  • Chest pain
  • Joint pain
  • Clubbing of the fingertips – the tissue beneath the nail thickens and the fingertips become rounded and bulbous

Signs of a Lung Infection

If you develop a lung infection, your symptoms usually get worse within a few days. This is known as an infective exacerbation and it can cause:

  • Coughing up even more phlegm, which may be more green than usual or smell unpleasant
  • Worsening shortness of breath
  • Feeling very tired
  • Coughing up blood
  • Chest pain that's worse when breathing
  • Feeling generally unwell

When to seek immediate medical advice: If you experience bluish skin/lips, confusion, high fever, rapid breathing, or severe chest pain, seek emergency medical attention immediately.

Causes of Bronchiectasis

Bronchiectasis is caused by the airways of the lungs becoming damaged and widened. This can be due to an infection or another condition. Sometimes, the cause is not known.

Common Causes

In around half of all cases of bronchiectasis, no obvious cause can be found. Some of the more common triggers include:

  • Childhood infections – severe pneumonia, whooping cough, tuberculosis, or measles
  • Immunodeficiency – weakened immune system making lungs more vulnerable
  • Allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis (ABPA) – allergy to fungi that causes lung inflammation
  • Aspiration – stomach contents accidentally passing into lungs
  • Cystic fibrosis – genetic disorder causing mucus buildup in lungs
  • Cilia abnormalities – problems with the hair-like structures that clear mucus
  • Connective tissue diseases – rheumatoid arthritis, Sjögren's syndrome, Crohn's disease

Note: The damage to the lungs occurs through a cycle of inflammation and infection that progressively worsens over time.

Diagnosing Bronchiectasis

You should see your GP for advice if you develop a persistent cough, so they can look for a possible cause.

Your GP will ask about your symptoms and may listen to your lungs with a stethoscope. The lungs of people with bronchiectasis often make a distinctive crackling noise.

Referral to a Specialist

If your GP suspects you could have bronchiectasis, you'll be referred to a respiratory consultant for further testing.

Diagnostic Tests

  • HRCT scan – the most effective test to diagnose bronchiectasis, showing detailed images of the airways
  • Blood tests – to check immune function and look for infections
  • Phlegm test – to check for bacteria or fungi
  • Sweat test – to check for cystic fibrosis
  • Lung function test – to measure how well your lungs are working
  • Bronchoscopy – using a camera to look inside the lungs

Important: A chest X-ray may be done initially to rule out other serious conditions like lung cancer.

Treating Bronchiectasis

The damage to the lungs associated with bronchiectasis is permanent, but treatment can help prevent the condition getting worse.

Lifestyle Measures

  • Stop smoking if you smoke
  • Have the flu vaccine every year
  • Get the pneumococcal vaccine
  • Exercise regularly
  • Keep well hydrated
  • Eat a balanced diet

Airway Clearance Techniques

These exercises help remove mucus from your lungs:

  • Active cycle of breathing techniques (ACBT) – a cycle of different breathing exercises
  • Postural drainage – using gravity to help drain mucus
  • Handheld devices – like flutter, RC cornet, or Acapella devices

Medications

  • Nebulised medications – inhaled medications to thin mucus
  • Bronchodilators – to relax lung muscles and make breathing easier
  • Antibiotics – to treat bacterial infections

Surgery

Surgery is rare and usually only considered when bronchiectasis affects only a single section of the lung and other treatments haven't been effective.

Remember: Treatment is usually a combination of medication, exercises, and devices to help clear your airways. Working with a physiotherapist can be very helpful.

Complications of Bronchiectasis

In some cases, people with bronchiectasis can develop serious complications that require emergency treatment.

Coughing Up Large Amounts of Blood

A rare but serious complication is coughing up large amounts of blood (massive haemoptysis). This can occur when a section of one of the blood vessels supplying the lungs suddenly splits open.

Symptoms include:

  • Coughing up more than 100ml of blood in 24 hours
  • Breathing difficulties from blood obstructing airways
  • Feeling lightheaded, dizzy with cold, clammy skin from blood loss

Emergency: Massive haemoptysis is a medical emergency. If you think someone is experiencing this, phone 999 for an ambulance immediately.

Treatment typically involves a procedure called bronchial artery embolisation (BAE) to stop the bleeding.
