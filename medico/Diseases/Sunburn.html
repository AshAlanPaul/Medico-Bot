About Sunburn

Sunburn is skin damage caused by ultraviolet (UV) rays. It usually causes the skin to become red, sore, warm, tender and occasionally itchy for about a week.

The skin will normally start to flake and peel after a few days and will usually fully heal within 7 days.

Important: While sunburn is often short-lived and mild, it's important to try to avoid it because it can increase your chances of developing serious health problems, such as skin cancer, in later life.

Why Sunburn Happens

It's easy to underestimate your exposure to the sun when outside because the redness doesn't usually develop for several hours. Breezes and getting wet may cool your skin, so you don't realize you're getting burnt.

Treating Sunburn

If you or your child has sunburn, you should get out of the sun as soon as possible – head indoors or into a shady area.

Do

  • Cool the skin by sponging with cold water or having a cold bath or shower
  • Apply cold compresses to the affected area
  • Drink plenty of fluids to cool you down and prevent dehydration
  • Take pain relief such as ibuprofen or paracetamol to relieve pain
  • Avoid all sunlight by covering up affected areas until fully healed

Pharmacy advice: Your nearest pharmacist can advise you on the best treatment for sunburn. They can recommend appropriate creams and lotions to soothe the skin.

When to Seek Medical Advice

Contact your GP practice if:

You have sunburn and feel unwell or have any concerns, particularly if:

  • You're burnt over a large area
  • You have blistering or swelling of the skin
  • You experience chills
  • You have a high temperature (38°C/100.4°F or above)
  • You experience dizziness, headaches, and nausea
  • A young child or baby has sunburn

If your GP practice is closed, phone 111.

Severe sunburn may require special burn cream and burn dressings from your GP. Very occasionally, hospital treatment may be needed.

Preventing Sunburn

Skin should be protected from strong sunlight by covering up with suitable clothing, seeking shade, and applying sunscreen.

Timing and Conditions

In the UK, the risk of getting sunburnt is highest from March to October, particularly from 11am to 3pm when the sun's rays are strongest.

You can also burn in other conditions:

  • Light reflecting off snow, sand, concrete, and water
  • Cloudy and cool conditions
  • At high altitudes

Protective Clothing

  • Wide-brimmed hat that shades face, neck, and ears
  • Long-sleeved tops
  • Trousers or long skirts in close-weave fabrics
  • Sunglasses with proper UV protection

Sunscreen Guidelines

When buying sunscreen, make sure it:

  • Has 'UVA' in a circle logo and at least 4-star UVA protection
  • Has at least SPF30 protection against UVB
  • Is applied liberally (about 35ml for an average adult)
  • Is reapplied every 2 hours and after swimming or sweating

Application tip: Apply sunscreen 30 minutes before going out and again just before you go out. Most people don't apply enough sunscreen.

Risks and Complications

Who's Most at Risk?

  • People with pale, white, or light brown skin
  • People with freckles or red/fair hair
  • Those who tend to burn rather than tan
  • People with many moles
  • Those only occasionally exposed to intense sun
  • People in hot countries with intense sun
  • Those with family history of skin cancer

Long-term Risks

  • Actinic (solar) keratoses – rough, scaly pre-cancerous spots
  • Skin cancer – both melanoma and non-melanoma types
  • Eye problems including cataracts
  • Premature ageing of the skin and wrinkling

Special Advice for Children

  • Children under 6 months should be kept out of direct strong sunlight
  • From March to October, children should cover up and use SPF30+ sunscreen
  • Children under 5 should take vitamin D supplements even when getting sun

Serious warning: Repeated sunburn significantly increases your risk of developing skin cancer later in life. Always protect your skin from excessive sun exposure.
