About Post-Polio Syndrome

Post-polio syndrome is a slow progressive condition that affects some people that have had polio. It's not life threatening but it can cause problems in your ability to carry out daily activities.

Polio is a serious viral infection that used to be common in the UK and worldwide. It's rarer now because it can be prevented with vaccination.

In around 1 in 100 people, the polio virus causes paralysis, which in rare cases can be life threatening.

Cases of polio in the UK fell dramatically when routine vaccination was introduced in the mid-1950s.

Note: Post-polio syndrome typically appears 15-40 years after the initial polio infection and affects polio survivors.

Symptoms of Post-Polio Syndrome

The symptoms of post-polio syndrome include:

  • Muscle weakness
  • Shrinking of the muscles (atrophy)
  • Tight joints (contractures)
  • Pain in muscles or joints
  • Chronic fatigue including physical tiredness and brain fatigue
  • Swallowing and speech problems
  • Respiratory problems like breathlessness and sleep apnoea
  • Cramps and muscle twitching
  • Being sensitive to cold temperatures

The muscle weakness and atrophy usually affects muscles that were previously affected by polio. Sometimes it affects other muscles.

This can impact your mobility and your ability to do everyday tasks like brushing your teeth or drying your hair.

Fatigue Alert: Chronic fatigue is one of the most common and troublesome symptoms of post-polio syndrome, affecting both physical energy and cognitive function.

Causes of Post-Polio Syndrome

Post-polio syndrome is caused by the polio virus. It only affects people who've had polio although it can take 15 – 40 years after the infection for it to develop. It's not known if there's anything that can be done to prevent it.

The exact cause behind who'll experience post-polio syndrome after having the polio virus is unclear.

It could be because of gradual degeneration (breaking down) of nerve cells (motor neurones) in the brainstem or spinal cord that were damaged by the polio virus. This gradual change explains why it can take years for post-polio syndrome symptoms to appear.

Important: Post-polio syndrome is not contagious and cannot be passed from person to person.

Diagnosis of Post-Polio Syndrome

It can be difficult to diagnose post-polio syndrome. There are no specific tests for it, and it can be mistaken for other conditions.

Your GP might suspect post-polio syndrome if:

  • You had polio followed by a long period of stable symptoms (usually 15 years)
  • Your symptoms have developed gradually

The symptoms of post-polio syndrome are slow and progressive. The sudden appearance of symptoms is more likely to be caused by something else.

Diagnostic Tests

Tests that might be offered include:

  • Blood tests and scans of your spine
  • Electromyography (EMG) tests
  • Sleep studies
  • Tests to check your heart function and heart rate
  • MRI or CT scans of your brain, spine and muscles
  • Lung function tests
  • Tests to investigate swallowing problems

Treatment & Management

As there's no cure for post-polio syndrome, treatment will help you manage your symptoms and quality of life.

You'll likely have a multidisciplinary team of healthcare professionals working together to care for you including neurologists, respiratory specialists, physiotherapists, and occupational therapists.

Exercise and Rest
▼

Exercise can help slow down the progressive muscle weakness that post-polio syndrome causes. However, being active can become difficult because your symptoms might become worse after exercise or activity.

Your healthcare team might suggest pacing techniques including:

  • Prioritising tasks
  • Finding different ways of doing tasks
  • Taking regular breaks and rest periods
  • Doing regular gentle exercise
Pain Management
▼

Medication to relieve pain might be used in addition to pacing techniques. Options include:

  • Over-the-counter painkillers (aspirin, paracetamol, ibuprofen)
  • Stronger anti-inflammatory drugs and opiates
  • Medications like gabapentin or pregabalin

Always consult your GP before taking medications long-term.

Mobility Aids
▼

You might benefit from mobility aids like:

  • Braces to support weakened muscles and joints
  • Walking sticks for balance
  • Electric scooters or wheelchairs

Many mobility aids are provided for free by the NHS.

Breathing Problems
▼

If you have breathing problems, you might benefit from:

  • CPAP machines for sleep apnoea
  • Breathing exercises with a respiratory physiotherapist
  • Vaccinations (pneumococcal, COVID-19, flu)
  • Stopping smoking if you smoke

Living with Post-Polio Syndrome

Healthy Eating and Weight Control

If you're overweight, you might be putting your weakened muscles under further pressure. This can cause problems with your energy levels and overall health.

Losing weight (if you're overweight) can help improve your symptoms and general health. Eating a healthy, balanced diet can help you reduce and control your weight.

Psychological Support

The symptoms of post-polio syndrome can be distressing. Developing post-polio syndrome after living with the polio virus as a child can cause anxiety, isolation and stress, which can lead to depression.

Speak to your GP if you're feeling down and you no longer enjoy things you used to.

Support Tip: Remember that while post-polio syndrome presents challenges, many people successfully manage their symptoms and maintain good quality of life with proper support and treatment.
