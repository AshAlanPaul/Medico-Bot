About Chickenpox

Chickenpox is a mild and common illness that mostly affects children but can be caught at any age.

Chickenpox (known medically as varicella) is most common in children under the age of 10. Children usually catch chickenpox in winter and spring, particularly between March and May.

Note: Chickenpox is usually a mild illness in children but can be more serious in adults and people with weakened immune systems.

Causes of Chickenpox

Chickenpox is caused by a virus called the varicella-zoster virus. It's spread quickly and easily from someone who is infected.

The virus spreads through:

  • Coughing and sneezing (airborne droplets)
  • Direct contact with the fluid from chickenpox blisters
  • Contact with items contaminated by the virus

Important: Chickenpox is infectious from 1 to 2 days before the rash appears and remains infectious until all blisters have crusted over.

Symptoms of Chickenpox

Chickenpox causes a rash of red, itchy spots that turn into fluid-filled blisters. They then crust over to form scabs, which eventually drop off.

Where Spots Appear

The spots normally appear in clusters and tend to be:

  • Behind the ears
  • On the face
  • Over the scalp
  • On the chest and belly
  • On the arms and legs

Spots can also appear anywhere on the body, including inside the mouth, on palms, soles, and in the nappy area.

Before the Rash Appears

Before the rash appears, you or your child may have mild flu-like symptoms:

  • Feeling sick
  • High temperature (38°C/100.4°F or over)
  • Aching, painful muscles
  • Headache
  • Generally feeling unwell
  • Loss of appetite

Contact your GP immediately if: You develop chest pain, difficulty breathing, signs of dehydration, infected blisters, or if symptoms suddenly get worse. Also contact your GP if you're pregnant, have a weakened immune system, or have a baby under 4 weeks old.

Chickenpox Treatment

There is no specific cure for chickenpox, but you can get remedies from your pharmacy that can help relieve symptoms.

Symptom Relief

  • Paracetamol - to help bring down a fever (avoid ibuprofen unless advised by a doctor)
  • Calamine lotion and cooling gels - to ease itching
  • Antihistamines - may help with severe itching

In most children, the blisters crust up and fall off naturally within 1 to 2 weeks.

Adults with chickenpox may be helped by taking antiviral medicine if it's diagnosed early enough.

Important: Do not give aspirin to children under 16 years old as it can cause a serious condition called Reye's syndrome.

Preventing the Spread

To prevent spreading chickenpox to others:

  • Keep children off school or nursery until all spots have crusted over
  • Adults should stay off work until all blisters have crusted over
  • Avoid public areas while infectious
  • Stay away from people at risk of serious problems

Chickenpox stops being infectious when all the blisters have crusted over, usually 5 to 6 days after the rash starts.

Remember: Chickenpox is infectious from 1 to 2 days before the rash appears, so it can spread before you know you have it.

High-Risk Groups

Some people are at special risk of serious problems if they catch chickenpox:

  • Pregnant women
  • Newborn babies
  • People with weakened immune systems (e.g., cancer treatment, steroid tablets)

These people should speak to their GP as soon as they are exposed to chickenpox or develop symptoms.

They may need a blood test to check if they are immune to chickenpox and may require special treatment.

Urgent: If you're in a high-risk group and think you've been exposed to chickenpox, contact your GP immediately - don't wait for symptoms to appear.

Chickenpox in Pregnancy

If you're pregnant, chickenpox can occasionally cause complications.

Your risk of developing pneumonia is slightly higher if you're pregnant, especially if you smoke.

Risks to the Baby

If you get chickenpox during the first 28 weeks of pregnancy, there is a small risk your baby could develop foetal varicella syndrome (FVS), which can cause:

  • Scarring
  • Eye defects
  • Shortened limbs
  • Brain damage

If infected 7 days before or after giving birth, your newborn may develop a more serious type of chickenpox.

Important: If you're pregnant and think you've been exposed to chickenpox, contact your GP or midwife immediately.
