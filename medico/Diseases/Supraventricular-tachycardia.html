Supraventricular Tachycardia

Supraventricular tachycardia is a condition where the heartbeat is abnormally fast.

It can happen to anyone at any age and often occurs for the first time in children or young adults.

In most cases, supraventricular tachycardia is harmless. It doesn't usually last long and can settle without treatment.

Why It Happens

Some people are born with an abnormality of the heart's natural 'wiring' or conduction system. This allows a short circuit to develop. Under certain circumstances, this allows the heart to race very fast.

One example of this is Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome. This is where there's an abnormal electrical connection between the:

  • Atria (upper heart chambers), and
  • The ventricles (lower heart chambers)

Note: Some people with this condition can develop supraventricular tachycardias because of this short circuit.

Symptoms of Supraventricular Tachycardia

If you have supraventricular tachycardia, you'll usually feel your heart racing in your chest or throat.

The heart rate is usually between 140 to 180 beats per minute but can be as high as 250. A normal heartbeat should be 60 to 100 beats per minute at rest.

How SVT Affects the Body

During a supraventricular tachycardia, the heart muscle contracts very fast. This means that it cannot relax properly between contractions to fill with blood. This reduces the amount of blood being pumped around the body.

Therefore, you may also feel:

  • Chest pain
  • Dizziness
  • Light-headedness
  • Fatigue (tiredness)
  • Breathlessness

Important: On rare occasions, you may faint because of a drop in blood pressure. These symptoms vary in length and frequency.

Causes of Supraventricular Tachycardia

Common causes of supraventricular tachycardia in people at risk of the condition include:

  • Stress
  • Anxiety
  • A change in posture
  • Some medications
  • Alcohol
  • Caffeine
  • Smoking
  • Recreational drugs

Underlying Heart Conditions

Some people are born with heart conditions that make them more susceptible to SVT, such as:

  • Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome
  • Atrioventricular nodal reentrant tachycardia (AVNRT)
  • Atrioventricular reentrant tachycardia (AVRT)

Remember: Many people with SVT have no underlying heart disease, and the condition may occur in otherwise healthy individuals.

Diagnosing Supraventricular Tachycardia

If your doctor thinks that you might have supraventricular tachycardia, you'll be referred for tests. These can include:

  • Electrocardiogram (ECG) - records the electrical activity of your heart
  • Ambulatory heart monitor - worn for 24-48 hours to capture irregular heart rhythms
  • Echocardiogram - ultrasound of the heart to check structure and function
  • Stress test - monitors heart during physical activity
  • Electrophysiology study - detailed test to map the heart's electrical system

What Doctors Look For

During diagnosis, healthcare providers will:

  • Identify the specific type of tachycardia
  • Determine the underlying cause
  • Assess any structural heart problems
  • Evaluate the frequency and duration of episodes

Treating Supraventricular Tachycardia

The treatment for supraventricular tachycardia varies from person to person. It depends on:

  • The type of supraventricular tachycardia you have
  • Your symptoms
  • Treatment of any underlying cause
  • Your age
  • Your overall health

Types of Treatment

Supraventricular tachycardia treatments focus on controlling the rate and rhythm of the heart. They include:

  • Medication - like beta blockers and other anti-arrhythmic drugs
  • Electric shock treatment (DC cardioversion) - in an urgent case
  • Radiofrequency ablation - a procedure to destroy the abnormal heart tissue causing the arrhythmia
  • Vagal maneuvers - simple techniques that can sometimes stop an episode

Immediate Actions During an Episode

If you experience an SVT episode, you can try:

  • Vagal maneuvers (as instructed by your doctor)
  • Resting and relaxing
  • Drinking cold water
  • Avoiding triggers

Emergency: If you experience chest pain, severe shortness of breath, or fainting during an episode, seek immediate medical attention.

Good to know: Many people with SVT live normal, active lives with proper management and treatment. Regular follow-ups with your cardiologist are important for monitoring your condition.
