About Febrile Seizures

A febrile seizure is a fit that can happen when a child has a fever. Febrile seizures are also sometimes called febrile convulsions. They are relatively common and, in most cases, aren't serious.

Around one in 20 children will have at least one febrile seizure at some point. They most often occur between the ages of six months and three years.

Important: During a febrile seizure, the child's body usually becomes stiff, they lose consciousness and their arms and legs twitch. Some children may wet themselves. This is known as a tonic clonic seizure.

What to Do During a Seizure

  • Place the child in the recovery position - on their side on a soft surface
  • Keep their airway open and prevent injury
  • Stay with the child and note how long the seizure lasts
  • Don't put anything in their mouth during the seizure

Emergency: If it's your child's first seizure, or it lasts longer than five minutes, take them to the nearest hospital immediately or dial 999 for an ambulance.

Types of Febrile Seizures

Simple Febrile Seizure (80% of cases)

  • Tonic clonic seizure (body stiffens, limbs twitch)
  • Lasts less than 15 minutes
  • Doesn't reoccur within 24 hours

Complex Febrile Seizure (20% of cases)

  • Lasts longer than 15 minutes
  • Symptoms may affect only one area of the body
  • May recur within 24 hours
  • Child doesn't fully recover within one hour

Symptoms of Febrile Seizures

The main symptom is a fit that occurs while a child has a fever (38°C/100.4°F or above). Febrile seizures often occur during the first day of a fever.

During Simple Febrile Seizures

  • Body becomes stiff and limbs twitch
  • Loss of consciousness
  • May wet or soil themselves
  • May vomit and foam at the mouth
  • Eyes may roll back
  • Usually lasts less than five minutes
  • Sleepiness for up to an hour afterwards

Seek immediate medical help if:

  • First-time seizure
  • Seizure lasts longer than five minutes
  • Suspected serious illness like meningitis
  • Breathing difficulties

Causes of Febrile Seizures

Febrile seizures are linked to fevers, but the exact cause is unknown. They're thought to be related to how the brain and nervous system respond to high temperatures.

Family History

About one in four children with febrile seizures has a family history of the condition. Genetic factors may make some children more susceptible.

Common Associated Infections

  • Viral infections (chickenpox, flu)
  • Middle ear infections
  • Tonsillitis
  • Urinary tract infections
  • Gastroenteritis
  • Respiratory infections

Vaccinations

In rare cases, febrile seizures can occur after vaccinations, but the risk is very low (1 in 3,000-4,000 for MMR vaccine).

Diagnosing Febrile Seizures

Febrile seizures can often be diagnosed from a description of what happened. Further tests may be needed if the cause of infection isn't clear.

Information Needed

  • How long the seizure lasted
  • What happened during the seizure
  • Whether the child recovered within one hour
  • Previous seizure history

Possible Tests

  • Blood or urine tests to identify infection source
  • Electroencephalogram (EEG) to measure brain activity
  • Lumbar puncture in rare cases to rule out serious infections

Note: Most children don't need extensive testing unless symptoms are unusual or there are concerns about serious underlying conditions.

Treating Febrile Seizures

In many cases, febrile seizures don't need specific treatment, but proper care during and after the seizure is important.

During a Seizure

  • Place child in recovery position
  • Stay with them and time the seizure
  • Don't put anything in their mouth
  • Seek medical help for first seizures or those lasting over 5 minutes

Managing Fever

  • Paracetamol or ibuprofen can reduce fever
  • Remove unnecessary clothing and bedding
  • Ensure adequate fluid intake to prevent dehydration
  • Never give aspirin to children under 16

Recurring Febrile Seizures

About one third of children will have another febrile seizure. Recurrence is more likely if:

  • First seizure occurred before 18 months
  • Family history of seizures or epilepsy
  • Initial fever was brief or temperature was below 40°C
  • Child had complex febrile seizure
  • Child attends daycare (more exposure to infections)

Good to know: Most children make a complete recovery after febrile seizures. The risk of developing epilepsy later in life is small (1-2% for simple febrile seizures, 5% for complex ones).
