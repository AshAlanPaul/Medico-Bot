About Psoriasis

Psoriasis is a skin condition that causes red, flaky, crusty patches of skin covered with silvery scales.

What is Psoriasis?

These patches normally appear on your elbows, knees, scalp and lower back, but can appear anywhere on your body. Most people are only affected with small patches. In some cases, the patches can be itchy or sore.

Prevalence: Psoriasis affects around 2% of people in the UK. It can start at any age, but most often develops in adults under 35 years old. The condition affects men and women equally.

Disease Pattern

Psoriasis is a long-lasting (chronic) disease that usually involves periods when you have no symptoms or mild symptoms, followed by periods when symptoms are more severe.

Note: The severity of psoriasis varies greatly from person to person. For some people it's just a minor irritation, but for others it can have a major impact on their quality of life.

Symptoms of Psoriasis

Psoriasis typically causes patches of skin that are dry, red and covered in silver scales. Some people find their psoriasis causes itching or soreness.

Common Types of Psoriasis

Plaque Psoriasis (Psoriasis Vulgaris)

This is the most common form, accounting for about 80% of cases. Its symptoms are dry, red skin lesions, known as plaques, which are covered in silver scales.

Scalp Psoriasis

This can occur on parts of your scalp or on the whole scalp. It causes red patches of skin covered in thick silvery-white scales.

Nail Psoriasis

In about half of all people with psoriasis, the condition affects the nails. Psoriasis can cause your nails to develop tiny dents or pits, become discoloured, or grow abnormally.

Guttate Psoriasis

Guttate psoriasis causes small drop-shaped sores on your chest, arms, legs and scalp. This type sometimes occurs after a streptococcal throat infection.

Inverse (Flexural) Psoriasis

This affects folds or creases in your skin, such as the armpits, groin, between the buttocks and under the breasts.

When to see a doctor: You should see your GP if you think you may have psoriasis. Early diagnosis and treatment can help manage symptoms effectively.

Causes of Psoriasis

Psoriasis occurs when skin cells are replaced more quickly than usual. It's not known exactly why this happens, but research suggests it's caused by a problem with the immune system.

Immune System Problems

In people with psoriasis, T-cells (immune system cells) start to attack healthy skin cells by mistake. This causes the deepest layer of skin to produce new skin cells more quickly than usual.

Genetic Factors

Psoriasis runs in families. 1 in 3 people with psoriasis has a close relative with the condition. However, having the genes doesn't necessarily mean you'll develop psoriasis.

Common Triggers

  • Injury to your skin (cut, scrape, insect bite or sunburn)
  • Drinking excessive amounts of alcohol
  • Smoking
  • Stress
  • Hormonal changes
  • Certain medicines
  • Throat infections
  • Other immune disorders

Important: Psoriasis isn't contagious, so it can't be spread from person to person.

Diagnosing Psoriasis

A GP can often diagnose psoriasis based on the appearance of your skin.

Diagnostic Process

  • Physical examination - visual inspection of affected skin areas
  • Medical history - discussion of symptoms and family history
  • Skin biopsy - in rare cases, a small sample of skin may be examined under a microscope
  • Referral to specialist - may be referred to a dermatologist for complex cases

Treating Psoriasis

There's no cure for psoriasis, but a range of treatments can improve symptoms and the appearance of skin patches.

Treatment Categories

Topical Treatments

  • Emollients - moisturising treatments to reduce itching and scaling
  • Steroid creams - reduce inflammation and slow skin cell production
  • Vitamin D analogues - slow skin cell production and reduce inflammation
  • Coal tar - reduces scales, inflammation and itchiness

Phototherapy

  • UVB phototherapy - uses ultraviolet B light to slow skin cell production
  • PUVA - combines psoralen medication with UVA light
  • Combination therapy - light therapy combined with other treatments

Systemic Treatments

  • Non-biological medications - oral treatments like methotrexate and ciclosporin
  • Biological treatments - injected medications that target specific parts of the immune system

Treatment approach: Treatments are usually started with mild options and progress to stronger treatments if needed. Often, different types of treatment are used in combination for better results.

Living with Psoriasis

Although psoriasis is just a minor irritation for some people, it can have a significant impact on quality of life for those more severely affected.

Self-Care Strategies

  • Keep up your treatment - use treatments as prescribed even when symptoms improve
  • Regular reviews - maintain regular contact with your healthcare team
  • Healthy lifestyle - balanced diet and regular exercise can help manage symptoms
  • Stress management - stress can trigger flare-ups
  • Skin care - gentle skin care routines and avoiding triggers

Emotional Impact

Because of the effect that psoriasis can have on physical appearance, low self-esteem and anxiety are common. This can lead to depression, especially if the psoriasis gets worse.

Psoriatic Arthritis

Some people with psoriasis develop psoriatic arthritis, which causes tenderness, pain and swelling in the joints and connective tissue.

Support: Many people with psoriasis find that getting involved in support groups helps them cope with the condition. Support groups can increase self-confidence and reduce feelings of isolation.
