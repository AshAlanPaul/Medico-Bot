Introduction to Panic Disorder

Panic disorder is where you have recurring and regular panic attacks, often for no apparent reason.

Everyone experiences feelings of anxiety and panic at certain times during their lifetime. It's a natural response to stressful or dangerous situations.

However, for someone with panic disorder, feelings of anxiety, stress and panic occur regularly and at any time.

Understanding Anxiety

Anxiety is a feeling of unease. It can range from mild to severe and can include feelings of worry and fear. There are several conditions that can cause severe anxiety including:

  • Phobias – an extreme or irrational fear of an object, place, situation, feeling or animal
  • Generalised anxiety disorder (GAD) – a long-term condition that causes excessive anxiety and worry relating to a variety of situations
  • Post-traumatic stress disorder – a condition with psychological and physical symptoms caused by distressing or frightening events

Panic Attacks

A panic attack occurs when your body experiences a rush of intense psychological (mental) and physical symptoms.

You may experience an overwhelming sense of fear, apprehension and anxiety. As well as these feelings, you may also have physical symptoms such as:

  • Nausea
  • Sweating
  • Trembling
  • A sensation that your heart is beating irregularly (palpitations)

Important: Panic attacks can be very frightening and intense, but they're not dangerous. An attack won't cause you any physical harm.

Symptoms of Panic Disorder

The symptoms of a panic attack can be very frightening and distressing. Symptoms tend to occur suddenly, without warning and often for no apparent reason.

As well as overwhelming feelings of anxiety, a panic attack can also cause a variety of other symptoms, including:

  • A sensation that your heart is beating irregularly (palpitations)
  • Sweating
  • Trembling
  • Hot flushes
  • Chills
  • Shortness of breath
  • A choking sensation
  • Chest pain
  • Nausea
  • Dizziness
  • Feeling faint
  • Numbness or pins and needles
  • Dry mouth
  • A need to go to the toilet
  • Ringing in your ears
  • A feeling of dread or a fear of dying

Duration and Frequency

Most panic attacks last for five to 20 minutes. Some attacks have been reported to have lasted up to an hour.

People with panic disorder have panic attacks on a recurring basis. Some people have attacks once or twice a month, while others have them several times a week.

Note: The physical symptoms of a panic attack can be so intense they can make you feel like you're having a heart attack. However, these symptoms won't result in you having a heart attack.

Causes of Panic Disorder

As with many mental health conditions, the exact cause of panic disorder isn't fully understood. It's thought that panic disorder is probably caused by a combination of physical and psychological factors.

Possible Causes

  • Traumatic life experiences: A trauma, such as bereavement, can sometimes trigger feelings of panic and anxiety
  • Genetics: Having a close family member with panic disorder is thought to increase a person's risk of developing it
  • Neurotransmitters: An imbalance of chemicals in the brain may increase your risk
  • Increased sensitivity to carbon dioxide: Some people with panic disorder are more sensitive to carbon dioxide
  • Catastrophic thinking: Interpreting minor physical symptoms in a catastrophic way can trigger panic attacks

Diagnosing Panic Disorder

Everyone who has panic disorder will experience panic attacks. However, not everyone who has panic attacks is diagnosed with panic disorder.

Panic disorder will only be diagnosed after experiencing recurrent and unexpected panic attacks, and if the attacks are followed by at least one month of continuous worry or concern about having further attacks.

Medical Assessment

Your GP will:

  • Ask you to describe your symptoms and how often they occur
  • Carry out a physical examination to rule out any physical conditions
  • Discuss your feelings, emotions and personal life

Remember: It's important to be honest with your GP about your symptoms so they can make the correct diagnosis and recommend the most appropriate treatment.

Treatment for Panic Disorder

The main aim in treating panic disorder is to reduce the number of panic attacks and ease the severity of symptoms.

Psychological Therapy

Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) is thought to be one of the most effective psychological treatments for panic disorder. It involves:

  • Identifying negative thoughts and beliefs
  • Replacing them with more realistic and balanced ones
  • Learning behavioral techniques to manage panic attacks
  • Practicing breathing techniques

Medication

Several types of medication can be used to treat panic disorder:

  • Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs): Most commonly prescribed antidepressants for panic disorder
  • Tricyclic antidepressants: Alternative if SSRIs aren't suitable
  • Pregabalin: An anticonvulsant that's also effective for anxiety
  • Clonazepam: Used for epilepsy and sometimes prescribed for panic disorder

Important Medication Information

  • Antidepressants can take 2-4 weeks to become effective
  • Never stop taking medication without medical advice
  • Attend regular check-ups with your GP
  • Report any side effects to your doctor

Self-Help Techniques

There are several self-help techniques you can use to help manage the symptoms of panic disorder.

During a Panic Attack

  • Stay where you are: If possible, remain in place until the attack passes
  • Focus: Concentrate on non-threatening objects around you
  • Slow deep breathing: Breathe slowly and deeply, counting to three on each breath
  • Challenge your fear: Remind yourself that the attack isn't dangerous and will pass
  • Creative visualisation: Imagine a peaceful place or situation
  • Don't fight it: Accept the attack and let it pass

Lifestyle Changes

  • Regular exercise: Helps reduce stress and improve mood
  • Healthy diet: Maintain stable blood sugar levels
  • Relaxation techniques: Yoga, meditation, or breathing exercises
  • Avoid triggers: Caffeine, alcohol, and smoking can worsen symptoms

Complications of Panic Disorder

Panic disorder is a treatable condition, but it's very important to seek medical help as soon as possible. Left untreated, panic disorder can become a debilitating and isolating illness.

Potential Complications

  • Agoraphobia: Fear of being in situations where escape might be difficult
  • Other phobias: Development of additional fears and phobias
  • Impact on children: Can affect development and learning in young people
  • Substance misuse: Increased risk of alcohol or drug problems
  • Social isolation: Difficulty maintaining relationships and social activities

Good to know: With proper treatment and support, most people with panic disorder can significantly reduce their symptoms and improve their quality of life.
