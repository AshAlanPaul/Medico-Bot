About Mouth Cancer

Mouth cancer, also known as oral cancer, is where a tumour develops on the surface of the tongue, mouth, lips or gums.

Tumours can also occur in the salivary glands, tonsils and the pharynx (the part of the throat from your mouth to your windpipe) but these are less common.

Symptoms of mouth cancer include:

  • red or white patches on the lining of your mouth or tongue
  • ulcers
  • a lump

Important:

Speak to your GP if these symptoms do not heal within 3 weeks, especially if you're a heavy drinker or smoker.

Types of Mouth Cancer

Squamous cell carcinoma is the most common type of mouth cancer, accounting for nine out of 10 cases.

Less common types include:

  • Oral malignant melanoma – cancer starting in melanocytes
  • Adenocarcinomas – cancers that develop inside salivary glands

Who is Affected

Mouth cancer is an uncommon type of cancer that most commonly affects adults aged 50-74 years old. It's more common in men than women.

Symptoms of Mouth Cancer

The most common symptoms of mouth cancer include:

  • red or white patches in the mouth or throat
  • a lump
  • ulcers

Other symptoms may include:

  • persistent pain in the mouth
  • pain or difficulty when swallowing (dysphagia)
  • changes in your voice, or speech problems
  • swollen lymph nodes (glands) in your neck
  • unexplained weight loss
  • bleeding or numbness in the mouth
  • a tooth that becomes loose for no obvious reason
  • difficulty moving your jaw

When to seek medical advice: Many of these symptoms can be caused by less serious conditions, but it's strongly recommended that you visit your GP if any of these symptoms have lasted for more than 3 weeks.

Dental Check-ups

Mouth cancer tends not to cause noticeable symptoms during initial stages. Regular dental check-ups are important, particularly if you are a smoker, heavy drinker or betel chewer.

Causes of Mouth Cancer

The two leading causes of mouth cancer in the UK are drinking too much alcohol and smoking.

Both alcohol and tobacco are carcinogenic, meaning they contain chemicals that can damage DNA in cells and lead to cancer.

High Risk: The risk of mouth cancer increases significantly in somebody who is both a heavy smoker and heavy drinker.

Other Risk Factors

Other risk factors for mouth cancer include:

  • chewing tobacco or other smokeless tobacco products
  • chewing betel nuts with or without tobacco
  • a poor diet
  • human papilloma virus (HPV)
  • poor oral hygiene

How Mouth Cancer Spreads

Mouth cancer can spread:

  • Directly – into nearby tissues like surrounding skin or jaw
  • Via the lymphatic system – through glands throughout the body

Diagnosing Mouth Cancer

If you have symptoms of mouth cancer, your GP will carry out a physical examination and ask about your symptoms. If mouth cancer is suspected, you'll be referred to hospital for further tests.

Biopsy Methods

Several biopsy methods may be used:

  • Punch biopsy – for easily accessible areas
  • Fine needle aspiration (FNA) – for neck swellings
  • Panendoscopy – for throat or nasal cavity areas

Further Tests

If cancer is confirmed, further tests may include:

  • X-rays
  • MRI scans
  • CT scans
  • PET scans

Staging and Grading

Cancer is staged (how far it has spread) and graded (how aggressive it is):

  • Early mouth cancer (usually curable)
  • Intermediate mouth cancer (may be curable)
  • Advanced mouth cancer (not usually curable but treatable)

Treating Mouth Cancer

Your treatment depends on the type, size, grade and spread of the cancer, as well as your general health.

Treatment Options

Main treatment options include:

  • Surgery – removing cancerous cells and sometimes surrounding tissue
  • Radiotherapy – using high-energy X-rays to kill cancerous cells
  • Chemotherapy – using powerful medications to kill cancerous cells

Before Treatment

Before treatment begins:

  • Full dental examination is needed
  • Stopping smoking and drinking increases treatment success

Surgery Types

Surgical options include:

  • Photodynamic therapy (PDT) for early stages
  • Partial glossectomy (tongue removal)
  • Jawbone removal and reconstruction
  • Lymph node removal

Radiotherapy Side Effects

Radiotherapy can cause:

  • Sore, red skin
  • Mouth ulcers
  • Dry mouth
  • Loss of taste
  • Tiredness

Complications of Mouth Cancer

Difficulty Swallowing (Dysphagia)

Surgery and radiotherapy can affect swallowing ability, which can lead to malnutrition and chest infections from food entering lungs.

Speech Problems

Treatment can affect the complex interaction of muscles needed for clear speech. Speech and language therapists can help with rehabilitation.

Emotional Impact

Living with mouth cancer can cause significant emotional disruption and may trigger depression. Signs include:

  • Feeling down or hopeless
  • No longer taking pleasure in things you used to enjoy

Support: Contact your GP if you think you may be depressed. Effective treatments include antidepressant medication and talking therapies.

Living with Mouth Cancer

Work and Employment

If you have cancer, you're covered by the Disability Discrimination Act. Your employer must make 'reasonable adjustments' such as:

  • Time off for treatment and medical appointments
  • Flexibility with working hours and tasks

Financial Support

Financial support options include:

  • Statutory Sick Pay if employed
  • Employment and Support Allowance if not working
  • Carer's Allowance for caregivers

Emotional Support

Helpful resources include:

  • Trained counsellors or psychologists
  • Local support groups
  • Online forums and chatrooms

For Caregivers

If you're caring for someone with mouth cancer:

  • Look after your own health with regular meals
  • Manage your emotional wellbeing
  • Seek support from friends, family and support groups

Prevention: The most effective ways to prevent mouth cancer are not smoking, keeping to recommended alcohol limits, and eating a healthy Mediterranean-style diet with plenty of fresh vegetables and fruits.
