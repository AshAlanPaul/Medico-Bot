Epilepsy

Epilepsy is a condition that affects the brain and is defined by repeated seizures.

Prevalence

Epilepsy affects more than 600,000 people in the UK. Almost one in every 100 people has the condition.

How Epilepsy Works

The cells in the brain, known as neurons, conduct electrical signals. They communicate with each other in the brain using chemical messengers. During a seizure, there are abnormal bursts of neurons firing off electrical impulses. This can cause the brain and body to behave strangely.

The severity of seizures can differ from person to person. Some people experience an odd feeling with no loss of awareness, or may have a "trance-like" state for a few seconds or minutes. Others lose consciousness and have convulsions (uncontrollable shaking of the body).

Note: Some people might only have a single seizure. If they do not have a high risk of having further seizures, they would not be regarded as having epilepsy.

Symptoms and Seizure Types

People with epilepsy can experience any type of seizure, although most people have a consistent pattern of symptoms. Seizures can occur when you're awake or asleep.

Types of Seizures

The type of seizure depends on how much of the brain it affects:

  • Focal (or partial) seizures – where only a small part of the brain is affected
  • Generalised seizures – where most or all of the brain is affected

Focal Seizures

Focal Aware Seizures ▼

Focal aware seizures are where you remain fully conscious throughout.

Symptoms can include:

  • A general strange feeling that is hard to describe
  • A "rising" feeling in your tummy
  • An intense feeling that events have happened before (déjà vu)
  • Experiencing an unusual smell or taste
  • A tingling sensation, or "pins and needles", in your arms and legs
  • A sudden intense feeling of fear or joy
  • Stiffness or twitching in part of the body

These seizures are sometimes known as "warnings" or "auras", because they can be a sign that another type of seizure is on its way.

Focal Impaired Awareness Seizures ▼

Focal impaired awareness seizures are when you lose your sense of awareness. You won't remember what happened after this type of seizure.

Symptoms can involve random bodily behaviour, like:

  • Smacking your lips
  • Rubbing your hands
  • Making random noises
  • Moving your arms around
  • Picking at clothes
  • Fiddling with objects
  • Adopting an unusual posture
  • Chewing or swallowing

Generalised Seizures

Absences ▼

Absence seizures mainly affect children, but they also occur in adults. They cause the person to lose awareness of their surroundings, usually for up to 15 seconds. The person will seem to stare vacantly into space.

Myoclonic Seizures ▼

These types of seizures cause your arms, legs or upper body to jerk or twitch, as if you have received an electric shock. They often only last for a fraction of a second.

Tonic-Clonic Seizures ▼

Tonic-clonic seizures or convulsions have 2 stages. Your body will initially become stiff and then your arms and legs will begin twitching. You'll lose consciousness and some people will wet themselves. The seizure normally lasts a minute, but can last longer.

This type of seizure is what most people think of as an epileptic fit.

First Aid and Emergency Response

PHONE 999 IF:
  • The seizure has not stopped after 5 minutes
  • The person has more than one seizure without recovering in between
  • You know it's the person's first seizure
  • The person is injured, has breathing problems, or needs emergency medical attention
  • The person's behaviour after a seizure is unsafe

What to Do If Someone Has a Seizure

  • Protect them from injury by removing any dangerous or potentially harmful objects nearby
  • Cushion their head with your hands or soft material
  • Do not restrain them or attempt to move them (unless they are in immediate danger)
  • Don't put anything in their mouth
  • Stay calm, and stay with them until they regain consciousness

When the convulsions have stopped, put them into the recovery position until they've recovered.

Status Epilepticus

Status epilepticus is the name for any seizure that lasts longer than 5 minutes, or a series of seizures where the person does not regain consciousness in between. This is a medical emergency and requires treatment as soon as possible.

Emergency Treatment: If you're trained to treat status epilepticus, you may use medications like buccal midazolam. If seizures continue after treatment, phone 999 immediately.

Causes and Diagnosis

In some cases of epilepsy a cause cannot be found. If there's an identifiable cause, it usually involves the brain being affected by a condition.

Possible Causes

Structural Causes ▼
  • Cerebrovascular disease (problems with blood vessels that supply the brain)
  • Brain tumours
  • Severe head injuries
Genetic Causes ▼

A genetic abnormality can cause epilepsy. Sometimes epilepsy is hereditary (passed on from one or both parents) or it can be a new gene abnormality.

Infectious Causes ▼

Infections like meningitis can cause damage to the brain which can result in epilepsy. Viral infections like HIV can also cause epilepsy.

Seizure Triggers

For many people with epilepsy, seizures can occur without any obvious trigger. However, certain circumstances can sometimes come before a seizure:

  • Stress
  • Lack of sleep
  • Withdrawal from alcohol following heavy drinking
  • Some medications
  • Illegal drugs
  • Menstrual cycle or period
  • Flashing lights (affects less than 5% of people with epilepsy)

Tracking Seizures: Keeping a seizure diary is a good way to help find out what might trigger your seizures. Record each seizure and make a note of what you were doing.

Diagnosis

Epilepsy is usually difficult to diagnose quickly. In most cases, it cannot be confirmed until you have had more than one seizure.

If you've had a seizure, you'll be referred to a specialist in epilepsy, usually a neurologist.

Diagnostic Tests

MRI Scan ▼

An MRI scan can often detect possible causes of epilepsy, such as defects in the structure of your brain.

EEG (Electroencephalogram) ▼

An EEG test can detect unusual brain activity associated with epilepsy. It measures the electrical activity of your brain through electrodes placed on your scalp.

Treatment Options

Most people with epilepsy can be successfully treated with anti-seizure medication (ASMs). ASMs do not cure epilepsy, but can prevent seizures from occurring.

Anti-Seizure Medications (ASMs)

There are many different ASMs. They work by changing the levels of the chemicals in your brain that conduct electrical impulses. This reduces the chance of a seizure.

Examples of commonly used ASMs include sodium valproate, carbamazepine, lamotrigine and levetiracetam.

Important: Never suddenly stop taking an ASM because it could cause a seizure. Always follow your doctor's instructions carefully.

Other Treatment Options

Brain Surgery ▼

Surgery may be considered when:

  • A single area of the brain is causing seizures (focal seizures)
  • Removing that part of the brain would not cause any significant loss of brain function
Vagus Nerve Stimulation (VNS) ▼

VNS involves surgically implanting a small electrical device that stimulates the vagus nerve. This can help reduce the frequency and severity of seizures.

Ketogenic Diet ▼

A ketogenic diet is high in fats and low in carbohydrates and protein. It's thought that it may make seizures less likely by altering the chemical composition of the brain. This should only be used under medical supervision.

Living with Epilepsy

Regular exercise and a healthy diet are recommended for everyone with epilepsy. They can help maintain overall health and wellbeing.

Important Considerations

Driving ▼

Legal Requirement: You must tell the Driving and Vehicle Licence Authority (DVLA) if you've had any epileptic seizures or blackouts. You must stop driving right away.

The DVLA may issue a licence if your seizures have never caused you to lose awareness or affected your ability to safely control a vehicle.

Pregnancy ▼

There's no reason why women with epilepsy cannot have a healthy pregnancy. However, it's better if the pregnancy is planned because there's a slightly higher risk of complications.

Some ASMs increase the chances of birth defects. If you're planning a pregnancy, talk to your epilepsy specialist about your medication.

Alcohol ▼

Alcohol can interact with anti-seizure medication. ASMs can heighten the effects of alcohol, and alcohol can make the side effects of ASMs worse.

Heavy drinking can disrupt sleep patterns, increasing chances of having a seizure.

SUDEP (Sudden Unexpected Death in Epilepsy)

When somebody with epilepsy dies and there's no clear cause, it's known as sudden unexpected death in epilepsy (SUDEP).

The risk of SUDEP for someone with epilepsy is low. Things that may lead to SUDEP include:

  • Having seizures which cause loss of consciousness and convulsions
  • Poorly controlled epilepsy
  • Being a young adult (in particular male)
  • Having sleep seizures
  • Having seizures when alone
  • Drinking large amounts of alcohol

Support Available: If you're worried that your epilepsy is poorly controlled, contact your epilepsy specialist. There are also support organizations that can provide help and guidance.
