About Constipation

Constipation is a common condition that affects people of all ages. It can mean that you're not passing stools regularly or you're unable to completely empty your bowel.

Constipation can also cause your stools to be hard and lumpy, as well as unusually large or small.

The severity of constipation varies from person to person. Many people only experience constipation for a short time, but for others, constipation can be a long-term (chronic) condition that causes significant pain and discomfort and affects quality of life.

Who's affected

Constipation can occur in babies, children and adults. It's estimated that around 1 in every 7 adults and up to 1 in every 3 children in the UK has constipation at any one time.

The condition affects twice as many women as men and is also more common in older adults and during pregnancy.

Symptoms of Constipation

When you're constipated, passing stools becomes more difficult and less frequent than usual.

Normal bowel habits vary from person to person. Some adults go to the toilet more than once a day, whereas others may only go every 3 or 4 days.

Other symptoms of constipation can include:

  • Stomach ache and cramps
  • Feeling bloated
  • Feeling sick
  • Loss of appetite
  • Stools that appear dry, hard and lumpy
  • Stools that are abnormally large or small

In children: Constipation in children may also cause loss of energy, irritability, foul-smelling wind, and general feeling of being unwell.

Causes of Constipation

Constipation usually occurs when stools remain in the colon (large intestine) for too long, and the colon absorbs too much water from the stools, causing them to become hard and dry.

Common causes include:

  • Not eating enough fibre
  • A change in your routine or lifestyle
  • Ignoring the urge to pass stools
  • Side effects of certain medications
  • Not drinking enough fluids
  • Anxiety or depression
  • Lack of exercise

Medication that can cause constipation:

  • Antacids
  • Antidepressants
  • Iron supplements
  • Opioid painkillers
  • Some blood pressure medications

Pregnancy: About 2 in every 5 women experience constipation during pregnancy, mostly during the early stages due to hormonal changes.

Diagnosing Constipation

Constipation is a very common condition. Your pharmacist won't usually need to carry out any tests or procedures, but will confirm a diagnosis based on your symptoms and medical history.

Constipation can be defined as:

  • Opening the bowels less than 3 times a week
  • Needing to strain to open your bowels on more than a quarter of occasions
  • Passing a hard or pellet-like stool on more than a quarter of occasions

When to see your GP

You should see your GP for advice if:

  • You notice any rectal bleeding
  • You notice any unexplained weight loss
  • You have persistent tiredness
  • Your constipation lasts longer than 14 days without improvement

Treating Constipation

Treatment for constipation depends on the cause, how long you've had it and how severe your symptoms are.

Lifestyle changes

Recommended changes:

  • Increase your daily intake of fibre (aim for 18-30g per day)
  • Drink plenty of fluids to avoid dehydration
  • Exercise more regularly
  • Don't ignore the urge to pass stools
  • Try resting your feet on a low stool while on the toilet

Laxatives

If lifestyle changes don't help, your pharmacist may recommend laxatives:

Types of laxatives:

  • Bulk-forming laxatives - Help stools retain fluid
  • Osmotic laxatives - Increase fluid in the bowels
  • Stimulant laxatives - Stimulate bowel muscles to move stools

Important: Always follow dosage instructions and consult with a healthcare professional before taking laxatives long-term.

Complications of Constipation

Constipation rarely causes any complications or long-term health problems. Treatment is usually effective, particularly if it's started promptly.

Possible complications include:

  • Haemorrhoids (piles) - Swollen blood vessels in the lower rectum
  • Faecal impaction - Dry, hard stools collect in the rectum
  • Bowel incontinence - Leakage of liquid stools
  • Anal fissures - Small tears around the anus

Seek medical attention if you experience rectal bleeding, as this could indicate a more serious condition.

Preventing Constipation

There are several effective ways to prevent constipation through diet and lifestyle changes.

Prevention strategies:

Diet and Nutrition

  • Eat plenty of fibre-rich foods (fruits, vegetables, wholegrains)
  • Drink adequate fluids throughout the day
  • Include seeds and oats in your diet
  • Aim for 30g of fibre daily

Lifestyle Habits

  • Exercise regularly (at least 150 minutes per week)
  • Never ignore the urge to pass stools
  • Establish a regular toilet routine
  • Allow enough time and privacy for bowel movements

Remember: Gradual changes to your diet and lifestyle are most effective for long-term prevention of constipation.
