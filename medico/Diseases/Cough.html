About Cough

A cough is a reflex action to clear your airways of mucus and irritants such as dust or smoke. It's rarely a sign of anything serious.

Most coughs clear up within 3 weeks and don't require any treatment.

Types of cough: A dry cough is tickly and doesn't produce phlegm. A chesty cough produces phlegm to help clear your airways.

When to Seek Help

Speak to a pharmacist for:

  • Advice about coughs
  • Cough treatments

Speak to a GP if:

  • You've had a cough for more than 3 weeks
  • Your cough is particularly severe
  • You cough up blood
  • You experience shortness of breath, breathing difficulties or chest pain
  • You have other worrying symptoms like unexplained weight loss, persistent voice changes, or lumps in your neck

Urgent advice for children:

Contact your GP or phone 111 if your child:

  • Is under 3 months old with temperature of 38°C or above
  • Is older than 3 months with temperature of 39°C or above
  • Has fewer wet nappies than usual
  • Isn't getting better after a few days
  • Is taking less than half their usual amount during feeds

If you're very concerned about your child, trust your instincts and seek medical advice.

Causes of Cough

Short-term Coughs (Usually clear within 3 weeks)

  • Upper respiratory tract infections (colds, flu, laryngitis, sinusitis, whooping cough)
  • Lower respiratory tract infections (acute bronchitis, pneumonia)
  • Allergies (allergic rhinitis, hay fever)
  • Flare-up of long-term conditions (asthma, COPD, chronic bronchitis)
  • Inhaled dust or smoke
  • Coronavirus (COVID-19)

Persistent Coughs

  • Long-term respiratory tract infections like chronic bronchitis
  • Asthma (usually with wheezing, chest tightness, shortness of breath)
  • Allergies
  • Smoking (smoker's cough can be a symptom of COPD)
  • Bronchiectasis (abnormally widened airways)
  • Postnasal drip (mucus dripping down throat from nose)
  • Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) - throat irritation from stomach acid
  • Certain medications like ACE inhibitors for high blood pressure

Rarely, a persistent cough can be a symptom of more serious conditions like lung cancer, heart failure, pulmonary embolism, or tuberculosis.

Cough Treatment

Treatment isn't always necessary for short-term coughs as they usually get better on their own within a few weeks.

Self-care measures:

  • Rest
  • Drink plenty of fluids
  • Take painkillers such as paracetamol or ibuprofen

Cough Medicines and Remedies

Medicines that suppress coughs or stop phlegm production are not usually recommended as there's little evidence they're better than simple home remedies.

A homemade remedy containing honey and lemon is likely to be just as useful and safer. Note: Honey shouldn't be given to babies under one year due to risk of infant botulism.

Treating Underlying Causes

  • Asthma: Treated with inhaled steroids
  • Allergies: Avoid allergens and take antihistamines
  • Bacterial infections: Treated with antibiotics
  • GORD: Treated with antacids and acid-reducing medication
  • COPD: Treated with bronchodilators

Important: If you smoke, quitting is likely to help improve your cough significantly.

Coughs in Children

Children can experience coughs from the same causes as adults, but there are some conditions more common in children:

  • Bronchiolitis: Mild respiratory infection with cold-like symptoms
  • Croup: Causes distinctive barking cough and harsh breathing sounds
  • Whooping cough: Intense hacking bouts of coughing, vomiting, and 'whooping' sound when breathing in

Occasionally, a persistent cough in a child can be a sign of a serious long-term condition such as cystic fibrosis.
