About Lung Cancer

Lung cancer is one of the most common and serious types of cancer.

There are usually no signs or symptoms in the early stages of lung cancer, but many people with the condition eventually develop symptoms including:

  • A persistent cough
  • Coughing up blood
  • Persistent breathlessness
  • Unexplained tiredness and weight loss
  • An ache or pain when breathing or coughing

Types of Lung Cancer

Cancer that begins in the lungs is called primary lung cancer. There are 2 main types of primary lung cancer:

  • Non-small-cell lung cancer – the most common type, accounting for more than 80% of cases
  • Small-cell lung cancer – a less common type that usually spreads faster than non-small-cell lung cancer

Who's Affected

Lung cancer mainly affects older people. It's rare in people younger than 40, and the rates of lung cancer rise sharply with age. Lung cancer is most commonly diagnosed in people aged 70 to 74.

Important: Although people who have never smoked can develop lung cancer, smoking is the main cause (accounting for over 85% of cases).

Symptoms of Lung Cancer

Symptoms of lung cancer develop as the condition progresses and there are usually no signs or symptoms in the early stages.

Main Symptoms

  • A cough that doesn't go away after 2 or 3 weeks
  • A long-standing cough that gets worse
  • Persistent chest infections
  • Coughing up blood
  • An ache or pain when breathing or coughing
  • Persistent breathlessness
  • Persistent tiredness or lack of energy
  • Loss of appetite or unexplained weight loss

Less Common Symptoms

  • Changes in the appearance of your fingers (finger clubbing)
  • A high temperature (fever) of 38C (100.4F) or above
  • Difficulty swallowing or pain when swallowing
  • Wheezing
  • A hoarse voice
  • Swelling of your face or neck
  • Persistent chest or shoulder pain

See your doctor: If you have any of these symptoms, you should speak to your GP, especially if you have a persistent cough or other respiratory symptoms that don't go away.

Causes of Lung Cancer

Most cases of lung cancer are caused by smoking, although people who have never smoked can also develop the condition.

Smoking

Smoking cigarettes is the single biggest risk factor for lung cancer. It's responsible for more than 85% of all cases.

Tobacco smoke contains more than 60 different toxic substances, which can lead to the development of cancer. If you smoke more than 25 cigarettes a day, you are 25 times more likely to get lung cancer than a non-smoker.

Passive Smoking

If you don't smoke, frequent exposure to other people's tobacco smoke (passive smoking) can increase your risk of developing lung cancer.

Radon

Radon is a naturally occurring radioactive gas that comes from tiny amounts of uranium present in all rocks and soils. It can sometimes be found in buildings and can damage your lungs if breathed in.

Occupational Exposure and Pollution

Exposure to certain chemicals and substances used in several occupations and industries has been linked to a slightly higher risk of developing lung cancer, including:

  • Arsenic
  • Asbestos
  • Beryllium
  • Cadmium
  • Coal and coke fumes
  • Silica
  • Nickel

Risk Reduction: The most effective way to reduce your risk of lung cancer is to avoid smoking and secondhand smoke exposure.

Diagnosing Lung Cancer

Speak to your GP if you have symptoms of lung cancer. Your GP will ask about your general health and what symptoms you've been experiencing.

Chest X-ray

A chest X-ray is usually the first test used to diagnose lung cancer. Most lung tumours show up on X-rays as a white-grey mass.

CT Scan

A CT scan is usually carried out after a chest X-ray. It uses X-rays and a computer to create detailed images of the inside of your body.

PET-CT Scan

A PET-CT scan may be carried out if the results of the CT scan show you have cancer at an early stage. This scan can show where there are active cancer cells.

Bronchoscopy and Biopsy

If the CT scan shows there might be cancer in the central part of your chest, you'll have a bronchoscopy. This procedure allows a doctor to remove a small sample of cells from inside your lungs for testing.

Staging

Once tests have been completed, it should be possible to work out what stage your cancer is, which helps determine the best treatment approach.

Early Detection: The earlier lung cancer is detected, the better the chances of successful treatment. Don't ignore persistent respiratory symptoms.

Treating Lung Cancer

Treatment for lung cancer depends on several factors, including the type of lung cancer, the size and position of the cancer, how far advanced it is, and your overall health.

Surgery

There are 3 main types of lung cancer surgery:

  • Lobectomy – removing one or more large parts of the lung
  • Pneumonectomy – removing the entire lung
  • Wedge resection – removing a small piece of the lung

Radiotherapy

Radiotherapy uses pulses of radiation to destroy cancer cells. It can be used to try to cure lung cancer if surgery isn't possible, or to control symptoms when a cure isn't possible.

Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy uses powerful cancer-killing medication to treat cancer. It can be used before or after surgery, to relieve symptoms, or combined with radiotherapy.

Other Treatments

Other treatments include biological therapies, radiofrequency ablation, cryotherapy, and photodynamic therapy, depending on the specific circumstances.

Treatment Team: Treatment for lung cancer is carried out by a team of specialists who work together to provide the best possible care for each individual patient.

Living with Lung Cancer

Living with lung cancer involves managing symptoms, emotional effects, and practical considerations.

Managing Breathlessness

Breathlessness is common in people with lung cancer. Some ways to manage it include:

  • Breathing in slowly through your nose and out through your mouth
  • Making daily activities easier
  • Using a fan to direct cool air towards your face
  • Eating smaller, more frequent meals

Pain Management

Some people with lung cancer experience pain. There are effective ways to manage cancer pain through medications and other approaches.

Emotional Support

Having cancer can lead to a range of emotions including shock, anxiety, relief, sadness and depression. Support is available through counseling, support groups, and talking to healthcare professionals.

Financial Support

If you have to reduce or stop work because of cancer, you may be entitled to financial support such as Statutory Sick Pay, Employment and Support Allowance, or Carer's Allowance.

Support Available: There are many resources and support services available for people living with lung cancer and their families. Don't hesitate to ask for help.

Preventing Lung Cancer

If you smoke, the best way to prevent lung cancer and other serious conditions is to stop smoking as soon as possible.

Quitting Smoking

However long you have been smoking, it's always worth quitting. Every year you don't smoke decreases your risk of getting serious illnesses. After 10 years of not smoking, your chances of developing lung cancer falls to half that of someone who smokes.

Healthy Diet

Research suggests that eating a low-fat, high-fibre diet, including at least 5 portions a day of fresh fruit and vegetables and plenty of whole grains, can reduce your risk of lung cancer.

Regular Exercise

There's strong evidence to suggest that regular exercise can lower the risk of developing lung cancer and other types of cancer. Adults should do at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity each week.

Most Important Prevention: Not smoking is the single most effective way to prevent lung cancer. If you smoke, seek help to quit as soon as possible.
