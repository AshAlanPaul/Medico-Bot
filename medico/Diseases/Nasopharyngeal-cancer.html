Nasopharyngeal Cancer

About Nasopharyngeal Cancer Symptoms Causes & Risk Factors Diagnosis Treatment

About Nasopharyngeal Cancer

Nasopharyngeal cancer is a rare type of cancer that affects the part of the throat connecting the back of the nose to the back of the mouth (the pharynx).

This cancer shouldn't be confused with other types of cancer that also affect the throat, such as laryngeal cancer and oesophageal cancer.

Important: Nasopharyngeal cancer is rare, and many people don't have symptoms until the cancer reaches an advanced stage. Regular check-ups are important if you're at higher risk.

Symptoms of Nasopharyngeal Cancer

It's often difficult to recognise nasopharyngeal cancer because the symptoms are similar to other, less serious conditions.

Symptoms can include:

  • A lump in the neck
  • Hearing loss - usually only in one ear
  • Tinnitus - hearing sounds from inside the body
  • A blocked or stuffy nose
  • Nosebleeds

When to see your GP: Speak to your GP if you develop any worrying symptoms, particularly if they don't improve after a few weeks. While they're very unlikely to be caused by nasopharyngeal cancer, it's best to get them checked out.

Causes and Risk Factors

The exact cause of nasopharyngeal cancer is unknown, but several factors can increase your risk of developing the condition.

Risk Factors Include:

  • Being of south Chinese or north African descent
  • Having a diet very high in salt-cured meats and fish
  • Being exposed to the Epstein-Barr virus (EBV)
  • Having a job where you're regularly exposed to hardwood dust
  • Having a first-degree relative who's had the condition
  • Exposure to human papilloma virus (HPV)

About 3 times as many men as women are affected by nasopharyngeal cancer. The average age at diagnosis is about 50.

Note: Having risk factors doesn't mean you'll definitely develop nasopharyngeal cancer, but it's important to be aware and discuss any concerns with your doctor.

Diagnosing Nasopharyngeal Cancer

If your GP suspects nasopharyngeal cancer, they'll refer you to hospital for further tests.

Diagnostic Tests May Include:

  • Nasendoscopy - A thin, flexible telescope is inserted through your nose to examine your throat
  • Imaging scans - MRI or CT scans to check for tumors and determine if cancer has spread
  • Panendoscopy - A detailed examination of your nose and throat under general anaesthetic
  • Biopsy - A small tissue sample is removed for laboratory examination

Once these tests are complete, your doctors will be able to confirm whether you have nasopharyngeal cancer and determine its stage.

Staging: The cancer will be 'staged' to describe how large it is and how far it has spread, which helps determine the best treatment approach.

Treatment Options

If you're diagnosed with nasopharyngeal cancer, you'll be cared for by a multidisciplinary team (MDT) of specialists who will discuss the best treatment options for your case.

Main Treatment Options

The two main treatments for nasopharyngeal cancer are:

  • Radiotherapy - Using radiation to kill cancer cells
  • Chemotherapy - Using medication to kill cancer cells

In most cases, a combination of radiotherapy and chemotherapy will be used. Surgery isn't usually used because it's difficult for surgeons to access the affected area.

Radiotherapy

Radiotherapy is the most commonly used treatment. It can be used alone for early-stage cancers or combined with chemotherapy for more advanced cancers.

Advanced techniques like intensity-modulated radiation therapy (IMRT) are often used to maximize the dose to the tumor while minimizing effects on healthy tissue.

Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy may be used before or alongside radiotherapy for more advanced nasopharyngeal cancers. It's usually given through a vein every 3 to 4 weeks over several months.

Follow-up Care

After treatment ends, you'll need regular follow-up appointments and scans to monitor your recovery and check for any signs of the cancer returning.

Support: Treatment can be challenging, but support is available from your healthcare team. Don't hesitate to discuss any concerns about side effects or your treatment plan.
