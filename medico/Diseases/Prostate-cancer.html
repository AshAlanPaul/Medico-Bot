About Prostate Cancer

Prostate cancer is the most common cancer in men and anyone with a prostate in the UK. It usually develops slowly, so there may be no signs for many years.

What is the Prostate?

The prostate is a small gland in the pelvis, about the size of a satsuma. It's located between the penis and the bladder and surrounds the urethra. Its main function is to help in the production of semen.

Risk Factors

  • Age – Risk increases with age, most cases develop in people aged 50+
  • Ethnicity – More common in people of African-Caribbean descent
  • Family history – Higher risk if close relatives had prostate cancer
  • Obesity – May increase risk
  • Diet – High calcium diets may increase risk

Symptoms of Prostate Cancer

Symptoms often only appear when the prostate is large enough to affect the urethra. Common symptoms include:

  • Needing to urinate more frequently, often during the night
  • Difficulty starting to urinate (hesitancy)
  • Straining or taking a long time while urinating
  • Weak urine flow
  • Feeling that your bladder hasn't emptied fully
  • Needing to rush to the toilet

Note: These symptoms are more commonly caused by benign prostate enlargement than cancer. Always consult your doctor for proper diagnosis.

Causes of Prostate Cancer

The exact causes of prostate cancer are not fully understood, but several factors can increase risk:

  • Age – Risk increases significantly after age 50
  • Family history – Having a brother or father with prostate cancer increases risk
  • Genetics – Certain inherited genes may increase susceptibility
  • Ethnicity – Higher incidence in African-Caribbean populations
  • Diet – High calcium intake may be linked to increased risk
  • Obesity – May be associated with more aggressive prostate cancer

Diagnosing Prostate Cancer

There is no single test for prostate cancer. Diagnosis typically involves:

Initial Assessment

  • Urine test to check for infection
  • PSA (Prostate-Specific Antigen) blood test
  • Digital Rectal Examination (DRE)

Further Testing

  • Biopsy – Taking tissue samples from the prostate
  • MRI or CT scans
  • Bone scan if spread is suspected

PSA Testing: PSA levels can be raised for many reasons besides cancer. A raised PSA doesn't necessarily mean cancer is present.

Testing for Prostate Cancer

There is currently no national screening programme for prostate cancer in the UK due to concerns about overdiagnosis and overtreatment.

PSA Screening Considerations

  • PSA tests can produce false-positive results
  • May detect slow-growing cancers that never cause symptoms
  • Treatment side effects can significantly impact quality of life
  • Balancing benefits against potential harms is complex

If you're considering PSA testing, discuss the benefits and risks thoroughly with your GP.

Treating Prostate Cancer

Treatment depends on the cancer stage, your age, overall health, and personal preferences.

Treatment Options

  • Active Surveillance – Monitoring low-risk cancers
  • Watchful Waiting – For older patients with other health issues
  • Surgery – Radical prostatectomy
  • Radiotherapy – External beam or brachytherapy
  • Hormone Therapy – To block testosterone effects
  • Chemotherapy – For advanced cancer
  • Newer Treatments – HIFU, cryotherapy (still in trials)

Potential Side Effects

  • Erectile dysfunction
  • Urinary incontinence
  • Bowel problems
  • Fatigue

Important: All treatments carry risks of side effects. Discuss these thoroughly with your healthcare team before making decisions.

Living With Prostate Cancer

A prostate cancer diagnosis can affect many aspects of daily life, but support is available.

Managing Side Effects

  • Erectile Dysfunction – Medications, vacuum pumps, counseling
  • Urinary Incontinence – Pelvic floor exercises, medications, surgery
  • Emotional Support – Counseling, support groups, talking therapies

Financial Support

If cancer affects your ability to work, you may be eligible for:

  • Statutory Sick Pay
  • Employment and Support Allowance
  • Carer's Allowance (for caregivers)

Relationships and Support

Open communication with family and friends is important. Consider joining support groups or online communities to connect with others facing similar challenges.

Support Available: Organizations like Prostate Cancer UK offer specialist nurse helplines, online communities, and comprehensive information resources.
