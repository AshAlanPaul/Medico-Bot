About Breast Cancer

Breast cancer is the most common type of cancer in the UK.

Most women who get it (8 out of 10) are over 50, but younger women, and in rare cases, men can also get breast cancer.

If it's treated early enough, breast cancer can be prevented from spreading to other parts of the body.

The Breasts

The breasts are made up of fat, connective tissue and thousands of tiny glands called lobules, which produce milk. When a woman has a baby, the milk is delivered to the nipple through tiny tubes called ducts, which allow her to breastfeed.

Types of Breast Cancer

There are several different types of breast cancer, which can develop in different parts of the breast. Breast cancer is often divided into non-invasive and invasive types.

Non-invasive Breast Cancer

Non-invasive breast cancer is also known as cancer or carcinoma in situ. This cancer is found in the ducts of the breast and hasn't developed the ability to spread outside the breast.

Invasive Breast Cancer

Invasive cancer has the ability to spread outside the breast, although this doesn't necessarily mean it has spread.

Note: The most common form of breast cancer is invasive ductal breast cancer, which develops in the cells that line the breast ducts and accounts for about 80% of all breast cancer cases.

Symptoms of Breast Cancer

The first symptom of breast cancer most women notice is a lump or an area of thickened tissue in their breast.

Most breast lumps (90%) aren't cancerous, but it's always best to have them checked by your doctor.

You should speak to your GP if you notice any of the following:

  • A lump or area of thickened tissue in either breast
  • A change in the size or shape of one or both breasts
  • Discharge from either of your nipples (which may be streaked with blood)
  • A lump or swelling in either of your armpits
  • Dimpling on the skin of your breasts
  • A rash on or around your nipple
  • A change in the appearance of your nipple, such as becoming sunken into your breast

Important: Breast pain alone isn't a symptom of breast cancer, but any persistent changes should be checked by a doctor.

Causes and Risk Factors of Breast Cancer

The causes of breast cancer aren't fully understood, making it difficult to say why one woman may develop breast cancer and another may not.

However, there are risk factors that are known to affect your likelihood of developing breast cancer.

Age

The risk of developing breast cancer increases with age. The condition is most common among women over 50 who have been through the menopause. About 8 out of 10 cases of breast cancer occur in women over 50.

Family History

If you have close relatives who have had breast cancer or ovarian cancer, you may have a higher risk of developing breast cancer.

Previous Diagnosis

If you've previously had breast cancer or early non-invasive cancer cell changes in breast ducts, you have a higher risk of developing it again.

Other Risk Factors

  • Being overweight or obese after menopause
  • Alcohol consumption
  • Hormone replacement therapy (HRT)
  • Radiation exposure
  • Early menstruation or late menopause

Remember: Having risk factors doesn't mean you will definitely develop breast cancer, and many people with breast cancer have no known risk factors.

Diagnosing Breast Cancer

If you have suspected breast cancer, either because of your symptoms or because your mammogram has shown an abnormality, you'll be referred to a specialist breast clinic for further tests.

Mammogram and Breast Ultrasound

A mammogram produces an X-ray of your breasts. You may also need an ultrasound scan, which uses high-frequency sound waves to produce an image of the inside of your breasts.

Biopsy

A biopsy is where a sample of tissue cells is taken from your breast and tested to see if it's cancerous. This is the only definitive way to diagnose breast cancer.

Staging and Grading

If cancer is diagnosed, further tests determine the stage (how far it has spread) and grade (how abnormal the cells look) of the cancer.

Note: Early diagnosis significantly improves treatment outcomes. Regular screening is recommended for women over 50.

Treating Breast Cancer

Treatment for breast cancer depends on the type of cancer, its stage and grade, and your overall health.

Surgery

Surgery is usually the first type of treatment for breast cancer. The main types are:

  • Breast-conserving surgery - removing the cancerous lump and a small area of surrounding tissue
  • Mastectomy - removing the entire breast

Radiotherapy

Radiotherapy uses controlled doses of radiation to kill cancer cells. It's usually given after surgery to kill any remaining cancer cells.

Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy uses anti-cancer medications to kill cancer cells. It may be used before surgery to shrink tumors or after surgery to destroy remaining cancer cells.

Hormone Therapy

Some breast cancers are stimulated to grow by hormones. Hormone therapy works by blocking these hormones or lowering their levels in the body.

Biological Therapy

Biological therapies target specific characteristics of cancer cells, such as proteins that allow them to grow rapidly.

Good to know: Treatment plans are personalized for each patient, and many women receive a combination of these treatments.

Living with Breast Cancer

Being diagnosed with breast cancer can affect daily life in many ways, depending on what stage it's at and what treatment you're having.

Recovery and Follow-up

After treatment has finished, you'll be invited for regular check-ups to monitor your recovery and check for any signs of recurrence.

Body Image and Relationships

Breast cancer treatment can affect how you feel about your body and your relationships. Support is available to help you cope with these changes.

Support Services

There are many support services available, including counseling, support groups, and financial assistance for those who need it.

Remember: You don't have to face breast cancer alone. There are many resources and support networks available to help you through your journey.

Preventing Breast Cancer

As the causes of breast cancer aren't fully understood, it's not possible to know if it can be prevented altogether.

Lifestyle Factors

Some lifestyle choices may help reduce your risk:

  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Exercise regularly
  • Limit alcohol consumption
  • Breastfeed if possible
  • Limit hormone therapy after menopause

Screening

Regular breast screening (mammograms) can help detect breast cancer early when it's most treatable.

For High-Risk Women

Women with a significantly increased risk may consider preventive medications or surgery.

Important: While you can't guarantee prevention, these measures may help reduce your risk of developing breast cancer.
