About Chronic Lymphocytic Leukaemia

Chronic lymphocytic leukaemia (CLL) is a type of cancer that affects the white blood cells and tends to progress slowly over many years.

It mostly affects people over the age of 60 and is rare in people under 40. Children are almost never affected.

In CLL, the bone marrow produces too many white blood cells called lymphocytes that aren't fully developed and don't work properly.

Over time this can cause a range of problems, such as an increased risk of infections, persistent tiredness, swollen glands, and unusual bleeding or bruising.

Note: CLL is different from other types of leukaemia, including chronic myeloid leukaemia, acute lymphoblastic leukaemia and acute myeloid leukaemia.

Symptoms of CLL

CLL doesn't usually cause any symptoms early on and may only be picked up during a blood test carried out for another reason.

When symptoms develop, they may include:

  • Getting infections often
  • Anaemia – persistent tiredness, shortness of breath and pale skin
  • Bleeding and bruising more easily than normal
  • A high temperature (fever)
  • Night sweats
  • Swollen glands in your neck, armpits or groin
  • Swelling and discomfort in your tummy
  • Unintentional weight loss

Important: You should speak to your GP if you have any persistent or worrying symptoms. These symptoms can have other causes besides cancer, but it's important to get them checked out.

Diagnosing CLL

Most cases of CLL are detected during blood tests carried out for another reason.

Diagnostic Process

Your GP may:

  • Ask about your symptoms and medical history
  • Carry out a physical examination
  • Send off a blood sample for testing

Tests Used

  • Blood tests – Full blood count to check for abnormal lymphocytes
  • X-rays and scans – Chest X-ray, ultrasound, CT scan
  • Bone marrow biopsy – Sample taken from hip bone
  • Lymph node biopsy – Removal of swollen lymph gland
  • Genetic tests – To check for unusual genes in cancerous cells

Treating CLL

Treatment for CLL largely depends on what stage the condition is at when it's diagnosed.

Stages of CLL

  • Stage A – Fewer than 3 areas of enlarged lymph glands
  • Stage B – 3 or more areas of enlarged lymph glands
  • Stage C – Enlarged lymph glands or spleen with low blood cell counts

Treatment Options

Monitoring: Early-stage CLL may only require regular check-ups without immediate treatment.

Chemotherapy: Main treatment for advanced CLL using medications like:

  • Fludarabine
  • Cyclophosphamide
  • Rituximab

Other Treatments

  • Stem cell or bone marrow transplants
  • Radiotherapy to shrink enlarged glands
  • Surgery to remove swollen spleen
  • Antibiotics and other medications to prevent infections

Treatment outlook: Treatment can often help keep CLL under control for many years. It may go into remission but can relapse later.

Complications of CLL

Infections

People with CLL have a weakened immune system and are more vulnerable to infections.

Richter's Syndrome

In up to 1 in 20 people with CLL, the condition changes to become similar to an aggressive form of non-Hodgkin lymphoma.

Autoimmune Haemolytic Anaemia

About 1 in 10 people with CLL develop this condition where the immune system attacks red blood cells.

Psychological Effects

Being diagnosed with CLL can be very distressing. Support is available through healthcare teams and cancer support organizations.

Support available: Macmillan Cancer Support provides excellent help and support. Their helpline is 0808 808 00 00, Monday to Friday, 9am to 8pm.

Causes & Risk Factors

It's not known what causes most cases of leukaemia, but several risk factors increase your chances of developing CLL.

Risk Factors

  • Family history – Having a parent or sibling with CLL slightly increases risk
  • Ethnicity – Most common in people of European, American and Australian origin
  • Age – Risk increases with age, most common in people over 60
  • Gender – Men are about twice as likely to develop CLL
  • Medical conditions – Certain conditions like pneumonia, shingles, and autoimmune disorders

Note: There's no proven link with radiation or chemical exposure, diet or infections. You can't catch CLL from anyone else or pass it on.
