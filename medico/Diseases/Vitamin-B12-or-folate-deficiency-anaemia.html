Vitamin B12/Folate Deficiency Anemia

Vitamin B12 or folate deficiency anemia occurs when a lack of these vitamins causes the body to produce abnormally large red blood cells that can't function properly.

Red blood cells carry oxygen around the body using a substance called hemoglobin. Anemia is the general term for having either fewer red blood cells than normal or having an abnormally low amount of hemoglobin in each red blood cell.

When to see your GP: See your GP if you think you may have a vitamin B12 or folate deficiency. These conditions can often be diagnosed based on your symptoms and blood test results.

Importance of Early Diagnosis

It's important for vitamin B12 or folate deficiency anemia to be diagnosed and treated as soon as possible because, although many of the symptoms improve with treatment, some problems caused by the condition can be irreversible if left untreated.

Symptoms of Vitamin B12/Folate Deficiency

Vitamin B12 and folate perform several important functions in the body, including keeping the nervous system healthy. A deficiency in either of these vitamins can cause a wide range of problems.

General Anemia Symptoms

General symptoms of anemia may include:

  • Extreme tiredness (fatigue)
  • Lack of energy (lethargy)
  • Breathlessness
  • Feeling faint
  • Headaches
  • Pale skin
  • Noticeable heartbeats (palpitations)
  • Tinnitus (hearing sounds from inside the body)
  • Loss of appetite and weight loss

Specific Vitamin B12 Deficiency Symptoms

Additional symptoms of vitamin B12 deficiency may include:

  • A pale yellow tinge to your skin
  • A sore and red tongue (glossitis)
  • Mouth ulcers
  • Pins and needles (paraesthesia)
  • Changes in the way that you walk and move around
  • Disturbed vision
  • Irritability and depression
  • Changes in thinking, feeling and behavior
  • Decline in mental abilities such as memory and judgment

Specific Folate Deficiency Symptoms

Additional symptoms of folate deficiency may include:

  • Reduced sense of taste
  • Diarrhea
  • Numbness and tingling in the feet and hands
  • Muscle weakness
  • Depression

Important: Some people can experience problems related to vitamin deficiency despite having normal blood levels. If you have symptoms, it's important to discuss them with your GP even if previous tests were normal.

Causes of Vitamin B12/Folate Deficiency

Causes of Vitamin B12 Deficiency

Pernicious Anemia

This is the most common cause of vitamin B12 deficiency in the UK. It's an autoimmune condition where your immune system attacks cells in your stomach that produce intrinsic factor, which is needed to absorb vitamin B12.

Other causes of vitamin B12 deficiency include:

  • Diet: Not getting enough vitamin B12 from your diet (common in vegans)
  • Stomach conditions: Conditions or surgeries that affect stomach function
  • Intestinal conditions: Conditions like Crohn's disease that affect absorption
  • Medication: Certain medications like proton pump inhibitors

Causes of Folate Deficiency

Causes of folate deficiency include:

  • Diet: Not eating enough folate-rich foods
  • Malabsorption: Conditions affecting nutrient absorption
  • Excessive urination: Conditions causing frequent urination
  • Medication: Certain medications that reduce folate levels
  • Increased need: Pregnancy, cancer, or blood disorders

Vitamin B12 Sources

  • Meat
  • Fish (salmon, cod)
  • Milk and dairy products
  • Eggs
  • Yeast extract (Marmite)
  • Fortified foods

Folate Sources

  • Broccoli
  • Brussels sprouts
  • Asparagus
  • Peas
  • Chickpeas
  • Brown rice

Diagnosing Vitamin B12/Folate Deficiency

A diagnosis of vitamin B12 or folate deficiency anemia can often be made by your GP based on your symptoms and blood test results.

Blood Tests

Different types of blood tests can help identify deficiencies by checking:

  • Hemoglobin levels
  • Red blood cell size
  • Vitamin B12 levels in blood
  • Folate levels in blood

Note: Current blood tests measure total vitamin B12, including both active and inactive forms. Some people may have normal total B12 levels but still experience deficiency symptoms if most of the B12 is in inactive form.

Identifying the Cause

If deficiency is confirmed, your GP may arrange further tests to identify the underlying cause, which helps determine the most appropriate treatment.

Specialist Referral

You may be referred to specialists such as:

  • Haematologist: For blood disorders
  • Gastroenterologist: For absorption issues
  • Dietitian: For dietary-related deficiencies

Treating Vitamin B12/Folate Deficiency

Most people can be easily treated with injections or tablets to replace the missing vitamins.

Treating Vitamin B12 Deficiency

Vitamin B12 deficiency is usually treated with injections of hydroxocobalamin:

  • Initial treatment: Injections every other day for two weeks
  • Maintenance: Injections every three months (or more frequently if needed)
  • Diet-related deficiency: May be treated with daily tablets instead

Treating Folate Deficiency

Folate deficiency anemia is treated with daily folic acid tablets:

  • Usually taken for about four months
  • May be needed longer if the underlying cause persists
  • Often combined with dietary improvements

Important: Before starting folic acid treatment, your GP will check your vitamin B12 levels. Folic acid treatment can sometimes mask an underlying vitamin B12 deficiency, which could affect your nervous system if left untreated.

Monitoring Treatment

Your treatment will be monitored with follow-up blood tests:

  • Initial check: 10-14 days after starting treatment
  • Follow-up: Around 8 weeks to confirm success
  • Long-term: Annual checks may be needed if at risk of recurrence

Complications of Vitamin B12/Folate Deficiency

Although most cases can be effectively treated, complications can develop if the deficiency persists untreated.

General Anemia Complications

All types of anemia can lead to:

  • Abnormally fast heartbeat (tachycardia)
  • Heart failure

Vitamin B12 Deficiency Complications

Specific complications of B12 deficiency include:

  • Neurological problems: Vision problems, memory loss, nerve damage
  • Temporary infertility
  • Increased stomach cancer risk (with pernicious anemia)
  • Neural tube defects in pregnancy

Folate Deficiency Complications

Specific complications of folate deficiency include:

  • Temporary infertility
  • Increased cardiovascular disease risk
  • Increased cancer risk
  • Pregnancy complications: Premature birth, low birth weight
  • Neural tube defects in unborn babies

Prevention: Most complications can be prevented with early diagnosis and appropriate treatment. If you're pregnant or planning pregnancy, taking folic acid supplements as recommended can prevent neural tube defects.
