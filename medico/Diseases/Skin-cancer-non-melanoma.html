About Non-Melanoma Skin Cancer

Skin cancer is one of the most common cancers in the world. Non-melanoma skin cancer refers to a group of cancers that slowly develop in the upper layers of the skin.

The term 'non-melanoma' distinguishes these more common kinds of skin cancer from the less common skin cancer known as melanoma, which spreads faster in the body.

Types of Non-Melanoma Skin Cancer

  • Basal cell carcinoma: Starts in the cells lining the bottom of the epidermis and accounts for about 75% of skin cancers
  • Squamous cell carcinoma: Starts in the cells lining the top of the epidermis and accounts for about 20% of skin cancers

When to see a doctor: Speak to your GP if you have any skin abnormality that hasn't healed after 4 weeks. Although it is unlikely to be skin cancer, it is best to be sure.

Symptoms of Non-Melanoma Skin Cancer

The main symptom is the appearance of a lump or discoloured patch on the skin that doesn't heal. Non-melanoma skin cancer most often appears on areas of skin regularly exposed to the sun.

Basal Cell Carcinoma Symptoms

  • Small red or pink lump
  • Pearly-white or 'waxy' looking lump
  • Red, scaly patch
  • May become crusty, bleed or develop into a painless ulcer

Squamous Cell Carcinoma Symptoms

  • Firm pink lump
  • Flat, scaly and crusted surface
  • Often tender to touch
  • Bleeds easily
  • May develop into an ulcer

When to Seek Medical Advice

If you develop a lump, lesion or skin discolouration that hasn't healed after 4 weeks, speak to your GP. While it is unlikely to be cancer, it is best to be sure.

Causes of Non-Melanoma Skin Cancer

Most skin cancer is caused by ultraviolet (UV) light damaging the DNA in skin cells. The main source of UV light is sunlight.

UV Light Sources

  • Sunlight (UVA and UVB)
  • Sunlamps and tanning beds
  • Repeated sunburn

Risk Factors

  • Pale skin that does not tan easily
  • Red or blonde hair
  • Blue eyes
  • Older age
  • Large number of moles or freckles
  • Family history of skin cancer
  • Previous skin cancer diagnosis
  • Weakened immune system
  • Exposure to certain chemicals

Prevention: You can reduce your chances by avoiding overexposure to UV light, using sunscreen, and avoiding sunbeds and sunlamps.

Diagnosing Non-Melanoma Skin Cancer

A diagnosis usually begins with a visit to your GP who will examine your skin and decide whether you need further assessment by a specialist.

Biopsy Procedure

If skin cancer is suspected, you may be referred to a dermatologist or plastic surgeon who will perform a biopsy. This involves:

  • Removing part or all of the tumour
  • Studying it under a microscope
  • Usually done under local anaesthetic

Further Tests

For basal cell carcinoma, further tests are usually not needed as the cancer rarely spreads. For squamous cell carcinoma, additional tests may include:

  • Physical examination of lymph nodes
  • Fine needle aspiration if needed

Treating Non-Melanoma Skin Cancer

Surgery is the main treatment for non-melanoma skin cancer. Treatment is successful for more than 90% of people with this condition.

Surgical Treatments

  • Surgical excision: Cutting out the cancer with surrounding healthy tissue
  • Curettage and electrocautery: For smaller cancers
  • Mohs micrographic surgery: For high-risk areas like nose or eyes

Non-Surgical Treatments

  • Cryotherapy: Freezing the cancer with liquid nitrogen
  • Radiotherapy: Using radiation to destroy cancer cells
  • Photodynamic therapy (PDT): Using light-sensitive cream and strong light
  • Chemotherapy creams: Applied directly to the skin
  • Imiquimod cream: Encourages immune system to attack cancer

Success rates: Treatment for non-melanoma skin cancer is completely successful in approximately 90% of cases. The risk of spreading to other parts of the body is very low.
