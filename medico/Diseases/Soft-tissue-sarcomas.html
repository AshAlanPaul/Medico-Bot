About Soft Tissue Sarcomas

Soft tissue sarcomas are a group of rare cancers affecting the tissues that connect, support and surround other body structures and organs.

Tissues that can be affected by soft tissue sarcomas include:

  • Fat
  • Muscle
  • Blood vessels
  • Deep skin tissues
  • Tendons and ligaments

Soft tissue sarcomas can develop in almost any part of the body, including the legs, arms and the trunk (torso).

Types of Soft Tissue Sarcomas

  • Leiomyosarcomas – develop in muscle tissue
  • Liposarcomas – develop in fat tissue
  • Angiosarcomas – develop in the cells of the blood or lymph vessels
  • Gastrointestinal stromal tumours (GISTs) – develop in connective tissues supporting digestive organs

Note: Soft tissue sarcomas account for around 1 in every 100 cancers diagnosed in the UK.

Causes & Risk Factors

Cancer develops when cells multiply uncontrollably, forming tumours. In most soft tissue sarcomas, the exact cause is unclear, but several factors increase the risk:

  • Age – can occur at any age but more common in middle-aged or elderly people
  • Genetic conditions – such as neurofibromatosis type 1 and retinoblastoma
  • Previous radiotherapy – some people develop sarcomas years after previous cancer treatment
  • Chemical exposure – including vinyl chloride, dioxins and phenoxyacetic herbicides

Kaposi's sarcoma is a specific type caused by human herpesvirus type 8 (HHV-8) in people with weakened immune systems (such as those with HIV).

Signs and Symptoms

As soft tissue sarcomas can develop in most parts of the body, they can cause a wide range of symptoms depending on their location:

  • A soft, painless lump under the skin or deeper that can't be easily moved and gets bigger over time
  • Abdominal pain, feeling of fullness, or constipation (if near the stomach)
  • Cough or breathlessness (if near the lungs)

Important: You should speak to your GP if you have a worrying lump or any other troublesome symptoms. A lump the size of a golf ball or larger should be regarded as suspicious and needs to be investigated urgently.

There are often no obvious symptoms in the early stages of soft tissue sarcomas.

Diagnosing Soft Tissue Sarcomas

If your GP suspects cancer, they will refer you for hospital tests. A diagnosis is usually made by a hospital specialist based on:

  • Your symptoms
  • Physical examination
  • Biopsy – where a sample of suspected cancerous tissue is removed and analyzed
  • Imaging tests – mainly MRI scans, but other scans may also be used

These tests help determine the cancer's stage (how far it has spread) and grade (how aggressive it is).

Note: You should be referred for further tests if your GP suspects soft tissue sarcoma based on NICE guidelines for cancer recognition and referral.

Treating Soft Tissue Sarcomas

Your care team will help decide the most appropriate treatment based on factors such as cancer stage and grade, tumor location, and your overall health.

Main Treatment Options

Surgery

The main treatment for early-stage sarcomas involves removing the cancerous tumor along with a section of surrounding healthy tissue. Every effort is made to preserve function and appearance, but in some cases, amputation may be necessary.

Radiotherapy

Often used after surgery to destroy remaining cancer cells. May also be used before surgery to shrink tumors or to relieve symptoms when surgery isn't possible.

Chemotherapy

Used before surgery to shrink tumors, or for sarcomas that cannot be surgically removed. Involves anti-cancer medication given intravenously.

Side effects: Both radiotherapy and chemotherapy can cause temporary but unpleasant side effects including fatigue, nausea, and hair loss.

Outlook

The outlook depends largely on how far the cancer has advanced when diagnosed:

  • Early detection – If detected early and removed completely, there is a possibility of cure
  • Follow-up care – Regular check-ups are needed after treatment to monitor for recurrence
  • Rehabilitation – Long-term physiotherapy and occupational therapy may be needed
  • Advanced stages – A cure may not be possible if detected late, but treatment can help slow spread and control symptoms

Remember: While soft tissue sarcomas are serious, many people respond well to treatment, especially when detected early.
