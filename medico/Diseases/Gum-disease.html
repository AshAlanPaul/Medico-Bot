Gum Disease Information

About Gum Disease Symptoms Causes Treatment Complications Prevention

About Gum Disease

Gum disease is a very common condition where the gums become swollen, sore or infected.

Most adults in the UK have gum disease to some degree and most people experience it at least once. It's much less common in children.

Stages of Gum Disease

  • Gingivitis: Early stage with bleeding gums and bad breath
  • Periodontitis: Advanced stage affecting tissues that support teeth
  • Advanced Periodontitis: Can lead to bone damage and tooth loss

Dental Emergency: If you have a dental problem, always phone your regular dental practice first. If not registered with a dentist, seek advice on dental emergencies.

Dental Check-ups

Regular dental check-ups are crucial for detecting and treating gum problems early:

  • Good oral health: Check-ups every 1-2 years
  • Previous gum disease: More frequent visits needed
  • High risk factors (smoking, diabetes): More frequent monitoring

Symptoms of Gum Disease

Healthy gums should be pink, firm and keep your teeth securely in place without bleeding when touched or brushed.

Early Symptoms (Gingivitis)

  • Red and swollen gums
  • Bleeding gums after brushing or flossing

Advanced Symptoms (Periodontitis)

  • Bad breath (halitosis)
  • Unpleasant taste in mouth
  • Loose teeth making eating difficult
  • Gum abscesses (painful pus collections)

Acute Necrotising Ulcerative Gingivitis (ANUG)

A rare but severe condition with symptoms including:

  • Bleeding, painful gums
  • Painful ulcers
  • Receding gums between teeth
  • Bad breath and metallic taste
  • Difficulty swallowing or talking
  • High fever

Important: Gum disease isn't always painful. Regular dental check-ups are essential for early detection.

Causes of Gum Disease

Poor oral hygiene is the most common cause of gum disease, but several factors can increase your risk.

Plaque Build-up

Plaque is a sticky film of bacteria that forms on teeth. When not removed:

  • Bacteria in plaque irritate gums
  • Plaque hardens into tartar (calculus)
  • Tartar requires professional removal
  • Acid from plaque causes tooth decay

Risk Factors

  • Smoking: Significantly increases risk
  • Age: More common in older adults
  • Family history: Genetic predisposition
  • Diabetes: Increases susceptibility
  • Weakened immune system: HIV/AIDS, chemotherapy
  • Malnutrition: Poor nutrient intake
  • Stress: Can affect immune response

Treating Gum Disease

Good oral hygiene is the foundation of gum disease treatment, with additional dental procedures for advanced cases.

Oral Hygiene Practices

  • Brush teeth for 2 minutes twice daily
  • Use fluoride toothpaste
  • Floss regularly (preferably daily)
  • Avoid smoking
  • Attend regular dental check-ups

Dental Treatments

Scale and Polish

Professional cleaning to remove plaque and tartar buildup.

Root Planing

Deep cleaning under gums to remove bacteria from tooth roots. May require local anaesthetic.

Surgical Treatment

For severe cases, periodontal surgery or tooth extraction may be necessary.

Treating ANUG (Acute Condition)

Requires immediate dental attention with:

  • Antibiotics (metronidazole or amoxicillin)
  • Painkillers (paracetamol or ibuprofen)
  • Special mouthwashes (chlorhexidine or hydrogen peroxide)

Mouthwash Considerations

Antiseptic mouthwasches can help control plaque but:

  • Chlorhexidine can stain teeth brown
  • Rinse between brushing and using mouthwash
  • Consult dentist for appropriate type and usage

Complications of Gum Disease

Untreated gum disease can lead to serious dental and overall health problems.

Dental Complications

  • Recurrent gum abscesses
  • Damage to periodontal ligament
  • Bone loss in jaw
  • Receding gums
  • Loose teeth
  • Tooth loss

ANUG Complications

If not properly treated, ANUG can cause:

  • Complete destruction of gums between teeth
  • Permanent holes in gums from ulcers
  • Loose and unstable teeth
  • Persistent bad breath and bleeding
  • Gangrene in rare cases (tissue death)

Associated Health Risks

Gum disease has been linked to increased risk of:

  • Cardiovascular disease
  • Lung infections
  • Premature labour in pregnancy
  • Low birth weight babies

Note: While associations exist, clear evidence of direct causation is still being researched.

Prevention of Gum Disease

Preventing gum disease is primarily about maintaining excellent oral hygiene and regular dental care.

Prevention Strategies

  • Brush teeth thoroughly twice daily
  • Floss between teeth daily
  • Use fluoride toothpaste
  • Attend regular dental check-ups
  • Avoid smoking and tobacco products
  • Maintain balanced nutrition
  • Manage stress levels
  • Control underlying health conditions (like diabetes)

When to See a Dentist

Schedule a dental appointment if you experience:

  • Painful, swollen or bleeding gums
  • Persistent bad breath
  • Loose teeth
  • Receding gums
  • Changes in bite or tooth alignment

Remember: Early detection and treatment of gum disease can prevent serious complications and tooth loss. Regular dental visits are your best defense against advanced gum disease.
