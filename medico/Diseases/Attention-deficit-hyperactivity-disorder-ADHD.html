About ADHD

Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is a group of behavioural symptoms that include:

  • Difficulty concentrating and paying attention
  • Hyperactivity
  • Impulsiveness

What is ADHD?

ADHD is a neurodevelopmental disorder that affects both children and adults. It can impact various aspects of life including school, work, and relationships.

Note: ADHD symptoms tend to be noticed at an early age and may become more noticeable when a child's circumstances change, like when they start school.

Symptoms of ADHD in Children and Teenagers

The main symptoms of ADHD in children and teenagers are divided into two categories:

Inattentiveness

The main signs of inattentiveness are:

  • Having a short attention span
  • Being easily distracted
  • Making careless mistakes
  • Appearing forgetful
  • Losing things
  • Being unable to stick at tedious or time-consuming tasks
  • Appearing to be unable to listen to or carry out instructions
  • Constantly changing activity or task
  • Having difficulty organising tasks

Hyperactivity and Impulsiveness

The main signs of hyperactivity and impulsiveness are:

  • Being unable to sit still, especially in quiet surroundings
  • Constantly fidgeting
  • Being unable to concentrate on tasks
  • Excessive physical movement
  • Excessive talking
  • Being unable to wait their turn
  • Acting without thinking
  • Interrupting conversations
  • Little or no sense of danger

Important: Some children with ADHD may also have other conditions such as anxiety disorder, depression, or learning difficulties.

Symptoms of ADHD in Adults

If you're an adult with ADHD, you may find that:

  • You get easily distracted and find it hard to notice details
  • It's hard to listen to other people
  • It's hard to follow instructions
  • You find it hard to organise yourself
  • You find it hard to wait
  • You fidget and can't sit still
  • You're forgetful and tend to lose or misplace things
  • You easily get irritable, impatient or frustrated
  • You feel restless or edgy
  • You find stress hard to handle
  • You tend to do things on the spur of the moment

ADD (Attention Deficit Disorder)

Some people with ADHD have problems with inattentiveness, but not with hyperactivity or impulsiveness. This form of ADHD is also known as attention deficit disorder (ADD). ADD can sometimes go unnoticed because the symptoms may be less obvious.

Note: Adults with ADHD may also experience related conditions such as depression, personality disorders, or obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD).

Causes of ADHD

The exact cause of ADHD isn't fully understood. A combination of the following factors might be responsible:

Genetics

ADHD tends to run in families. The genes you inherit from your parents are a factor in developing the condition. The way ADHD is inherited is complex and isn't thought to be related to a single gene.

Brain Function and Structure

Research has identified some possible differences in the brains of people with ADHD. Some studies involving brain scans have suggested that certain areas of the brain may be a different size in people with ADHD. Other studies have suggested that people with ADHD may have a chemical imbalance in the brain.

Groups at Risk

Certain people are also believed to be more at risk of ADHD, including people:

  • Who were born prematurely or with a low birthweight
  • With epilepsy
  • With brain damage - which happened either in the womb or after a severe head injury

Research: Ongoing research continues to explore the complex causes and mechanisms behind ADHD.

Diagnosing ADHD

Speak to your GP if you think that you or your child may have ADHD.

For Children

If you're worried about your child, it may help to speak to their teachers before seeing your GP. This means you can find out if they have any concerns about your child's behaviour.

Diagnosis Process

Your GP can't formally diagnose ADHD. They can discuss your concerns with you and refer you for a specialist assessment, if necessary. Diagnosis typically involves comprehensive evaluation by specialists such as paediatricians or psychiatrists.

Important: Early diagnosis and intervention can help manage ADHD symptoms effectively and improve quality of life.

Treating ADHD

Treatment for ADHD can help relieve the symptoms. ADHD can be treated using medication or therapy. A combination of both is often best. Treatment is usually arranged by a specialist.

Medication

Medication might help you or your child concentrate better, be less impulsive and feel calmer. Various types of medication are available, and your specialist will determine the most appropriate option based on individual needs.

Therapy

You might be offered various forms of therapy including:

  • Psychoeducation
  • Behaviour therapy
  • Parent training
  • Education programmes
  • Cognitive behavioural therapy
  • Social skills training

Other Approaches

There are other treatments that involve altering your diet or taking supplements, though these should be discussed with healthcare professionals.

Management: With proper treatment and support, people with ADHD can learn to manage their symptoms effectively and lead successful, fulfilling lives.
