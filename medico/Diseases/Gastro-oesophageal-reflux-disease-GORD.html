About Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD)

Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) is a common condition, where acid from the stomach leaks up into the oesophagus (gullet).

It usually occurs as a result of the ring of muscle at the bottom of the oesophagus becoming weakened.

GORD causes symptoms such as heartburn and an unpleasant taste in the back of the mouth. It may just be an occasional nuisance for some people, but for others it can be a severe, lifelong problem.

GORD can often be controlled with self-help measures and medication. Occasionally, surgery to correct the problem may be needed.

What to do if you have GORD

You can often control the symptoms of GORD by making some lifestyle changes and taking over-the-counter medication.

You don't necessarily need to see your GP if you only have symptoms occasionally. Ask your pharmacist for advice on treatments.

When to see your GP

Visit your GP if you're worried about your symptoms, or if:

  • you have symptoms several times a week
  • over-the-counter medications aren't helping
  • your symptoms are severe
  • you have difficulty swallowing
  • you have possible signs of a more serious problem

Note: This topic focuses on GORD in adults.

Symptoms of GORD

The main symptoms of gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) are heartburn and acid reflux.

Heartburn

Heartburn is an uncomfortable burning sensation in the chest. It's usually felt just below your breastbone, but can spread up to the throat in some people.

Acid reflux

Acid reflux is where acid and other stomach contents are brought back up (regurgitated) into your throat and mouth. It usually causes an unpleasant, sour taste at the back of your mouth.

Other symptoms

If you have GORD, you may also experience:

  • a sore, inflamed oesophagus (oesophagitis)
  • bad breath
  • bloating and belching
  • feeling or being sick
  • difficulty swallowing
  • pain when swallowing
  • a sore throat and hoarseness
  • a persistent cough or wheezing
  • tooth decay and gum disease

Note: If you also have asthma, the symptoms may get worse as a result of stomach acid irritating your airways.

Causes of GORD

Gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) is usually caused by the ring of muscle at the bottom of the oesophagus (gullet) becoming weakened.

Normally, this ring of muscle opens to let food into your stomach and closes to stop stomach acid leaking back up into your oesophagus. But for people with GORD, stomach acid is able to pass back up into the oesophagus.

Who's most at risk of GORD?

The following factors may increase your risk of developing GORD:

  • Being overweight or obese - this can place increased pressure on your stomach
  • Eating large amounts of fatty foods - the stomach takes longer to get rid of stomach acid
  • Smoking, alcohol, coffee or chocolate - these may relax the muscles at the bottom of the oesophagus
  • Pregnancy - temporary changes in hormone levels and increased pressure
  • Hiatus hernia - when part of your stomach pushes up through your diaphragm
  • Gastroparesis - when the stomach takes longer to get rid of stomach acid
  • Certain medicines - some can cause GORD or make symptoms worse
  • Stress

Genetic factors: GORD can sometimes affect several members of the same family, suggesting inherited genes may play a role.

Diagnosing GORD

Your GP will often be able to diagnose gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) based on your symptoms. They may prescribe medication to treat it without needing to carry out any tests.

When tests may be needed

You'll usually only need to be referred for tests in hospital if:

  • your GP is unsure whether you have GORD
  • your symptoms are persistent, severe or unusual
  • prescription medications aren't controlling your symptoms
  • your GP thinks you might benefit from surgery
  • you have signs of a potentially more severe condition

Tests for GORD

Tests you may have include:

  • Endoscopy - examines the inside of your oesophagus
  • Barium swallow or barium meal - assesses swallowing ability
  • Manometry - measures pressure in your oesophagus
  • 24-hour pH monitoring - measures acidity level
  • Blood tests - to check for anaemia

Procedure note: Endoscopy is usually carried out while you're awake, but you may be given a sedative to help you relax.

Treating GORD

Heartburn and gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) can often be treated with self-help measures and over-the-counter medicines. If these don't help, your GP can prescribe stronger medication or refer you to a specialist.

Self-help measures

You may find the following measures can help reduce heartburn and other symptoms of GORD:

  • Eat smaller, more frequent meals
  • Avoid trigger foods and drinks
  • Don't wear tight clothing
  • Raise the head of your bed
  • Try to relax and manage stress
  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Stop smoking

Medication

A number of different medications can be used to treat symptoms of GORD:

  • Over-the-counter medicines: Antacids, alginates, low-dose PPIs and H2-receptor antagonists
  • Proton-pump inhibitors (PPIs): Reduce acid production
  • H2-receptor antagonists (H2RAs): Alternative acid reducers

Surgery and procedures

Surgery may be an option if medications aren't helping or you don't want long-term medication. The main procedure is laparoscopic Nissen fundoplication (LNF).

Important: Always consult with your doctor before making changes to your medication or treatment plan.

Complications of GORD

A number of possible complications can occur as a result of having gastro-oesophageal reflux disease (GORD) for a long time.

Oesophageal ulcers

The stomach acid that leaks into the oesophagus can damage the lining, which can cause ulcers to form. These ulcers can bleed, causing pain and making it difficult to swallow.

Scarred and narrow oesophagus

Repeated damage to the oesophagus can cause it to become scarred and narrowed (oesophageal stricture), making swallowing difficult and painful.

Barrett's oesophagus

About 1 in 10 people with GORD develop Barrett's oesophagus, where cells in the lining change. While it doesn't usually cause noticeable symptoms, there's a small risk these cells could become cancerous.

Oesophageal cancer

It's estimated that 1 in every 10 to 20 people with Barrett's oesophagus will develop oesophageal cancer within 10 to 20 years.

Symptoms of oesophageal cancer include:

  • Difficulty swallowing
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Persistent indigestion
  • Hoarseness
  • Persistent cough and/or coughing blood
  • Vomiting

Medical attention needed: Speak to your doctor if you experience any swallowing difficulties, or any other unusual or persistent symptoms.
