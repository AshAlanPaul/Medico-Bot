About Obesity

The term 'obese' describes a person who's very overweight, with a lot of body fat. It's a common problem in the UK that's estimated to affect around one in every four adults and around one in every five children aged 10 to 11.

Defining Obesity

The most widely used method for classifying weight is body mass index (BMI). For most adults:

  • 18.5 to 24.9 - healthy weight
  • 25 to 29.9 - overweight
  • 30 to 39.9 - obese
  • 40 or above - severely obese

Risks of Obesity

Obesity can lead to serious and potentially life-threatening conditions, including:

  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Coronary heart disease
  • Certain types of cancer
  • Stroke
  • Psychological problems like depression

Important: Obesity reduces life expectancy by an average of 3 to 10 years, depending on severity. It's estimated that obesity contributes to at least 1 in every 13 deaths in Europe.

Causes of Obesity

Obesity is generally caused by consuming more calories than you burn off through physical activity. The excess energy is stored by the body as fat.

Poor Diet

Obesity develops gradually over time as a result of poor diet and lifestyle choices:

  • Eating large amounts of processed or fast food high in fat and sugar
  • Drinking too much alcohol
  • Eating larger portions than needed
  • Drinking too many sugary drinks
  • Comfort eating

Lack of Physical Activity

Many people have sedentary lifestyles with jobs that involve sitting most of the day and limited physical activity during leisure time.

Other Factors

  • Genetics: While rare genetic conditions can cause obesity, most cases are due to environmental factors
  • Medical conditions: Such as underactive thyroid or Cushing's syndrome
  • Medications: Some drugs can contribute to weight gain

Note: While genetics may play a role in appetite and metabolism, environmental factors like poor eating habits learned during childhood are often more significant contributors to obesity.

Diagnosing Obesity

Body mass index (BMI) is widely used as a simple way to determine whether a person is a healthy weight for their height.

Visiting Your GP

If you're overweight or obese, visit your GP for advice about losing weight safely. Your GP may ask about:

  • Your lifestyle - diet, physical activity, smoking, and alcohol consumption
  • Possible underlying causes for weight gain
  • How you feel about being overweight
  • Your motivation to lose weight
  • Family history of obesity and related conditions

Additional Measurements

Your GP may also measure:

  • Blood pressure
  • Glucose and cholesterol levels
  • Waist circumference

Waist Measurement Guide: Generally, men with a waist circumference of 94cm (37in) or more and women with 80cm (about 31.5in) or more are more likely to develop obesity-related health problems.

Treating Obesity

The best way to treat obesity is through a combination of healthy eating and regular physical activity.

Diet

To lose weight at a safe rate of 0.5 to 1kg (1lb to 2lbs) a week, most people need to reduce their energy intake by 600 calories a day.

A healthy diet should include:

  • Plenty of fruit and vegetables
  • Wholegrain starchy foods
  • Some milk and dairy foods
  • Some protein sources
  • Limited high-fat and high-sugar foods

Exercise

Adults should aim for at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity activity per week, such as:

  • Brisk walking
  • Cycling
  • Swimming
  • Dancing

Other Strategies

  • Set realistic weight loss goals
  • Eat mindfully and avoid distractions
  • Involve family and friends for support
  • Monitor progress regularly

Medical Treatments

In some cases, medication like orlistat or weight loss surgery may be recommended, but these are usually considered only when lifestyle changes haven't been successful.

Remember: There's no "quick fix" for obesity. Weight loss programmes take time and commitment, but even losing 3% of your original body weight can significantly reduce health risks.

Important: Avoid fad diets that recommend unsafe practices like fasting or cutting out entire food groups. These don't teach sustainable healthy eating habits.
