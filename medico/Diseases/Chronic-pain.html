What is Chronic Pain?

Chronic pain is a common condition affecting over one third of adults in the UK.

Chronic pain is pain that continues for longer than 3 months, either as part of another health condition, or despite investigations and treatment.

Phone your GP if:

  • You experience pain for longer than 12 weeks
  • You think you might have chronic pain
  • You're concerned about worsening pain
  • You already have chronic pain and you've developed new symptoms

How Your Body Feels Pain

The brain and nerves make up the central nervous system. Nerves carry messages between our body and brain constantly.

Chronic pain can cause changes in your brain and nervous system. These changes can cause the brain to continue sending pain signals even when there's no harm or damage.

Acute Pain vs Chronic Pain

Acute Pain: Usually explained by injury/illness, reduces with healing, short-term disruption

Chronic Pain: Not always explained, not always helped by medicine, causes lasting changes to life

Managing Chronic Pain

Chronic pain is a long-term condition that may not be cured. Traditional treatments like pain medication only have limited benefit for some people.

Finding the right strategies and techniques for you is the best way to self-manage your pain.

Living with Chronic Pain

Finding the best strategies and techniques for you is the best way to manage your chronic pain.

Managing Your Activities

Many people with chronic pain avoid being active because they're worried it will make their pain worse. However, being underactive can make pain worse in the long term.

Pacing

Pacing helps you manage daily activities in a way that's right for your body, reducing the risk of flare-ups.

Activity and Exercise

Finding suitable activities and exercises can help manage symptoms and improve overall health.

  • Walking - start with short walks and gradually increase
  • Dancing or moving to music
  • Exercising in water
  • Exercise classes

Medication

Medication can be part of a pain management plan alongside other strategies.

Opioids: Useful for short-term pain but typically reduce pain for only about 10% of people long-term.

Anti-depressants: Some can be useful for pain whether there's low mood or not.

Chronic Pain and Mental Health

Pain doesn't just affect our bodies - it can also affect your mental health. The close links between emotion and pain centers make it almost impossible to have pain without difficult emotions.

Coping with Flare-ups of Chronic Pain

A flare-up is a period where pain increases and becomes more difficult to cope with. Flare-ups are a normal part of chronic pain and not necessarily a sign things are getting worse.

What Causes Flare-ups?

  • Overactivity or underactivity
  • Stress or worry
  • Low mood and depression
  • Sometimes no obvious cause

Managing Flare-ups

Be prepared: Have an action plan for when pain flares up

Pace yourself: Continue using pacing strategies during flare-ups

Manage stress: Learning relaxation skills can help break the pain-stress cycle

Sleep Management

Chronic pain can cause sleep problems, which can increase stress levels and make pain worse.

Fibromyalgia

Fibromyalgia is a long-term condition that causes pain all over the body.

Symptoms

The main symptom is widespread pain that might feel like aching, burning, or sharp stabbing pain.

  • Increased sensitivity to touch, light, temperature, noise
  • Fatigue and muscle stiffness
  • Difficulty sleeping
  • Problems with memory and concentration ("fibro-fog")
  • Headaches and irritable bowel syndrome

Speak to your GP if:

  • You think you might have fibromyalgia

Causes

The exact cause is unknown, but likely involves multiple factors including altered pain messages in the central nervous system and genetics.

Treatment

There's no cure for fibromyalgia, but treatments can help relieve symptoms and make the condition easier to live with.

Exercise and movement: Physiotherapy can help develop tailored exercise programs

Talking therapies: CBT and ACT can help manage the stress of living with fibromyalgia

Medication: Antidepressants can help relieve pain and treat sleep problems

Important: Traditional pain medications aren't always helpful for fibromyalgia. Learning self-management approaches is often more effective.
