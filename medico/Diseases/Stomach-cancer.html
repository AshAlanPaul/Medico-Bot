About Stomach Cancer

Stomach cancer, or gastric cancer, is a fairly uncommon type of cancer that develops in the lining of the stomach.

The initial symptoms of stomach cancer are often vague and easy to mistake for other less serious conditions, which means it's often diagnosed at an advanced stage.

Types of Stomach Cancer

  • Adenocarcinoma: Most common type (over 95% of cases)
  • Lymphoma: Develops in lymphatic tissue
  • Gastrointestinal stromal tumors (GISTs): Develop in muscle or connective tissue

Important: It's crucial to get any possible symptoms of stomach cancer checked by your GP as soon as possible, as early diagnosis improves treatment outcomes.

Symptoms of Stomach Cancer

Early symptoms of stomach cancer can be similar to common digestive issues, making them easy to overlook.

Early Symptoms

  • Persistent indigestion and heartburn
  • Trapped wind and frequent burping
  • Feeling very full or bloated after meals
  • Persistent stomach pain
  • Feeling sick
  • Difficulty swallowing

Advanced Symptoms

  • Blood in stools or black stools
  • Loss of appetite
  • Weight loss
  • Tiredness
  • Swelling in stomach
  • Anaemia
  • Yellowing of skin and eyes (jaundice)

When to Seek Medical Help

See your GP if you have indigestion along with weight loss, anaemia, or persistent vomiting. Also seek medical advice if you have difficulty swallowing.

Causes and Risk Factors

While the exact cause of stomach cancer isn't clear, several factors can increase your risk.

Main Risk Factors

  • Age: Most common in people over 55
  • Gender: Men are twice as likely to develop it
  • Smoking: Doubles the risk
  • H. pylori infection: Bacterial infection in the stomach
  • Diet: High in salted, pickled, or processed foods
  • Family history: Close relatives with the condition
  • Previous stomach surgery

Dietary Factors

  • High consumption of pickled vegetables and salted fish
  • Diets low in fiber and high in processed foods
  • High intake of smoked meats

Prevention: A high-fiber diet with 5 portions of fruit and vegetables daily can help protect against stomach cancer.

Diagnosing Stomach Cancer

Early diagnosis is crucial for successful treatment of stomach cancer.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Endoscopy: Examination of stomach with a camera
  • Biopsy: Tissue sample for laboratory testing
  • Blood tests: Check overall health and detect anaemia
  • CT or PET scans: Imaging to assess cancer spread
  • Laparoscopy: Minimally invasive surgical examination

Staging and Grading

After diagnosis, cancer is staged (how far it has spread) and graded (how quickly it may grow):

  • Stage 1-4: Higher numbers indicate more advanced cancer
  • Low to high grade: Indicates growth speed

Note: Most stomach cancers are diagnosed at stage 3 or 4, which makes cure more difficult.

Treating Stomach Cancer

Treatment depends on the cancer's stage, location, and your overall health.

Main Treatment Options

  • Surgery: Removal of part or all of the stomach
  • Chemotherapy: Medicine to kill cancer cells
  • Radiotherapy: Radiation to destroy cancer cells
  • Targeted therapy: Medications like trastuzumab

Surgical Procedures

  • Partial gastrectomy: Removal of part of stomach
  • Total gastrectomy: Removal of entire stomach
  • Endoscopic surgery: Minimally invasive removal

Success rates: A cure is possible in 20-30% of stomach cancer cases when detected early enough for complete tumor removal.

Living With Stomach Cancer

Coping with stomach cancer involves both physical and emotional challenges.

Recovery and Support

  • Take time to recover after treatment
  • Attend regular follow-up appointments
  • Seek support from family, friends, and support groups
  • Communicate openly with your healthcare team

Diet After Surgery

  • Eat smaller, more frequent meals
  • Adjust to new stomach capacity gradually
  • Focus on nutritious, easy-to-digest foods
  • Regular vitamin B12 injections may be needed

Support available: Cancer charities like Macmillan Cancer Support provide valuable information and support for people living with stomach cancer and their families.
