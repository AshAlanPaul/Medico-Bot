About Loss of Libido

Loss of libido (sex drive) is a common problem affecting up to 1 in 5 men – and even more women – at some point in their life.

It's often linked to professional and personal stress, or important life-changing events such as pregnancy, childbirth or breastfeeding.

However, an unexpected loss of libido – especially when it lasts for a long time or keeps returning – can also indicate an underlying personal, medical or lifestyle problem, which can be upsetting to both partners in a relationship.

When to seek help: If you're concerned about your libido, especially if your diminished sex drive distresses you or affects your relationship, make an appointment to see your GP to discuss any underlying causes and possible medical or psychological treatments.

Relationship Problems

Relationship problems are among the most common causes of loss of libido. Consider whether you're happy in your relationship and if you have any doubts or worries that may be the real reason for your loss of sexual desire.

Common Relationship Issues

  • Overfamiliarity – In long-term relationships, you may become overfamiliar with your partner and feel erotic dissatisfaction
  • Performance issues – Problems like erectile dysfunction, ejaculation issues, painful sex, or vaginismus can make sex difficult or unfulfilling
  • Emotional distance – Lack of emotional connection can significantly impact sexual desire

Support available: Your GP may recommend psychosexual counselling – a form of relationship therapy where you and your partner can discuss any sexual or emotional issues that may be contributing to your loss of libido.

Stress, Anxiety and Exhaustion

Stress, anxiety and exhaustion can be all-consuming and have a major impact on your happiness and sex drive.

Depression

Depression is very different from simply feeling unhappy. It's a serious illness where you may have feelings of extreme sadness that can last for a long time and interfere with your daily life, including your sex life.

Important: Low sex drive can also be a side effect of many antidepressants. Speak to your GP if you're already taking antidepressants and think they may be causing your problems, as you may be able to switch to a different medication.

Drugs and Alcohol

Drinking excess amounts of alcohol can reduce your sex drive. Drug misuse is also linked to a loss of sex drive.

Medical Causes

Age-Related Changes

Many people lose some interest in sex as they get older, mainly as a result of falling levels of sex hormones, age-related health problems, or the side effects of medication.

  • Men – Can develop low testosterone levels, causing fatigue, depression and reduced sex drive
  • Women – Approaching menopause, levels of oestrogen begin to fall, which can affect libido

Hormonal Problems

  • Underactive thyroid – Can cause tiredness, weight gain, depression and reduced libido
  • Hyperprolactinaemia – Raised levels of prolactin in the blood can negatively affect sex drive

Chronic Medical Conditions

Long-term conditions such as cardiovascular disease, diabetes and obesity can also have a negative effect on your libido.

Medication and Contraception

Contraception

Some women have reported a decreased sex drive while using some types of hormonal contraception:

  • Combined contraception (pill, vaginal ring, patch)
  • Progestogen-only pill
  • Contraceptive implant
  • Depo-Provera injection

Medications That Can Affect Libido

  • Medication for high blood pressure, including diuretics
  • Antidepressants, particularly SSRIs
  • Medications for seizures (fits)
  • Medications for psychosis
  • Medicines that block testosterone effects or production

Medical advice: See your GP if you're worried that medication you're taking is responsible for your reduced sex drive. They can review your medication and switch your prescription to something less likely to affect your libido if necessary.

Remember: Loss of libido is a common issue and help is available. Don't hesitate to speak with healthcare professionals who can provide appropriate support and treatment options.
