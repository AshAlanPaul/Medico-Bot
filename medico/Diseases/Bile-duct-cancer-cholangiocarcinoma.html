Bile Duct Cancer Information

About Bile Duct Cancer Symptoms Causes Diagnosis Treatment Prevention

About Bile Duct Cancer

Bile duct cancer (cholangiocarcinoma) is a rare but aggressive type of cancer.

The bile duct system, or 'biliary' system, is made up of a series of tubes that begin in the liver and end in the small intestine. Bile is a fluid the digestive system uses to help break down fats and digest foods.

Types of Bile Duct Cancer

There are 2 main types of bile duct cancer, depending on where the cancer begins:

  • Intrahepatic bile duct cancer - starts in a part of the bile duct inside the liver
  • Extrahepatic bile duct cancer - starts in part of the bile duct outside the liver

Who is affected?

Most cases of bile duct cancer occur in people over the age of 65. The condition affects men and women almost equally.

Important: Bile duct cancer doesn't usually cause any symptoms until the flow of bile from the liver is blocked. In most cases, the condition is at an advanced stage by this time.

Symptoms of Bile Duct Cancer

Bile duct cancer doesn't usually cause any symptoms until the flow of bile from the liver is blocked. In most cases, the condition is at an advanced stage by this time.

The blockage will cause bile to move back into the blood and body tissue, resulting in symptoms such as:

  • Jaundice - yellowing of the skin and whites of the eyes, itchy skin, pale stools and dark-coloured urine
  • Unintentional weight loss
  • Abdominal pain - most people feel a dull ache in the upper right-hand side of their abdomen (stomach)
  • High temperature (fever) of 38C (100.4F) or above and shivering
  • Loss of appetite

When to seek medical advice: Always visit your GP if you have jaundice. While jaundice is unlikely to be caused by bile duct cancer, it could indicate an underlying problem with the liver, such as hepatitis.

Causes of Bile Duct Cancer

The exact cause of bile duct cancer is unknown, although some things can increase the risk of developing the condition.

Cancer begins with a change (mutation) in the structure of the DNA in cells, which can affect how they grow. This means that cells grow and reproduce uncontrollably, producing a lump of tissue called a tumour.

Risk Factors

  • Age - Your chances of developing cancer of the bile duct increase as you get older
  • Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC) - a rare type of liver disease
  • Bile duct abnormalities - such as choledochal cysts and Caroli's disease
  • Biliary stones - similar to gallstones, but form inside the liver
  • Parasitic infection - liver flukes from eating undercooked fish
  • Exposure to toxins - such as asbestos and polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs)
  • Other factors - hepatitis B and C, cirrhosis, HIV, diabetes, obesity, and smoking

Note: Around 10 to 20% of people with PSC will develop bile duct cancer. Your risk of developing bile duct cancer is thought to be higher if you have PSC and you smoke.

Diagnosing Bile Duct Cancer

Bile duct cancer can be a challenging condition to diagnose. You usually need several different tests before an accurate diagnosis can be made.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Blood tests - to detect tumor markers
  • Ultrasound scans - high-frequency sound waves to build up a picture of the inside of your body
  • CT scans - series of X-rays assembled into a detailed three-dimensional image
  • MRI scans - uses strong magnetic field and radio waves to produce a picture
  • Endoscopic retrograde cholangio-pancreatography (ERCP) - allows bile ducts to show up more clearly on X-ray
  • Percutaneous transhepatic cholangiography (PTC) - detailed X-ray image of bile duct
  • Biopsy - taking a small sample of tissue to check for cancerous cells

Staging

A widely used method of staging bile duct cancer is a number staging system:

  • Stage 1A - the cancer is contained inside the bile duct
  • Stage 1B - the cancer is beginning to spread beyond the walls of the bile duct
  • Stage 2 - the cancer has spread into nearby tissue, such as the liver
  • Stage 3 - the cancer has spread into the lymph nodes and major blood vessels
  • Stage 4 - the cancer has spread into distant organs, such as the lungs

Treating Bile Duct Cancer

Most cases of bile duct cancer cannot be cured. Instead, treatment is most commonly used to relieve symptoms.

Treatment Options

  • Surgery - to remove cancerous tissue, possibly including part of the bile duct, gallbladder, nearby lymph nodes, or a large part of the liver
  • Stenting - to unblock the bile duct and relieve symptoms
  • Radiotherapy - to relieve symptoms, slow the spread of cancer, and prolong life
  • Chemotherapy - to relieve symptoms, slow down the rate cancer spreads, and prolong life
  • Clinical trials - experimental treatments and targeted therapies

Important: In cases of stage 1 and stage 2 bile duct cancer, a cure may be possible by surgically removing the affected part. However, only a small proportion of bile duct cancer cases are diagnosed early enough to be suitable for surgery.

Cancer Treatment Team

Due to the rarity of bile duct cancer, you are likely to be referred to a specialist hepatobiliary unit with experience in treating the condition. Your multidisciplinary team may include:

  • Liver surgeon
  • Medical or clinical oncologist
  • Pathologist
  • Radiologist
  • Cancer nurse
  • Gastroenterologist

Preventing Bile Duct Cancer

There are no guaranteed ways to avoid getting bile duct cancer, although it is possible to reduce your chances of developing the condition.

Prevention Strategies

  • Stop smoking - particularly important if you have primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC)
  • Drink alcohol in moderation - to prevent liver damage (cirrhosis)
  • Minimize exposure to hepatitis B and C - through vaccination and safe practices
  • Avoid liver fluke infection - ensure fish is thoroughly cooked when traveling in high-risk areas

Note: The 3 most effective steps to reduce your chances of developing bile duct cancer are: giving up smoking (if you smoke), drinking alcohol in moderation, and minimizing your exposure to the hepatitis B and C viruses.

Hepatitis Prevention

A vaccine is available that provides immunisation against hepatitis B. Vaccination would usually only be recommended for people in high-risk groups.

To avoid getting hepatitis C, never share any drug-injecting equipment with others, and avoid sharing any object that could be contaminated with blood.
