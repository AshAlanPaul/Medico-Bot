About Acne

Acne is a common skin condition that affects most people at some point. It causes spots, oily skin and sometimes skin that's hot or painful to touch.

Acne most commonly develops on the:

  • Face – this affects almost everyone with acne
  • Back – this affects more than half of people with acne
  • Chest – this affects about 15% of people with acne

Types of Spots

There are 6 main types of spot caused by acne:

  • Blackheads – small black or yellowish bumps that develop on the skin
  • Whiteheads – have a similar appearance to blackheads, but may be firmer
  • Papules – small red bumps that may feel tender or sore
  • Pustules – similar to papules, but have a white tip in the centre
  • Nodules – large hard lumps that build up beneath the surface of the skin
  • Cysts – the most severe type of spot; large pus-filled lumps

Self-care tips: Don't wash affected areas more than twice daily, use mild cleansers, avoid squeezing spots, and choose non-comedogenic cosmetics.

Causes of Acne

Acne is caused when tiny holes in the skin, known as hair follicles, become blocked.

Sebaceous glands are tiny glands found near the surface of your skin. The glands are attached to hair follicles, which are small holes in your skin that an individual hair grows out of.

Testosterone

Teenage acne is thought to be triggered by increased levels of a hormone called testosterone, which occurs during puberty.

Acne in Families

Acne can run in families. If your parents had acne, it's likely that you'll also develop it.

Acne in Women

More than 80% of cases of adult acne occur in women. It's thought that many cases are caused by changes in hormone levels at certain times:

  • Periods – some women have a flare-up of acne just before their period
  • Pregnancy – many women have symptoms of acne during the first 3 months
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome – a common condition that can cause acne

Myth busting: Acne is not caused by poor diet, dirty skin, or sexual activity. These are common misconceptions.

Diagnosing Acne

Your pharmacist can diagnose acne by looking at your skin. This involves examining your face and possibly your chest and back for different types of spot.

How many spots you have and how painful and inflamed they are will help determine how severe your acne is.

Severity Grades

  • Grade 1 (mild) – mostly whiteheads and blackheads
  • Grade 2 (moderate) – multiple papules and pustules on face
  • Grade 3 (moderately severe) – numerous inflamed spots, back and chest affected
  • Grade 4 (severe) – large, painful pustules and nodules

Treating Acne

Treatment for acne depends on how severe it is. It can take several months of treatment before acne symptoms improve.

Treatments from your GP

See your GP if your acne is more widespread or over-the-counter medication hasn't worked.

Prescription medications include:

  • Topical retinoids
  • Topical antibiotics
  • Azelaic acid
  • Antibiotic tablets
  • Combined oral contraceptive pill (for women)
  • Isotretinoin tablets

Important: Isotretinoin can cause birth defects and should not be used during pregnancy. Always consult with a healthcare professional before starting any treatment.

Complications of Acne

Acne scarring can sometimes develop as a complication of acne. Any type of acne spot can lead to scarring, but it's more common when the most serious types of spots burst and damage nearby skin.

Types of Acne Scars

  • Ice pick scars – small, deep holes in the skin
  • Rolling scars – bands of scar tissue under the skin
  • Boxcar scars – round or oval depressions in the skin

Treating Acne Scarring

Treatments for acne scarring are regarded as a type of cosmetic surgery. Options include:

  • Dermabrasion
  • Laser treatment
  • Punch techniques
  • Subcision

Note: While treatment can certainly improve the appearance of your scars, it can't get rid of them completely. Most people notice a 50-75% improvement.
