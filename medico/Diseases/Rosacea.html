Rosacea

Rosacea is a common but poorly understood long-term skin condition that mainly affects the face.

It can be controlled to some degree with long-term treatment, but sometimes the changes in physical appearance can have a significant psychological impact.

Important: Rosacea is a relapsing condition, which means there are periods when symptoms are particularly bad, but less severe at others.

When to See Your GP

See your GP if you have persistent symptoms that could be caused by rosacea. Early diagnosis and treatment can help stop the condition getting worse.

There's no specific test for rosacea, but your GP will often be able to diagnose the condition by examining your skin, asking about your symptoms, and asking about possible triggers you may have.

Symptoms of Rosacea

Rosacea causes a range of symptoms, although not everyone will experience them all. Most people with rosacea have periods when their symptoms are particularly troublesome, followed by periods when their symptoms are less so.

Main Symptoms Include:

  • Flushing - skin turns red for short periods
  • Persistent facial redness - like a blush that doesn't go away
  • Visible blood vessels - small blood vessels become permanently visible
  • Papules and pustules - red bumps and pus-filled swellings
  • Thickened skin - usually around the nose (rhinophyma)

Other Associated Symptoms:

  • Sensitive skin - burning, itching, stinging and pain
  • Dry, rough skin
  • Raised red patches (plaques) on your skin
  • Facial swelling (lymphoedema)

Note: Permanent damage to the face (scarring) almost never occurs in rosacea.

Causes & Triggers of Rosacea

The exact cause of rosacea is unknown, although a number of potential factors have been suggested.

Possible Contributing Factors:

  • Blood vessel abnormalities - may explain flushing and persistent redness
  • Skin peptides - activated by external triggers
  • Microscopic mites - demodex folliculorum mites
  • Helicobacter pylori bacteria - bacteria found in digestive system
  • Genetics - appears to run in families

Common Triggers:

  • Exposure to sunlight
  • Stress
  • Hot or cold weather
  • Strenuous exercise
  • Alcohol and caffeine
  • Hot drinks
  • Spicy foods
  • Certain medications

Tip: Keeping a diary can help identify your personal triggers and manage your symptoms more effectively.

Treating Rosacea

Although rosacea can't be cured, treatment can help keep the symptoms under control. Long-term treatment is usually necessary.

Treatment Approaches:

Topical Treatments +
  • Metronidazole cream or gel
  • Azelaic acid cream or gel
  • Ivermectin cream
  • Brimonidine tartrate gel (for redness)
Oral Treatments +
  • Antibiotics - tetracycline, doxycycline, erythromycin
  • Isotretinoin (for severe cases)
  • Other medications for specific symptoms
Procedures +
  • Laser treatment
  • Intense pulsed light (IPL) treatment
  • Surgical treatments for rhinophyma

Important: Some treatments require specialist supervision and may have side effects. Always follow your doctor's advice.

Prevention & Self-care

Managing rosacea involves both medical treatment and lifestyle adjustments to minimize symptoms.

Self-help Measures:

  • Avoid known triggers
  • Use gentle skincare products
  • Apply sun protection daily
  • Manage stress through relaxation techniques
  • Keep a symptom diary
  • Use camouflage makeup if needed

Skincare Tips:

  • Clean skin twice daily with gentle cleansers
  • Use lukewarm water, not hot
  • Choose fragrance-free, hypoallergenic products
  • Moisturize regularly
  • Avoid harsh toners and astringents

Support: Many people find support through organizations like Changing Faces, which offers skin camouflage services and emotional support.

Ocular Rosacea

Rosacea that affects your eyes (ocular rosacea) can lead to a number of eye problems, some of which can be serious.

Symptoms of Ocular Rosacea:

  • Feeling like there is something in your eyes
  • Dry eyes
  • Irritated and bloodshot eyes
  • Inflammation of the eyelids (blepharitis)

Serious Symptoms Requiring Immediate Attention:

  • Eye pain
  • Sensitivity to light (photophobia)
  • Deterioration in vision

Urgent: Contact your GP immediately if you experience serious eye symptoms. If this isn't possible, visit your nearest accident and emergency department.

If keratitis (cornea inflammation) isn't treated promptly by an ophthalmologist, there's a risk of permanent vision loss.
