Liver Tumours

Liver tumours can be non-cancerous (benign) or cancerous (malignant). Boys are affected more commonly than girls.

More children than ever are surviving childhood cancer. There are new and better drugs and treatments, and we can now also work to reduce the after-effects of having had cancer in the past.

The Liver

The liver is the largest organ in the body. It lies across the upper part of the abdomen and is larger on the right-hand side of the body than on the left.

The liver has many important functions including:

  • Producing proteins that help blood clot and prevent excessive bleeding
  • Breaking down harmful substances and waste products
  • Processing carbohydrates and fats for energy
  • Storing glucose and vitamins
  • Producing bile to help digest fats

The liver has an amazing ability to repair itself and can function normally even if only a small part of it is working.

Types of Liver Tumours

There are 2 main types of primary liver cancer in children:

Hepatoblastoma

This usually occurs in children under 5 years old.

Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)

This is rarer and usually occurs in older children.

Causes

The cause of most primary liver tumours in the western world is unknown. However, in other parts of the world, hepatocellular cancer (HCC) is commonly related to liver infection from hepatitis B.

Symptoms and Diagnosis

Signs and Symptoms

The most common symptoms include:

  • A lump or swelling in the abdomen, which can be painful
  • Weight loss
  • Loss of appetite
  • Feeling sick (nausea) and being sick (vomiting)

Diagnosis

Various tests are used to diagnose liver tumours:

  • Ultrasound scan and X-rays
  • CT (computerised tomography) scans
  • MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) scans
  • Blood tests to measure alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) levels
  • Biopsy to take a sample of cells from the tumour

PRETEXT Grouping System

This unique system assesses the extent of cancer in the liver:

  • PRETEXT 1 – 1 liver sector affected, removable with straightforward surgery
  • PRETEXT 2 – 2 adjoining sectors affected, removable with more extensive surgery
  • PRETEXT 3 – 2-3 sectors affected, removable with major surgery
  • PRETEXT 4 – all 4 sectors affected, requires liver transplant

Treatment Options

Hepatoblastoma Treatment

Treatment depends on PRETEXT staging and whether cancer has spread:

  • PRETEXT 1, 2 and 3 tumours are 'standard risk'
  • PRETEXT 4 is 'high risk'
  • Chemotherapy is given first to shrink the tumour
  • Surgery to remove remaining tumour follows chemotherapy
  • Further chemotherapy after surgery

Hepatocellular Carcinoma Treatment

Treatment approach differs:

  • Surgery is the main treatment
  • Chemotherapy may be less effective
  • Other treatments like chemoembolisation may be used
  • Targeted drugs that work differently from conventional chemotherapy

Surgery Options

All children who are able will have surgery following chemotherapy:

  • If lungs are affected, lung surgery comes first
  • Liver tumour removal follows 1-2 weeks later
  • PRETEXT 4 tumours require liver transplant

Side Effects of Treatment

Treatment can cause side effects including:

  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Hair loss
  • Increased risk of infection
  • Bruising and bleeding
  • Tiredness
  • Diarrhoea

Late Side Effects

Chemotherapy can cause long-term effects:

  • Hearing problems
  • Kidney problems
  • Heart problems
  • Slightly increased risk of developing another cancer later in life

Follow-up Care and Support

Follow-up Care

After treatment ends, regular monitoring includes:

  • Blood tests to measure AFP levels
  • Scans and chest X-rays
  • More than three quarters of children with hepatoblastoma are cured
  • Outlook for hepatocellular carcinoma is not quite as good

Recurrence

If cancer comes back after initial treatment:

  • AFP levels in blood will start to rise again
  • Can come back in the liver or other parts of the body

Clinical Trials

Many children have treatment as part of clinical research trials to improve understanding of best treatment approaches.

Emotional Support

As a parent, learning your child has cancer is devastating. You may experience many emotions including fear, guilt, sadness, anger and uncertainty. These are all normal reactions.

Your child may also have powerful emotions throughout their experience with cancer. There are many healthcare professionals and support organizations available to help you through this difficult time.
