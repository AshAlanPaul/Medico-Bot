Osteosarcoma

Osteosarcoma is a type of bone cancer that occurs more commonly in older children and teenagers, and is very rarely seen in children under 5.

More children than ever are surviving childhood cancer with new and better drugs and treatments. Understanding more about the cancer your child has and the treatments available can help parents cope during this difficult time.

What is Osteosarcoma?

Osteosarcoma often starts at the end of the long bones, where new bone tissue forms as a young person grows. Any bone in the body can be affected, but the most common sites are in the arms and legs, particularly around the knee and shoulder joints.

There are several different types of osteosarcoma, including:

  • Conventional osteosarcoma (most common)
  • Parosteal osteosarcoma
  • Periosteal osteosarcoma
  • Telangiectatic osteosarcoma
  • Small cell osteosarcoma

Causes of Osteosarcoma

Like most childhood cancers, a definite cause of osteosarcoma is unknown. However, several risk factors have been identified:

  • Hereditary retinoblastoma – Children with this rare eye tumor have increased risk
  • Previous cancer treatment – Children who had radiotherapy and chemotherapy
  • Genetic factors – Certain genetic conditions may increase susceptibility

Note: Osteosarcoma is not caused by injuries or damage to the bone, although an injury may draw attention to an existing bone tumor.

Signs and Symptoms

Early detection is crucial for successful treatment. Common symptoms include:

  • Bone pain – The most common symptom, initially may come and go, then becomes more severe and constant, especially at night
  • Swelling – Around the affected bone
  • Bone fractures – Sometimes discovered when a bone weakened by cancer breaks after minor trauma
  • Limping – If the tumor is in the leg or pelvis

Important: These symptoms can be caused by many things other than cancer. However, any persistent bone pain should be checked by a doctor. Symptoms are often initially attributed to sporting injuries.

Diagnosing Osteosarcoma

Diagnosis typically involves several steps:

Initial Assessment

  • Physical examination by GP
  • X-rays of the painful area
  • Referral to specialist hospital or bone tumor center

Further Testing

  • Biopsy – Small piece of tumor removed for microscopic examination
  • Blood tests – Check general health
  • Imaging scans – MRI, CT scan, bone scan to check for spread
  • Chest X-ray – Check if cancer has spread to lungs

Staging and Grading

Grading

Grading refers to how cancer cells look under the microscope:

  • Low-grade – Slow-growing, less likely to spread
  • High-grade – Fast-growing, more likely to spread (most osteosarcomas)

Staging System

  • Stage 1A – Low-grade, contained within bone
  • Stage 1B – Low-grade, spread outside bone
  • Stage 2A – High-grade, contained within bone
  • Stage 2B – High-grade, spread outside bone (most common)
  • Stage 3 – Cancer has spread to other parts of the body

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on tumor size, position, and stage, typically involving a combination of approaches:

Chemotherapy

Very important part of treatment, given both before and after surgery to:

  • Shrink the main tumor before surgery
  • Reduce risk of cancer returning after surgery
  • Typically uses combination of anti-cancer drugs

Surgery

Performed at specialist orthopedic centers:

  • Limb-sparing surgery – Removing part of affected bone and replacing with prosthesis or bone graft
  • Amputation – Required if cancer affects blood vessels and nerves

Radiotherapy

May be used occasionally to destroy cancer cells using high-energy rays.

New Treatments

Mifamurtide (Mepact®) is a biological therapy that may be given with chemotherapy after surgery to prevent recurrence.

Side Effects and Follow-up

Treatment Side Effects

  • Chemotherapy – Nausea, vomiting, hair loss, infection risk, fatigue, diarrhea
  • Radiotherapy – Skin irritation, tiredness
  • Surgery – Specific complications explained by surgeon

Long-term Effects

Some children may develop late side effects years later:

  • Reduced bone growth
  • Reduced fertility
  • Changes in heart function
  • Slight increased risk of another cancer

Follow-up Care

Regular check-ups are essential, including:

  • Regular clinic visits and X-rays
  • Possible additional surgeries for limb lengthening as child grows
  • Monitoring for any late effects of treatment

Hope and Support: Many children with osteosarcoma are cured. Support organizations and healthcare teams are available to help families through treatment and beyond.
