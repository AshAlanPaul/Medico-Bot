About Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma

Non-Hodgkin lymphoma is an uncommon cancer that develops in the lymphatic system, which is a network of vessels and glands spread throughout your body.

The lymphatic system is part of your immune system. Clear fluid called lymph flows through the lymphatic vessels and contains infection-fighting white blood cells known as lymphocytes.

In non-Hodgkin lymphoma, the affected lymphocytes start to multiply in an abnormal way and begin to collect in certain parts of the lymphatic system, such as the lymph nodes (glands). The affected lymphocytes lose their infection-fighting properties, making you more vulnerable to infection.

The most common symptom of non-Hodgkin lymphoma is a painless swelling in a lymph node, usually in the neck, armpit or groin.

Who's Affected?

Non-Hodgkin lymphoma can occur at any age, but your chances of developing the condition increase as you get older, with most cases diagnosed in people over 65. Slightly more men than women are affected.

Important: If you notice persistent swollen glands with no signs of infection, speak to your healthcare provider for proper evaluation.

Symptoms of Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma

The most common symptom of non-Hodgkin lymphoma is a painless swelling in a lymph node, usually in the neck, armpit or groin.

Lymph nodes, also known as lymph glands, are pea-sized lumps of tissue found throughout the body. They contain white blood cells that help to fight against infection.

Other Symptoms

Some people with non-Hodgkin lymphoma also have other more general symptoms. These can include:

  • Night sweats
  • Unintentional weight loss
  • A high temperature (fever)
  • A persistent cough or feeling of breathlessness
  • Persistent itching of the skin all over the body

When to seek medical advice: Speak to your GP if you have any of these symptoms, particularly if you have persistently swollen glands with no other signs of infection.

Causes of Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma

Non-Hodgkin lymphoma is caused by a change (mutation) in the DNA of a type of white blood cell called lymphocytes. The exact reason why this happens isn't known.

DNA gives cells a basic set of instructions, such as when to grow and reproduce. The mutation in the DNA changes these instructions, so the cells keep growing. This causes them to multiply uncontrollably.

Risk Factors

While the cause of the initial mutation is unknown, several factors can increase your risk:

  • Medical conditions that weaken your immune system (HIV, autoimmune diseases)
  • Immunosuppressant medication after organ transplant
  • Previous Epstein-Barr virus infection
  • Helicobacter pylori infection
  • Previous chemotherapy or radiotherapy
  • Family history (slightly increased risk)

Diagnosing Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma

The only way to confirm a diagnosis of non-Hodgkin lymphoma is by carrying out a biopsy.

This is a minor surgical procedure where a sample of affected lymph node tissue is removed and studied in a laboratory.

Further Testing

If a biopsy confirms diagnosis, further testing determines the stage:

  • Blood tests
  • Bone marrow sample
  • CT, MRI, or PET scans
  • Chest X-ray
  • Lumbar puncture

Staging

  • Stage 1 - Cancer limited to one group of lymph nodes
  • Stage 2 - Two or more lymph node groups affected on same side of diaphragm
  • Stage 3 - Lymph nodes affected on both sides of diaphragm
  • Stage 4 - Cancer has spread to other organs

Treating Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma

Treatment depends on the type and stage of lymphoma, as well as your overall health.

Main Treatment Options

  • Chemotherapy - Cancer-killing medications
  • Radiotherapy - Targeted radiation treatment
  • Monoclonal antibody therapy - Targeted biological treatment
  • Steroid medication - Often combined with chemotherapy
  • Watchful waiting - For slow-growing lymphomas

Treatment planning: Your multidisciplinary team will create a personalized treatment plan based on your specific type of lymphoma and overall health.

Side Effects

Common side effects of treatment may include:

  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Increased infection risk
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Hair loss
  • Fertility issues

Complications of Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma

Some people treated for non-Hodgkin lymphoma experience long-term problems, even after successful treatment.

Weakened Immune System

Having a weakened immune system is common during and after treatment, making you more vulnerable to infections.

Infertility

Chemotherapy and radiotherapy can cause temporary or permanent infertility. Discuss fertility preservation options with your care team before treatment.

Second Cancers

Treatment increases the risk of developing another type of cancer later in life. Regular follow-up care is essential.

Long-term monitoring: Regular follow-up appointments are crucial to monitor for potential complications and ensure early detection of any recurring issues.

Other Health Problems

Treatment may increase risk of:

  • Heart and lung disease
  • Kidney and thyroid problems
  • Diabetes
  • Cataracts
  • Depression
