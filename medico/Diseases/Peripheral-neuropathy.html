About Peripheral Neuropathy

Peripheral neuropathy develops when nerves in the body's extremities – such as the hands, feet and arms – are damaged. The symptoms depend on which nerves are affected.

In the UK, it's estimated that almost 1 in 10 people aged 55 or over are affected by some degree of peripheral neuropathy.

The peripheral nervous system

The peripheral nervous system is the network of nerves that lie outside the central nervous system (the brain and spinal cord).

It includes different types of nerves with their own specific functions:

  • Sensory nerves – responsible for transmitting sensations, such as pain and touch
  • Motor nerves – responsible for controlling muscles
  • Autonomic nerves – responsible for regulating automatic functions of the body, such as blood pressure and bladder function

Important: It's important to see your GP if you experience the early symptoms of peripheral neuropathy, such as pain, tingling or loss of sensation in the feet, loss of balance or weakness, or a cut or ulcer on your foot that isn't getting better.

Symptoms of Peripheral Neuropathy

Symptoms vary according to the type of peripheral neuropathy and may develop quickly or slowly.

Main types of peripheral neuropathy:

  • Sensory neuropathy – damage to nerves that carry sensations to the brain
  • Motor neuropathy – damage to nerves that control movement
  • Autonomic neuropathy – damage to nerves that control involuntary bodily processes
  • Mononeuropathy – damage to a single nerve

Sensory neuropathy symptoms:

  • Prickling and tingling sensation (pins and needles)
  • Numbness and reduced ability to feel pain or temperature changes
  • Burning or sharp pain, usually in the feet
  • Feeling pain from light touch (allodynia)
  • Loss of balance or coordination

Motor neuropathy symptoms:

  • Twitching and muscle cramps
  • Muscle weakness or paralysis
  • Thinning (wasting) of muscles
  • Foot drop – difficulty lifting up the front part of your foot

Autonomic neuropathy symptoms:

  • Constipation or diarrhea
  • Feeling sick, bloating and belching
  • Low blood pressure causing dizziness
  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Excessive sweating or lack of sweating
  • Sexual function problems
  • Bladder and bowel control issues

Causes of Peripheral Neuropathy

Diabetes is the most common cause of peripheral neuropathy in the UK. Neuropathy can also be caused by other health conditions and certain medications.

Diabetes

Peripheral neuropathy caused by diabetes is called diabetic polyneuropathy. It's probably caused by high levels of glucose in your blood damaging the tiny blood vessels that supply your nerves.

Risk factors for diabetic neuropathy include:

  • Poorly controlled blood sugar
  • Smoking
  • Regular consumption of large amounts of alcohol
  • Age over 40 years

Other causes include:

  • Excessive alcohol drinking for years
  • Low levels of vitamin B12 or other vitamins
  • Physical damage to the nerves
  • Underactive thyroid gland
  • Certain infections (shingles, Lyme disease, HIV)
  • Inflammation of blood vessels
  • Chronic liver or kidney disease
  • Certain types of cancer
  • Genetic conditions
  • High levels of toxins in the body
  • Autoimmune conditions

Medication side effects: Some medications may cause peripheral neuropathy as a side effect, including certain chemotherapy drugs, some antibiotics, and medications like phenytoin.

Diagnosing Peripheral Neuropathy

A number of tests may be used to diagnose peripheral neuropathy and its underlying cause.

Diagnostic tests include:

  • Physical examination – testing sensation, strength and reflexes
  • Blood tests – to check for diabetes or vitamin deficiencies
  • Nerve conduction test (NCS) – measures speed and strength of nerve signals
  • Electromyography (EMG) – measures electrical activity of muscles
  • Lumbar puncture – to test cerebrospinal fluid
  • Nerve biopsy – rarely needed, examines nerve tissue under microscope
  • Scans – X-ray, CT scan, or MRI to look for underlying causes

Early diagnosis: Generally, the sooner peripheral neuropathy is diagnosed, the better the chance of limiting the damage and preventing further complications.

Treating Peripheral Neuropathy

Treatment may include treating any underlying cause or symptoms you're experiencing.

Treating the underlying cause:

  • Better control of diabetes through lifestyle changes
  • Vitamin B12 deficiency treatment with injections or tablets
  • Stopping medications that may be causing neuropathy
  • Corticosteroids or immunosuppressants for inflammatory conditions

Relieving nerve pain:

Standard painkillers are often ineffective for neuropathic pain. Medications that may help include:

  • Amitriptyline
  • Duloxetine
  • Pregabalin and gabapentin
  • Capsaicin cream
  • Lidocaine plaster
  • Tramadol (for short-term use)

Other treatments:

  • Physiotherapy for muscle weakness
  • Splints or walking aids
  • Medication for specific symptoms like constipation or erectile dysfunction
  • Botulinum toxin injections for excessive sweating

Complications of Peripheral Neuropathy

Peripheral neuropathy can sometimes cause other medical problems, particularly if left untreated.

Diabetic foot ulcer:

An open wound or sore on the skin that's slow to heal, common in people with diabetic polyneuropathy. Numb feet make it easy to injure without noticing.

Gangrene:

If wounds become infected and aren't treated, they can lead to tissue death. In severe cases, amputation may be necessary.

Cardiovascular autonomic neuropathy (CAN):

Damage to peripheral nerves that control blood circulation and heartbeat, causing symptoms like:

  • Inability to exercise for more than short periods
  • Orthostatic hypotension (dizziness when standing up)
  • Risk of abnormal heart rhythms

Foot care is crucial: If you have diabetes, you should examine your feet regularly and get them checked by a podiatrist. Proper foot care can prevent serious complications.
