Dehydration Information

About Dehydration Symptoms Causes Treatment Prevention

About Dehydration

Dehydration occurs when your body loses more fluid than you take in.

When the normal water content of your body is reduced, it upsets the balance of minerals (salts and sugar) in your body, which affects the way it functions.

Water makes up over two-thirds of the healthy human body. It lubricates the joints and eyes, aids digestion, flushes out waste and toxins, and keeps the skin healthy.

Early warning signs include:

  • Feeling thirsty and lightheaded
  • A dry mouth
  • Tiredness
  • Having dark coloured, strong-smelling urine
  • Passing urine less often than usual

In babies: A baby may be dehydrated if they have a sunken soft spot on their head, few or no tears when they cry, fewer wet nappies, or appear drowsy.

Symptoms of Dehydration

Dehydration can be mild, moderate or severe, depending on how much of your body weight is lost through fluids.

Common symptoms include:

  • Dizziness or light-headedness
  • Headache
  • Tiredness
  • Dry mouth, lips and eyes
  • Passing small amounts of urine infrequently

Severe Dehydration - Seek Immediate Medical Help

  • Feeling unusually tired or confused
  • Dizziness when standing up that doesn't go away
  • Not passing urine for eight hours
  • Rapid or weak pulse
  • Fits (seizures)
  • Low level of consciousness

Dehydration in babies

  • Sunken soft spot (fontanelle) on their head
  • Few or no tears when they cry
  • Dry mouth
  • Fewer wet nappies
  • Dark yellow urine
  • Drowsiness
  • Fast breathing
  • Cold and blotchy-looking hands and feet

Causes of Dehydration

Dehydration is caused by not drinking enough fluid or by losing more fluid than you take in. Fluid is lost through sweat, tears, vomiting, urine or diarrhoea.

Common causes:

  • Illness - Gastroenteritis with persistent diarrhoea and vomiting
  • Sweating - From fever, exercise, or heavy work in hot conditions
  • Alcohol - Acts as a diuretic, making you urinate more
  • Diabetes - High blood glucose causes frequent urination

Who's at risk?

  • Babies and infants - sensitive to small amounts of fluid loss
  • Older people - may not recognize they're becoming dehydrated
  • People with long-term health conditions
  • Athletes - lose large amounts of fluid through sweat

Hyponatremia: Drinking too much water over a short period can reduce sodium levels in the blood, causing nausea, vomiting, and in severe cases, brain swelling.

Treating Dehydration

The best way to treat dehydration is to rehydrate the body by drinking plenty of fluids.

For adults and older children:

  • Water, diluted squash, or diluted fruit juice
  • A sweet drink can help replace lost sugar
  • A salty snack can help replace lost salt
  • Oral rehydration solutions from pharmacies

For babies and infants:

  • Breastmilk or formula - don't dilute formula
  • Smaller amounts of fluid more frequently
  • Avoid fruit juice, especially with diarrhoea and vomiting
  • Oral rehydration solution in addition to usual feeds

Severe dehydration requires hospital treatment:

  • Intravenous fluids (saline drip)
  • Nasogastric tube for fluid administration
  • Close monitoring of electrolyte levels

When to see a doctor

Contact your GP if symptoms continue despite drinking fluids, or if your baby has had six or more episodes of diarrhoea or three or more vomiting episodes in 24 hours.

Preventing Dehydration

You should drink plenty of fluids to avoid becoming dehydrated.

General prevention tips:

  • Drink water regularly throughout the day
  • Be guided by your thirst
  • Drink more in hot weather, during exercise, and when ill
  • Pale or clear-coloured urine is a good sign of hydration

During exercise and hot weather:

  • Increase fluid intake significantly
  • Drink before, during, and after exercise
  • Consider sports drinks for prolonged intense exercise
  • Avoid overhydration - stop if you feel discomfort

During illness:

  • Start replacing fluids as soon as vomiting or diarrhoea begins
  • Use oral rehydration solutions
  • Take small, frequent sips if unable to hold down large amounts

For children:

  • Offer healthy drinks regularly
  • Increase fluids during hot weather and physical activity
  • Monitor for signs of dehydration during illness
  • Use diluted squash or oral rehydration solutions when needed
