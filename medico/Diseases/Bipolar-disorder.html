Introduction to Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder, formerly known as manic depression, is a condition that affects your moods, which can swing from one extreme to another.

People with bipolar disorder have periods or episodes of:

  • Depression – feeling very low and lethargic
  • Mania – feeling very high and overactive (less severe mania is known as hypomania)

Symptoms of bipolar disorder depend on which mood you're experiencing. Unlike simple mood swings, each extreme episode can last for several weeks (or even longer), and some people may not experience a "normal" mood very often.

Depression Phase

During an episode of depression, you may have overwhelming feelings of worthlessness, which can potentially lead to thoughts of suicide.

Mania Phase

During a manic phase, you may feel very happy and have lots of energy, ambitious plans and ideas. You may spend large amounts of money on things you can't afford and wouldn't normally want.

Emergency Help: If you're feeling suicidal or having severe depressive symptoms, contact your GP, care co-ordinator or local mental health emergency services as soon as possible. You can also call the Samaritans free on 116 123, available 24/7.

Symptoms of Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder is characterised by extreme mood swings that can range from extreme highs (mania) to extreme lows (depression). Episodes often last for several weeks or months.

Depression Symptoms

  • Feeling sad, hopeless or irritable most of the time
  • Lacking energy
  • Difficulty concentrating and remembering things
  • Loss of interest in everyday activities
  • Feelings of emptiness or worthlessness
  • Feelings of guilt and despair
  • Difficulty sleeping or waking up early
  • Suicidal thoughts

Mania Symptoms

  • Feeling very happy, elated or overjoyed
  • Talking very quickly
  • Feeling full of energy
  • Feeling self-important
  • Being easily distracted
  • Not feeling like sleeping
  • Making risky decisions or spending large sums of money
  • Experiencing psychosis (seeing or hearing things that aren't there)

Patterns: Some people experience rapid cycling (swinging quickly between highs and lows) or mixed states (symptoms of depression and mania together).

Causes of Bipolar Disorder

The exact cause of bipolar disorder is unknown. Experts believe there are a number of factors that work together to make a person more likely to develop the condition.

Chemical Imbalance

Bipolar disorder is widely believed to be the result of chemical imbalances in the brain. The chemicals responsible for controlling brain functions (neurotransmitters like noradrenaline, serotonin and dopamine) may be imbalanced.

Genetics

The condition seems to run in families, suggesting a genetic component. Family members of a person with bipolar disorder have an increased risk of developing it themselves.

Triggers

Stressful circumstances often trigger symptoms, including:

  • Breakdown of relationships
  • Physical, sexual or emotional abuse
  • Death of a close family member or loved one
  • Physical illness
  • Sleep disturbances
  • Overwhelming problems in everyday life

Diagnosing Bipolar Disorder

If your GP thinks you may have bipolar disorder, they'll usually refer you to a psychiatrist (a medically qualified mental health specialist).

Specialist Assessment

You'll be assessed by a psychiatrist who will ask questions about your symptoms, when you first experienced them, and how you feel during episodes. They'll also ask about your medical background and family history.

Other Tests

Depending on your symptoms, you may need tests to check for physical problems like thyroid issues. If diagnosed with bipolar disorder, you'll need regular physical health check-ups with your GP.

Important: If your illness puts you at risk of harming yourself, your GP will arrange an immediate appointment with a specialist.

Treating Bipolar Disorder

Treatment aims to reduce the severity and number of episodes of depression and mania to allow as normal a life as possible.

Treatment Options

  • Medication - Mood stabilizers taken daily to prevent episodes
  • Psychological treatment - Talking therapies to help deal with depression
  • Learning triggers - Recognizing warning signs of approaching episodes
  • Lifestyle advice - Regular exercise, healthy diet, and good sleep habits

Medications

Several medications are available to help stabilize mood swings:

  • Lithium carbonate
  • Anticonvulsant medicines
  • Antipsychotic medicines

Treatment Success: Most people find that a combination of different treatment methods works best for controlling bipolar disorder.

Pregnancy Considerations

If you have bipolar disorder and are trying to conceive or become pregnant, speak to your healthcare professional about managing your treatment during pregnancy.

Living with Bipolar Disorder

Although usually a long-term condition, effective treatments combined with self-help techniques can limit the condition's impact on your everyday life.

Healthy Lifestyle

Eating well and keeping fit are important. Exercise can help reduce symptoms, particularly depressive symptoms, and provide routine.

Self-Management

Learning to recognize triggers and warning signs, developing coping strategies, and maintaining a healthy lifestyle are key aspects of self-management.

Support Resources

Helpful organizations include:

  • Bipolar UK
  • Mind
  • Rethink
  • Samaritans (116 123)

Remember: Avoiding drugs and alcohol is crucial as they can worsen symptoms and interfere with treatment effectiveness.

Crisis Support

If you're experiencing suicidal thoughts or severe symptoms, contact emergency mental health services immediately or call the Samaritans at 116 123.
