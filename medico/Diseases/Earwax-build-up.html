Earwax Build-up

Earwax is made inside your ears to keep them clean. It usually falls out on its own. Sometimes too much can build up and block the ears.

A build-up of earwax is a common problem. It can often be treated using eardrops bought from a pharmacy.

Important: Do not try to remove a build-up of earwax yourself with your fingers, a cotton bud or any other object. This can damage your ear and push the wax further down.

Symptoms of Earwax Build-up

A build-up of earwax in your ear can cause:

  • Earache - pain or discomfort in the ear
  • Hearing loss - reduced ability to hear sounds
  • Tinnitus - hearing sounds from inside your body
  • Itchiness - in or around the ear
  • Ear infections - increased risk of infections

These problems will usually get better once the build up of earwax has been removed.

Treatment Options

Eardrops

If the earwax is only causing minor problems, you can try buying some eardrops from a pharmacy. Using drops may make your hearing or symptoms a little worse at first before getting better.

These can help soften the earwax so that it falls out naturally. Common types include:

  • Drops containing sodium bicarbonate
  • Olive oil-based drops
  • Almond oil-based drops

Note: Eardrops aren't suitable for everyone and some can irritate the skin. You shouldn't use eardrops if you have a hole or tear in your eardrum.

Medical Treatments

If pharmacy treatment doesn't work, speak to your GP practice. Treatments may include:

  • Ear irrigation - a quick and painless procedure where an electric pump is used to push water into your ear and wash the earwax out
  • Microsuction - a quick and painless procedure where a small device is used to suck the earwax out of your ear

These treatments are not suitable for everyone. Your doctor can discuss this with you.

Causes of Earwax Build-up

Some people regularly get blocked ears because they naturally produce a lot of earwax.

Other causes include:

  • Producing naturally hard or dry earwax
  • Having narrow or hairy ear canals
  • Being elderly, as earwax becomes drier with age
  • Bony growths in the outer part of the ear canal

Earwax can also block your ear if you often insert objects into your ear, such as cotton buds, ear plugs or hearing aids.

When to Seek Medical Advice

Speak to your GP practice if:
  • You're worried about your symptoms
  • Eardrops haven't helped after 1 week

Diagnosing Earwax Build-up

Your GP or practice nurse will look inside your ears to check if they're blocked. They might carry out some simple hearing tests.

If standard treatments don't help, your GP may refer you to an ear, nose and throat (ENT) specialist.

Remember: Always consult with healthcare professionals before attempting any earwax removal procedures at home.
