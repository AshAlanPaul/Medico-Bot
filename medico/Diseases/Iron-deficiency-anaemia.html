Iron Deficiency Anaemia

About Iron Deficiency Anaemia Causes Diagnosis Treatment Complications

About Iron Deficiency Anaemia

Iron deficiency anaemia is a condition where a lack of iron in the body leads to a reduction in the number of red blood cells.

Iron is used to produce red blood cells, which help store and carry oxygen in the blood. If you have fewer red blood cells than is normal, your organs and tissues won't get as much oxygen as they usually would.

Symptoms of Iron Deficiency Anaemia

Many people with iron deficiency anaemia only have a few symptoms. The severity of the symptoms largely depends on how quickly anaemia develops.

Common symptoms include:

  • Tiredness and lack of energy (lethargy)
  • Shortness of breath
  • Noticeable heartbeats (heart palpitations)
  • Pale complexion

Less common symptoms include:

  • Headaches
  • Tinnitus (hearing sounds from inside the body)
  • Altered sense of taste
  • Feeling itchy
  • Sore or abnormally smooth tongue
  • Hair loss
  • Pica (desire to eat non-food items)
  • Difficulty swallowing (dysphagia)

When to see your GP: See your GP if you experience symptoms of iron deficiency anaemia. They should be able to diagnose the condition using a simple blood test.

Causes of Iron Deficiency Anaemia

Iron deficiency anaemia occurs when the body doesn't have enough iron, leading to decreased production of red blood cells.

Monthly Periods

In women of reproductive age, periods are the most common cause of iron deficiency anaemia. Women with heavy periods (menorrhagia) are particularly at risk.

Pregnancy

It's very common for women to develop iron deficiency during pregnancy because the body needs extra iron to ensure the baby has a sufficient blood supply.

Gastrointestinal Blood Loss

Bleeding in the gastrointestinal tract is the most common cause in men and post-menopausal women. This can be caused by:

  • Stomach ulcers
  • Stomach or bowel cancer
  • Inflammatory bowel disease
  • Regular use of NSAIDs

Important: If you're 60 years or older with iron deficiency anaemia, your GP should refer you to a specialist to rule out bowel cancer.

Diagnosing Iron Deficiency Anaemia

A simple blood test can usually confirm the diagnosis of iron deficiency anaemia.

Blood Test

To diagnose iron deficiency anaemia, a blood sample is taken and a full blood count is made. If you have anaemia:

  • Your haemoglobin levels will be lower than normal
  • You'll have fewer red blood cells than normal
  • Your red blood cells may be smaller and paler than usual

Your GP may also test for ferritin, a protein that stores iron. Low ferritin levels indicate low iron stores in your body.

Finding the Cause

Your GP will ask about your diet, medications, menstrual pattern (if applicable), and family history to determine the underlying cause.

Note: Iron deficiency anaemia is common during pregnancy. Your GP will only look for alternative causes if your haemoglobin is particularly low.

Treating Iron Deficiency Anaemia

Treatment usually involves taking iron supplements and changing your diet, as well as treating the underlying cause.

Iron Supplements

Your GP will prescribe an iron supplement, usually ferrous sulphate, taken as a tablet twice daily. Some people may experience side effects including:

  • Abdominal pain
  • Constipation or diarrhea
  • Heartburn
  • Feeling sick
  • Black stools

Dietary Advice

Iron-rich foods include:

  • Dark-green leafy vegetables (watercress, curly kale)
  • Iron-fortified cereals or bread
  • Brown rice
  • Pulses and beans
  • Nuts and seeds
  • Meat, fish and tofu
  • Eggs
  • Dried fruit

Warning: Store iron supplements out of reach of children. An overdose in a young child can be fatal.

Complications of Iron Deficiency Anaemia

Iron deficiency anaemia rarely causes serious long-term complications, but it can affect daily life.

Tiredness

The condition can make you feel tired and lethargic, affecting productivity at work and ability to exercise.

Increased Risk of Infections

Research shows iron deficiency anaemia can affect your immune system, increasing vulnerability to infection.

Heart and Lung Problems

Adults with severe anaemia may develop tachycardia (abnormally fast heartbeat) or heart failure.

Pregnancy Complications

Pregnant women with severe anaemia have increased risk of complications and postnatal depression.

Good to know: Most complications can be prevented with proper treatment and monitoring.
