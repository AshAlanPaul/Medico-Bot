Cystic Fibrosis

About Cystic Fibrosis Symptoms Causes Diagnosis Treatment

About Cystic Fibrosis

Cystic fibrosis is an inherited condition in which the lungs and digestive system can become clogged with thick, sticky mucus.

It can cause problems with breathing and digestion from a young age. Over many years, the lungs become increasingly damaged and may eventually stop working properly.

Prevalence: It's estimated that 1 in every 2,500 babies born in the UK has cystic fibrosis. Most cases are now identified through newborn screening.

Main Symptoms

  • Recurring chest infections
  • Difficulty putting on weight
  • Frequent, wet-sounding coughs
  • Diarrhoea
  • Occasional wheezing and shortness of breath

Related Conditions: People with cystic fibrosis can develop diabetes, osteoporosis, liver problems, and other related health issues.

Outlook

Cystic fibrosis is a progressive condition that tends to get worse over time. While treatments have improved life expectancy considerably, most people with cystic fibrosis will have a shorter-than-average life expectancy.

Life Expectancy: Currently, about half of people with cystic fibrosis live past age 40. Children born with the condition today are likely to live longer due to medical advancements.

Symptoms of Cystic Fibrosis

Cystic fibrosis can cause a range of problems, primarily affecting the lungs and digestive system. Symptoms usually start in early childhood but can sometimes develop later.

Lung Problems

Cystic fibrosis causes sticky mucus to clog the lungs and airways, leading to:

  • Recurring chest infections due to mucus providing ideal breeding ground for bacteria
  • Persistent inflammation of airways (bronchiectasis)
  • Increased coughing
  • Wheezing and shortness of breath

Digestive System Problems

Mucus can also block parts of the digestive system, causing:

  • Serious bowel obstruction in newborns (meconium ileus)
  • Jaundice
  • Diarrhea or large, smelly stools
  • Problems with weight gain and growth due to malnutrition
  • Diabetes from late childhood/adulthood

Other Problems

  • Sinusitis and nasal polyps
  • Thin, weakened bones (osteoporosis)
  • Joint swelling and pain (arthritis)
  • Fertility problems - most men are infertile, women can usually conceive
  • Liver problems
  • Stress incontinence

Causes of Cystic Fibrosis

Cystic fibrosis is caused by a faulty gene that a child inherits from both their parents.

Genetic Inheritance

The faulty gene means cells struggle to move salt and water across cell walls, leading to thick, sticky mucus buildup in the lungs and digestive system.

How Cystic Fibrosis is Inherited

To be born with cystic fibrosis, a child must inherit two copies of the faulty gene - one from each parent.

Carrier Status: It's estimated that around 1 in every 25 people in the UK are carriers of the faulty gene that causes cystic fibrosis.

Inheritance Probabilities

If both parents are carriers:

  • 25% chance: Child won't inherit any faulty genes
  • 50% chance: Child will be a carrier (inherit one faulty gene)
  • 25% chance: Child will have cystic fibrosis (inherit two faulty genes)

Diagnosing Cystic Fibrosis

Tests can diagnose cystic fibrosis at any age, but most cases are now detected through newborn screening.

Newborn Screening

All babies in Scotland are offered a newborn blood spot test at 5-8 days old to detect cystic fibrosis and other conditions.

Process: A health professional pricks the baby's heel and collects blood drops on a special card for laboratory testing.

Confirming Diagnosis

If screening suggests cystic fibrosis, further tests are needed:

  • Sweat test: Measures salt levels in sweat (higher in cystic fibrosis)
  • Genetic test: Checks blood or saliva for the faulty gene

Carrier Testing

Testing to see if you carry the faulty gene may be recommended if:

  • You have a close relative who is a known carrier
  • You have a close relative with cystic fibrosis
  • Your partner is known to carry the cystic fibrosis gene

Treating Cystic Fibrosis

While there's no cure for cystic fibrosis, treatments can help control symptoms, prevent complications, and improve quality of life.

Specialist Care: People with cystic fibrosis are supported by a team of healthcare professionals at specialist cystic fibrosis centers.

Medications for Lung Problems

  • Antibiotics to prevent and treat chest infections
  • Medicines to make lung mucus thinner
  • Ivacaftor to reduce mucus levels (suitable for fewer than 5% of patients)
  • Bronchodilators to widen airways
  • Steroid medication for nasal polyps

Airway Clearance Techniques

Special techniques help keep lungs and airways clear:

  • Active cycle of breathing techniques (ACBT)
  • Autogenic drainage
  • Modified postural drainage
  • Airway clearance devices

Dietary and Nutritional Support

Proper nutrition is vital for normal development and health:

  • Digestive enzyme capsules with all meals
  • High-calorie, high-fat, high-protein diet
  • Vitamin and mineral supplements

Lung Transplants

In severe cases where lungs stop working properly, a lung transplant may be recommended.

Transplant Success: Up to 9 out of 10 people survive at least one year after a lung transplant, with better outcomes than many other transplant patients.

Managing Associated Problems

  • Regular exercise for bone and joint health
  • Bisphosphonates for osteoporosis
  • Insulin and special diet for diabetes
