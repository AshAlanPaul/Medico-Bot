About Chronic Kidney Disease

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) is a long-term condition where the kidneys do not work effectively.

CKD does not usually cause symptoms until it reaches an advanced stage. It's usually detected at earlier stages by blood and urine tests.

Symptoms of Advanced Kidney Disease

The main symptoms of advanced kidney disease include:

  • Tiredness
  • Swollen ankles, feet or hands (due to water retention)
  • Shortness of breath
  • Nausea
  • Blood in the urine

Why Does It Happen?

The kidneys are 2 bean-shaped organs that filter waste products from the blood before converting them into urine. The kidneys also:

  • Help maintain blood pressure
  • Maintain correct levels of chemicals in your body
  • Produce the active form of vitamin D that keeps bones healthy
  • Produce erythropoietin, which stimulates red blood cell production

Who is affected: CKD is common and mainly associated with ageing. It's estimated that about 1 in 5 men and 1 in 4 women between 65-74 have some degree of CKD.

Symptoms of Chronic Kidney Disease

Most people with CKD have no symptoms because the body can tolerate even a large reduction in kidney function.

A change in kidney function is usually discovered through routine blood or urine tests.

Symptoms of Kidney Failure

If kidney failure occurs, symptoms may include:

  • Weight loss and poor appetite
  • Swollen ankles, feet or hands
  • Shortness of breath
  • Blood or protein in urine
  • Increased need to urinate, particularly at night
  • Insomnia
  • Itchy skin
  • Muscle cramps
  • High blood pressure
  • Nausea
  • Erectile dysfunction in men

These are general symptoms and can be caused by many less serious conditions. If you're worried by any symptoms, arrange to see your GP.

Causes of Chronic Kidney Disease

Kidney disease is most often caused by other conditions that put strain on the kidneys.

High Blood Pressure

High blood pressure (hypertension) and diabetes are the most common causes of kidney disease.

Hypertension causes damage by putting strain on small blood vessels in the kidneys, preventing proper filtering.

Diabetes

If diabetes is poorly controlled, excess glucose can damage the tiny filters in the kidneys.

It's estimated that 20-40% of people with type 1 diabetes and 30% with type 2 diabetes develop kidney damage.

Other Causes

  • Glomerulonephritis (inflammation of the kidney)
  • Pyelonephritis (kidney infection)
  • Polycystic kidney disease (inherited condition)
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus
  • Long-term use of certain medications
  • Blockages from kidney stones or prostate disease

Diagnosis of Chronic Kidney Disease

Chronic kidney disease is most frequently diagnosed through blood and urine tests.

Screening

Annual screening is recommended for high-risk groups:

  • People with high blood pressure
  • People with diabetes
  • People with cardiovascular disease
  • People with family history of kidney disease

Glomerular Filtration Rate (GFR)

GFR measures how many milliliters of waste fluid your kidneys can filter per minute. A healthy pair should filter more than 90ml/min.

Staging

CKD is classified into 5 stages based on eGFR levels:

  • Stage 1-2: Mild kidney damage with normal or slightly reduced function
  • Stage 3: Moderate reduction in kidney function
  • Stage 4: Severe reduction in kidney function
  • Stage 5: Kidney failure (eGFR below 15)

Treating Chronic Kidney Disease

Lifestyle Changes

Recommended Changes

  • Stop smoking
  • Eat a healthy, low-fat, balanced diet
  • Restrict salt intake to less than 6g daily
  • Avoid NSAIDs like ibuprofen
  • Moderate alcohol intake
  • Lose weight if overweight
  • Exercise regularly

Medications

Blood pressure control is vital. ACE inhibitors and ARBs are commonly prescribed to protect kidneys.

Treatment for Kidney Failure

When kidneys stop working completely, options include:

  • Dialysis: Artificial filtering of blood
  • Kidney transplant: Surgical replacement of failed kidneys
  • Supportive care: Managing symptoms without dialysis or transplant

Transplant success: About 90% of kidney transplants still function after five years, with many working for 20+ years.

Preventing Chronic Kidney Disease

In most cases, CKD cannot be completely prevented, but you can reduce the chances of developing it.

Key Prevention Strategies

  • Manage existing conditions: Control diabetes and high blood pressure
  • Stop smoking: Smoking increases cardiovascular risks
  • Healthy diet: Low salt, balanced nutrition
  • Limit alcohol: Stay within recommended guidelines
  • Regular exercise: At least 150 minutes weekly
  • Use painkillers carefully: Avoid improper use of NSAIDs

Remember: Early detection through regular screening is crucial, especially if you're in a high-risk group.

Living with Chronic Kidney Disease

Relationships and Support

Coming to terms with CKD can be challenging. Open communication with family and seeking professional support can help.

Financial Support

Various benefits may be available if CKD affects your ability to work, including Statutory Sick Pay and Disability Living Allowance.

Medication Safety

Some over-the-counter medicines can be harmful. Always check with your doctor before taking new medications.

Safe Medication Guidelines

  • Headaches: Paracetamol is safe, avoid soluble products high in sodium
  • Coughs/Colds: Avoid products with decongestants if you have high blood pressure
  • Muscle/Joint Pain: Use topical preparations when possible

Quality of Life

With proper management, most people with CKD can maintain good quality of life. Supportive care teams can help with physical, emotional, and practical aspects of living with kidney disease.
