Glandular Fever Information

About Glandular Fever Symptoms Causes Treatment Complications Prevention

About Glandular Fever

Glandular fever is a type of viral infection that mostly affects young adults. It's also known as infectious mononucleosis, or 'mono'.

Common Symptoms

  • High temperature (fever)
  • Severely sore throat
  • Swollen glands in the neck
  • Fatigue (extreme tiredness)

Recovery: While symptoms can be very unpleasant, most should pass within 2-3 weeks. Fatigue may occasionally last several months.

Who is Affected

  • Most common in teenagers and young adults
  • Childhood infections often cause mild or no symptoms
  • Lifelong immunity develops after initial infection
  • Unlikely to develop glandular fever again

Emergency Symptoms - Go to A&E or call 999 if:

  • Rasping breath or breathing difficulties
  • Difficulty swallowing fluids
  • Intense abdominal pain

Symptoms of Glandular Fever

Symptoms typically develop 1-2 months after infection with the Epstein-Barr virus (EBV).

Main Symptoms

  • High temperature (fever)
  • Severely sore throat - usually more painful than previous sore throats
  • Swollen glands in neck and possibly other areas
  • Extreme fatigue (tiredness)

Other Symptoms

  • General feeling of being unwell
  • Aching muscles
  • Chills and sweats
  • Loss of appetite
  • Pain around or behind eyes
  • Swollen tonsils affecting breathing
  • Red throat with fluid discharge
  • Small red/purple spots on roof of mouth
  • Rash
  • Puffiness around eyes
  • Tender or swollen abdomen
  • Jaundice (yellowing of skin and eyes)

Symptom Timeline

  • Most symptoms: Resolve within 2-3 weeks
  • Sore throat: Most severe for 3-5 days, then improves
  • Fever: Typically lasts 10-14 days
  • Fatigue: Often persists for weeks, sometimes months

Causes of Glandular Fever

Glandular fever is caused by the Epstein-Barr virus (EBV), primarily spread through saliva.

How it Spreads

  • Kissing: Often called the 'kissing disease'
  • Sharing items: Food, drinks, toothbrushes
  • Respiratory droplets: Coughs and sneezes
  • Contaminated toys: In young children

Infection Process

  • Virus infects throat lining cells
  • Spreads to white blood cells
  • Travels through lymphatic system
  • Develops lifelong immunity after infection

EBV Carriers

Some people carry EBV without symptoms and can spread it:

  • Virus remains in saliva for months after recovery
  • Can be reactivated periodically
  • May cause symptoms to recur briefly

Treating Glandular Fever

There's no cure, but symptoms usually pass within weeks. Self-care measures can help manage symptoms.

Self-Care Treatment

  • Fluids: Drink plenty of water and unsweetened juice
  • Avoid alcohol: Can harm already weakened liver
  • Pain relief: Paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain and fever
  • Salt water gargle: For sore throat relief
  • Rest: Gradual increase in activity as energy returns
  • Avoid contact sports: For at least 1 month due to spleen risk

Medical Treatments

Antibiotics

Not effective against viruses, but may be prescribed for secondary bacterial infections like pneumonia.

Corticosteroids

May be used for:

  • Severely swollen tonsils causing breathing problems
  • Severe anaemia
  • Heart or neurological complications

Hospital Treatment Needed If:

  • Breathing difficulties or rasping breath
  • Unable to swallow fluids
  • Intense abdominal pain
  • May require IV fluids, antibiotics, or emergency spleen surgery

Preventing Spread

  • No need for isolation (most people are immune)
  • Avoid kissing while symptomatic
  • Don't share utensils, drinks, or toothbrushes
  • Clean contaminated items thoroughly
  • Return to work/school when feeling well enough

Complications of Glandular Fever

Most people recover completely, but some may experience complications.

Common Complications

Prolonged Fatigue

Affects more than 1 in 10 people, lasting 6+ months. May be related to chronic fatigue syndrome.

Blood Cell Reduction

  • Anaemia: Reduced red blood cells causing tiredness
  • Neutropenia: Reduced white blood cells increasing infection risk
  • Low platelets: Increased bruising and bleeding

Ruptured Spleen

Affects 1 in 500-1,000 cases. Symptoms include sharp left abdominal pain.

  • Can be life-threatening due to internal bleeding
  • May require emergency surgery (splenectomy)
  • Highest risk during weeks 2-3 of illness
  • Avoid contact sports for at least 1 month

Neurological Complications

Affects less than 1 in 100 cases:

  • Guillain-Barr√© syndrome (nerve inflammation)
  • Bell's palsy (facial muscle weakness)
  • Viral meningitis
  • Encephalitis (brain infection)

Secondary Infections

Weakened immune system may allow bacterial infections like pneumonia or pericarditis.

Prevention and Management

While complete prevention may be difficult, certain measures can reduce risk and manage recovery.

Prevention Strategies

  • Avoid close contact with infected individuals
  • Don't share eating/drinking utensils
  • Practice good hand hygiene
  • Avoid kissing when you or partner are unwell
  • Clean shared surfaces regularly

Recovery Management

  • Gradual activity: Increase slowly as energy returns
  • Balanced nutrition: Support immune system recovery
  • Adequate rest: Listen to your body's needs
  • Medical follow-up: Attend scheduled check-ups
  • Activity restrictions: Avoid strenuous exercise initially

When to Return to Normal Activities

  • Work/school: When feeling well enough
  • Exercise: Gradual return, avoid contact sports for 1 month
  • Social activities: When no longer contagious (usually 2-3 weeks)
  • Always consult your doctor about specific return timelines

Long-term Outlook

  • Most people make full recovery within weeks
  • Lifelong immunity develops after infection
  • Fatigue may persist but usually resolves
  • Serious complications are rare with proper care
  • Regular follow-up helps monitor any lingering issues
