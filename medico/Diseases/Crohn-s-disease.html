About Crohn's Disease

Crohn's disease is a long-term condition that causes inflammation of the lining of the digestive system.

Inflammation can affect any part of the digestive system, from the mouth to the back passage. But it mostly occurs in the last section of the small intestine (ileum) or the large intestine (colon).

Crohn's disease can affect people of all ages, but it's usually diagnosed between the age of 15 and 40.

Note: Crohn's disease is a chronic condition with periods of remission (few or no symptoms) and flare-ups (when symptoms are active).

Symptoms of Crohn's Disease

The symptoms of Crohn's disease vary. They depend on which part of the digestive system is inflamed.

Common symptoms include:

  • Recurring diarrhoea
  • Abdominal pain and cramping, which is usually worse after eating
  • Extreme tiredness (fatigue)
  • Unintended weight loss
  • Blood and mucus in your stool

Less common symptoms include:

  • High temperature (fever) of 38°C (100°F) or above
  • Feeling sick (nausea) and vomiting
  • Joint pain and swelling (arthritis)
  • Inflammation and irritation of the eyes (uveitis)
  • Areas of painful, red and swollen skin – most often the legs
  • Mouth ulcers
  • Anal pain and discharge due to perianal Crohn's

Speak to your GP if you have:

  • Diarrhoea for more than 7 days
  • Persistent abdominal pain
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Blood in your stool
  • Concern about your child's development

Causes of Crohn's Disease

The exact cause of Crohn's disease is unknown. A combination of factors may be responsible:

  • Genetics – genes you inherit from your parents
  • Immune system problems – where it attacks healthy bacteria in the gut and causes inflammation
  • Environmental factors – like air pollution, medication or previous infections
  • Smoking – smokers are twice as likely to develop Crohn's disease, and usually have more severe symptoms than non-smokers

Important: If you smoke, it is strongly advised that you make every effort to stop smoking, especially if you need to have surgery.

Diagnosing Crohn's Disease

Your GP will usually ask about your symptoms, diet, recent travel, medication, and family history of bowel conditions.

Initial tests may include:

  • Physical examination including checking pulse, blood pressure, and abdomen
  • Blood tests to check for inflammation, infection and anaemia
  • Stool sample analysis to check for blood, mucus, infection or inflammation

Specialist referral and further tests:

You may be referred to a gastroenterologist who might recommend:

  • Gastroscopy – to examine your food pipe and stomach
  • Colonoscopy – to examine your large bowel
  • Sigmoidoscopy – to examine the end of your large bowel
  • Colon capsule endoscopy (pill camera)
  • X-ray, MRI scan, or CT scan

Treatment for Crohn's Disease

There's currently no cure for Crohn's disease, but treatment can improve symptoms and keep inflammation under control.

Steroid medication

The first treatment is usually steroids to reduce inflammation quickly. These are effective but not suitable for long-term use due to side effects.

Immunosuppressants

Medicines to suppress your immune system may be given alongside steroids if symptoms flare up frequently.

Biological therapies

If other treatments don't work, biological therapies that block specific chemicals in the immune response may be used.

Surgery

Surgery may be an option if medications don't control symptoms or if there are serious complications. This might involve:

  • Widening narrow parts of the bowel
  • Removing inflamed sections of intestine
  • Creating an ileostomy (stoma) in some cases

Medical attention needed: If you experience side effects from biological therapies such as itchy skin, high temperature, joint pain, or swelling, seek immediate medical help.

Diet and Lifestyle with Crohn's Disease

There is no evidence that a particular diet causes or triggers flare-ups of Crohn's disease, but dietary changes may help control symptoms for some people.

Recommended dietary practices:

  • Eat 5 to 6 small meals rather than 3 main meals
  • Try to eat a healthy, varied diet including fruits, vegetables, nuts, seeds, proteins and wholegrains
  • Drink plenty of water

Do not make major changes to your diet without talking to your healthcare team. Consider:

  • Keeping a food diary to identify trigger foods
  • Taking food supplements if needed
  • Consulting with a dietitian

Fertility and Pregnancy

Most women with Crohn's disease can have normal pregnancies and healthy babies. However, discuss pregnancy plans with your healthcare team as medication adjustments may be needed.

Complications of Crohn's Disease

Over time, inflammation can damage sections of the digestive system, leading to complications such as:

  • Stricture – narrowing of the bowel
  • Fistula – a channel developing between your bowel and your skin, another section of bowel or a body organ

These problems usually need surgical treatment.

Patient registry: If you have Crohn's disease, your clinical team may share information with the National Congenital Anomaly and Rare Diseases Registration Service to help research. You can opt out at any time.
