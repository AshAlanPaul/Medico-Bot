About Malignant Brain Tumours

A malignant brain tumour is a fast-growing cancer that spreads to other areas of the brain and spine.

Generally, brain tumours are graded from 1 to 4, according to their behaviour, such as how fast they grow and how likely they are to grow back after treatment. A malignant brain tumour is either grade 3 or 4, whereas grade 1 or 2 tumours are usually classed as benign or non-cancerous.

Types of Brain Tumours

  • Primary brain tumours - Start in the brain
  • Secondary brain tumours - Start in another part of the body and spread to the brain
  • Gliomas - Develop from glial tissue that supports the brain's nerve cells

Important: Most malignant tumours are secondary cancers that started elsewhere in the body and spread to the brain.

Symptoms of Malignant Brain Tumours

The symptoms of a malignant brain tumour depend on how big it is and where it is in the brain.

Common Symptoms

  • Severe, persistent headaches - typically worse in the morning
  • Seizures (fits)
  • Persistent nausea, vomiting and drowsiness
  • Mental or behavioural changes
  • Memory problems
  • Personality changes
  • Progressive weakness or paralysis on one side of the body
  • Vision problems
  • Speech problems

Speak to your GP if you have persistent symptoms of a brain tumour. While it's unlikely to be a tumour, it's best to be sure by getting a proper diagnosis.

Causes of Malignant Brain Tumours

Most malignant brain tumours are caused by cancer that started somewhere else in the body and spread to the brain through the bloodstream.

Risk Factors

  • Age - Risk increases with age, though tumours can affect people of all ages
  • Family history - Slightly increased risk with family history
  • Radiotherapy - Previous head radiation treatment
  • Genetic conditions - Such as neurofibromatosis, tuberous sclerosis

Note: The exact cause of primary malignant brain tumours is often unknown. Unlike most tumours, those associated with genetic conditions tend to develop in childhood or early adulthood.

Diagnosing Malignant Brain Tumours

If you develop persistent symptoms of a brain tumour, your GP will examine you and may refer you to a neurologist for further assessment.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Neurological examination - Tests of nervous system function
  • CT scan - Detailed brain imaging using X-rays
  • MRI scan - Detailed brain imaging using magnetic fields
  • EEG - Records brain activity for abnormalities
  • Biopsy - Surgical removal of tissue for analysis

Early diagnosis is crucial for effective treatment of malignant brain tumours. Don't ignore persistent neurological symptoms.

Treating Malignant Brain Tumours

Treatment for malignant brain tumours typically involves a combination of approaches tailored to the individual case.

Treatment Options

  • Surgery - To remove as much of the tumour as possible
  • Radiotherapy - High-energy radiation to kill cancer cells
  • Chemotherapy - Medication to kill cancerous cells
  • Radiosurgery - Precise radiation treatment
  • Symptom management - Medications to control symptoms

Treatment plan: A multidisciplinary team will work with you to create the best treatment approach for your specific situation.

Recovery and Support

Recovery from brain tumour treatment requires comprehensive support and follow-up care.

Recovery Aspects

  • Follow-up appointments - Regular monitoring for recurrence
  • Supportive therapies - Physiotherapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy
  • Lifestyle adjustments - Driving restrictions, activity limitations
  • Emotional support - Counseling and support groups
  • Social support - Assistance with daily activities

Support is available: Many organizations provide information and support for brain tumour patients and their families, including The Brain Tumour Charity and Brain Tumour Research.
