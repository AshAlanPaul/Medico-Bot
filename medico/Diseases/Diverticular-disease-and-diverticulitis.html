Diverticular Disease Information

About Diverticular Disease Symptoms Causes Diagnosis Treatment Complications Prevention

About Diverticular Disease and Diverticulitis

Diverticular disease and diverticulitis are related digestive conditions that affect the large intestine (colon).

In diverticular disease, small bulges or pockets (diverticula) develop in the lining of the intestine. Diverticulitis is when these pockets become inflamed or infected.

Symptoms of Diverticular Disease

  • Lower abdominal pain
  • Feeling bloated

The majority of people with diverticula will not have any symptoms; this is known as diverticulosis.

Symptoms of Diverticulitis

  • More severe abdominal pain, especially on the left side
  • High temperature (fever) of 38C (100.4F) or above
  • Diarrhoea or frequent bowel movements

Important: Diverticular disease is one of the most common digestive conditions, often described as a 'Western disease' due to higher rates in European and North American countries.

Symptoms of Diverticular Disease and Diverticulitis

Symptoms include abdominal pain, bloating and a change in normal bowel habits.

Diverticulosis

If diverticula have been discovered during tests but you've never had abdominal pain or bouts of diarrhoea, there's a 70% to 80% chance you'll never have any symptoms from them.

Diverticular Disease

The most common symptom is intermittent pain in your lower abdomen, usually in the lower left-hand side. Other symptoms include:

  • Change in bowel habits (constipation or diarrhoea)
  • Bloating
  • Multiple trips to the toilet in the morning

Diverticulitis

Symptoms of diverticulitis are more severe and constant rather than intermittent. Additional symptoms include:

  • High temperature (fever)
  • General feeling of being tired and unwell
  • Nausea or vomiting

When to seek medical advice: Contact your GP as soon as possible if you think you have symptoms of diverticulitis. If you have severe pain, fever, or bleeding, seek immediate medical attention.

Causes of Diverticular Disease and Diverticulitis

Diverticular disease is caused by small bulges in the large intestine developing and becoming inflamed. If any become infected, this leads to diverticulitis.

The exact reason why diverticula develop is not known, but they are strongly associated with not eating enough fibre.

Risk Factors

  • Smoking - increases risk significantly
  • Being overweight or obese - puts additional pressure on the colon
  • History of constipation - leads to increased pressure in the colon
  • Use of NSAIDs - such as ibuprofen or naproxen
  • Family history - especially if developed under age 50
  • Age - more common in older adults

Dietary factor: Fibre makes stools softer and larger, requiring less pressure to move through the colon. Low fibre intake is the primary dietary risk factor.

Diagnosing Diverticular Disease and Diverticulitis

Diagnosis can be challenging as symptoms overlap with other conditions like IBS.

Diagnostic Methods

  • Blood tests - to rule out other conditions and check for infection
  • Colonoscopy - camera examination of the colon
  • CT scan - detailed imaging of the abdomen
  • CT pneumocolon - specialized CT scan with air contrast
  • Ultrasound - may be used in certain cases

Note: If you have a previous history of diverticular disease, your GP can often diagnose diverticulitis from symptoms and examination alone. New cases require more thorough investigation.

Treating Diverticular Disease and Diverticulitis

Diverticular Disease Treatment

Most cases can be treated at home with:

  • High-fibre diet
  • Paracetamol for pain relief (avoid NSAIDs)
  • Bulk-forming laxatives if constipated

Diverticulitis Treatment

Treatment depends on severity:

  • Mild cases: Antibiotics and pain relief at home
  • Severe cases: Hospital admission for IV antibiotics and fluids
  • Fluid diet: Recommended initially to rest the bowel

Surgical Options

Surgery may be needed for:

  • Serious complications
  • Recurrent episodes
  • Perforation or abscess formation

Emergency warning: Severe abdominal pain with fever could indicate a perforated bowel, which requires immediate medical attention and possible emergency surgery.

Complications of Diverticular Disease and Diverticulitis

Complications affect about 1 in 5 people with the condition, with higher risk in those under 50.

Common Complications

  • Bleeding - usually painless but can be severe
  • Abscess - pus-filled cavity requiring drainage
  • Fistula - abnormal tunnel between organs
  • Peritonitis - infection of abdominal lining (life-threatening)
  • Intestinal obstruction - partial or complete blockage
  • Urinary problems - if inflammation affects the bladder

Important: Most people with diverticula never develop complications. Proper management and dietary changes can significantly reduce risks.

Preventing Diverticular Disease and Diverticulitis

Eating a high-fibre diet is the most effective way to prevent diverticular disease and improve symptoms.

Recommended Fibre Intake

Adults should aim for 18g to 30g of fibre daily. Increase fibre gradually and drink plenty of fluids.

Excellent Fibre Sources

  • Fruits: Avocado, pears, oranges, apples, raspberries
  • Vegetables: Baked beans, kidney beans, peas, Brussels sprouts
  • Nuts: Almonds, peanuts, Brazil nuts
  • Whole grains: Wholemeal bread, brown rice, pasta, cereals
  • Breakfast cereals: All-Bran, Shredded Wheat, Bran Flakes

Prevention tip: People aged 50-70 who eat 25g of fibre daily have a 40% lower chance of hospital admission with diverticular disease complications.

Lifestyle Recommendations

  • Maintain healthy weight
  • Exercise regularly
  • Stay hydrated
  • Avoid smoking
  • Limit NSAID use when possible
