Hearing Loss

Hearing loss is a common problem that often develops with age or is caused by repeated exposure to loud noises.

It's estimated that there are more than 10 million (about 1 in 6) people in the UK with some degree of hearing impairment or deafness.

Hearing loss can occur suddenly, but usually develops gradually over time.

Note: Hearing loss affects people of all ages and can range from mild to profound. Early detection and intervention can significantly improve quality of life.

When to Get Medical Advice

Speak to your GP practice if:

  • You or your child are having problems with your hearing
  • You lose your hearing suddenly, in one or both ears

Your GP can check for any problems and may refer you to an audiologist (hearing specialist) or an ENT specialist for further tests.

Hearing tests are also available on the high street, but you may have to pay for these.

Sudden hearing loss: If you experience sudden hearing loss, seek medical attention immediately as this may require urgent treatment.

Signs and Symptoms of Hearing Loss

Early signs of hearing loss can be subtle and may develop gradually. Being aware of these signs can help with early detection.

Common Signs in Adults

  • Difficulty hearing other people clearly and misunderstanding what they say, especially in group situations
  • Asking people to repeat themselves frequently
  • Listening to music or watching television with the volume higher than other people need
  • Difficulty hearing the telephone or doorbell
  • Finding it difficult to tell which direction noise is coming from
  • Regularly feeling tired or stressed from having to concentrate while listening

Tinnitus: If you also hear a ringing, buzzing or hissing sound in your ears, this could be a sign of tinnitus, which is often associated with hearing loss.

Signs in Babies

You should consider seeing your GP if you notice that your baby or toddler:

  • Isn't startled by loud noises
  • Doesn't turn towards the source of a sound when under 4 months old
  • Doesn't say single words by the time they're 1 year old
  • Notices you when they see you, but not when you call their name
  • Hears some sounds but not others

Signs in Children

You should consider seeing your GP if you notice that your child:

  • Is slow to learn to talk, or they're not clear when they speak
  • Often asks you to repeat yourself or responds inappropriately to a question
  • Doesn't reply when you call them
  • Often talks very loudly
  • Often turns up the volume of the television so it's very loud
  • Watches other people to copy instructions, because they haven't heard

Causes of Hearing Loss

Hearing loss is the result of sound signals not reaching the brain. There are 2 main types of hearing loss, depending on where the problem lies:

Sensorineural Hearing Loss

Caused by damage to the sensitive hair cells inside the inner ear or damage to the auditory nerve. This can happen:

  • Naturally with age (presbycusis)
  • As a result of injury or trauma
  • From exposure to loud noises
  • Due to certain medications
  • From genetic conditions

Conductive Hearing Loss

When sounds are unable to pass from your outer ear to your inner ear, often because of:

  • Earwax buildup
  • Glue ear (fluid in the middle ear)
  • Ear infections
  • Perforated eardrum
  • Disorders of the hearing bones

Mixed hearing loss: It's also possible to have both sensorineural and conductive hearing loss. This is known as mixed hearing loss.

Some people are born with hearing loss, but most cases develop as you get older.

Treatment for Hearing Loss

The way hearing loss is treated depends on the cause and how severe it is.

Treatment for Sensorineural Hearing Loss

For nerve-related hearing loss, several options can help improve hearing and communication:

  • Digital hearing aids – available through the NHS
  • Bone anchored implants – suitable for people who cannot use hearing aids
  • Middle ear implants – for those unable to use conventional hearing aids
  • Cochlear implants – for people who find hearing aids aren't powerful enough
  • Lip reading and sign language – such as British Sign Language (BSL)

Treatment for Conductive Hearing Loss

Conductive hearing loss is sometimes temporary and can be treated with:

  • Medication for infections
  • Minor surgery for blockages or simple issues
  • More major surgery to fix the eardrum or hearing bones if needed

Modern solutions: Today's hearing technology is more advanced and discreet than ever before, offering significant improvements in hearing quality and comfort.

Preventing Hearing Loss

While it isn't always possible to prevent hearing loss from underlying conditions, you can reduce the risk of noise-induced hearing loss.

Do

  • Use headphones that block out more outside noise, rather than turning up the volume
  • Use ear protection equipment such as ear muffs or ear plugs if you work in a noisy environment
  • Use ear-protection equipment at loud concerts and other events with high noise levels
  • Be aware of the symptoms of common causes of hearing loss
  • Speak to your GP practice if you or your child are experiencing hearing problems

Don't

  • Do not have your television, radio or music on too loud
  • Do not insert objects into your ears or your children's ears – this includes fingers, cotton buds, cotton wool and tissue

Ear infections: You should also speak to your GP practice if you have signs of an ear infection, such as flu-like symptoms, severe earache, discharge or hearing loss.

Your employer should provide appropriate hearing protection if you work in a noisy environment such as a pub, nightclub, garage workshop or building site.
