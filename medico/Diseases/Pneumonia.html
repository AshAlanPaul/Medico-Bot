About Pneumonia

Pneumonia is swelling (inflammation) of the tissue in one or both lungs. It's usually caused by a bacterial infection.

At the end of the breathing tubes in your lungs are clusters of tiny air sacs. If you have pneumonia, these tiny sacs become inflamed and fill up with fluid.

Symptoms of Pneumonia

Symptoms can develop suddenly over 24 to 48 hours, or come on more slowly over several days.

Common Symptoms

  • Cough - may be dry or produce thick yellow, green, brown or blood-stained mucus
  • Difficulty breathing - rapid and shallow breathing, feeling breathless
  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Fever
  • Feeling generally unwell
  • Sweating and shivering
  • Loss of appetite
  • Chest pain - worse when breathing or coughing

Less Common Symptoms

  • Coughing up blood
  • Headaches
  • Fatigue
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Wheezing
  • Joint and muscle pain
  • Confusion (particularly in elderly)

When to see your GP: See your GP if you feel unwell with typical pneumonia symptoms. Seek urgent medical attention if you experience severe symptoms like rapid breathing, chest pain, or confusion.

Risk Groups

  • Babies and very young children
  • Elderly people
  • People who smoke
  • People with health conditions (asthma, cystic fibrosis, heart/kidney/liver conditions)
  • People with weakened immune systems

Types of Pneumonia

  • Bacterial pneumonia - Most common, caused by Streptococcus pneumoniae
  • Viral pneumonia - Caused by viruses like RSV or influenza
  • Aspiration pneumonia - Caused by breathing in foreign substances
  • Fungal pneumonia - Rare in the UK
  • Hospital-acquired pneumonia - Develops during hospital stay

Treating Pneumonia

Mild pneumonia can usually be treated at home with rest, antibiotics and plenty of fluids. More severe cases may need hospital treatment.

Treatment at Home

  • Antibiotics - Complete the full course as prescribed
  • Rest - Allow your body time to recover
  • Fluids - Drink plenty to avoid dehydration
  • Pain relief - Paracetamol or ibuprofen for pain and fever
  • Warm drinks - Honey and lemon can help relieve cough

Important: Always finish your prescribed course of antibiotics, even if you feel better. Stopping early can lead to antibiotic resistance.

Recovery Timeline

  • 1 week - Fever should have resolved
  • 4 weeks - Chest pain and mucus production reduced
  • 6 weeks - Cough and breathlessness substantially reduced
  • 3 months - Most symptoms resolved, but fatigue may persist
  • 6 months - Most people feel back to normal

When to Seek Further Help

Contact your GP if:

  • Symptoms don't improve within 3 days of starting antibiotics
  • Your condition is deteriorating
  • You're not improving as expected

Treatment in Hospital

Hospital treatment may be needed if symptoms are severe. This may include:

  • Intravenous antibiotics and fluids
  • Oxygen therapy to help breathing
  • In severe cases, ventilator support in ICU

Prevention

  • Practice good hygiene - cover coughs/sneezes, wash hands regularly
  • Avoid smoking - damages lungs and increases infection risk
  • Limit alcohol - excessive use weakens lung defenses
  • Consider vaccinations - pneumococcal and flu vaccines for at-risk groups

Note: Pneumonia isn't usually passed from person to person, so it's safe to be around others. However, people with weakened immune systems should avoid close contact until recovery is underway.
