About Rhabdomyosarcoma

Rhabdomyosarcoma is a type of soft tissue sarcoma (tumour). Most children with rhabdomyosarcoma are younger than 10 years old. It's more common in boys than girls.

Positive outlook: More children than ever are surviving childhood cancer. There are new and better drugs and treatments, and we can now also work to reduce the after-effects of having had cancer in the past.

Sarcomas are rare types of tumour that develop in the supporting tissues of the body. There are two main types:

  • Soft tissue sarcomas – develop in muscle, fat, blood vessels, or other supporting tissues
  • Bone sarcomas – develop in any of the bones of the skeleton

Rhabdomyosarcoma is the most common soft tissue sarcoma in children. These tumours develop from muscle or fibrous tissue and can grow in any part of the body.

Common Locations

The most common areas affected include:

  • Head and neck area
  • Bladder
  • Testes
  • Womb or vagina
  • Muscles or limbs
  • Chest or abdominal wall

Note: If the tumour is in the head or neck area, it can occasionally spread into the brain or the fluid around the spinal cord.

Causes

The exact causes of rhabdomyosarcoma are unknown, but research is ongoing.

Children with certain rare genetic disorders, such as Li-Fraumeni syndrome, have a higher risk of developing rhabdomyosarcoma.

Important Note for Parents

It's devastating to hear that your child has cancer, and at times it can feel overwhelming. There are many healthcare professionals and support organisations to help you through this difficult time.

Symptoms

The most common symptom is a lump or swelling. Other symptoms depend on the part of the body affected:

By Location

  • Head or neck area: Blockage and discharge from nose or throat; occasionally swollen and protruding eye
  • Abdomen (tummy): Pain or discomfort; difficulty going to the toilet (constipation)
  • Bladder: Blood in urine; difficulty passing urine
  • Other areas: Visible lumps or swelling in muscles or limbs

When to seek medical attention: If you notice any persistent lumps, swelling, or unusual symptoms in your child, consult a healthcare professional for proper evaluation.

Diagnosis

Different tests are usually needed to diagnose rhabdomyosarcoma. Your child may need a biopsy to remove a sample from the tumour for examination under a microscope.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Biopsy – removal of tissue sample for analysis
  • Chest X-ray – to check the lungs
  • Ultrasound, CT, or MRI scans – to determine tumour size and location
  • Blood tests and bone marrow tests – to check for spread

Staging

The 'stage' describes the cancer's size and whether it has spread. Staging helps doctors decide on the most effective treatment.

The staging system considers:

  • Where in the body the tumour started
  • Whether it's localized or has spread (metastatic disease)

Understanding diagnosis: Your child's specialist will explain the specific staging system used and what it means for your child's treatment plan.

Treatment

Rhabdomyosarcomas are rare tumours and should be treated at specialist centres. Treatment depends on tumour size, location, and whether it has spread.

Main Treatment Types

  • Chemotherapy – uses anti-cancer drugs to destroy cancer cells
  • Surgery – removes the tumour when possible
  • Radiotherapy – uses high-energy rays to destroy cancer cells

Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy can be given:

  • To shrink the tumour before surgery
  • After surgery to reduce the risk of recurrence

Surgery

When possible, surgery aims to remove all or as much of the tumour as possible without damaging surrounding tissue. Chemotherapy is usually given first to shrink the tumour.

Radiotherapy

Radiotherapy may be given after surgery to the area where the rhabdomyosarcoma started.

Side Effects

Side effects depend on the treatment and the body area being treated. Most are temporary and gradually disappear after treatment stops.

  • Chemotherapy: Nausea, hair loss, tiredness, increased infection risk
  • Radiotherapy: Tiredness, skin redness/darkening in treated area

Late Side Effects

Some children may develop long-term side effects years after treatment:

  • Reduced bone growth
  • Infertility
  • Changes in heart and kidney function
  • Slight increased risk of another cancer later in life

Follow-up care: Children who've had cancer receive close monitoring for any signs of late effects throughout their lives.

Clinical Trials

Many children receive treatment as part of clinical research trials that aim to improve cancer treatment. Participation is completely voluntary.

Expert care: The Children's Cancer and Leukaemia Group (CCLG) helps produce treatment guidelines and supports research into childhood cancers.

Follow-up Care and Support

After treatment, doctors will regularly check your child to ensure the cancer hasn't returned and monitor for any complications.

Emotional Support

As a parent, learning your child has cancer is one of the most difficult situations you can face. Common emotions include:

  • Fear and anxiety
  • Guilt and sadness
  • Anger and uncertainty

These are all normal reactions. Your child may also experience powerful emotions throughout their cancer journey.

Support Resources

The Children's Cancer and Leukaemia Group (CCLG) provides valuable resources including "Children & Young People's Cancer; A Parent's Guide" which discusses the emotional impact and suggests sources of help and support.

Remember that you're not alone – there are many healthcare professionals and support organizations ready to help you and your family through this challenging time.
