The Testicles

It can be embarrassing to talk about your testicles and any changes to them that you've noticed. Understanding more about what they do might help.

Facts About Testicles

Testicles are small and oval-shaped. They hang below your penis in a sac called the scrotum.

From puberty, your testicles produce a hormone called testosterone. This gives you a lower voice, hair on your face and body, and makes your muscles get bigger. You also need it for your sex drive and for getting an erection.

The testicles make sperm from puberty (around 13 to 14 years old) onwards. When sperm meets with a female egg during sex, this can cause pregnancy.

Lymph Nodes (Glands)

Lymph nodes are small and round, and connect to each other by tiny tubes that carry fluid called lymph. We have lymph nodes throughout our bodies. They are part of the lymphatic system, which protects us from infections.

Sometimes cancer cells from the testicle can spread to lymph nodes at the back of the abdomen (tummy). You'll have a scan to check your lymph nodes.

Note: Understanding your anatomy can help you feel more comfortable discussing any concerns with healthcare professionals.

Testing for Testicular Cancer

You may have tests done by your GP or at the hospital. They will help your doctors see whether you have testicular cancer.

Visiting Your GP

If you think you might have some of the symptoms of testicular cancer, you should go to your GP. They'll be able to talk to you about your symptoms and examine your testicles if necessary.

If your doctor thinks there is a chance you might have cancer, they might arrange for you to have an ultrasound test. This uses sound waves to take a picture of the testicles.

At the Hospital

If you need more tests you'll be referred to a hospital specialist (urologist), who will examine you and do the following tests:

  • Blood tests to check for chemicals in the blood called tumour markers
  • An ultrasound scan of the scrotum and testicles

Removing a Testicle

If your ultrasound shows the lump is almost certainly cancer, an operation to remove a testicle (orchidectomy) may be necessary. This will not stop you having sex or becoming a dad in the future.

Further Tests

If testicular cancer is diagnosed you will need some other tests:

  • More blood tests to check organ function
  • Chest X-rays to check your lungs
  • CT, MRI, or PET scans to check if cancer has spread

Remember: Having tests and waiting for results can be stressful. Talking about how you feel and getting support from family, friends, and healthcare professionals can help.

Treating Testicular Cancer

Treatment for testicular cancer can be very successful. Surgery and chemotherapy are the 2 main types of treatment. Often people have both treatments.

Most young men with testicular cancer are cured, even if the cancer has spread.

Surgery

Surgery to remove the testicle (orchidectomy) is one of the main treatments. The surgeon makes a small cut through your lower abdomen and removes the testicle through this cut.

During the operation, the surgeon can put a false testicle into your scrotum so that you'll look the same as before.

After the Operation

You'll probably feel tired and need to take things easy for about a week. You can expect to make a quick recovery after this operation.

If one of your testicles is removed, you will still be able to have sex and make someone pregnant. Your other testicle should produce enough testosterone and sperm.

Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy is when you're given anti-cancer drugs to destroy cancer cells. You might be given chemotherapy:

  • After surgery to reduce the chance of cancer coming back
  • To destroy cancer cells that have spread
  • If the cancer comes back

Side Effects

Common temporary side effects include:

  • Hair loss
  • Tiredness
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Increased infection risk

Important: Chemotherapy can affect fertility. Your doctor will discuss sperm banking with you before treatment starts.

Life After Treatment

Being diagnosed with cancer can have a big impact on your life, even after you've finished treatment.

After Treatment

Once you finish your treatment, you'll have regular follow-up appointments at the hospital with blood tests and sometimes scans.

How Will I Feel?

You might find it embarrassing to talk about how the cancer or treatment has affected you. Try not to let this put you off talking about things - it can be really helpful to let someone know how you're feeling.

Sex and Fertility

Some cancers and cancer treatments can affect fertility, but your doctors will always consider this when planning treatment.

  • If only one testicle is removed, your fertility should not be affected
  • Chemotherapy may temporarily or permanently affect sperm production
  • Sperm banking before treatment can help preserve fertility

Support: If you're struggling with any aspect of life after treatment, don't hesitate to reach out to your healthcare team for support and guidance.
