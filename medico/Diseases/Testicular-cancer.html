Testicular Cancer Information

About Testicular Cancer Symptoms Diagnosis Treatment Causes & Risk Factors

About Testicular Cancer

Cancer of the testicle is one of the less common cancers. It tends to mostly affect men and anyone with testicles between 15 and 49 years of age.

The most common symptom is a painless lump or swelling in one of the testicles. It can be the size of a pea or it may be much larger.

Other symptoms can include:

  • A dull ache in the scrotum
  • A feeling of heaviness in the scrotum

Important: It's important to be aware of what feels normal for you. Get to know your body and speak to your GP if you notice any changes.

Types of Testicular Cancer

The most common type is 'germ cell testicular cancer', which accounts for around 95% of all cases. There are two main subtypes:

  • Seminomas - account for 50-55% of testicular cancers
  • Non-seminomas - include teratomas, embryonal carcinomas, and other types

Good news: Testicular cancer is one of the most treatable types of cancer, with excellent recovery rates when detected early.

Symptoms of Testicular Cancer

The most common symptom of testicular cancer is a lump or swelling in one of your testicles.

The lump or swelling can be about the size of a pea, but may be larger.

Note: Most lumps or swellings in the scrotum aren't in the testicle and aren't a sign of cancer. But they should never be ignored. Visit your GP as soon as you notice a lump or swelling in your scrotum.

Associated Symptoms

Testicular cancer can also cause other symptoms, including:

  • Dull ache or sharp pain in your testicles or scrotum
  • Feeling of heaviness in your scrotum
  • Change in texture or increase in firmness of a testicle
  • Noticeable difference between one testicle and the other

When Cancer Spreads

If testicular cancer spreads to other parts of the body, symptoms may include:

  • Persistent cough
  • Coughing up blood
  • Shortness of breath
  • Swelling in the neck
  • Lower back pain

Urgent: If you notice any unusual changes in your testicles, don't wait - speak to your GP immediately. Early detection saves lives.

Diagnosing Testicular Cancer

If your GP suspects testicular cancer, you'll be referred for further testing within 2 weeks.

Diagnostic Tests

The main diagnostic methods include:

  • Physical examination - including checking for lumps and abnormalities
  • Scrotal ultrasound - uses sound waves to create images of the testicles
  • Blood tests - to detect tumor markers like AFP and HCG
  • CT/MRI scans - to check if cancer has spread

Stages of Testicular Cancer

Testicular cancer is staged from 1 to 4:

  • Stage 1 - Cancer is contained within the testicle
  • Stage 2 - Cancer has spread to nearby lymph nodes
  • Stage 3 - Cancer has spread to lymph nodes in chest or neck
  • Stage 4 - Cancer has spread to lungs, liver, bones or brain

Remember: The only way to definitively confirm testicular cancer is through examination of tissue, which usually requires surgical removal of the affected testicle.

Treating Testicular Cancer

Testicular cancer is highly treatable, especially when caught early. Treatment options include:

Main Treatment Methods

  • Surgery (Orchidectomy) - removal of the affected testicle
  • Chemotherapy - using drugs to kill cancer cells
  • Radiotherapy - using radiation to destroy cancer cells
  • Surveillance - careful monitoring in early-stage cases

Sperm Banking

Before treatment begins, you may want to consider sperm banking:

  • Freezing sperm for future use
  • Important if treatment might affect fertility
  • Available at most NHS cancer centers

Life After Treatment

After treatment:

  • Most men can still father children with one testicle
  • Sexual function is usually unaffected
  • Testosterone replacement may be needed if both testicles are removed
  • Regular follow-ups are essential to monitor for recurrence

Excellent prognosis: The cure rate for testicular cancer is over 95% for all stages combined, making it one of the most curable cancers.

Causes & Risk Factors

While the exact causes aren't fully understood, several factors increase the risk of developing testicular cancer:

Main Risk Factors

  • Undescended testicles - the most significant risk factor
  • Family history - having a close relative with testicular cancer
  • Previous testicular cancer - higher risk in the other testicle
  • Age - most common in men aged 15-49
  • Ethnicity - more common in white men

Other Risk Factors

  • HIV or AIDS
  • Infertility
  • Smoking
  • Certain genetic conditions

Self-examination: Regular testicular self-examination can help with early detection. Get familiar with what's normal for you and report any changes to your doctor.

Important: Having risk factors doesn't mean you'll get testicular cancer, and many people with testicular cancer have no known risk factors.
