Bone Cancer in Teenagers & Young Adults

This information is specifically for teenagers and young adults dealing with bone cancer. There are different types of bone cancer, and most of this information applies to all types.

Note: If you're looking for information about bone cancer in people of all ages, please refer to our general bone cancer section.

Types of Bone Cancer

The two most common bone cancers affecting teenagers are:

  • Osteosarcoma - Most likely to affect bones in the leg, especially around the knee joint
  • Ewing sarcoma - Most common in the pelvis or leg bones, can sometimes start in soft tissue

Causes

The cause of bone cancer is unknown. Because it's more common in young people, doctors think it may be linked to changes that happen when bones are growing. Ongoing research continues to investigate possible causes.

Important: Remember that nothing you've done has caused the cancer. People often think a knock or injury might have caused bone cancer, but there's no evidence for this.

Signs and Symptoms

Bone cancer symptoms vary, and many can be mistaken for everyday aches and pains. Main symptoms include:

  • Pain or tenderness that doesn't go away and may worsen at night
  • Swelling around the affected area
  • Reduced movement or limping
  • Bones that break easily
  • Tiredness, fever, loss of appetite, or weight loss

When to see a doctor: If you have any of these symptoms, or are worried you might have a bone tumour, see your GP. They can arrange tests if needed. Remember - most people with these symptoms won't have bone cancer.

Understanding Your Bones

Knowing what your bones do might help you understand what bone cancer is and how it affects your body.

What Bones Do

Bones are like human scaffolding that help keep you upright. The human skeleton has more than 200 bones of different shapes and sizes. Bones do many important things:

  • Work with joints and connective tissue so you can move around
  • Protect parts of your body from injury
  • Store calcium
  • Contain bone marrow, which makes blood cells

Joints and Connective Tissue

Different types of connective tissue help bones move:

  • Ligaments - Tough cords that attach bones to each other
  • Cartilage - Slippery material that stops bones rubbing together
  • Muscles - Tough, stretchy tissue that provides strength
  • Tendons - Strong cords that attach muscles to bones

Tests for Bone Cancer

There are several tests you might have when visiting your GP or at the hospital to determine if you have bone cancer.

Visiting Your GP

If you have symptoms of bone cancer, your GP will talk to you about your symptoms, examine you, and usually arrange tests such as X-rays and blood tests.

Hospital Tests

At the hospital, doctors may arrange:

  • X-rays
  • Bone scans
  • Biopsies (taking small tissue samples)
  • MRI and/or CT scans

Further Tests

If bone cancer is diagnosed, you might need additional tests to check your overall health:

  • Blood tests
  • Heart health tests
  • Chest X-rays
  • Kidney function tests

Support: Having tests and waiting for results can be an anxious time. Talking about how you feel and getting support from family, friends, your specialist nurse or doctor can help.

Treating Bone Cancer

The three main treatments for bone cancer are chemotherapy, surgery, and radiotherapy. Most people have a combination of these treatments.

Treatment Planning

Your doctors will plan your treatment based on:

  • The type of bone cancer you have
  • Where the cancer is located
  • Whether it has spread
  • How fast-growing the cancer is

Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy uses anti-cancer drugs to destroy cancer cells. It's given in cycles with rest periods in between. The specific drugs used depend on whether you have Ewing sarcoma or osteosarcoma.

Surgery Options

  • Limb-sparing surgery - Removing the cancer while preserving the limb
  • Amputation - Removing the limb when necessary
  • Joint replacement - For cancers near joints

Radiotherapy

Radiotherapy uses high-energy X-rays to destroy cancer cells. It's mainly used for Ewing sarcoma and is generally given daily for about 5 weeks.

Recovery: After surgery, physiotherapy is crucial for recovery. It can feel like hard work but is really important for regaining movement and strength.

Life After Treatment

Life after bone cancer treatment involves adapting to changes and focusing on recovery and quality of life.

After Limb-Sparing Surgery

The long-term effects depend on which part of your body is affected and what kind of operation you had. Physiotherapy continues to be important for maintaining function.

After Amputation

After amputation, you'll be fitted with an artificial limb (prosthesis). Modern prostheses allow for many activities including walking, swimming, running, and sports.

Emotional Wellbeing

It's normal to experience various emotions after treatment. You might feel tearful, angry, or sad at times. Important support options include:

  • Your specialist nurse or key worker
  • Family and friends
  • Counselors
  • Other young people in similar situations

Follow-up Care

When you finish treatment, your doctor will discuss long-term risks and arrange regular follow-up appointments to monitor your health and recovery.

Remember: Getting used to changes in your body takes time. Be patient with yourself and don't hesitate to seek support when you need it.
