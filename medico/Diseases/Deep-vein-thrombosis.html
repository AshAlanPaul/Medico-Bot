About Deep Vein Thrombosis

Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is a blood clot that develops within a deep vein in the body, usually in the leg. Blood clots that develop in a vein are also known as venous thrombosis.

DVT usually occurs in a deep leg vein, a larger vein that runs through the muscles of the calf and thigh. It can also occur in the pelvis or abdomen.

DVT and pulmonary embolism together are known as venous thromboembolism (VTE).

Important: If you think you have deep vein thrombosis, seek urgent medical attention from your GP or call 111.

Symptoms of DVT

In some cases, there may be no symptoms of DVT. If symptoms do occur they can include:

  • Pain, swelling and tenderness in one of your legs (usually your calf or thigh)
  • A heavy ache in the affected area
  • Warm skin in the area of the clot
  • Red skin, particularly at the back of your leg below the knee

DVT usually (although not always) affects one leg. The pain may be worse when you bend your foot upward towards your knee.

Emergency Warning Signs

Phone 999 or go to A&E immediately if you have:

  • Pain and swelling of the leg, along with either breathlessness or chest pain

These could be signs of a pulmonary embolism, which is a medical emergency.

Causes and Risk Factors

Each year, DVT affects around 1 person in every 1,000 in the UK. Anyone can develop DVT, but it becomes more common over the age of 40.

Main risk factors include:

  • Having a history of DVT or pulmonary embolism
  • Having a family history of blood clots
  • Being inactive for long periods – such as after an operation or during a long journey
  • Blood vessel damage
  • Certain conditions that cause blood to clot more easily – such as cancer, heart disease, thrombophilia
  • Pregnancy
  • Being overweight or obese
  • Taking the combined contraceptive pill or hormone replacement therapy (HRT)

Note: The combined contraceptive pill and HRT both contain oestrogen, which causes the blood to clot more easily than normal.

Diagnosing DVT

See your GP as soon as possible if you think you may have DVT. They'll ask about your symptoms and medical history.

Diagnostic tests include:

D-dimer test

A specialized blood test that detects pieces of blood clot that have been broken down and are loose in your bloodstream.

Ultrasound scan

Used to detect clots in your veins. A Doppler ultrasound can also measure blood flow speed to identify blockages.

Venogram

Used if other tests can't confirm diagnosis. Contrast dye is injected into a vein and X-rays track its movement.

Treatment for DVT

Treatment for DVT usually involves taking anticoagulant medicines to reduce the blood's ability to clot and stop existing clots getting bigger.

Anticoagulant medications:

  • Heparin – usually prescribed first because it works immediately
  • Warfarin – often used after initial treatment to prevent recurrence
  • DOACs – Directly acting oral anticoagulants like rivaroxaban and apixaban

Additional treatments:

  • Compression stockings – worn daily to prevent complications
  • Exercise – regular walking once compression stockings are prescribed
  • Leg elevation – helps relieve pressure in veins
  • IVC filters – in some cases, to trap large clot fragments

Important: Always follow your doctor's instructions carefully when taking anticoagulant medications and attend all follow-up appointments.

Complications of DVT

Pulmonary Embolism

The most serious complication of DVT. It happens when a piece of blood clot breaks off and travels to your lungs, blocking a blood vessel. This can be fatal.

Post-thrombotic Syndrome

Affects around 20-40% of people with a history of DVT. Symptoms include:

  • Calf pain and swelling
  • Rash
  • Ulcers on the calf (in severe cases)

Note: About 1 in 10 people with an untreated DVT develops a severe pulmonary embolism.

Preventing DVT

Lifestyle measures:

  • Not smoking
  • Eating a healthy, balanced diet
  • Taking regular exercise
  • Maintaining a healthy weight
  • Staying well hydrated

When travelling:

  • Drink plenty of water
  • Avoid excessive alcohol
  • Perform simple leg exercises
  • Take regular walking breaks
  • Wear compression stockings if recommended

In hospital:

If you're admitted to hospital, your risk will be assessed and preventative measures may include anticoagulant medications and compression devices.

Remember: There's no evidence that taking aspirin reduces your risk of developing DVT. Always consult with healthcare professionals about prevention strategies.
