Coeliac Disease

About Coeliac Disease Symptoms Causes Diagnosis Treatment Complications

About Coeliac Disease

Coeliac disease is a common autoimmune condition where the immune system in a person's intestine (gut) reacts when they eat gluten. This causes chronic inflammation.

What is Gluten?

Gluten is a protein found in wheat, barley and rye.

Gluten is found in food and drink that contains wheat, barley and rye, such as:

  • Most types of bread
  • Pasta
  • Cakes and biscuits
  • Many breakfast cereals
  • Pastry
  • Certain types of sauces
  • Some ready meals
  • Most beers and lagers

How Common is Coeliac Disease?

Coeliac disease affects 1 person in every 100. Only about one third of them are ever diagnosed.

Coeliac disease can be diagnosed at any age, but is most commonly diagnosed between ages 40-60.

Important: Reported cases of coeliac disease are twice as high in women than men.

Symptoms of Coeliac Disease

Symptoms of coeliac disease vary from person to person and can range from mild to severe. Some people may have no symptoms at all.

Common Symptoms

  • Severe diarrhoea, excessive wind and/or constipation
  • Persistent gastrointestinal symptoms like nausea and vomiting
  • Recurrent stomach pain, cramping or bloating
  • Iron, vitamin B12 or folic acid deficiency
  • Anaemia and tiredness
  • Unexpected weight loss (but not in all cases)
  • Mouth ulcers
  • Skin rash (dermatitis herpetiformis)
  • Depression
  • Repeated miscarriages
  • Neurological problems like ataxia and peripheral neuropathy

If you have any of these symptoms, speak to your GP. You must continue eating gluten as part of your diet until you've been diagnosed.

Symptoms in Children

Children with coeliac disease might not grow at the expected rate, or be late to reach puberty.

Causes of Coeliac Disease

It's not known exactly why people develop coeliac disease or why some have mild symptoms while others have severe symptoms.

Pre-existing Conditions

Coeliac disease is more common in people with certain conditions:

  • Type 1 diabetes
  • Down's syndrome
  • Autoimmune thyroid disease
  • Close family members with coeliac disease

Genetics

Coeliac disease often runs in families. If you have a close relative with the condition, your chance of developing it is approximately 10%.

If you have an identical twin with coeliac disease, there's an 85% chance you'll also develop the condition.

Diagnosing Coeliac Disease

Coeliac disease can be diagnosed at any age, and both children and adults can show symptoms. Delayed diagnosis is common - it can take an average of 13 years to diagnose.

Blood Tests

Most people with coeliac disease have antibodies that show up in their blood. Blood tests are 95% accurate at diagnosing coeliac disease.

Important: Before Blood Test

You must eat gluten-containing foods in more than one meal every day for at least 6 weeks before the test for accurate results.

Who Should Have a Blood Test?

  • People with type 1 diabetes or autoimmune thyroid disease
  • People with dermatitis herpetiformis or IBS
  • People with anaemia without obvious cause
  • People with digestive symptoms like frequent diarrhoea or abdominal pain
  • Close relatives of people with coeliac disease

Biopsy

If blood tests are positive, a biopsy may be needed to confirm diagnosis. This involves taking a small sample of the intestine lining to examine for signs of coeliac disease.

Warning: Do not start a gluten-free diet until diagnosis is confirmed. This could affect test results.

Treating Coeliac Disease

The only treatment for confirmed coeliac disease is a lifelong gluten-free diet.

How Does a Gluten-Free Diet Help?

Following a gluten-free diet helps prevent gluten damaging the lining of the intestines and improves or removes symptoms.

If you continue to eat foods that contain gluten after diagnosis, your symptoms will return and could cause long-term damage to your health.

Dietary Support

After diagnosis, you'll be seen by a dietitian who will help you:

  • Ensure your diet is balanced and contains all necessary nutrients
  • Advise on adjusting your diet and lifestyle to be gluten-free
  • Provide guidance on gluten-free food alternatives
  • Offer information about gluten-free prescriptions

Treatment Timeline

Symptoms should improve within weeks of starting a gluten-free diet. However, it may take up to 2 years for the digestive system to heal completely.

Annual Health Checks

After starting treatment, you'll need annual coeliac disease health checks to monitor your condition and discuss any concerns.

Complications of Coeliac Disease

Complications only tend to affect people who continue to eat gluten, or those who had delayed diagnosis.

Possible Complications

  • Lactose intolerance: Temporary condition that usually disappears on a gluten-free diet
  • Nutritional deficiencies: Malabsorption can lead to anaemia and other deficiencies
  • Osteoporosis: Increased risk due to poor absorption of bone nutrients
  • Cancer: Slightly increased risk of lymphoma and bowel cancer if untreated

Osteoporosis prevention: If you have coeliac disease, make sure you're eating enough calcium and take vitamin D supplements to protect bone health.

Good news: Following a strict gluten-free diet significantly reduces the risk of all these complications.
