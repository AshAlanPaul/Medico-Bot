Fibromyalgia

Fibromyalgia is a long-term condition that causes pain all over the body.

Symptoms of Fibromyalgia

The main symptom of fibromyalgia is widespread pain that might feel like:

  • An ache
  • A burning sensation
  • A sharp stabbing pain
  • A mixture of these feelings

The pain is likely to be continuous, but it might be better or more severe at different times.

Other Common Symptoms

  • Increased sensitivity to sensations like touch, light, temperature, noise
  • Fatigue (extreme tiredness)
  • Muscle stiffness
  • Difficulty sleeping
  • Problems with mental processes ("fibro-fog") - memory and concentration issues
  • Headaches
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS)
  • Dizziness and clumsiness
  • Feeling too hot or too cold
  • Restless legs syndrome
  • Tingling, numbness, or burning sensations in hands and feet
  • Painful periods
  • Anxiety and depression

Speak to your GP or healthcare professional if: you think you might have fibromyalgia.

Causes of Fibromyalgia

It's not clear why some people develop fibromyalgia. The exact cause is unknown, but it's likely that many factors are involved.

Altered Pain Messages

Changes in the way your central nervous system (brain, nerves and spinal cord) sends and receives information to your body might cause fibromyalgia. Fibromyalgia is a type of chronic pain.

Genetics

Some people are more likely than others to develop fibromyalgia because of genes inherited from their parents.

Triggers

Fibromyalgia is often triggered by a stressful event, either physical or emotional. Possible triggers include:

  • Injury
  • Viral infection
  • Giving birth
  • Operation
  • Relationship breakdown
  • Being in an abusive relationship
  • Death of a loved one

Sometimes there isn't an obvious trigger.

Diagnosing Fibromyalgia

Diagnosing fibromyalgia can be difficult. There's no specific test to diagnose the condition, and symptoms can vary and resemble other conditions.

Diagnostic Process

  • Your GP will ask about how symptoms affect your daily life
  • Physical examination to check for signs of other conditions
  • Urine and blood tests to rule out other conditions
  • X-rays and other scans if needed

Diagnostic Criteria

For fibromyalgia to be diagnosed, certain criteria usually have to be met:

  • Pain in multiple areas of your body
  • Symptoms have stayed at similar level for at least 3 months
  • Symptoms can't be explained by any other reason
  • Feeling unrefreshed after sleep
  • Problems thinking or remembering (cognitive difficulties)
  • Fatigue

Common Co-existing Conditions

  • Depression
  • Anxiety
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS)
  • Osteoarthritis
  • Hypermobility spectrum disorder

Treating Fibromyalgia

Fibromyalgia is a long-term condition with no cure, but there are treatments to help relieve symptoms and make the condition easier to live with.

Exercise, Movement and Activity

  • Exercise and movement can help manage pain
  • Physiotherapists can develop tailored exercise programmes
  • Occupational therapy helps manage daily activities
  • Helps prevent loss of strength and increases fitness

Group Support

Pain management programmes run by specialists can provide support to develop coping skills and manage activity levels.

Talking Therapies

  • Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)
  • Acceptance and commitment therapy (ACT)
  • Help manage the stress of living with fibromyalgia

Medication

  • Medication can't treat fibromyalgia but may help reduce some symptoms
  • Antidepressants can help relieve pain and treat sleep problems
  • Exercise, CBT and physiotherapy are usually recommended first

Alternative Therapies

Some people try complementary treatments like acupuncture or massage, though scientific evidence for long-term benefits is limited.

Important: Always check with your GP or healthcare professional before trying complementary or herbal remedies, as they can react with other medications.
