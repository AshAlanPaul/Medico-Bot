Chronic Pancreatitis

About Chronic Pancreatitis Symptoms Causes Diagnosis Treatment Complications

About Chronic Pancreatitis

Chronic pancreatitis is a condition where the pancreas (a small organ located behind the stomach and below the ribcage) becomes permanently damaged from inflammation.

It's different from acute pancreatitis, where the inflammation is only short-term.

The most common symptom is repeated episodes of abdominal pain, which can be severe.

Medical advice: Always visit your GP if you're experiencing severe pain – it's a warning sign that something is wrong.

Who's Affected

Chronic pancreatitis can affect people of any age, but is most common in middle-aged men aged between 45 and 54.

Symptoms of Chronic Pancreatitis

The most common symptom is repeated episodes of abdominal pain that usually develops in the middle or left side of the abdomen and can sometimes travel along your back.

The pain has been described as a burning or shooting pain which comes and goes, but can last for several hours or days.

Advanced Symptoms

As the condition progresses, additional symptoms may develop:

  • Greasy, foul-smelling stools that are difficult to flush
  • Weight loss
  • Loss of appetite
  • Jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes)
  • Diabetes symptoms – feeling very thirsty, urinating frequently, feeling very tired
  • Ongoing nausea and vomiting

When to seek help: Visit your GP if you develop symptoms of jaundice or persistent vomiting, in addition to severe pain.

Causes of Chronic Pancreatitis

Alcohol Consumption

Long-term alcohol misuse is responsible for around 7 out of every 10 cases of chronic pancreatitis. Heavy drinking over many years can cause repeated damage to the pancreas.

Other Causes

  • Smoking – Can speed up the progress of the condition
  • Immune system problems – Autoimmune pancreatitis where the immune system attacks the pancreas
  • Genetic factors – Inherited gene mutations that disrupt pancreatic function
  • Other factors – Injury to pancreas, blocked pancreatic ducts, radiotherapy

In up to 3 out of 10 people with the condition, the cause can't be identified – known as 'idiopathic' chronic pancreatitis.

Diagnosing Chronic Pancreatitis

Chronic pancreatitis can usually only be diagnosed by carrying out scans of the pancreas.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Stool sample analysis
  • Ultrasound scan – Sound waves to create pancreas image
  • CT scan – Detailed three-dimensional imaging
  • Endoscopic ultrasonography – Camera and ultrasound via endoscope
  • MRCP – Special MRI to examine pancreas and bile ducts
  • Biopsy – To rule out pancreatic cancer if needed

Treating Chronic Pancreatitis

Lifestyle Changes

Stop drinking alcohol: The most important step to prevent further damage and reduce pain.

Stop smoking: Smoking speeds up the progress of chronic pancreatitis.

Dietary changes: Low-fat, high-protein, high-calorie diet with fat-soluble vitamin supplements may be recommended.

Medications

  • Enzyme supplements – To help digestion
  • Pain relief – Starting with mild painkillers, progressing to stronger options if needed
  • Steroid medication – For autoimmune pancreatitis

Surgical Options

Surgery may be considered for severe pain that doesn't respond to medication:

  • Endoscopic surgery to remove stones
  • Pancreas resection (removing damaged parts)
  • Total pancreatectomy (removing entire pancreas) in severe cases

Important: People who don't smoke and avoid alcohol tend to experience less pain and live longer than those who continue these habits after diagnosis.

Complications of Chronic Pancreatitis

Psychological Effects

Living with chronic pain can cause stress, anxiety and depression. Support and treatment are available.

Diabetes

Around a third of people with chronic pancreatitis develop diabetes, usually many years after diagnosis, when the pancreas can no longer produce insulin.

Pseudocysts

Fluid-filled sacs that develop on the pancreas surface, affecting about 1 in 10 people with the condition.

Pancreatic Cancer

Chronic pancreatitis slightly increases the risk of developing pancreatic cancer, though the risk remains relatively small.

Support available: If you're experiencing psychological difficulties, contact your GP. Effective treatments are available for stress, anxiety and depression.
