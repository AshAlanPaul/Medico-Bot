Anorexia Nervosa

Anorexia nervosa is an eating disorder. It's a serious, potentially life-threatening, mental health condition.

Important: People with anorexia are very anxious about their weight and body shape. They try to keep their weight as low as possible by strictly controlling what they eat.

Many people with anorexia will also:

  • Exercise excessively
  • Use laxatives
  • Vomit after eating
  • Take stimulant drugs

Symptoms of Anorexia

Main Symptoms

  • Fear of 'being fat' or gaining weight
  • Problems with self-esteem and body image regarding food and weight
  • Restricting food intake severely
  • Keeping body weight low to unhealthy levels

Physical Symptoms

Due to insufficient nutrition, physical symptoms may include:

  • Fine downy hair (lanugo) growing on the body
  • More hair on the face
  • Sparse and thin pubic hair
  • Slow or irregular heartbeat
  • Abdominal pain, bloating, or constipation
  • Swelling in feet, hands, or face
  • Extreme tiredness and low blood pressure
  • Feeling cold or having low body temperature
  • Dizziness and light-headedness

In children: Anorexia can delay puberty and growth spurts. Children may be shorter than peers and gain less weight than expected during puberty.

Getting Help

Speak to your GP if you think you have an eating problem or anorexia. Early intervention is crucial for recovery.

Helping Someone Else

If someone close to you shows signs of anorexia:

  • Talk to them about how they feel
  • Encourage them to seek help
  • Avoid pressure or criticism
  • Offer to go with them to see their GP
  • Seek advice from healthcare professionals
  • Consider support groups for loved ones

Diagnosing Anorexia

Your GP will ask questions about your weight and eating habits:

  • Recent weight loss patterns
  • Feelings about weight and body image
  • Regular vomiting habits
  • Menstrual cycle changes
  • Physical examinations (weight, pulse, blood pressure)
  • Blood tests

Referral to specialist: If anorexia is suspected, your GP will refer you to an eating disorder specialist for detailed assessment.

Treatment for Anorexia

Treatment usually involves a combination of:

  • Talking therapy - to address underlying psychological issues
  • Supervised weight gain - to restore healthy weight safely

Early treatment is crucial to reduce the risk of serious complications. Treatment approaches differ for adults and those under 18.

Complications of Anorexia

Long-term anorexia can lead to severe, sometimes permanent health problems.

Increased Risks

  • Poor circulation and cardiovascular problems
  • Heart conditions (coronary heart disease, irregular heartbeat)
  • Health problems from malnutrition
  • Mineral imbalances causing weakness, dehydration, and confusion

Specific Health Conditions

  • Low blood pressure and anemia
  • Dental problems from vomiting
  • Low blood sugar levels
  • Kidney and liver damage
  • Heart failure
  • Osteoporosis and muscle weakness
  • Loss of sex drive and impotence
  • Permanent constipation from laxative misuse

Anorexia and Pregnancy

Anorexia can affect fertility and pregnancy:

  • Irregular or stopped periods don't mean infertility
  • Continue using birth control if not planning pregnancy
  • Increased risk of miscarriage, premature birth, and low birth weight
  • Extra care needed during pregnancy even after recovery
