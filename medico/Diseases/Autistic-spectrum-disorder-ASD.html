About Autism

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD), usually called autism, is something you're born with. Autism means that the way you think about and experience the world is different to most people. This means you can behave differently to most people, and have different strengths and difficulties.

Autism is highly variable – the word 'spectrum' refers to how autism is experienced differently by different people. Autism is considered a spectrum because it's different for every autistic person – some autistic people might need more support than others to live the lives they want to lead.

Talking About Autism

Many people who have been diagnosed with autism prefer using the term 'autistic' to describe themselves – this is known as identity-first language. They consider autism to be part of their identity, not a condition to be treated.

How Common is Autism?

At least 1 in 100 people in Scotland are autistic. Currently, between 1 in 3 and 1 in 4 people diagnosed with autism are assigned female at birth.

Note: Autism is always present from birth, but it might not be recognised or diagnosed until adulthood. Early intervention can be helpful for autistic children.

Characteristics of Autism

Every autistic person is different and has different experiences. However, there are some characteristics that are common in autistic people.

Common Characteristics

  • Using language and talking differently to most people
  • Using facial expressions, tone of voice, and gestures differently
  • Difficulty making and maintaining friendships
  • Being good at seeing patterns or solutions
  • Having set ways of doing things
  • Being very good at understanding structured systems
  • Having good attention to detail
  • Having passionate interests
  • Different sensory experiences

Autism and Gender

At the moment, men and those assigned male at birth are diagnosed with autism more often than women and those assigned female at birth. Women and those assigned female at birth can often show characteristics in slightly different ways and are more likely to 'mask' autism characteristics.

Environment matters: Some environments can be very difficult for autistic people. When autistic people are in an environment with people who understand their individual needs, they're likely to feel more relaxed and perform better.

Diagnosing Autism

The signs of autism can be different depending on the person and how old they are. Because autism is present from birth, it can usually be diagnosed in childhood, but it may only be recognised later in life.

Signs in Children

Babies and Toddlers ▼
  • Start talking later than most children
  • Seem less aware of others around them
  • Make repetitive movements when excited or upset
  • May not smile back or point to show interest
Children Aged 4 and Up ▼
  • Speak differently to most children
  • Struggle to keep conversations going
  • Find it difficult to make and keep friends
  • Struggle with changes to routine

Signs in Teenagers and Adults

As people age and experience different environments, they might notice different signs of autism. Many people are diagnosed as teenagers or adults based on noticing that they think and behave differently from most other people.

Important: To be diagnosed with autism, a person has to have a lot of autistic traits from birth, and those traits need to have a big effect on their life.

Living with Autism

Like everyone else, autistic people can be happy and healthy in the right environment. However, the right environment for an autistic person can be different to the right environment for a non-autistic person.

Therapies and Support

There's no cure for autism, and most autistic people wouldn't want to be 'cured'. However, many autistic people can benefit from support in learning skills to overcome some of the difficulties they experience.

Common Challenges

  • Meltdowns and shutdowns - responses to being overwhelmed
  • Challenging behaviour - often a result of distress or frustration
  • Sensory sensitivities - strong reactions to certain environments

Creating the Right Environment

Autism is covered by the Equality Act (2010), which means that schools and employers are required to make "reasonable adjustments" to ensure autistic people are comfortable in their environment.

Supportive environments include: quiet spaces, clear communication, consistent routines, and understanding of individual needs.

Facts and Myths About Autism

What Does NOT Cause Autism

  • Bad parenting
  • Vaccines, or any ingredients in vaccines
  • Trauma or distress at a young age
  • Diet
  • Infections

Fake Treatments to Avoid

There's no cure for autism, but many people incorrectly believe there are ways to cure it. Some fake treatments are dangerous and all are unhelpful.

Warning: Avoid any treatment that aims to 'train out' autistic behaviours or that claims to 'cure' autism. These can be harmful and are not supported by scientific evidence.

How to Spot Fake Treatments

  • Claim to 'cure' autism or help people 'recover'
  • Work quickly or instantly
  • Can be done at home without training
  • Claim doctors don't want you to know about them
  • Charge a lot of money

Always consult healthcare professionals before trying any treatment for autism. Your GP or specialist can provide information about safe, effective support options.
