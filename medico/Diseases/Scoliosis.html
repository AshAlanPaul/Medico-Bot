About Scoliosis

Scoliosis is the abnormal twisting and curvature of the spine. It is usually first noticed by a change in appearance of the back.

Typical Signs Include:

  • A visibly curved spine
  • One shoulder being higher than the other
  • One shoulder or hip being more prominent than the other
  • Clothes not hanging properly
  • A prominent ribcage
  • A difference in leg lengths

Back pain is common in adults with scoliosis. Young people with scoliosis may also experience some discomfort but it's less likely to be severe.

When to get professional help: If you or your child have signs of scoliosis, make an appointment to see your GP. They can examine your back and can refer you for an X-ray for confirmation.

Emergency warning: Rarely scoliosis can cause cauda equina syndrome. If you experience loss of feeling/pins and needles between your inner thighs or genitals, numbness around your back passage, or difficulty controlling urine or bowel movements, seek immediate medical attention.

What Causes Scoliosis?

In around eight out of every 10 cases, a cause for scoliosis is not found. This is known as idiopathic scoliosis.

Who is Affected

It can develop at any age, but is most common in children aged 10-15. In the UK, around three or four in every 1,000 children need treatment for scoliosis. It's more common in females than males.

Causes of Scoliosis

In most cases, the cause of scoliosis is unknown and it cannot usually be prevented. It is not thought to be linked with things such as bad posture, exercise or diet.

If the cause of scoliosis is unknown, it is called idiopathic scoliosis. About eight out of every 10 cases of scoliosis are idiopathic.

Known Causes

In a small number of cases, a cause is identified.

Other Health Conditions

  • Cerebral palsy – a condition that affects the brain and nerves
  • Muscular dystrophy – a genetic condition that causes muscle weakness
  • Neurofibromatosis – a genetic condition that causes benign tumors

Birth Defects

In rare cases, babies can be born with scoliosis. This is known as congenital scoliosis, caused by abnormal spine development in the womb.

Long-term Damage

In adults, scoliosis can sometimes be caused by gradual deterioration to the parts of the spine, known as degenerative scoliosis.

Diagnosing Scoliosis

Scoliosis can usually be diagnosed after a physical examination of the spine, ribs, hips and shoulders.

You may be asked to bend forward to see if any areas are particularly prominent. For example, one of your shoulders may be higher than the other or there may be a bulge in your back.

Scans

The orthopaedic specialist will take an X-ray to confirm the diagnosis of scoliosis. The X-ray images will also help determine the shape, direction, location and angle of the curve.

In some cases, scans such as MRI or CT scans may also be recommended.

Treating Scoliosis in Children

If your child has scoliosis, their treatment will depend on their age and how severe it is.

Main Treatment Options:

  • Observation – Monitoring the curve over time
  • Casting – For very young children
  • Bracing – To prevent curve progression
  • Surgery – For severe cases

Observation

Treatment is not always necessary for very young children because their condition often corrects itself as they grow.

Bracing

If the curve is getting worse, your specialist may recommend a back brace while the child is growing. A brace cannot cure scoliosis but it may stop the curve from getting worse.

Surgery

For younger children, growing rods may be inserted. For teenagers and young adults whose spine has stopped growing, spinal fusion surgery may be performed.

Surgery risks: Spinal fusion surgery carries risks including bleeding, infection, implant failure, and rarely, nerve damage. Discuss these thoroughly with your surgeon.

Treating Scoliosis in Adults

Back pain is one of the main problems caused by scoliosis in adults, so treatment is mainly aimed at pain relief.

Medication

Painkilling medication is usually recommended. Over-the-counter painkillers are often recommended initially, with stronger options available if needed.

Exercise

General strengthening and stretching exercises can improve posture and flexibility, and may help control back pain. Some people may benefit from physiotherapy.

Surgery

Surgery for adults is usually only recommended if the curve is severe, getting worse, causing significant pain, or compressing nerves.

Important: Surgery is a major operation with significant risks and a long recovery period. It's generally better at relieving leg pain than back pain.

Possible Complications of Scoliosis

Physical complications of scoliosis are rare, although serious problems can develop if it's left untreated.

Emotional Issues

Having a visibly curved spine or wearing a back brace may cause problems related to body image, self-esteem and overall quality of life, particularly for children and teenagers.

Lung and Heart Problems

In severe cases, the ribcage can be pushed against the heart and lungs, causing breathing problems and making it difficult for the heart to pump blood around the body.

Nerve Compression

In some cases, the bones in the spine compress nearby nerves, which can cause back and leg pain, numbness or weakness in the legs.

Support: Contact support groups like Scoliosis Association UK for information and support. Talking with others who have scoliosis can help improve confidence.
