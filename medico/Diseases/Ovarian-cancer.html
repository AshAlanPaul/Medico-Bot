About Ovarian Cancer

Ovarian cancer is most common in women and anyone with ovaries who has been through the menopause (usually over the age of 50). It can affect women and anyone with ovaries of any age.

As the symptoms of ovarian cancer can be similar to those of other conditions, it can be difficult to recognise. However, there are early symptoms to look out for, such as persistent bloating, pain in the pelvis and lower stomach, and difficulty eating.

It's important to speak to your GP if you experience these symptoms, particularly over a long period of time.

Understanding Ovarian Cancer

The ovaries are a pair of small organs in the female reproductive system that contain and release an egg once a month. This is known as ovulation.

Different types of ovarian cancer affect different parts of the ovaries. Epithelial ovarian cancer, which affects the surface layers of the ovary, is the most common type.

Important: A cervical screening test (smear test) cannot detect ovarian cancer. Speak to your GP if you have persistent symptoms.

Symptoms of Ovarian Cancer

The symptoms of ovarian cancer can be difficult to recognise, particularly in its early stages. This is because they are often the same as symptoms of other less serious conditions, such as irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) or pre-menstrual syndrome (PMS).

Main Symptoms

Three main symptoms are more frequent:

  • Increased abdominal size and persistent bloating (not bloating that comes and goes)
  • Persistent pelvic and abdominal pain
  • Difficulty eating and feeling full quickly, or feeling nauseous

Other Possible Symptoms

  • Back pain
  • Needing to pass urine more frequently than usual
  • Pain during sex
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Fatigue
  • Changes in bowel habits

When to see your GP: Speak to your GP if you have these symptoms regularly (on most days for 3 weeks or more). If you've already seen your GP and the symptoms continue or get worse, go back and explain this.

Causes of Ovarian Cancer

Several possible causes of ovarian cancer have been identified, along with risks that may make developing the condition more likely.

Increased Risk Factors

  • Age: Risk increases with age, most cases occur after menopause
  • Family history: Having close relatives with ovarian or breast cancer
  • Genetic factors: Inherited faulty genes like BRCA1 and BRCA2
  • Ovulation: Each ovulation causes damage that needs repair
  • Hormone replacement therapy (HRT): Small increased risk
  • Endometriosis: May increase risk
  • Obesity: Being overweight or obese

Genetic Risk

Only about 1 in 10 cases of ovarian cancer are thought to be caused by a faulty gene. You may be at high risk if you have multiple relatives with ovarian or breast cancer, especially if they were diagnosed at a young age.

Diagnosing Ovarian Cancer

Speak to your GP as soon as possible if you have any symptoms of ovarian cancer. Early diagnosis improves treatment outcomes.

Initial Assessment

Your GP will:

  • Gently feel your abdomen
  • Ask about your symptoms and family history
  • May carry out an internal examination
  • May take a blood sample or refer you for an ultrasound scan

Diagnostic Tests

  • Blood test (CA125): Checks for a protein that may indicate ovarian cancer
  • Ultrasound scan: Creates images of your ovaries to check for abnormalities
  • CT or MRI scans: Provide detailed images to see if cancer has spread
  • Laparoscopy: Uses a camera to examine the ovaries directly
  • Biopsy: Taking a small tissue sample for testing

Staging and Grading

If cancer is diagnosed, it will be staged (1-4) to show how far it has spread and graded (low, moderate, high) to show how abnormal the cells look under a microscope.

Treating Ovarian Cancer

Treatment for ovarian cancer usually involves a combination of surgery and chemotherapy.

Surgery

Most people with ovarian cancer will have surgery, which may involve:

  • Removing both ovaries and fallopian tubes
  • Removing the womb (hysterectomy)
  • Removing the omentum (fatty tissue in the abdomen)
  • Removing lymph nodes and other affected tissue

Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy uses anti-cancer medication to kill cancer cells:

  • Often given after surgery to kill remaining cancer cells
  • Sometimes given before surgery to shrink tumors
  • Usually involves 6 cycles of treatment
  • Common side effects include nausea, fatigue, and hair loss

Other Treatments

  • Radiotherapy: Rarely used, mainly for specific circumstances
  • Clinical trials: Access to new treatments and medications
  • Psychological support: Counseling and support services

Recovery: Surgery recovery takes 6-12 weeks. Chemotherapy can cause fatigue - don't hesitate to ask for practical help from family and friends.

Living With Ovarian Cancer

Being diagnosed with ovarian cancer can affect daily life in many ways, but support is available.

Recovery and Follow-up

  • Regular check-ups every 2-3 months after treatment
  • Time off work (4-12 weeks after surgery)
  • Gradual return to normal activities
  • Managing menopausal symptoms if ovaries are removed

Relationships and Sex

Cancer treatment can affect relationships and sex life:

  • Early menopause if ovaries are removed
  • Vaginal dryness and loss of sexual desire
  • Fatigue affecting intimacy
  • Emotional impact on relationships

Financial Support

If you can't work due to cancer, you may be entitled to:

  • Statutory sick pay
  • Employment and support allowance
  • Carer's allowance for family members
  • Other benefits based on circumstances

Support available: Don't hesitate to ask for help from healthcare professionals, support groups, or counseling services. You don't have to face cancer alone.

Preventing Ovarian Cancer

While there's no guaranteed way to prevent ovarian cancer, certain factors may help reduce your risk.

Reducing Ovulation

Anything that stops ovulation can help minimize ovarian cancer risk:

  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding
  • Contraceptive pill use
  • Hysterectomy with ovary removal

Healthy Lifestyle

  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Regular exercise
  • Balanced, low-fat diet
  • Avoid smoking

Screening for High-Risk Individuals

Screening may be available if you're at high risk due to:

  • Strong family history of ovarian/breast cancer
  • Inherited faulty genes (BRCA1/BRCA2)
  • Screening typically starts at age 35 or 5 years before youngest relative's diagnosis

Remember: There's currently no reliable screening test for ovarian cancer for the general population. Being aware of symptoms and seeking medical advice promptly is crucial.
