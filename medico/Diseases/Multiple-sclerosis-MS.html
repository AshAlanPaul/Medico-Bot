About Multiple Sclerosis

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a condition that affects the brain and/or spinal cord. It can cause symptoms like problems with vision, arm or leg movement, sensation or balance.

It's a lifelong condition that can sometimes cause serious disability. In many cases, it's possible to treat symptoms. Average life expectancy is slightly reduced for people with MS.

It's often diagnosed in people in their 20s and 30s, although it can develop at any age. It's about two to three times more common in women than men.

Key Information: MS is an autoimmune condition where the immune system attacks the protective covering of nerves, disrupting communication between the brain and the rest of the body.

Symptoms of Multiple Sclerosis

Multiple sclerosis (MS) can cause a wide range of symptoms and can affect any part of the body. It affects everyone differently.

The symptoms are unpredictable. Some people's symptoms develop and worsen over time, while for others, they come and go. Periods when symptoms get worse are known as relapses. Periods when symptoms improve or disappear are known as remissions.

Common Symptoms

  • Muscle spasms, stiffness and weakness
  • Vision problems - temporary vision loss, colour blindness, eye pain
  • Abnormal sensations - persistent numbness or tingling
  • Mobility problems - clumsiness, balance issues, tremors
  • Pain - both neuropathic and musculoskeletal pain
  • Bladder and bowel problems
  • Sexual problems
  • Speech and swallowing difficulties
  • Fatigue - overwhelming sense of exhaustion
  • Mental health issues - depression, anxiety
  • Problems with thinking, learning and planning

When to see a doctor: Speak to your GP if you think you have symptoms of MS. The symptoms can be similar to several other conditions, so they're not necessarily caused by MS.

What Causes Multiple Sclerosis?

Exactly why someone develops multiple sclerosis (MS) isn't known. It's not caused by anything you've done and it's not clear if you can prevent it.

It's likely to be partly caused by genes you inherit from your parents and partly by outside factors that may trigger the condition.

Potential Risk Factors

  • Genetics - MS isn't directly inherited, but having a relative with MS increases your risk
  • Lack of sunlight and vitamin D - MS is more common in countries far from the equator
  • Smoking - smokers are about twice as likely to develop MS
  • Viral infections - particularly Epstein-Barr virus (glandular fever)

Understanding MS: MS is an autoimmune condition where the immune system mistakes the myelin sheath (protective covering of nerves) for a foreign substance and attacks it.

Diagnosing Multiple Sclerosis

It can be hard to tell if you have multiple sclerosis (MS) because some of the symptoms are like other conditions. You may need to rule out other possible causes of your symptoms first.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Neurological examination
  • Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scan
  • Lumbar puncture
  • Blood tests - to rule out other conditions

Types of Multiple Sclerosis

Once diagnosed, your neurologist will identify which type of MS you have:

  • Relapsing remitting MS - periods of symptoms (relapses) followed by improvement (remissions)
  • Primary progressive MS - symptoms get steadily worse over time
  • Secondary progressive MS - starts as relapsing remitting but later becomes progressive

Diagnostic Process: Diagnosis typically involves a combination of neurological examination, MRI scans, and sometimes lumbar puncture to confirm MS and determine the type.

Treating Multiple Sclerosis

Disease-Modifying Therapies

Although there isn't a cure for MS, there are medicines that can help to reduce the number and severity of relapses in some people. These are called disease-modifying therapies.

Treating Relapses

Treatment for a relapse might involve a five-day course of steroid tablets taken at home. Steroids can help speed up your recovery from a relapse.

Symptom Management

Various treatments are available to manage specific MS symptoms:

  • Fatigue - exercise, healthy sleep patterns, energy-saving techniques
  • Muscle spasms and stiffness - physiotherapy, medications like baclofen
  • Pain - painkillers, neuropathic pain medications
  • Mobility problems - exercise programmes, mobility aids
  • Bladder and bowel problems - medications, catheterisation if needed
  • Emotional problems - antidepressants, therapy like CBT
  • Speech and swallowing difficulties - speech and language therapy

Emergency: Phone your specialist MS nurse or GP if you think you're having a relapse.

Living with Multiple Sclerosis

Coming to terms with a long-term condition like MS can put a strain on you, your family and your friends. Be honest about how you feel and let your family and friends know what they can do to help.

Pregnancy and Family Planning

Being diagnosed with MS shouldn't affect your ability to have children. Women with MS can have a normal pregnancy, deliver a healthy baby and breastfeed.

Financial Support

If you have to stop work or work part-time because of your MS, you may find it hard to cope financially. Various forms of financial support may be available.

Driving

If you have MS, you must tell the Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency (DVLA) and inform your insurance company.

Care and Support

If you find it difficult to look after yourself, your local authority may be able to provide you with some help. Ask for a care and support needs assessment.

Support Available: There are many resources and support services available for people living with MS and their families. Don't hesitate to ask for help from your healthcare team.
