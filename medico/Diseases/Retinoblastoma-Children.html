Retinoblastoma in Children

Retinoblastoma is an uncommon eye cancer that usually affects children under 5, although it can affect children of any age. Treatment is very effective and nearly all children with retinoblastoma are cured.

More children than ever are surviving childhood cancer. There are new and better drugs and treatments, and we can now also work to reduce the after-effects of having had cancer in the past.

Note: Retinoblastoma develops in the light-sensitive lining of the eye (called the retina).

Two Forms of Retinoblastoma:

  • Heritable form – this is genetic or inherited; there are often tumours in both eyes (bilateral) or sometimes only in 1 eye
  • Non-heritable form – this is not passed on in the family; there's 1 tumour in only 1 eye (unilateral)

Encouraging: It's devastating to hear that your child has cancer, but there are many healthcare professionals and support organizations to help you through this difficult time.

Causes of Retinoblastoma

The heritable form of retinoblastoma, which accounts for about 2 in every 5 cases, is caused by a genetic abnormality. This means that an abnormal gene allows the tumour to develop.

This abnormal gene may either be inherited from a parent or happen for the first time at an early stage of development in the womb.

Genetic Support: Genetic counselling and support is available for families in which a member has retinoblastoma. Children born into families with a history of retinoblastoma are offered blood testing and regular screening.

The genetic abnormality in the heritable form of retinoblastoma is now well-understood. But we don't know what causes non-heritable retinoblastoma.

Signs and Symptoms

Some children with a family history of retinoblastoma are picked up by screening before they have any symptoms.

Common Signs Include:

  • White pupil (leukocoria) – a white pupil that does not reflect light, often noticed in flash photography
  • Squint – misalignment of the eyes
  • Painful red eye – if the tumour is large
  • Poor vision – in the affected eye

Important: If you notice a white reflection in your child's eye in photographs, especially with flash, consult a doctor promptly.

Diagnosing Retinoblastoma

Tests are likely to involve an examination under anaesthetic (EUA) where an eye specialist (ophthalmologist) examines your child's eye while they're asleep.

Unlike nearly all other types of cancer, retinoblastomas can be diagnosed just by their appearance. So it's not usually necessary to take a sample of tissue (a biopsy).

Diagnostic Tests May Include:

  • Examination under anaesthetic (EUA) – detailed eye examination while the child is asleep
  • Ultrasound scan – uses sound waves to examine the eye and surrounding area
  • MRI scan – detailed imaging of the eye and head
  • Genetic blood testing – to check for the Rb gene

Staging

The stage of cancer describes its size and whether it has spread from where it first started.

Intraocular Retinoblastoma +

There is cancer in 1 or both eyes, but it has not spread to other parts of the eye or the tissues surrounding the eye. This stage may be sub-divided into 5 grades (A–E), depending on the size and position of the cancer and on whether there's any damage to the eye.

Extraocular Retinoblastoma +

The cancer has spread outside the eye into surrounding tissue, or to other parts of the body.

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on the number, position and size of the tumours in the eye. The aim of treatment is to first get rid of the cancer, and secondly to try to keep the sight in the eye.

Treatment for Smaller Tumors:

  • Cryotherapy – freezing the tumors
  • Laser therapy – using heat to destroy tumors
  • Radioactive plaque – small radioactive disc placed over the tumor
  • Thermotherapy – heat treatment, often combined with other therapies

Treatment for Larger Tumors:

  • Chemotherapy – anti-cancer drugs to destroy cancer cells
  • Surgery – removal of the eye if the tumor is very large and vision is lost
  • Radiotherapy – high-energy rays to destroy cancer cells

Treatment Success: At least 9 out of every 10 children with retinoblastoma are cured with proper treatment.

Follow-up Care and Support

Following treatment, the eye specialist will frequently examine your child's eye under anaesthetic to check that the retina is healthy, the cancer has not come back, and no new tumors have developed.

Long-term Follow-up:

  • Regular examinations in pediatric oncology clinic
  • Genetic counselling for children with heritable form
  • Close monitoring into adulthood for potential other cancers
  • Encouragement of healthy lifestyle to reduce cancer risk

Emotional Support: As a parent, learning your child has cancer is one of the most difficult situations. It's normal to experience fear, guilt, sadness, anger and uncertainty. Support is available through healthcare teams and organizations.

Hope: With modern treatments and ongoing research, the outlook for children with retinoblastoma continues to improve, with excellent survival rates and quality of life.
