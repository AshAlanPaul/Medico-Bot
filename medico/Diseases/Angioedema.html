Angioedema

Angioedema is the swelling of the deeper layers of the skin, caused by a build-up of fluid.

The symptoms of angioedema can affect any part of the body, but swelling usually affects the:

  • Eyes
  • Lips
  • Genitals
  • Hands
  • Feet

Many people with angioedema also experience urticaria (hives) - a raised, red and itchy rash that appears on the skin.

Symptoms of Angioedema

Swollen Skin

The swelling is caused by a collection of fluid in the deep layers of the skin. It most often affects:

  • Hands, feet, eyes, lips, or genitals
  • Appears suddenly and lasts 1-3 days
  • May cause prickling sensation before swelling
  • Usually not itchy (unlike hives)

Other Symptoms

  • Hot or painful sensation in swollen areas
  • Swelling of throat and tongue (difficulty breathing)
  • Swelling of the eyes affecting vision
  • Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea
  • Bladder problems and difficulty urinating

Causes of Angioedema

Allergic Angioedema

Caused by immune system mistakenly attacking harmless substances. Common triggers:

  • Foods: nuts, shellfish, milk, eggs
  • Medications: penicillin, aspirin, NSAIDs
  • Insect bites and stings
  • Latex products

Idiopathic Angioedema

No identifiable cause. Possible triggers include:

  • Anxiety or stress
  • Minor infections
  • Hot or cold temperatures
  • Exercise

Drug-induced Angioedema

Most commonly caused by ACE inhibitors for high blood pressure. Other medications include:

  • Bupropion (smoking cessation)
  • Antidepressants (SSRIs)
  • Painkillers (NSAIDs, COX-II inhibitors)
  • Statins, proton pump inhibitors

Hereditary Angioedema

Caused by genetic mutation affecting C1-inh protein production. Triggers include:

  • Trauma, surgery, or infection
  • Oral contraceptive pill
  • Pregnancy

Diagnosing Angioedema

Diagnosis involves examining affected skin and discussing symptoms. Further tests may include:

Allergy Testing

  • Skin prick tests
  • Blood tests for immune reactions
  • Food and exposure diaries

Drug-induced Diagnosis

Withdrawing suspected medication under medical supervision to see if symptoms resolve.

Hereditary Angioedema

Blood test to check C1-inh protein levels. Managed by specialist immunology clinics.

Idiopathic Angioedema

Diagnosis of exclusion after all other causes have been ruled out.
