Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD)

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) is an anxiety disorder caused by very stressful, frightening or distressing events.

Someone with PTSD often relives the traumatic event through nightmares and flashbacks, and may experience feelings of isolation, irritability and guilt.

They may also have problems sleeping, such as insomnia, and find concentrating difficult.

Causes of PTSD

The type of events that can cause PTSD include:

  • Serious road accidents
  • Violent personal assaults, such as sexual assault, mugging or robbery
  • Prolonged sexual abuse, violence or severe neglect
  • Witnessing violent deaths
  • Military combat
  • Being held hostage
  • Terrorist attacks
  • Natural disasters, such as severe floods, earthquakes or tsunamis

When to seek help: It's normal to experience upsetting thoughts after trauma, but if symptoms persist beyond 4 weeks, consult your GP.

Symptoms of PTSD

The symptoms of PTSD can have a significant impact on your day-to-day life and generally fall into these categories:

Re-experiencing

  • Flashbacks
  • Nightmares
  • Repetitive and distressing images or sensations
  • Physical sensations – such as pain, sweating, nausea or trembling

Avoidance and Emotional Numbing

  • Avoiding people or places that remind you of the trauma
  • Avoiding talking about your experience
  • Emotional numbing and feeling disconnected
  • Isolation and withdrawal from activities

Hyperarousal (Feeling 'On Edge')

  • Irritability
  • Angry outbursts
  • Sleeping problems (insomnia)
  • Difficulty concentrating

Children with PTSD may show symptoms like bedwetting, separation anxiety, or re-enacting trauma through play.

Causes of PTSD

PTSD develops in about 1 in 3 people who experience severe trauma. Certain factors increase susceptibility:

  • Previous depression or anxiety
  • Lack of support from family or friends
  • Genetic factors and family history
  • Previous traumatic experiences

Biological Factors

People with PTSD have been found to have:

  • Abnormal levels of stress hormones
  • Changes in brain structure and function
  • Smaller hippocampus (memory and emotion center)

Treatment for PTSD

The main treatments for PTSD are psychotherapy and medication. Treatment can be successful even years after the traumatic event.

Psychotherapy

  • Trauma-focused CBT – 8-12 weekly sessions to process traumatic memories
  • EMDR – Eye movement desensitization and reprocessing
  • Group therapy – Sharing experiences with others who have PTSD

Medication

Antidepressants may be prescribed when:

  • Psychological treatment isn't preferred or effective
  • There's ongoing threat of further trauma
  • Underlying depression affects treatment ability

Watchful waiting may be recommended for mild symptoms, as 2 in 3 people improve naturally within weeks.

Complex PTSD

Complex PTSD may be diagnosed in adults or children who have repeatedly experienced traumatic events, such as violence, neglect or abuse.

Symptoms of Complex PTSD

  • Feelings of shame or guilt
  • Difficulty controlling emotions
  • Periods of dissociation (losing attention)
  • Physical symptoms like headaches and stomach aches
  • Relationship difficulties
  • Destructive or risky behavior

Treatment Approach

Treatment typically occurs in three stages:

  • Stabilization – Learning to control feelings and use grounding techniques
  • Trauma-focused therapy – Processing traumatic memories
  • Reintegration – Developing better relationships and life skills

Self-help Guide

If you think you're experiencing PTSD or complex PTSD, it's important to visit your GP. Self-help strategies can help manage symptoms while waiting for professional assessment.
