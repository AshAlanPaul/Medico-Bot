Hodgkin Lymphoma in Children

Hodgkin lymphoma is a type of blood cancer. It can affect children at any age but is more common in older teenagers and young adults.

More children than ever are surviving childhood cancer. There are new and better drugs and treatments, and we can now also work to reduce the after-effects of having had cancer in the past.

Important: It's devastating to hear that your child has cancer. At times it can feel overwhelming, but there are many healthcare professionals and support organisations to help you through this difficult time.

What is Hodgkin lymphoma?

Hodgkin lymphoma is a type of cancer that occurs in the lymphatic system. There are 2 main types:

  • Classical Hodgkin lymphoma – affects 95% of patients
  • Lymphocyte Predominant Hodgkin Lymphoma (LPHL) – affects 5% of patients

The lymphatic system

The lymphatic system is part of the immune system, the body's natural defence against infection and disease. It includes:

  • Bone marrow
  • Thymus
  • Spleen
  • Lymph nodes (lymph glands)

Hodgkin lymphoma is most likely to start in the lymph nodes in the neck. There are 2 main types of lymphoma: non-Hodgkin lymphoma (NHL) and Hodgkin lymphoma (HL), which require different treatments.

Causes

The exact cause of Hodgkin lymphoma is not known. In most people that develop Hodgkin lymphoma, no cause is found.

However, there is increasing evidence that:

  • Infections, such as the virus that causes glandular fever, may play a part
  • Poor immunity may be a risk factor
  • Very rarely, more than one family member may develop it, but it's not inherited
  • You cannot catch it from another person

Signs and Symptoms

The first sign of Hodgkin lymphoma is usually a painless swelling of one gland, or a group of lymph glands, which continues for some weeks or even months.

Common locations include:

  • Neck or above the collarbone (most often only on one side)
  • Chest (may cause cough or breathlessness)

Note: Children's glands can also become swollen with common infections like sore throat or cold. Persistent swelling should be evaluated by a doctor.

Other symptoms may include:

  • High temperature (fever)
  • Night sweats
  • Weight loss
  • Itchy skin

Diagnosis

A biopsy is required to diagnose Hodgkin lymphoma. This involves removing a swollen lymph gland and examining the cells under a microscope.

If Hodgkin lymphoma is diagnosed, further tests are carried out:

  • X-rays
  • Blood tests
  • CT, MRI and PET scans
  • Bone marrow sample

These tests help determine the exact size, position, and spread of the lymphoma.

Staging

Staging describes the size and position of the cancer and whether it has spread. Treatment depends on the stage.

Staging System

  • Stage 1 – Only 1 group of lymph nodes affected, on one side of the diaphragm
  • Stage 2 – 2 or more groups of lymph nodes affected, but only on one side of the diaphragm
  • Stage 3 – Lymphoma in lymph nodes both above and below the diaphragm
  • Stage 4 – Lymphoma has spread outside lymph nodes to other organs

Letter Codes

  • A – No symptoms
  • B – Has fever, night sweats, or significant weight loss
  • E – Lymphoma has grown to tissue outside lymph nodes

Treatment

Children with Hodgkin lymphoma are usually treated with chemotherapy, and sometimes radiotherapy is also needed.

Chemotherapy

This uses anti-cancer (cytotoxic) drugs to destroy cancer cells. Combination drugs are used every few weeks over several months to increase effectiveness and reduce side effects.

Radiotherapy

Used in less than half of patients, radiotherapy uses high-energy rays to destroy cancer cells while minimizing harm to normal cells.

Side Effects

During treatment, side effects may include:

  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Hair loss
  • Low blood count (increased infection risk)
  • Tiredness
  • Changes in taste and bowel habits

Late Side Effects

Some children may develop side effects years after treatment, affecting organs like the heart, kidneys, and lungs. There's also a small increased risk of developing another cancer later in life, and fertility may be affected.

Positive outlook: About 90% of children who develop Hodgkin lymphoma are cured. Follow-up care continues for about five years after treatment completion.

Lymphocyte Predominant Hodgkin Lymphoma (LPHL)

This is a rare type of Hodgkin lymphoma that affects around 5% of patients.

Key characteristics:

  • Grows at a slower rate than classical Hodgkin lymphoma
  • Requires less intensive treatment
  • Usually presents as a single swollen gland that grows slowly
  • Most patients are diagnosed at an early stage
  • Usually treated with surgery or low-dose chemotherapy

Note: LPHL may return many years after treatment, but it is rarely life-threatening. There is a link between LPHL and developing more aggressive non-Hodgkin lymphoma.
