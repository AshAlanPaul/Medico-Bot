About Bronchitis

Bronchitis is an infection of the main airways of the lungs (bronchi), causing them to become irritated and inflamed.

The main symptom is a cough, which may bring up yellow-grey mucus (phlegm). Bronchitis may also cause a sore throat and wheezing.

When to See Your GP

Most cases of bronchitis can be treated easily at home with rest, non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) and plenty of fluids.

You only need to see your GP if your symptoms are severe or unusual:

  • Your cough is severe or lasts longer than three weeks
  • You have a constant fever (38°C/100.4°F or above) for more than three days
  • You cough up mucus streaked with blood
  • You have an underlying heart or lung condition

Types of Bronchitis

There are two main types of bronchitis:

  • Acute bronchitis - Usually clears up within a few weeks
  • Chronic bronchitis - Symptoms last for at least three months and may recur

Note: Chronic bronchitis is often part of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) and is commonly caused by smoking.

Symptoms of Bronchitis

The main symptom of bronchitis is a hacking cough. It is likely that your cough will bring up thick yellow-grey mucus (phlegm), although this does not always happen.

Other symptoms of bronchitis are similar to those of other infections and may include:

  • Sore throat
  • Headache
  • Runny or blocked nose
  • Aches and pains
  • Tiredness

If you have bronchitis, your cough may last for several weeks after other symptoms have gone. You may also find that the continual coughing makes your chest and stomach muscles sore.

Chronic Bronchitis Symptoms

Chronic bronchitis, often part of COPD, can include:

  • Wheezing
  • Increasing breathlessness when exercising or moving around
  • A persistent cough that brings up mucus
  • Frequent chest infections

When to see your GP: Contact your doctor if your cough is very severe, lasts longer than three weeks, you have a high fever for more than three days, cough up blood, or develop rapid breathing or chest pains.

Causes of Bronchitis

The bronchitis infection can be caused by either a virus or bacteria, although viral bronchitis is much more common.

In most cases, bronchitis is caused by the same viruses that cause the common cold or flu. The virus is contained in the millions of tiny droplets that come out of the nose and mouth when someone coughs or sneezes.

Irritant Substances

Bronchitis can also be triggered by breathing in irritant substances, such as:

  • Smog and air pollution
  • Chemicals in household products
  • Tobacco smoke

Smoking is the main cause of long-term (chronic) bronchitis, and it can affect people who inhale second-hand smoke, as well as smokers themselves.

Occupational Exposure

You may be at risk of bronchitis if you are often exposed to materials that can damage your lungs, such as:

  • Grain dust
  • Textiles (fabric fibres)
  • Ammonia
  • Strong acids
  • Chlorine

Important: Smoking significantly increases your risk of developing chronic bronchitis and can make symptoms worse. Quitting smoking is the most important step you can take to improve your lung health.

Treating Bronchitis

Most cases of bronchitis do not require treatment from a GP, and the symptoms can be easily managed at home.

There is no cure for chronic bronchitis, but healthy living will help. In particular, you should stop smoking if you smoke.

Managing Symptoms at Home

If you have bronchitis:

  • Get plenty of rest
  • Drink lots of fluids - this helps prevent dehydration and thins the mucus in your lungs
  • Treat headaches, fever, and aches with paracetamol or ibuprofen

There is little evidence that cough medicines work. As an alternative, try making your own mixture of honey and lemon, which can help soothe a sore throat and ease your cough.

Antibiotics

Your GP will not routinely prescribe antibiotics, as bronchitis is nearly always caused by a virus. Antibiotics have no effect on viruses.

Antibiotics may be recommended only for people at increased risk of complications, such as:

  • Premature babies
  • Elderly people over 80
  • People with history of heart, lung, kidney or liver disease
  • People with weakened immune systems

Chronic Bronchitis Treatment

Chronic bronchitis is treated similarly to COPD:

  • Medications called mucolytics can make mucus easier to cough up
  • Pulmonary rehabilitation exercise programmes can help you cope better with symptoms
  • Stopping smoking is essential

Recovery tip: Most cases of acute bronchitis clear up on their own within a few weeks. Getting plenty of rest and staying hydrated are the most effective treatments.
