Kidney Stones Information

About Kidney Stones Symptoms Causes Diagnosis Treatment Prevention

About Kidney Stones

Kidney stones can develop in one or both kidneys and most often affect people aged 30 to 60.

They're quite common, with around 3 in 20 men and up to 2 in 20 women developing them at some stage of their lives.

The medical term for kidney stones is nephrolithiasis, and if they cause severe pain it's known as renal colic.

What are Kidney Stones?

Kidney stones are hard deposits made of minerals and salts that form inside your kidneys. They can affect any part of your urinary tract.

Note: Most kidney stones can be treated without surgery. Drinking plenty of water helps pass small stones naturally.

Symptoms of Kidney Stones

Very small kidney stones are unlikely to cause many symptoms. It may even go undetected and pass out painlessly when you urinate.

Symptoms usually occur if the kidney stone:

  • Gets stuck in your kidney
  • Starts to travel down the ureter
  • Causes an infection

Common Symptoms

  • A persistent ache in the lower back, sometimes felt in the groin
  • Periods of intense pain in the back or side of your abdomen
  • Feeling restless and unable to lie still
  • Nausea (feeling sick)
  • Needing to urinate more often than normal
  • Pain when you urinate
  • Blood in your urine

Emergency: If you experience severe pain along with fever, chills, or vomiting, seek immediate medical attention as this could indicate a serious infection.

Causes of Kidney Stones

Kidney stones are usually formed following a build-up of certain chemicals in the body.

This build-up may be any of the following:

  • Calcium
  • Ammonia
  • Uric acid
  • Cysteine

Risk Factors

You're more likely to develop kidney stones if you:

  • Don't drink enough fluids
  • Eat a high-protein, low-fiber diet
  • Have a family history of kidney stones
  • Have had kidney stones before
  • Have certain medical conditions

Prevention tip: Staying well-hydrated is the most effective way to prevent kidney stones from forming.

Diagnosing Kidney Stones

Your GP will usually be able to diagnose kidney stones from your symptoms and medical history.

Tests may include:

  • Urine tests to check for infections and pieces of stones
  • Blood tests to check kidney function and substance levels
  • Imaging tests like CT scans, X-rays, or ultrasound

Imaging Tests

Various imaging techniques can help confirm the diagnosis and identify the precise location of kidney stones:

  • CT scans - Most accurate method for detecting stones
  • X-rays - Can detect many types of stones
  • Ultrasound - Non-invasive method using sound waves

Treating Kidney Stones

Most kidney stones are small enough (less than 4mm in diameter) to be passed out in your urine and can probably be treated at home.

Treatment Options

Depending on the size and location of the stone, treatment may include:

  • Pain medication - For symptom management
  • Increased fluid intake - To help pass small stones
  • ESWL - Using shock waves to break up stones
  • Ureteroscopy - Using a scope to remove stones
  • Surgery - For very large stones

Self-care: Drink enough water to keep your urine colorless. If you're passing a stone, try to collect it for analysis by filtering your urine.

Preventing Kidney Stones

The best way of preventing kidney stones is to make sure you drink plenty of water each day to avoid becoming dehydrated.

Keeping your urine diluted helps to stop waste products getting too concentrated and forming stones.

Prevention Strategies

  • Drink 2-3 liters of water daily
  • Reduce salt intake
  • Limit animal protein
  • Avoid foods high in oxalates if prone to calcium stones
  • Maintain a healthy weight

Remember: If you've had kidney stones before, you have about a 50% chance of developing them again within 5-10 years. Prevention is key!
