About Pancreatic Cancer

Pancreatic cancer is caused by the abnormal and uncontrolled growth of cells in the pancreas â€“ a large gland that's part of the digestive system.

Around half of all new cases are diagnosed in people aged 75 or over. It's uncommon in people under 40 years of age.

Note: The pancreas has two functions - producing digestive enzymes (exocrine) and producing hormones like insulin (endocrine). Most pancreatic cancers occur in the exocrine pancreas.

Symptoms of Pancreatic Cancer

In the early stages, a tumour in the pancreas doesn't usually cause any symptoms, which can make it difficult to diagnose.

Main Symptoms

  • Pain in the stomach or back - May come and go initially, worse when lying down or after eating
  • Unexpected weight loss - Due to digestive enzyme disruption
  • Jaundice - Yellowing of skin and eyes, dark urine, pale stools

Other Symptoms

  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Bowel changes
  • Fever and shivering
  • Indigestion
  • Blood clots
  • Diabetes symptoms (in some cases)

Important: These symptoms can be caused by many different conditions and aren't usually cancer. However, you should contact your GP if you're concerned or if these symptoms start suddenly.

Causes of Pancreatic Cancer

While the exact cause isn't fully understood, several risk factors have been identified that can increase your chances of developing pancreatic cancer.

Main Risk Factors

  • Age - Mainly affects people aged 50-80
  • Smoking - About 1 in 3 cases associated with tobacco use
  • Diabetes - Increases risk, though most diabetics won't develop it
  • Chronic pancreatitis - Long-term inflammation of pancreas
  • Family history - About 1 in 10 cases are inherited
  • Helicobacter pylori infection - Stomach bacteria infection

Prevention: Stopping smoking is the most effective way to reduce your risk of pancreatic cancer.

Diagnosing Pancreatic Cancer

Diagnosis involves several tests to confirm the presence of cancer and determine its stage and spread.

Diagnostic Tests

  • Blood tests - Check for jaundice and other markers
  • Ultrasound scan - Initial imaging of abdomen
  • CT scan - Detailed cross-sectional imaging
  • MRI scan - Magnetic resonance imaging
  • PET scan - Shows metabolic activity of tissues
  • Endoscopic procedures - EUS and ERCP for close examination
  • Biopsy - Tissue sample for definitive diagnosis

Note: Early diagnosis is challenging because symptoms often don't appear until the cancer is advanced. Regular check-ups are important for high-risk individuals.

Treating Pancreatic Cancer

Treatment depends on the cancer's type, location, stage, and your overall health. A combination of approaches is often used.

Treatment Options

  • Surgery - To remove the tumour when possible
  • Chemotherapy - Medications to kill cancer cells
  • Radiotherapy - Radiation to shrink tumours

Surgical Procedures

  • Whipple procedure - Removal of pancreatic head
  • Distal pancreatectomy - Removal of tail and body
  • Total pancreatectomy - Complete pancreas removal
  • Palliative surgery - To relieve symptoms

Important: Pancreatic cancer is difficult to treat, especially when diagnosed at advanced stages. Treatment focuses on removing the cancer when possible or controlling symptoms and slowing growth when cure isn't possible.

Recovering from Pancreatic Cancer

Recovery from pancreatic cancer treatment can be challenging and requires comprehensive support.

Recovery Process

  • Pain management - Adequate pain relief after surgery
  • Gradual eating - Starting with fluids, progressing to solid foods
  • Dietary support - Working with dietitians for nutritional needs
  • Follow-up chemotherapy - Often needed after surgery
  • Long-term monitoring - Regular check-ups to detect recurrence

Support: Recovery involves both physical healing and emotional support. Many people benefit from cancer support groups and counseling services during this challenging time.
