About Cerebral Palsy

Cerebral palsy is a condition caused by either damage to the brain, or abnormal development of the brain. This damage normally occurs before birth or during a child's early development.

Cerebral palsy is not normally progressive in nature, meaning it doesn't typically get worse over time. It usually causes abnormal muscle tone and movement patterns.

Important: Cerebral palsy affects muscle control, movement, and coordination, but it doesn't define a person's potential or abilities.

Types of Cerebral Palsy

Spastic Cerebral Palsy

This is the most common type of abnormal muscle tone. Someone with spastic cerebral palsy is likely to experience a tightening of the muscles throughout the body. This tightening can be persistent or intermittent.

Dyskinetic Cerebral Palsy

Individuals with this type may have dystonia or athetosis. This describes abnormal muscle control where muscles can shift from tight to floppy without warning, resulting in involuntary movements.

Ataxic Cerebral Palsy

This is the least common type of cerebral palsy. It can be identified by shaky movement and poor balance. Someone with ataxic cerebral palsy may struggle with fine motor movements and coordination.

Mixed Cerebral Palsy

Someone with mixed cerebral palsy may have signs of more than one type of abnormal muscle tone at the same time.

Note: Each person with cerebral palsy experiences unique symptoms and challenges. The type and severity can vary significantly between individuals.

Symptoms of Cerebral Palsy

The symptoms of cerebral palsy can vary from person to person and are dependent on the type of brain injury or abnormality.

Someone with cerebral palsy may:

  • Struggle with movement and balance, and may be unable to walk
  • Struggle to speak, and possibly need to use electronic aids
  • Have difficulties with eating, drinking and swallowing
  • Have problems with their vision
  • Experience pain symptoms
  • Experience fatigue (tiredness)
  • Have problems with sleep
  • Have epilepsy
  • Have a learning disability
  • Display behavioural problems
  • Have bladder control problems
  • Have problems with their digestive system (constipation)
  • Struggle to control their saliva
  • Struggle to process information from their senses

Secondary Symptoms

These are caused by the impact of the condition on the body, rather than directly by cerebral palsy:

  • Tightness in joints and muscles
  • Dislocations
  • Changes in physical abilities
  • Changes in pain levels
  • Increased fatigue

Causes of Cerebral Palsy

Cerebral palsy is caused by damage to the brain during pregnancy, birth or early development.

There are some factors that can increase the risk of a child developing cerebral palsy:

  • Premature birth (being born early)
  • Neonatal encephalopathy (caused by lack of oxygen to the brain)
  • Stroke or bleeding in the baby's brain during pregnancy or after birth
  • Infection during pregnancy or after birth
  • Meningitis
  • Head injuries

Important: A child can develop cerebral palsy despite their parents having done everything right during pregnancy and childbirth. The parents of a child with cerebral palsy are not to blame.

It's not always possible to tell why a child has developed cerebral palsy.

Diagnosing Cerebral Palsy

There isn't one single test that can diagnose cerebral palsy. Medical professionals need to see how a child develops over time to make a diagnosis.

Most children are diagnosed by two years old. Some children are diagnosed earlier if they've spent time in medical settings, for example children who were born premature.

To diagnose cerebral palsy, medical professionals will:

  • Examine the child's medical history
  • Assess movement, balance, and reactions
  • Use scans like MRI to check for brain damage or abnormal development

Waiting for diagnosis: Waiting for a diagnosis can be very stressful for parents. It's natural to feel anxious when your child is unwell. Support is available for parents during this challenging time.

Treatment and Support

There are many resources and treatments available to help both children and adults with cerebral palsy. Everyone with cerebral palsy will have individual needs that may change over time.

Physiotherapy

Targeted physiotherapy can help improve and maintain posture, coordination, strength and balance. Physiotherapists may recommend specialist mobility equipment and advise about orthotic splints.

Occupational Therapy

Occupational therapists help people who struggle to complete everyday tasks find new ways of completing them. They can show how to break down activities into smaller movements and recommend medical aids and technologies.

Speech and Language Therapy

If someone is struggling to talk, a speech and language therapist can help with techniques to improve speech or recommend communication aids. They can also help with swallowing issues.

Orthotics

Orthotic devices support parts of the body to help improve movement and activity. An orthotist will examine movement abilities and make personalized recommendations.

Medication

Although medication cannot cure cerebral palsy, some types may help ease symptoms like muscle relaxants for those with spastic or dystonic cerebral palsy.

Surgery

Surgery may be recommended to help with certain symptoms, such as procedures on muscles, bones and nerves to help mobility.

Support available: Mental health support is available for both individuals with cerebral palsy and their parents through the NHS and various support organizations.

Living with Cerebral Palsy

While cerebral palsy is a non-progressive condition (it doesn't normally get worse over time), it may not stay the same throughout life. Symptoms can change with age, even in adulthood.

Cerebral palsy doesn't normally shorten life expectancy. People with cerebral palsy often go on to live long, rich and rewarding lives.

When to contact your GP: Contact your GP if you or your child is experiencing a change in condition or if symptoms are getting worse.

With the right support and treatments, individuals with cerebral palsy can achieve their full potential and lead fulfilling lives.

Remember: Cerebral palsy is just one aspect of a person's identity. With proper support and accommodations, people with cerebral palsy can pursue education, careers, relationships, and all aspects of life.
