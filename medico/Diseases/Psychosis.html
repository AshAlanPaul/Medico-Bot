Understanding Psychosis

Psychosis is a mental health problem that causes people to perceive or interpret things differently from those around them. This might involve hallucinations or delusions.

Main Symptoms

The two main symptoms of psychosis are:

  • Hallucinations – where a person hears, sees and, in some cases, feels, smells or tastes things that aren't there; a common hallucination is hearing voices
  • Delusions – where a person believes things that, when examined rationally, are obviously untrue – for example, thinking your next door neighbour is planning to kill you

The combination of hallucinations and delusional thinking can often severely disrupt perception, thinking, emotion, and behaviour.

Experiencing the symptoms of psychosis is often referred to as having a psychotic episode.

Important: Psychosis isn't a condition in itself – it's triggered by other conditions. Early treatment usually has better long-term outcomes.

Symptoms of Psychosis

Someone who develops psychosis will have their own unique set of symptoms and experiences, according to their particular circumstances.

Main Symptoms

  • Hallucinations – perceiving things that don't exist in reality
  • Delusions – unshakeable beliefs in something implausible or obviously untrue
  • Confused and disturbed thoughts – rapid speech, switching topics mid-sentence
  • Lack of insight and self-awareness – unaware their behavior is strange

Types of Hallucinations

  • Sight – seeing colors, shapes, people or animals that aren't there
  • Sounds – hearing angry, unpleasant or sarcastic voices
  • Touch – feeling touched when nobody is there
  • Smell – detecting strange or unpleasant odors
  • Taste – experiencing constant unpleasant tastes

Types of Delusions

  • Paranoid delusions – believing someone is planning to harm them
  • Delusions of grandeur – believing they have imaginary powers or authority

Emergency: If someone is having a psychotic episode that's rapidly getting worse, contact their crisis team or the duty psychiatrist at the nearest A&E department.

Causes of Psychosis

The causes of psychosis can be classified into three main categories:

Psychological Causes

  • Schizophrenia
  • Bipolar disorder
  • Severe stress or anxiety
  • Severe depression
  • Lack of sleep

Medical Conditions

  • HIV and AIDS
  • Malaria
  • Alzheimer's disease
  • Parkinson's disease
  • Brain tumors
  • Multiple sclerosis

Substance-Related Causes

  • Alcohol misuse
  • Drug misuse (cocaine, amphetamines, cannabis, LSD, etc.)
  • Withdrawal from alcohol or drugs
  • Medication side effects

Brain Chemistry

Research suggests that excess dopamine in the brain plays an important role in psychosis, disrupting important brain functions like memory, emotion, social behavior, and self-awareness.

Diagnosing Psychosis

You should visit your GP if you're experiencing psychotic episodes. Early treatment usually has better long-term outcomes.

Initial Assessment

Your GP will ask questions to determine the cause of your psychosis, including:

  • Whether you're taking any medication
  • Whether you've been taking illegal substances
  • How your moods have been
  • How you've been functioning day-to-day
  • Family history of mental health conditions
  • Details of hallucinations and delusions

Referral to Specialists

You may be referred to:

  • Community mental health team
  • Crisis resolution team
  • Early intervention team

Note: People with psychosis often have a lack of insight and may not recognize they need help. Family and friends may need to help them seek treatment.

Treatment for Psychosis

Treatment for psychosis involves a combination of antipsychotic medicines, psychological therapies, and social support.

Antipsychotic Medications

Antipsychotics work by blocking the effect of dopamine in the brain. They can:

  • Reduce feelings of anxiety or aggression within hours
  • Take several days or weeks to reduce hallucinations or delusions
  • Be taken orally or as injections

Common Side Effects

  • Drowsiness
  • Shaking and trembling
  • Restlessness
  • Muscle twitches and spasms
  • Blurred vision
  • Dizziness
  • Weight gain

Psychological Therapies

  • Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) – helps identify unhelpful thinking patterns
  • Family therapy – helps both the person and their family cope with the condition
  • Self-help groups – provide support from others with similar experiences

Important: Never stop taking prescribed medication unless advised by a healthcare professional. Suddenly stopping can trigger a relapse of symptoms.

Complications of Psychosis

Psychosis can lead to several serious complications that require careful management.

Self-Harm and Suicide

  • 1 in 10 people with psychosis have a history of self-harm
  • 1 in 5 people with psychosis will attempt suicide
  • 1 in 25 people with psychosis will die by suicide

Substance Misuse

People with psychosis are much more likely to have drug or alcohol misuse problems, possibly because these substances provide short-term symptom relief.

Long-term Medication Complications

  • Weight gain – common with many antipsychotics
  • Metabolic syndrome – increased risk of diabetes, high cholesterol, and high blood pressure
  • Tardive dyskinesia (TD) – involuntary movements that can sometimes be permanent

Emergency Support

If you're feeling suicidal or know someone who is:

  • Call Samaritans: 116 123
  • Go to your nearest A&E department
  • Contact NHS 24 '111' service
  • Speak to a friend, family member, or someone you trust

Recovery is possible: With proper treatment and support, many people with psychosis can manage their symptoms effectively and lead fulfilling lives. Early intervention and consistent treatment are key to successful recovery.
