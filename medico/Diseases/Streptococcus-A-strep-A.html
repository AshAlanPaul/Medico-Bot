Understanding Strep A

Group A Streptococcus (GAS), also known as Strep A, are bacteria commonly found on the skin or in the throat. Under some circumstances these bacteria can cause disease.

GAS infection commonly presents as a mild sore throat ('strep throat') and skin/soft tissue infections such as impetigo and cellulitis.

Testing: Strep A tests are not available through the NHS in Scotland for self-testing at home. Should you or your child need to be tested for Strep A, your GP or other healthcare professional will advise you.

Emergency Symptoms

Call 999 or go to A&E if:

  • Difficulty breathing (grunting noises or tummy sucking under ribs)
  • Pauses in breathing
  • Blue skin, tongue or lips
  • Floppy and won't wake up or stay awake

Contact your GP if you or your child:

  • Is getting worse
  • Is feeding or eating much less than normal
  • Shows signs of dehydration (sunken eyes, drowsiness)
  • Has not passed urine for 12 hours
  • Has high temperature (under 3 months: 38°C, over 3 months: 39°C+)
  • Feels hotter than usual or sweaty
  • Is very tired or irritable

If your GP is closed, phone the 111 service. If you feel that you or your child is seriously unwell, trust your own judgement and seek medical assistance.

GAS Infections

GAS bacteria can cause a wide variety of skin, soft tissue and respiratory tract infections ranging in severity from mild to life-threatening.

Common GAS Infections

  • Tonsillitis - Inflammation of the tonsils
  • Sore throat - Pharyngitis
  • Scarlet fever - Characterized by a red rash
  • Impetigo - Skin infection causing sores
  • Erysipelas - A type of cellulitis
  • Cellulitis - Skin and soft tissue infection
  • Pneumonia - Lung infection

Post-Streptococcal Complications

In rare cases, patients may develop complications such as:

  • Rheumatic fever
  • Glomerulonephritis (heart and kidney diseases caused by immune reaction)

iGAS Infections

GAS can very rarely cause more serious conditions, known as invasive group A Streptococcus (iGAS) infections.

Serious iGAS Conditions

  • Bacteraemia - Infection of the bloodstream
  • Septic arthritis - Joint infection
  • Meningitis - Inflammation of brain and spinal cord membranes
  • Necrotising fasciitis - Severe infection involving death of soft tissue
  • Streptococcal toxic shock syndrome - Rapidly progressive symptoms with low blood pressure and multi-organ failure

Risk Groups: iGAS infections tend to happen in the elderly, the very young, or people with underlying risk factors such as injecting drug use, alcoholism, immunosuppression or cancer.

Treatment & Prevention

Treating GAS Infections

Most GAS infections are relatively mild illnesses that clear up on their own without the need for antibiotics.

Often symptoms that look like GAS infections, like sore throats, are more commonly caused by viruses than GAS bacteria. If you or your child has a runny nose with their sore throat, it's likely to be a virus infection.

When Antibiotics Are Needed

Your healthcare professional will consider antibiotics if they think:

  • You need medication to get better or ease symptoms
  • You're at risk of serious complications
  • You have scarlet fever

Preventing GAS Infection

GAS are spread by close contact between individuals, through respiratory droplets and direct skin contact.

Do:

  • Wash your hands properly with soap for 20 seconds
  • Use a disposable tissue to catch coughs and sneezes
  • Keep away from others if you feel unwell

Don't:

  • Share contaminated food, utensils, cups, glasses, baths, bed linen or towels

Vaccination Protection

GAS can be a secondary infection, developing in people who are already unwell with illnesses like the flu. You can help protect your child from this risk by making sure they get their free flu vaccine.

New evidence also shows that the nasal spray flu vaccine offered to children may also protect them from GAS infection.
