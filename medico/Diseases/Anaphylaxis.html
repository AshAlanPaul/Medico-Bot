Anaphylaxis

Anaphylaxis is a severe, potentially life-threatening allergic reaction that can develop rapidly. It is also known as anaphylactic shock.

EMERGENCY WARNING: Anaphylaxis should always be treated as a medical emergency. Call 999 immediately if you suspect someone is having an anaphylactic reaction.

Signs of Anaphylaxis

  • Itchy skin or a raised, red skin rash
  • Swollen eyes, lips, hands and feet
  • Feeling lightheaded or faint
  • Swelling of the mouth, throat or tongue, which can cause breathing and swallowing difficulties
  • Wheezing
  • Abdominal pain, nausea and vomiting
  • Collapse and unconsciousness

What to Do in an Emergency

  • Use an adrenaline auto-injector if available
  • Call 999 immediately
  • Lie the person flat with legs raised (unless having breathing difficulties)
  • Give a second injection after 5-10 minutes if no improvement

Causes of Anaphylaxis

Anaphylaxis is caused by a problem with the immune system, which overreacts to a harmless substance and releases chemicals like histamine.

Common Triggers

Insect Stings

Most cases are caused by wasp and bee stings. About 1 in 100 people experience allergic reactions to stings.

Foods

More than half of food-related anaphylaxis cases are caused by peanuts. Other common triggers include:

  • Tree nuts (walnuts, cashews, almonds)
  • Milk
  • Fish and shellfish
  • Eggs
  • Some fruits (bananas, kiwi, grapes, strawberries)

Medicines

  • Antibiotics (particularly penicillin)
  • General anaesthetics
  • Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)

Risk levels: The risk of anaphylaxis from medicines is very small - about 1 in 5,000 for penicillin and 1 in 10,000 for general anaesthetics.

Treating Anaphylaxis

Immediate Action Required: If you think somebody is experiencing symptoms of anaphylaxis, use an adrenaline injector if available and dial 999 immediately.

Adrenaline Injections

Adrenaline causes blood vessels to become narrower, raises blood pressure, reduces swelling, and opens airways.

  • Inject as soon as a serious reaction is suspected
  • Hold the syringe in place for 5-10 seconds
  • Injections can be given through clothing
  • Give a second dose after 5-10 minutes if no improvement

Positioning and Resuscitation

  • Lie flat with legs raised (unless breathing difficulties)
  • Pregnant women should lie on left side
  • If unconscious, use recovery position
  • Perform CPR if breathing or heart stops

Hospital Treatment

Even after adrenaline, hospital observation for 6-12 hours is necessary. Treatment may include oxygen, fluids, and additional medications.

Preventing Anaphylaxis

Allergy Clinic Referral

You should be referred to a specialist allergy clinic for tests to identify triggers. Tests may include skin prick tests and blood tests.

Adrenaline Auto-injectors

If you've had previous anaphylaxis, you may be prescribed an auto-injector (EpiPen, Jext, or Emerade).

Important: Always carry your auto-injector, check expiry dates regularly, and don't delay using it if you suspect anaphylaxis.

Avoiding Triggers

Food

  • Check food labels carefully
  • Inform restaurant staff about allergies
  • Be aware of hidden allergens in sauces and processed foods

Insect Stings

  • Move away from insects slowly without panicking
  • Use insect repellent when outdoors
  • Consider desensitisation treatment if high risk

Medicines

Always inform healthcare professionals about medicine allergies. Alternatives are usually available for most medications.
