Appendicitis

Appendicitis is a painful swelling of the appendix. The appendix is a small, thin pouch about 5 to 10cm (2 to 4 inches) long. It's connected to the large intestine, where stools (faeces) are formed.

Nobody knows exactly why we have an appendix, but removing it isn't harmful. The appendix doesn't perform any important function in humans.

Pain Progression

  • Starts with pain in the middle of your abdomen that may come and go
  • Within hours, pain travels to lower right-hand side where appendix lies
  • Pain becomes constant and severe
  • Pressing on the area, coughing, or walking may worsen pain

Phone 999 for an ambulance if you have sudden pain that continues to get worse and spreads across your abdomen. These are signs your appendix may have burst, which can lead to potentially life-threatening complications.

If you're experiencing abdominal pain that's gradually getting worse, contact your GP or local out-of-hours service immediately. If unavailable, phone NHS 24's 111 service for advice.

Symptoms of Appendicitis

Main Symptoms

  • Pain starting in middle abdomen, moving to lower right side
  • Pain becomes constant and severe
  • Pain worsens with pressure, coughing, or walking
  • Feeling sick (nausea) and vomiting
  • Loss of appetite
  • Diarrhea
  • High temperature (fever) and flushed face

Conditions That Mimic Appendicitis

Appendicitis can be confused with:

  • Gastroenteritis
  • Severe irritable bowel syndrome (IBS)
  • Constipation
  • Bladder or urine infections
  • Crohn's disease
  • Pelvic infection
  • Ectopic pregnancy or menstrual pain (in women)

Important: Any condition that causes constant stomach pain requires urgent medical attention. If the appendix bursts, it can cause peritonitis - a serious infection of the inner lining of the abdomen.

Diagnosing Appendicitis

Appendicitis can be tricky to diagnose as typical symptoms are only present in about half of all cases. The appendix location can also vary between individuals.

Initial Examination

  • Your GP will ask about symptoms and examine your abdomen
  • They'll check if pain worsens when pressure is applied to appendix area
  • If symptoms are typical, immediate hospital referral for treatment

Further Tests (if diagnosis uncertain)

  • Blood test - to look for signs of infection
  • Pregnancy test - for women
  • Urine test - to rule out bladder infection
  • Ultrasound scan - to check if appendix is swollen
  • CT scan - for clearer imaging
  • Laparoscopy - direct inspection if diagnosis remains uncertain

If appendicitis is suspected, surgeons usually recommend removing the appendix rather than risking it bursting. This means some people have their appendix removed even if it's eventually found to be normal.

Treating Appendicitis

The standard treatment for appendicitis is surgical removal of the appendix (appendectomy), usually performed as soon as possible.

Surgical Procedures

Keyhole Surgery (Laparoscopy)

  • Preferred method with quicker recovery
  • 3-4 small incisions in abdomen
  • Special instruments inserted including camera
  • Gas pumped to inflate abdomen for better visibility
  • Usually discharged within 24-48 hours

Open Surgery

  • Used when appendix has burst or formed a mass
  • Single larger incision in lower right abdomen
  • Required if surgeon less experienced in laparoscopy
  • Necessary for people with previous open abdominal surgery
  • Recovery may take up to a week in hospital

Recovery

  • Keyhole surgery: return to normal activities in 2 weeks
  • Open surgery: avoid strenuous activities for 4-6 weeks
  • Pain and bruising common initially
  • Shoulder pain possible after keyhole surgery (from gas)
  • Constipation may occur - managed with fiber and hydration

Risks and Complications

  • Infection - antibiotics given to minimize risk
  • Bleeding under skin (hematoma)
  • Scarring at incision sites
  • Abscess (collection of pus)
  • Hernia at incision site
  • General anesthetic risks (rare)

Seek medical advice if you notice after surgery: increasing pain and swelling, repeated vomiting, high fever, discharge from wound, or wound feels hot to touch.

Complications of Appendicitis

If appendicitis isn't treated, the appendix can burst and cause potentially life-threatening infections. Phone 999 immediately if you have abdominal pain that suddenly gets much worse and spreads across your abdomen.

Peritonitis

If appendix bursts, bacteria spreads causing infection of the peritoneum (abdominal lining):

  • Severe continuous abdominal pain
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • High temperature (fever)
  • Rapid heartbeat
  • Shortness of breath with rapid breathing
  • Swelling of the abdomen

Abscesses

Painful collection of pus that can form around a burst appendix:

  • Occurs as body attempts to fight infection
  • Can also occur as surgery complication (1 in 500 cases)
  • Usually treated with antibiotics and drainage
  • Drainage performed under ultrasound or CT guidance

Prevalence: Appendicitis is common, affecting about 1 in every 13 people. It can develop at any age but is most common in young people aged 10-20 years.
