Liver Cancer

About Liver Cancer Causes Diagnosis Treatment

About Liver Cancer

Primary liver cancer is an uncommon but serious type of cancer that begins in the liver. This is a separate condition from secondary liver cancer, which occurs when cancer that first develops in another part of the body spreads to the liver.

Signs and Symptoms

Symptoms of liver cancer are often vague and don't appear until the cancer is at an advanced stage. They can include:

  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Loss of appetite
  • Feeling very full after eating, even if the meal was small
  • Feeling sick and vomiting
  • Pain or swelling in your abdomen
  • Jaundice (yellowing of your skin and whites of your eyes)
  • Itchy skin
  • Feeling very tired and weak

Important: Speak to your GP if you notice any of these symptoms. Although they are more likely to be the result of a more common condition, it's best to have them checked.

Causes of Liver Cancer

The exact cause of liver cancer is unknown, but many cases are linked to a problem with the liver called cirrhosis. This is where the tissue of the liver has become scarred and cannot perform many of its usual functions.

Common Causes of Cirrhosis

Heavy and Harmful Drinking

Excessive alcohol intake over many years can damage the liver. Every time you drink alcohol, your liver filters out the poisonous alcohol from your blood and some liver cells die.

Non-alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease

This occurs when small deposits of fat build up inside the tissue of the liver. It's associated with obesity and type 2 diabetes.

Hepatitis C

A long-term infection of hepatitis C can cause inflammation and scarring of the liver. Hepatitis C is spread by blood contact.

Hepatitis B

Like hepatitis C, hepatitis B can cause inflammation and scarring of the liver over time. It can be spread through contaminated blood and other bodily fluids.

Prevention: By avoiding or cutting down on alcohol, eating healthily, exercising regularly, and taking steps to reduce your risk of hepatitis B and C, you may significantly reduce your chances of developing liver cancer.

Diagnosing Liver Cancer

For many people, the first stage of diagnosing liver cancer is a consultation with a GP, although people at risk are usually tested regularly for the condition.

Surveillance for Liver Cancer

If you are in a high-risk group for developing liver cancer, regular screening (surveillance) is often recommended every 6 months. This usually involves:

  • Ultrasound scans to create an image of your liver
  • Blood tests for alpha-fetoprotein (AFP) protein

Further Testing

If initial tests suggest liver cancer, further tests may include:

  • CT scans for detailed three-dimensional images
  • MRI scans using magnetic fields and radio waves
  • Biopsy to test liver tissue samples
  • Laparoscopy using a small camera to examine the liver

Staging Liver Cancer

Liver cancer is staged using systems like the Barcelona Clinic Liver Cancer (BCLC) staging system, which has 5 stages from 0 to D, depending on tumor size, number, and liver function.

Early Detection: Regular check-ups for people at high risk help ensure the condition is diagnosed early when treatment is most effective.

Treating Liver Cancer

Treatment for liver cancer depends on the stage of the condition and can include surgery and medication.

Early Stage Treatment (Stage A)

If diagnosed early, a complete cure may be possible through:

  • Surgical resection - removing the affected section of liver
  • Liver transplant - replacing the liver with a donor's liver
  • Microwave or radiofrequency ablation - using heat to kill cancerous cells

Intermediate Stage Treatment (Stage B & C)

For intermediate stages, treatment focuses on slowing progression and relieving symptoms:

  • Chemotherapy (TACE procedure)
  • Medication like sorafenib
  • Alcohol injections for small tumors

Advanced Stage Treatment (Stage D)

For advanced liver cancer, treatment focuses on relieving symptoms of pain and discomfort rather than attempting to slow down the progression of the cancer.

Treatment Teams

Most hospitals use multidisciplinary teams (MDTs) to treat liver cancer. These are teams of specialists that work together to make decisions about the best treatment approach.

Hope: While liver cancer is serious, advances in treatment mean that many people can live longer and with better quality of life than in the past.
