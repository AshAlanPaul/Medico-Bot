About Nasal and Sinus Cancer

Nasal and sinus cancer affects the nasal cavity (the space behind your nose) and the sinuses (small, air-filled cavities inside your nose, cheekbones and forehead).

It's a rare type of cancer that most often affects men aged 50 to 60.

Nasal and sinus cancer is different to cancer in the area where the nose and throat connect. This is called nasopharyngeal cancer.

Important: Nasal and sinus cancer is rare, but early detection and treatment can significantly improve outcomes. Be aware of persistent symptoms and seek medical advice if concerned.

Symptoms of Nasal and Sinus Cancer

Most Common Symptoms

The most common symptoms of nasal and sinus cancer are:

  • A persistent blocked nose - which usually only affects 1 side
  • Nosebleeds
  • Mucus draining from the nose - which may be blood-stained
  • A decreased sense of smell

Note: These symptoms can be similar to more common and less serious conditions, such as a cold or sinusitis. However, persistent symptoms should be evaluated by a healthcare professional.

Later Stage Symptoms

At a later stage, symptoms can include:

  • Pain or numbness in the face
  • Swollen glands in the neck
  • Partial loss of vision or double vision
  • A bulging or persistently watering eye
  • A lump or growth on your face, nose or roof of your mouth

When to see a doctor: Speak to your GP if you notice any unusual or persistent symptoms. While they're very unlikely to be caused by nasal or sinus cancer, they're worth getting checked out.

Diagnosing Nasal and Sinus Cancer

If your GP thinks you might need some tests to determine what's causing your symptoms, you'll usually be referred to an ear, nose and throat (ENT) consultant in hospital.

Diagnostic Tests

Tests you may have include:

  • Nasal endoscopy - where a long, thin, flexible tube attached to a light source is inserted into your nose to examine the area
  • CT scan - computerised tomography scan to create detailed images
  • MRI scan - magnetic resonance imaging scan for detailed soft tissue images
  • Biopsy - where a small sample of tissue is removed and examined under a microscope

Diagnostic process: These tests help determine if cancer is present, its type, and how far it may have spread. This information is crucial for planning the most effective treatment approach.

Risk Factors for Nasal and Sinus Cancer

Several factors are known to increase the risk of developing nasal and sinus cancer:

  • Gender - men are more likely to develop nasal and sinus cancer than women
  • Occupational exposure - prolonged exposure to certain substances through your work, including:
    • Wood dust
    • Leather dust
    • Nickel
    • Chromium
    • Formaldehyde
  • Smoking - the more you smoke, the higher your risk of developing several types of cancer, including nasal and sinus cancer
  • Human papilloma virus (HPV) - a group of viruses that affect the skin and moist membranes, such as the mouth and throat

Occupational safety: If you work in industries with exposure to these substances, follow safety guidelines and use appropriate protective equipment to minimize your risk.

Treatments for Nasal and Sinus Cancer

The best treatment depends on several factors, including how far the cancer has spread and your general health.

Treatment Options

Treatment may include:

  • Surgery - to remove a tumour, which can be performed using:
    • Surgical incisions (open surgery)
    • Keyhole surgery through the nose (endoscopic microsurgery)
  • Radiotherapy - where high-energy radiation is used to:
    • Kill cancerous cells
    • Shrink a tumour before surgery
    • Destroy small amounts of tumour that may be left after surgery
  • Chemotherapy - where medicine is used to:
    • Help shrink or slow down tumour growth
    • Reduce the risk of cancer returning after surgery

Treatment Team

Your treatment will be organised by a head and neck cancer multidisciplinary team (MDT), who will discuss the treatment options with you. A combination of treatments will often be recommended.

Multidisciplinary approach: The MDT typically includes surgeons, oncologists, radiologists, pathologists, and specialist nurses who work together to create the most effective treatment plan for your specific situation.

Key Points to Remember

  • Nasal and sinus cancer is rare but treatable, especially when caught early
  • Persistent nasal symptoms should be evaluated by a healthcare professional
  • Multiple treatment options are available and often used in combination
  • A specialized medical team will guide your treatment journey
  • Follow-up care is important after treatment to monitor for recurrence
