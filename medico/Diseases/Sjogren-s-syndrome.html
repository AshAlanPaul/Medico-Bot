About Sjogren's Syndrome

Sjögren's (pronounced Show-grin's) syndrome is an autoimmune disorder where the body's immune system attacks glands that secrete fluid, such as the tear and saliva glands.

The effects of Sjögren's syndrome can be widespread. Certain glands become inflamed, which reduces the production of tears and saliva, causing the main symptoms of dry eyes and dry mouth.

Types of Sjogren's Syndrome

  • Primary: Develops by itself and not as the result of another condition
  • Secondary: Develops in combination with another autoimmune disorder, such as lupus or rheumatoid arthritis

Who's affected: Sjögren's syndrome most commonly affects people aged 40-60, with women accounting for about 90% of cases.

Symptoms of Sjogren's Syndrome

The most commonly reported symptoms are dry mouth and eyes, which can lead to other associated symptoms.

Symptoms of Dry Mouth

  • Tooth decay and gum disease
  • Dry cough
  • Difficulty swallowing and chewing
  • Hoarse voice
  • Difficulty speaking
  • Swollen salivary glands
  • Repeated oral thrush infections

Symptoms of Dry Eyes

  • Burning or stinging eyes
  • Itchy eyes
  • Feeling of grit in eyes
  • Irritated and swollen eyelids
  • Sensitivity to light
  • Tired eyes
  • Mucus discharge from eyes

Other Symptoms

  • Dry skin
  • Tiredness and fatigue
  • Muscle pain
  • Joint pain, stiffness and swelling
  • Vasculitis (inflammation of blood vessels)
  • Difficulty concentrating and remembering
  • Vaginal dryness

Causes of Sjogren's Syndrome

It's not known exactly what causes Sjögren's syndrome, but it's thought to be linked to a problem with the immune system.

Autoimmune Response

In Sjögren's syndrome, the immune system attacks the tear, saliva and vaginal glands, which are collectively known as the exocrine glands.

Possible Triggers

Most researchers believe primary Sjögren's syndrome is triggered by a combination of genetic and environmental factors. The female hormone oestrogen also seems to play a role.

Note: Sjögren's syndrome can be associated with other autoimmune conditions, such as rheumatoid arthritis or lupus. This is known as secondary Sjögren's syndrome.

Diagnosing Sjogren's Syndrome

Sjögren's syndrome can be difficult to diagnose because the symptoms are similar to those of other health conditions.

Screening Questions

  • Have you had daily, persistent, troublesome dry eyes for more than three months?
  • Do you keep having a sensation of grit in your eye?
  • Do you need to use eye drops containing tear substitutes more than three times a day?
  • Have you had a daily feeling of dry mouth for more than three months?
  • Do you keep getting swollen salivary glands?
  • Do you frequently drink liquids to help you swallow food?

Diagnostic Tests

  • Tear break-up time and Schirmer tests: Measure tear production and quality
  • Lip biopsy: Examines salivary gland tissue
  • Blood tests: Look for specific antibodies (anti-Ro and anti-La)
  • Salivary flow rate: Measures saliva production

Treating Sjogren's Syndrome

There's no cure for Sjögren's syndrome, but treatments help relieve symptoms such as eye and mouth dryness.

Eye Care

  • Artificial tears: Lubricating eye drops
  • Moisture chamber spectacles: Special glasses that help retain moisture
  • Punctal plugs: Small plugs that seal tear ducts

Mouth Care

Do

  • Maintain good oral hygiene
  • Increase fluid intake
  • Use sugar-free chewing gum
  • Suck ice cubes
  • Use mouth rinses regularly

Medications

  • Pilocarpine: Stimulates saliva and tear production
  • Hydroxychloroquine: Slows immune system attack on glands
  • NSAIDs: For muscle and joint pain

Important: Regular dental check-ups every six months are crucial for people with Sjögren's syndrome to prevent tooth decay and gum disease.

Complications of Sjogren's Syndrome

Sjögren's syndrome isn't usually life-threatening, but it is linked to more serious problems.

Non-Hodgkin Lymphoma

People with Sjögren's syndrome are at increased risk of developing non-Hodgkin lymphoma, though the overall risk is still low (around 5%).

Eye Damage

If dry eyes aren't treated, they can become inflamed and develop corneal ulcers, which can lead to vision loss if left untreated.

Pregnancy Considerations

Women with Sjögren's syndrome should be tested for certain antibodies before pregnancy, as they can cause temporary lupus rash in newborns or, rarely, heart defects.

Other Associated Conditions

  • Peripheral neuropathy
  • Raynaud's phenomenon
  • Kidney problems
  • Underactive thyroid gland
  • Irritable bowel syndrome
  • Teeth cavities
