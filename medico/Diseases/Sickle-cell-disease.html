About Sickle Cell Disease

Sickle cell disease is the name for a group of blood disorders. The most severe is sickle cell anaemia. These disorders are inherited, meaning they are passed on through your genes.

Sickle cell disease affects how your body produces red blood cells. Normal red blood cells are round – red blood cells affected by sickle cell disease harden and become sickle-shaped, like a crescent moon. This causes them to die too quickly and block blood vessels, leading to symptoms that are often painful.

Ethnic Backgrounds at Higher Risk

  • African
  • Asian
  • Caribbean
  • Eastern Mediterranean
  • Middle Eastern

Important: There are treatments to manage sickle cell disease, but no cure yet – it's a lifelong condition.

Symptoms and Diagnosis

The main symptoms of sickle cell disease are sickle cell crises (very painful episodes affecting different parts of the body), infections, and anaemia.

Emergency symptoms - seek immediate medical help:

  • High temperature (38°C/100.4°F or higher)
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Drowsiness, confusion, or slurred speech
  • Severe headache, stiff neck, or dizziness
  • Very pale skin or lips
  • Fits (seizures)
  • Serious pain not responding to home treatments

Sickle Cell Crises

Episodes of pain known as sickle cell crises happen when blood vessels become blocked. The pain can be severe and typically affects one part of the body at a time.

Common Areas Affected

  • Hands or feet (especially in young children)
  • Ribs and breastbone
  • Spine
  • Pelvis
  • Tummy
  • Legs and arms

Diagnosis

Sickle cell disease is usually diagnosed during pregnancy through screening. Newborn babies are also screened as part of the heel prick test.

Treating Sickle Cell Disease

People of all ages with sickle cell disease are supported by a team of medical professionals in a specialist sickle cell centre.

Managing Sickle Cell Crises

  • Over-the-counter painkillers like paracetamol and ibuprofen
  • Heating pads or warm towels on affected areas
  • Staying hydrated by drinking plenty of fluids
  • Relaxing distractions to help manage pain

Prevention Tips

  • Prevent dehydration by drinking plenty of fluids
  • Avoid alcohol
  • Stay warm and avoid extreme temperatures
  • Avoid activities that leave you severely out of breath
  • Don't smoke
  • Manage stress through relaxation techniques

Medications

Hydroxycarbamide may be prescribed to reduce sickle cell crises. Daily antibiotics are often needed to prevent infections.

Living with Sickle Cell Disease

Sickle cell disease is a lifelong illness, which can be managed well with treatment and self-help.

Surgery Precautions

Always inform your medical team about your sickle cell disease before any surgery involving general anaesthetic.

Pregnancy and Birth

You can have a healthy pregnancy with sickle cell disease, but extra monitoring and precautions are necessary.

Outlook: Most children with sickle cell disease lead normal, happy lives. Life expectancy varies depending on the type and severity, but newer treatments continue to improve outcomes.

Causes of Sickle Cell Disease

Sickle cell disease is caused by a gene problem that is inherited from your parents. It isn't something you can catch.

Inheritance Pattern

When both parents are carriers:

  • 25% chance: Child won't inherit any faulty genes
  • 50% chance: Child will be a carrier
  • 25% chance: Child will have sickle cell disease

Remember: Sickle cell disease is not caused by anything the parents did during or before pregnancy.

Sickle Cell Carriers and Screening

Sickle cell disease carriers are people who carry the faulty gene but do not have the disease. This is also called having the sickle cell trait.

Who is More Likely to be a Carrier?

  • African background
  • Asian background
  • Caribbean background
  • Eastern Mediterranean background
  • Middle Eastern background

Getting Tested

You can request a blood test from your GP at any time to find out if you're a sickle cell carrier. Every pregnant woman in Scotland is offered screening.

Carrier Health: Apart from certain rare situations, sickle cell carriers can lead completely normal and healthy lives.
