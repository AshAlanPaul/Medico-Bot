Anxiety Disorders in Children

It's normal for children to feel worried or anxious from time to time, like when they're starting school or nursery, or moving to a new area.

Anxiety is a feeling of unease, like worry or fear. It's an understandable reaction in children to change or a stressful event.

Important: For some children, anxiety affects their behaviour and thoughts on a daily basis, interfering with school, home and social life. If anxiety persists and impacts daily activities, professional help may be needed.

Signs of Anxiety in Children

Anxiety can make a child feel scared, panicky, embarrassed or ashamed. Look out for these signs:

  • Difficulty concentrating and focusing
  • Sleep problems - trouble sleeping or waking in the night
  • Eating changes - not eating properly
  • Irritability - quickly getting angry with outbursts
  • Constant worrying or negative thoughts
  • Physical symptoms - tension, fidgeting, frequent toilet use
  • Frequent crying and emotional outbursts
  • Clinginess beyond what's normal for their age
  • Physical complaints - tummy aches, feeling unwell
  • Withdrawal from previously enjoyed activities

Younger children may not recognize why they're feeling anxious. Separation anxiety is common in younger children, while older children tend to worry more about school performance, relationships, or health.

Types of Anxiety in Children

Specific Fears or Phobias

Young children commonly fear things like the dark or imaginary creatures. This becomes a phobia when the fear becomes overwhelming and affects daily life.

Generalized Anxiety

Feeling anxious most of the time without apparent reason. Can develop into generalized anxiety disorder with constant fear affecting daily tasks.

Separation Anxiety

Worrying about being away from parents or regular carers. Common in young children and can make settling into new environments difficult.

Social Anxiety

Not wanting to go out in public, see friends, or participate in activities. Becomes a disorder when everyday activities cause overwhelming fear.

School-based Anxiety

Anxiety about school, schoolwork, friendships, or bullying. May manifest as physical symptoms like tummy aches in the morning.

Note: Less common anxiety disorders like PTSD and OCD can also affect children but are more typically seen in adults.

When to Seek Help

Seek professional help for your child's anxiety if:

  • It's not improving or getting worse despite your efforts
  • It's significantly affecting their development, schooling, or relationships
  • It occurs very frequently or they feel anxious all the time
  • It causes frequent physical symptoms like nausea or headaches
  • They become very withdrawn from previously enjoyed activities

Long-term impact: Untreated anxiety can severely interfere with personal development and may persist into teenage years and adulthood, potentially leading to depression, hopelessness, or substance misuse.

Treatment Options

Getting Help

Start by talking to your GP or school nurse. Your child may be referred to local child and adolescent mental health services (CAMHS) with specialists including psychologists, psychiatrists, and therapists.

Psychological Therapies

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is particularly effective for anxiety in children. It helps change thinking patterns and develop coping strategies.

Medication

In some cases, antidepressants like SSRIs may be prescribed if psychological therapies haven't been effective. These take 2-4 weeks to work and require monitoring for side effects.

Support Services

Youth counseling services, telephone helplines, and online support can provide additional help, especially for children who prefer talking to someone outside their immediate circle.

Causes & Risk Factors

Genetic Factors

Some children are born more nervous and less able to cope with stress. Anxious personality may be partly inherited from parents.

Life Events

Traumatic or difficult events can trigger anxiety:

  • Frequent moving and school changes
  • Parental divorce or separation
  • Death of close relative or friend
  • Serious illness or injury
  • Family illness or disability
  • School issues like bullying or academic pressure
  • Involvement in crime
  • Abuse or neglect

Medical Conditions

Children with ADHD and autistic spectrum disorders may experience anxiety as part of their condition due to differences in brain functioning.

Parental support: Listening to your child about their worries and offering understanding and support is crucial. Don't hesitate to seek professional help when needed.
