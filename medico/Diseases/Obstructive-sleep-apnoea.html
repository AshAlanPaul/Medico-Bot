Obstructive Sleep Apnoea (OSA)

Obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA) is a relatively common condition where the walls of the throat relax and narrow during sleep, interrupting normal breathing.

This leads to regularly interrupted sleep, which can significantly impact quality of life and increases the risk of developing certain health conditions.

Types of Breathing Interruption

  • Apnoea – Total blockage of airflow for 10 seconds or more
  • Hypopnoea – Partial blockage causing 50%+ airflow reduction for 10+ seconds

Symptoms

Symptoms are often first noticed by partners or family members:

  • Loud snoring
  • Noisy and laboured breathing
  • Repeated periods of interrupted breathing with gasping or snorting
  • Night sweats
  • Frequent waking to urinate
  • Daytime tiredness and sleepiness

Risk Factors

  • Being overweight or obese
  • Male gender
  • Age 40+
  • Large neck size (men: 17+ inches)
  • Sedative medications
  • Alcohol consumption
  • Smoking
  • Family history
  • Nasal congestion

When to seek help: See your GP if you think you might have OSA. They can refer you to a sleep specialist for assessment.

Diagnosing Sleep Apnoea

OSA is typically diagnosed through sleep observation at a specialist clinic or using home testing equipment.

Initial Assessment

  • GP consultation and symptom review
  • Epworth Sleepiness Scale questionnaire
  • Physical examination including BMI and neck measurement
  • Blood tests to rule out other conditions

Sleep Testing Options

  • Home testing – Portable equipment monitors breathing, heart rate, and oxygen levels
  • Polysomnography – Comprehensive overnight study at sleep centre

Severity Classification

Based on Apnoea-Hypopnoea Index (AHI):

  • Mild – 5-14 episodes per hour
  • Moderate – 15-30 episodes per hour
  • Severe – More than 30 episodes per hour

Note: Treatment is most beneficial for moderate to severe OSA, though some people with mild cases may also benefit.

Treating Sleep Apnoea

OSA is a long-term condition usually requiring lifelong management through lifestyle changes and breathing devices.

Lifestyle Changes

  • Losing weight if overweight
  • Stopping smoking
  • Reducing alcohol consumption, especially before bed
  • Avoiding sedative medications
  • Sleeping on your side rather than back

Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP)

The most effective treatment for moderate to severe OSA:

  • Small pump delivers compressed air through a mask
  • Prevents throat closing during sleep
  • Available on NHS
  • Reduces symptoms and complication risks

Mandibular Advancement Device (MAD)

Dental appliance for mild OSA or when CPAP isn't tolerated:

  • Holds jaw and tongue forward
  • Increases throat space
  • Should be custom-fitted by experienced dentist

Surgical Options

Considered only when other treatments fail:

  • Tonsillectomy or adenoidectomy
  • Tracheostomy (rare)
  • Weight loss surgery for severe obesity

Potential Complications

Untreated OSA can increase risk of:

  • High blood pressure
  • Stroke or heart attack
  • Irregular heartbeat
  • Type 2 diabetes
  • Road accidents (due to tiredness)

Driving Alert: If diagnosed with OSA, you must inform the DVLA. Don't drive until symptoms are controlled.

Support Available: Organizations like the British Lung Foundation and Sleep Apnoea Trust provide valuable support and information for people living with OSA.
