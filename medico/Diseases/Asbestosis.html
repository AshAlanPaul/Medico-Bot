Asbestosis Information

About Asbestosis Causes Diagnosis Treatment Prevention

About Asbestosis

Asbestosis is a chronic (long-term) lung condition caused by prolonged exposure to asbestos.

Asbestos is a general term for a group of minerals made of microscopic fibres. In the past, it was widely used in construction.

Asbestos can be very dangerous. It does not present a health risk if it is undisturbed, but if material containing asbestos is chipped, drilled, broken or allowed to deteriorate, it can release a fine dust that contains asbestos fibres.

Symptoms of Asbestosis

Breathing in asbestos fibres may eventually scar the lungs of some people, which can lead to a number of symptoms, including:

  • Shortness of breath – this may only occur after physical activity at first, but it can eventually become a more constant problem
  • A persistent cough
  • Wheezing
  • Fatigue (extreme tiredness)
  • Chest pain
  • In more advanced cases, clubbed (swollen) fingertips

Treating Asbestosis

There is no cure for asbestosis once it has developed, because it is not possible to reverse the damage to the lungs.

One of the most important things someone with the condition can do is to stop smoking, if they smoke. This is because the symptoms are more likely to get worse in people who smoke.

Outlook

The outlook for asbestosis can vary significantly, depending on the extent of damage to the lungs and whether any other conditions are present.

People with asbestosis have a higher risk of developing other serious conditions, such as:

  • Pleural disease
  • Mesothelioma – a type of cancer that affects the membrane that covers the lungs, heart and gut
  • Lung cancer

Important: If you have symptoms and think you may have been exposed to asbestos in the past, see your GP for proper diagnosis.

Causes of Asbestosis

Asbestosis is caused by breathing in asbestos fibres. People working in certain trades are more likely to have been exposed to asbestos in the past.

What is Asbestos?

Asbestos is a general term for a group of minerals made of microscopic fibres. Materials containing asbestos used to be widely used in construction, because they are strong, durable and fire-resistant.

There were three main types of asbestos in commercial use:

  • Crocidolite ('blue asbestos')
  • Amosite ('brown asbestos')
  • Chrysotile ('white asbestos')

How Asbestos Fibres Affect the Lungs

When you inhale asbestos fibres, they are too difficult for the lung's defense cells to break down. This leads to inflammation and scarring of the lung tissue over time.

For asbestosis to develop, prolonged exposure – usually over many years – to relatively high numbers of asbestos fibres is necessary.

Occupations Associated with Asbestos Exposure

Occupations particularly associated with exposure to asbestos include:

  • Insulation workers
  • Boilermakers
  • Plumbers, pipefitters and steamfitters
  • Shipyard workers
  • Sheet metal workers
  • Plasterers
  • Chemical technicians
  • Heating, air-conditioning and refrigeration mechanics

Note: Nowadays, those most at risk include people whose jobs put them at risk of damaging any asbestos remaining in older buildings.

Diagnosing Asbestosis

When diagnosing asbestosis, your GP will first ask about your symptoms and listen to your lungs with a stethoscope.

If your lungs have been affected by asbestos, they will usually make a crackling noise when you breathe in.

Your GP will also ask about your work history, particularly about periods when you may have been exposed to asbestos.

Referral to a Specialist

If asbestosis is suspected, you will be referred to a specialist in lung diseases for tests to confirm any lung scarring. These may include:

  • A chest X-ray – to detect abnormalities in the structure of your lungs
  • A computerised tomography (CT) scan of the lungs – which produces more detailed images
  • Lung function tests – to assess the impact of damage on the lungs

Diagnosis: The specialist will also consider and rule out other possible causes of lung inflammation and scarring.

Treating Asbestosis

There is no cure for asbestosis, as the damage to the lungs is irreversible. However, you can take steps to reduce your symptoms and improve your quality of life.

Not Smoking

If you have been diagnosed with asbestosis and you smoke, it's very important to stop as soon as possible.

Smoking can make your symptoms of breathlessness worse and significantly increase your risk of developing lung cancer.

Vaccinations

If you have asbestosis, your lungs will be more vulnerable to infection.

It's recommended that you have the influenza (flu) vaccination and the pneumococcal vaccination to protect against serious respiratory infections.

Long-term Oxygen Therapy

If you have severe asbestosis, your body may not be getting all the oxygen it needs to function properly.

Oxygen therapy may be recommended if you have low levels of oxygen in your blood.

Medication

Most people with asbestosis will not benefit from any specific medication for the condition, unless you have another condition also affecting the lungs.

More severe cases may benefit from medicines, such as small doses of morphine to reduce breathlessness and cough.

Management: While there's no cure, proper management can help improve quality of life and slow disease progression.

Preventing Asbestosis

Although asbestos is no longer widely used in the UK, it's still important to take precautions to reduce your risk of exposure, because it's still found in many old buildings.

Asbestos in Your Home

If you are concerned that your house may contain asbestos, you can seek advice from an environmental health officer at your local authority or council.

Do not attempt to remove any materials that you think may contain asbestos yourself.

Asbestos in Your Workplace

If your job means you could potentially be exposed to asbestos fibres, make sure you are fully aware of what you can do to reduce your risk.

Do not attempt to remove any asbestos you come across, unless you have been trained in how to do this safely.

Working with Asbestos

Steps that may help reduce your exposure to asbestos during your work include:

  • Wearing protective equipment (such as a suitable face mask)
  • Cleaning up as you go (using a vacuum cleaner or wet rags, rather than sweeping)
  • Not using power tools whenever possible
  • Ensuring proper training and following safety protocols

Safety First: Always follow proper safety procedures when working in environments where asbestos may be present. Never attempt to handle asbestos without proper training and equipment.
