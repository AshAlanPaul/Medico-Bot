About Paget's Disease of the Nipple

Paget's disease of the nipple, also known as Paget's disease of the breast, is a rare type of breast cancer.

Note: This is different from Paget's disease of the bone. Paget's disease of the nipple refers specifically to a rare form of breast cancer that affects the nipple and areola.

What is Paget's Disease?

Paget's disease usually affects the skin of one nipple and produces eczema-like symptoms. It's important to distinguish this condition from common skin conditions like eczema or psoriasis.

Important: If you notice any changes in your nipple or breast skin, it's crucial to see a doctor for proper diagnosis rather than assuming it's a common skin condition.

Symptoms of Paget's Disease

Paget's disease typically affects one nipple and produces symptoms that may resemble eczema or other skin conditions.

Common Symptoms

  • Itchy, red rash on the nipple that can extend to the surrounding skin (areola)
  • Dry, red, flaky patches of skin similar to psoriasis
  • Small ulcers or sores on the nipple
  • Itchiness or burning sensation alongside visible skin changes
  • Bleeding from the skin of the nipple
  • Crusting or scaling of the nipple skin
  • Nipple discharge (may be yellowish or bloody)
  • Flattening or inversion of the nipple

When to see a doctor: If you experience any of these symptoms, especially if they affect only one breast, it's important to see your doctor promptly for evaluation.

Causes and Risk Factors

Paget's disease is usually a sign of breast cancer in tissue behind the nipple or in other breast tissue.

Underlying Breast Cancer

The condition can be associated with:

  • Invasive breast cancer - where cancerous cells invade surrounding breast tissue
  • Non-invasive breast cancer - where cancerous cells are contained in specific areas
  • In about half of cases, a lump is found in the breast
  • In some cases, the only evidence is the nipple changes

Risk Factors for Breast Cancer

  • Increasing age
  • Family history of breast cancer
  • Previous diagnosis of breast cancer
  • Previous benign breast lumps
  • Being overweight, especially after menopause
  • Alcohol consumption
  • Hormone replacement therapy (HRT)
  • Genetic factors (BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes)

Diagnosing Paget's Disease

Visit your GP immediately if you notice:

  • Any changes in the skin of your nipple or areola
  • Lumps in your breast
  • Nipple discharge, especially if bloody
  • Persistent itching or burning of the nipple

Diagnostic Process

The diagnosis typically involves:

  • Clinical examination of both breasts
  • Mammogram - X-ray of the breast (usually for women 35+)
  • Ultrasound scan - particularly for younger women with denser breast tissue
  • Skin biopsy - essential for confirming Paget's disease
  • Nipple discharge cytology - if there is discharge

Early detection: The sooner Paget's disease is diagnosed, the better the outcome is likely to be. Don't delay seeking medical advice for breast changes.

Treatment Options

Paget's disease is treated similarly to other forms of breast cancer, with surgery being the primary treatment.

Surgical Options

  • Mastectomy - removal of the entire breast, including nipple and areola
  • Breast-conserving surgery - removal of the tumor with a rim of healthy tissue, plus nipple and areola
  • Sentinel lymph node biopsy - to check if cancer has spread

Additional Treatments

Depending on the specific case, additional treatments may include:

  • Radiotherapy - to destroy any remaining cancer cells
  • Chemotherapy - for invasive cancer
  • Hormone therapy - if cancer is hormone-receptor positive
  • Biological therapy - for HER2-positive cancers

Reconstruction Options

After mastectomy, reconstruction options include:

  • Breast implants
  • Tissue flap reconstruction using body tissue
  • Nipple reconstruction or tattooing
  • External breast prostheses

Positive outlook: When detected and treated early, the prognosis for Paget's disease is generally good. Modern treatments offer excellent outcomes for many patients.

Prevention and Early Detection

Lifestyle Factors

While not all breast cancer can be prevented, these measures may help reduce risk:

  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Exercise regularly (at least 150 minutes per week)
  • Limit alcohol consumption
  • Eat a balanced diet rich in fruits and vegetables
  • Breastfeed if possible

Screening and Early Detection

  • Regular breast self-exams - know what's normal for your breasts
  • Clinical breast exams - by healthcare professionals
  • Mammograms - as recommended based on age and risk factors
  • Genetic counseling - if you have a strong family history
