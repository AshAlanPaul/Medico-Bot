Oesophageal Cancer

Oesophageal cancer is a type of cancer affecting the oesophagus (gullet) – the long tube that carries food from the throat to the stomach.

It mainly affects people in their 60s and 70s and is more common in men than women.

When to Get Medical Advice

Speak to your GP if you experience:

  • Swallowing difficulties
  • Heartburn on most days for three weeks or more
  • Any other unusual or persistent symptoms

Important: These symptoms can be caused by several conditions and in many cases won't be caused by cancer – but it's important to get them checked out.

Symptoms of Oesophageal Cancer

Oesophageal cancer doesn't usually have symptoms at first. As the cancer grows, symptoms develop:

Main Symptoms

  • Difficulty swallowing – Most common symptom, food may feel stuck
  • Persistent indigestion or heartburn
  • Bringing up food soon after eating
  • Loss of appetite and weight loss
  • Pain in upper tummy, chest or back

Other Possible Symptoms

  • Persistent vomiting
  • Persistent cough
  • Hoarseness
  • Tiredness and shortness of breath
  • Vomiting or coughing up blood (uncommon)

Causes and Risk Factors

The exact cause is unknown, but these factors increase risk:

  • GORD and Barrett's oesophagus – Long-term acid reflux can cause cell changes
  • Smoking – Tobacco irritates oesophageal lining
  • Alcohol – Excessive drinking causes inflammation
  • Obesity – Increases risk of GORD
  • Poor diet – Low fruit and vegetable intake

Rare Medical Conditions

  • Achalasia – Oesophagus loses ability to move food
  • Paterson-Brown Kelly syndrome – Causes anaemia and throat growths
  • Tylosis – Inherited skin condition

Prevention: Stopping smoking, reducing alcohol, maintaining healthy weight, and eating plenty of fruits and vegetables can help reduce risk.

Diagnosing Oesophageal Cancer

Diagnosis involves several steps to confirm cancer and determine its stage.

Main Diagnostic Tests

  • Endoscopy – Most common test, allows direct viewing and biopsy
  • Barium swallow – X-ray with contrast liquid to show blockages

Further Tests for Staging

  • CT scan – Detailed body imaging
  • Endoscopic ultrasound – Sound waves to examine area
  • PET scan – Shows cancer spread
  • Laparoscopy – Keyhole surgery examination

Staging System (TNM)

  • T (Tumour) – Size and location
  • N (Node) – Spread to lymph nodes
  • M (Metastasis) – Spread to other body parts

Treatment Options

Treatment depends on cancer stage and may involve multiple approaches.

Surgery

  • Oesophagectomy – Main treatment, removes affected oesophagus section
  • Endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR) – For very early cancer, no incisions
  • Stents – Keep oesophagus open for advanced cases

Chemotherapy

Used in different ways:

  • Before/after surgery to shrink cancer
  • With radiotherapy (chemoradiation) instead of surgery
  • To relieve symptoms in advanced cancer

Radiotherapy

Uses radiation to kill cancer cells:

  • With chemotherapy before surgery
  • As primary treatment with chemotherapy
  • For symptom relief in advanced cancer

Treatment Side Effects: Both chemotherapy and radiotherapy can cause side effects including nausea, fatigue, swallowing difficulties, and increased infection risk. These usually improve after treatment.

Living With Oesophageal Cancer

Cancer diagnosis and treatment can significantly impact daily life, but support is available.

Eating and Swallowing

  • Treatments available for swallowing difficulties
  • Temporary feeding tubes may be needed
  • Speech therapists and dietitians can help
  • Gradual progression from fluids to soft foods

Support and Emotional Wellbeing

  • Talk openly with family and friends
  • Join support groups or online forums
  • Access reliable information from cancer organizations
  • Take time for yourself when needed

Work and Financial Support

  • Employers must make reasonable adjustments
  • Statutory Sick Pay if unable to work
  • Employment and Support Allowance available
  • Carer's Allowance for caregivers

Palliative Care

If cure isn't possible, palliative care focuses on:

  • Pain relief and symptom management
  • Emotional and spiritual support
  • Care at home, hospital, or hospice
  • Respecting patient wishes and dignity

Support Available: Organizations like Cancer Research UK and Macmillan provide comprehensive information, support services, and practical advice for patients and caregivers.
