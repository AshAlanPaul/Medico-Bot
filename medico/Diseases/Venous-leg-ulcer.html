Venous Leg Ulcers

About Venous Leg Ulcers Symptoms Causes Diagnosis Treatment Prevention

About Venous Leg Ulcers

A leg ulcer is a long-lasting (chronic) sore that takes more than 4 to 6 weeks to heal. They usually develop on the inside of the leg, just above the ankle.

The symptoms of a venous leg ulcer include pain, itching and swelling in the affected leg. There may also be discoloured or hardened skin around the ulcer, and the sore may produce a foul-smelling discharge.

Important: See your GP if you think you have a leg ulcer, as it will need specialist treatment to help it heal.

Who's Affected?

Venous leg ulcers are estimated to affect around 1 in 500 people in the UK, although they become much more common with age. It's estimated that around 1 in 50 people over the age of 80 has one.

You're more at risk of developing one if you've previously had deep vein thrombosis (DVT) or find it difficult to walk because of problems such as:

  • Osteoarthritis
  • A leg injury
  • Obesity
  • Paralysis

Symptoms of Venous Leg Ulcers

Venous leg ulcers are open, often painful, sores in the skin that take more than a month to heal. They usually develop on the inside of the leg, just above the ankle.

If you have a venous leg ulcer, you may also have:

  • Swollen ankles (oedema)
  • Discolouration and darkening of the skin around the ulcer
  • Hardened skin around the ulcer
  • A heavy feeling in your legs
  • Aching or swelling in your legs
  • Red, flaky, scaly and itchy skin on your legs (varicose eczema)
  • Swollen and enlarged veins on your legs (varicose veins)
  • An unpleasant and foul-smelling discharge from the ulcer

Signs of Infection

Watch for these signs of infection:

  • Worsening pain
  • A green or unpleasant discharge coming from the ulcer
  • Redness and swelling of the skin around the ulcer
  • A high temperature (fever)

Causes of Venous Leg Ulcers

A venous leg ulcer can develop after a minor injury if there's a problem with the circulation of blood in your leg veins. If this happens, pressure inside the veins increases.

This constant high pressure can gradually damage the tiny blood vessels in your skin and make it fragile. As a result, your skin can easily break and form an ulcer after a knock or scratch.

Unless you have treatment to improve the circulation in your legs, the ulcer may not heal.

Risk Factors

  • Obesity – increases pressure in leg veins
  • Difficulty walking – weakens calf muscles affecting circulation
  • Previous DVT – blood clots can damage vein valves
  • Varicose veins – swollen veins caused by malfunctioning valves
  • Previous leg injury – can cause DVT or impair walking
  • Previous leg surgery – can prevent mobility
  • Increasing age – reduced mobility with aging

Diagnosing Venous Leg Ulcers

See your GP if you think you have a venous leg ulcer. The ulcer is unlikely to heal without specialist treatment.

Diagnosis is largely based on your symptoms and examination of your affected leg, although additional tests may be required.

Medical History and Examination

Your GP or practice nurse will ask about symptoms and underlying conditions such as:

  • Diabetes
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT)
  • Injury or surgery in the affected leg
  • A previous leg ulcer

Doppler Study

To rule out peripheral arterial disease, your GP or nurse will carry out a Doppler study to measure blood pressure in your ankles and compare it to your arms.

Treating Venous Leg Ulcers

With appropriate treatment, most venous leg ulcers heal within 3 to 4 months.

Treatment should always be carried out by a healthcare professional trained in compression therapy for leg ulcers.

Cleaning and Dressing

The first step is to remove any debris or dead tissue from the ulcer and apply an appropriate dressing to provide the best conditions for healing.

Compression Therapy

To improve vein circulation and treat swelling, your nurse will apply firm compression bandages designed to squeeze your legs and encourage blood flow towards your heart.

Important: You'll need to cut the bandage off if you experience severe pain at the front of your ankle or top of your foot, or if your toes become blue and swollen.

Self-Care During Treatment

  • Keep active by walking regularly
  • Elevate your affected leg whenever possible
  • Exercise your legs regularly
  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Stop smoking
  • Moderate alcohol consumption
  • Be careful not to injure your affected leg

Preventing Venous Leg Ulcers

You can help reduce your risk of developing venous leg ulcers in several ways.

Compression Stockings

If you've previously had a venous leg ulcer or are at risk, compression stockings may be recommended. These should be put on as soon as you get up and only taken off at night.

Weight Management

If you're overweight, losing weight can help prevent venous leg ulcers. Excess weight leads to high pressure in leg veins.

Treating Underlying Problems

Treating severe varicose veins may help prevent leg swelling or ulcers through procedures or surgery.

Remember: Once you've had a venous leg ulcer, another ulcer could develop within months or years. The most effective prevention method is wearing compression stockings at all times when you're out of bed.
