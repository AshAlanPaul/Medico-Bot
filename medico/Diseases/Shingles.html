About Shingles

Shingles, also known as herpes zoster, is an infection of a nerve and the skin around it. It's caused by the varicella-zoster virus, which also causes chickenpox.

How Shingles Develops

When you catch chickenpox, the virus stays dormant in your body. It can become active again later if your immune system is lowered, causing shingles.

Symptoms of Shingles

The main symptom of shingles is pain, followed by a rash that develops into itchy blisters. These look like chickenpox.

Early Symptoms

Sometimes shingles causes symptoms that develop a few days before the painful rash:

  • A headache
  • Burning, tingling, numbness or itchiness of the skin
  • A feeling of being generally unwell
  • A high temperature (fever)

The Shingles Rash

The shingles rash usually appears on one side of your body. It develops on the area of skin related to the affected nerve.

Examples of Shingles Rash Locations ▼

Shingles can appear on various parts of the body:

  • Back: Often appears as a band of blisters
  • Chest: Common location for shingles rash
  • Face: Can affect eyes and require urgent care
  • Neck: May cause significant discomfort

The rash develops into blisters that become yellowish, flatten and dry out. Scabs then form where the blisters were, which may leave slight scarring.

Shingles Pain

Most people with shingles experience a localized band of pain in the affected area.

The pain may be a constant, dull or burning sensation and its intensity can vary from mild to severe. You may have sharp stabbing pains from time to time, and the affected area of skin will usually be tender.

When to Seek Medical Advice

Contact your GP practice if:

You haven't had chickenpox before, you've been exposed to someone who has chickenpox or shingles and you:

  • Are pregnant
  • Have a weakened immune system
  • Are under 18 years old

If your GP is closed, phone 111.

Speak to a pharmacist if:

  • You're 18 years or over and have symptoms of shingles

If the pharmacist cannot treat you they may recommend you see your GP.

Causes of Shingles

Shingles is caused by the reactivation of the varicella-zoster virus, the same virus that causes chickenpox.

Risk Factors

Your immune system can be lowered by:

  • Stress
  • Other illnesses or conditions
  • Treatments like chemotherapy
  • Age (more common in older adults)

Note: It's possible to have shingles more than once, but it's very rare to get it more than twice.

Treating Shingles

There's no cure for shingles, but there are ways to ease your symptoms until the condition improves. Symptoms usually get better in 2 to 4 weeks.

Home Care

Do

  • Keep the rash as clean and dry as possible
  • Wear loose-fitting clothing
  • Use a cool damp cloth to soothe the skin and keep blisters clean
  • Try calamine lotion to help relieve itching

Don't

  • Do not let dressings or plasters stick to the rash
  • Do not use antibiotic cream – this slows healing

Medical Treatments

Your GP or pharmacist may recommend:

  • Painkillers like paracetamol or ibuprofen
  • Antiviral tablets in some cases
  • Stronger pain relief for severe cases

Preventing the Spread

You can't give shingles to other people, but others can catch chickenpox from you if they haven't had it before.

You're contagious until the last blister has dried and scabbed over.

Don't

  • Do not share towels or flannels
  • Do not go swimming
  • Do not play contact sports
  • Do not go to work or school if your rash is weeping and can't be covered

Protecting Vulnerable People

Avoid contact with:

  • Pregnant women who haven't had chickenpox before
  • People with weak immune systems
  • Babies less than 1 month old (unless it's your own baby)

Complications of Shingles

Complications are more likely if your immune system is low or you are elderly.

Possible Complications

  • Postherpetic neuralgia: Persistent pain after rash clears
  • Eye problems: If shingles affects the face
  • Skin infections: From bacterial infection of the rash
  • Ramsay Hunt syndrome: Affecting facial nerves
  • Neurological problems: In rare cases (encephalitis, meningitis)

Important: Shingles is rarely life threatening, but complications can be serious, especially in older adults.

Shingles Vaccine

A shingles vaccine is available and recommended for certain age groups. Speak to your GP about eligibility.
