E. coli O157 Information

About E. coli O157 Symptoms Transmission Treatment Prevention

Escherichia coli (E. coli) O157

Escherichia coli O157, sometimes called VTEC, is a bacterial infection. It can cause severe stomach pain, bloody diarrhoea and kidney failure.

E. coli O157 is found in the gut and faeces of many animals, particularly cattle. It's an uncommon cause of gastroenteritis but can be serious when it occurs.

Important: Always wash all vegetables, including salad leaves, that will be eaten raw, unless they have been pre-prepared and are labelled 'ready to eat'. Washing may reduce the risk of infection, but will not eliminate any risk of infection completely.

Symptoms of E. coli O157 Infection

Symptoms include diarrhoea, stomach cramps and occasionally fever. About half of people with the infection will have bloody diarrhoea.

People usually notice symptoms 3 to 4 days after they have been infected. But symptoms can start any time between 1 and 14 days afterwards.

These symptoms can last up to 2 weeks.

Serious complication: A small number of people with E. coli O157 infection go on to develop a serious condition called haemolytic uraemic syndrome (HUS). This can sometimes lead to kidney failure and death, although this is rare. The risk of HUS is highest in children aged under 5 years.

Contact your GP practice if: you or your child has bloody diarrhoea.

How E. coli O157 Spreads

E. coli O157 can be caught by:

  • Eating contaminated food - such as raw leafy vegetables or undercooked meat
  • Touching infected animals - or accidentally coming into contact with their faeces
  • Contact with infected people - particularly if you don't wash hands thoroughly after using the toilet or before handling food
  • Drinking contaminated water - from inadequately treated water supplies
  • Swimming or playing - in contaminated water such as ponds or streams

Treatment and Care at Home

There is no specific treatment for E. coli O157 infection. People who are infected can usually be cared for at home and most will get better without medical treatment.

It's important to drink plenty of fluids, as diarrhoea can lead to dehydration.

Important notes:

  • Antibiotics are not recommended and may increase the risk of complications
  • Anti-diarrhoea drugs such as loperamide (Imodium) are also not recommended as they may prolong exposure to the toxin

Prevention and Hygiene Measures

Strict hygiene measures are essential to stop others getting infected.

Do

  • Wash hands thoroughly with soap in running water and dry completely
  • Use liquid soap and warm water if possible
  • Everyone must wash hands after contact with an infected person
  • Always wash hands after using toilet or changing nappies
  • Wash hands before preparing or serving food
  • If infected, avoid cooking until 48 hours after symptoms clear
  • Wash soiled clothing separately at highest temperature (60Â°C)
  • Clean toilet seats, handles, and surfaces daily with hot water and detergent

Don't

  • Do not share towels or nappy changing mats
  • Do not clean soiled items in the kitchen

Returning to Work or School

Anyone who has had an E. coli O157 infection should stay away from work or school until they have been completely free of symptoms for 48 hours.

Most people are no longer infectious after about a week, although some people, particularly children, may carry E. coli O157 for several months after recovery.

Special considerations: If you work in health or social care, or your work involves handling food, you should consult with environmental health officers about when it's safe to return to work.

Children under 5: Should not swim in public swimming pools or share paddling pools until test results show they are no longer an infection risk.
