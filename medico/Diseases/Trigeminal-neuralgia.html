About Trigeminal Neuralgia Symptoms Causes Diagnosis Treatment

About Trigeminal Neuralgia

Trigeminal neuralgia is a sudden, severe facial pain, described as sharp, shooting or like an electric shock.

It usually occurs in sudden short attacks lasting from a few seconds to about two minutes, which stop just as abruptly.

In the vast majority of cases it affects part or all of one side of the face, with the pain most commonly felt in the lower part of the face. Very occasionally it affects both sides of the face, but not normally at the same time.

People with the condition may experience attacks of pain regularly for days, weeks or months at a time. In severe cases, attacks may occur hundreds of times a day.

When to seek medical advice

You should see your GP if you experience frequent or persistent facial pain, particularly if standard painkillers such as paracetamol and ibuprofen do not help and a dentist has ruled out any dental causes.

Your GP will try to identify the problem by asking about your symptoms and ruling out conditions that could be responsible for your pain.

However, diagnosing trigeminal neuralgia can be difficult, and it can take a few years for a diagnosis to be confirmed.

Important: If you experience frequent facial pain that doesn't respond to standard painkillers, seek medical advice. Early diagnosis and treatment can help manage this condition effectively.

Symptoms of Trigeminal Neuralgia

The main symptom of trigeminal neuralgia is sudden attacks of severe, sharp and shooting facial pain that last from a few seconds to about two minutes.

The pain is often described as an excruciating sensation, similar to an electric shock. The attacks can be so severe that you are unable to do anything during them.

Trigeminal neuralgia usually only affects one side of your face. In rare cases it can affect both sides, although not at the same time.

Symptom triggers

Attacks of trigeminal neuralgia can be triggered by certain actions or movements, such as:

  • Talking and smiling
  • Chewing and swallowing
  • Brushing your teeth
  • Washing your face
  • Shaving or putting on make-up
  • A light touch or cool breeze
  • Head movements
  • Vibrations, such as walking or car journeys

Living with the condition: Trigeminal neuralgia can significantly impact quality of life. The emotional strain of living with repeated episodes of pain can lead to psychological problems such as depression.

Causes of Trigeminal Neuralgia

Although the exact cause is not known, trigeminal neuralgia is often thought to be caused by compression of the trigeminal nerve or an underlying condition affecting this nerve.

The trigeminal nerve

The trigeminal nerve (also called the fifth cranial nerve) is the largest nerve inside the skull. You have two trigeminal nerves, one in each side of your face.

The nerve has three main branches:

  • Upper branch (ophthalmic) – carries sensory information from the skin above the eye, forehead and front of the head
  • Middle branch (maxillary) – carries sensory information from the cheek, side of the nose, upper jaw, teeth and gums
  • Lower branch (mandibular) – carries sensory information from the lower jaw, teeth and gums

Pressure on the trigeminal nerve

Evidence suggests that in up to 95% of cases, the cause of trigeminal neuralgia is pressure on the trigeminal nerve close to where it enters the brain stem.

In most cases, this pressure seems to be caused by an artery or vein compressing the trigeminal nerve.

Other underlying causes

Other reasons why the trigeminal nerve can become compressed or damaged include:

  • Tumors or cysts
  • Arteriovenous malformation (abnormal tangle of arteries and veins)
  • Multiple sclerosis (MS)

Diagnosing Trigeminal Neuralgia

As the pain caused by trigeminal neuralgia is often felt in the jaw, teeth or gums, it is common for people to visit their dentist initially, rather than their GP.

If you visit your dentist, they will ask you questions about your symptoms and investigate your facial pain using a dental X-ray and other means to look for other more common causes.

Important: If the dentist cannot find a cause, it is important not to undergo unnecessary treatment such as a root canal filling or an extraction. Do not try to persuade them to remove a particular tooth, as this will not solve the problem.

Seeing your GP

There is no specific test for trigeminal neuralgia, so a diagnosis is largely based on your symptoms and your description of the pain.

Your GP will consider other possible causes of your pain and may also examine your head and jaw to identify which parts are painful.

MRI scans

If your GP is not sure about your diagnosis, or if you have unusual symptoms, they may refer you for a magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scan of your head.

An MRI scan can help identify potential causes of your facial pain, such as sinusitis, tumors on one of the facial nerves, or nerve damage caused by MS.

Treating Trigeminal Neuralgia

There are a number of treatments available that can offer some relief from the pain caused by trigeminal neuralgia.

Identifying triggers and avoiding them can also help.

Avoid triggers

The painful attacks associated with trigeminal neuralgia can sometimes be triggered or made worse by various factors. Therefore, it may help to try to avoid these triggers when possible.

For example, if your pain is triggered by wind, it may help to avoid sitting near open windows and wear a scarf wrapped around your face in windy weather.

Medication

As normal painkillers are not effective in treating trigeminal neuralgia, you will normally be prescribed an alternative medication, such as an anticonvulsant medication to help control your pain.

These medications help relieve nerve pain by slowing down electrical impulses in the nerves and reducing their ability to transmit pain.

Carbamazepine

The anticonvulsant carbamazepine is currently the only medication licensed for the treatment of trigeminal neuralgia in the UK.

Common side effects include:

  • Tiredness and sleepiness
  • Dizziness
  • Difficulty concentrating and memory problems
  • Feeling unsteady on your feet

Surgery and procedures

If medication does not adequately control your symptoms or causes troublesome side effects, you may be referred to a specialist to discuss surgical options.

Procedures include:

  • Percutaneous procedures – inserting a needle through the cheek to treat the nerve
  • Stereotactic radiosurgery – using concentrated radiation to damage the nerve
  • Microvascular decompression – major surgery to relieve pressure on the nerve

Support and information: Living with trigeminal neuralgia can be challenging. Contacting support groups and staying informed about the condition can significantly improve your ability to manage it effectively.
