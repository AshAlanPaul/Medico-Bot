Lichen Planus Information

About Lichen Planus Symptoms Causes Diagnosis Treatment Complications

About Lichen Planus

Lichen planus is a non-infectious, itchy rash that can affect many areas of the body.

Affected areas can include the:

  • Arms, legs and trunk
  • Mouth (oral lichen planus)
  • Nails and scalp
  • Vulva, vagina and penis

The exact cause of lichen planus is unknown. However, the condition isn't infectious and doesn't usually run in families. It can't be passed on to other people, including sexual partners.

Who's Affected

Lichen planus is thought to affect 1-2% of the worldwide population. It's more common in adults over the age of 40.

Lichen planus of the skin affects men and women equally. However, oral lichen planus is more common in women. The mouth is affected in around 50% of all cases of lichen planus.

Important: A GP can usually diagnose lichen planus by examining the rash and asking about your symptoms. Oral lichen planus is often diagnosed by a dentist during routine dental check-ups.

Symptoms of Lichen Planus

The symptoms of lichen planus vary, depending on which area of the body is affected.

Lichen Planus of the Skin

The symptoms of lichen planus of the skin are:

  • Purple-red coloured bumps (papules) that are slightly raised, shiny and have a flat top
  • Thicker scaly patches can appear, usually around the ankles
  • Itchy skin

Lichen planus of the skin often affects the wrists, ankles and lower back, although other parts of the body can also be affected.

Oral Lichen Planus

The symptoms of oral lichen planus are:

  • A white pattern on the tongue and inner cheeks
  • Red and white patches in the mouth
  • Burning and discomfort in the mouth while eating or drinking
  • Painful red gums

Genital Lichen Planus

Lichen planus can affect the penis, vulva and vagina, causing symptoms such as:

  • Purple or white ring-shaped patches
  • Soreness, burning and rawness
  • Painful sexual intercourse
  • Discharge and potential scarring

Note: Mild cases of oral lichen planus don't usually cause any pain or discomfort. More severe cases can be quite painful and require treatment.

Causes of Lichen Planus

The cause of lichen planus is unknown.

It's thought to be related to the immune system, or an abnormal response of the immune system to certain medicines.

Autoimmune Reaction

In people with lichen planus, it's thought that the immune system becomes overactive, causing an excess amount of proteins to be produced, which inflame the skin. This is known as an autoimmune reaction.

Reaction to Medication

A reaction to certain medications is another possible cause of lichen planus. For example:

  • Gold injections - used for arthritis treatment
  • Antimalarial tablets

Important: Lichen planus isn't infectious, doesn't usually run in families and can't be passed on to others.

Diagnosing Lichen Planus

Lichen planus is often diagnosed by a GP or dentist examining the affected area.

Skin Lichen Planus

Your GP may be able to make a diagnosis based on the characteristic rash. However, lichen planus can be mistaken for other skin conditions, such as eczema.

If your doctor is unable to make a confident diagnosis by examining your skin, they may want to take a small skin sample (biopsy), which will be examined under a microscope.

Oral Lichen Planus

A dentist or doctor can diagnose oral lichen planus by looking at the inside of your mouth. A biopsy may be recommended to confirm the diagnosis.

Medical attention: If you suspect you have lichen planus, it's important to see a healthcare professional for proper diagnosis and treatment recommendations.

Treating Lichen Planus

There's no cure for lichen planus, so treatments aim to ease the symptoms and clear the rash. Mild lichen planus doesn't require treatment.

Self-Help Measures

For skin lichen planus:

  • Avoid washing with soap or bubble bath
  • Use plain warm water instead
  • Use an emollient to moisturise your skin

For oral lichen planus:

  • Avoid spicy foods and acidic drinks
  • Avoid sharp foods like crusty bread
  • Avoid alcohol, particularly spirits
  • Maintain good oral hygiene

Medical Treatments

Medical treatments for lichen planus include:

  • Corticosteroid creams and ointments - to reduce inflammation and itching
  • Corticosteroid tablets - for severe cases
  • Antihistamines - to reduce itchiness
  • Light treatment - UVB or PUVA therapy
  • Immunomodulating agents - for cases that don't respond to corticosteroids

Important: Always follow your healthcare provider's instructions when using medications, especially corticosteroids, as they can have side effects with long-term use.

Complications of Lichen Planus

Complications of lichen planus may include discoloured skin and, in some cases, erosive lichen planus may develop into certain types of cancer.

Skin Pigmentation

After the lichen planus rash has cleared up, the affected area of skin may be a different colour to what it was originally. There may be a brown or grey mark, which can sometimes last for months.

Erosive Lichen Planus

Erosive lichen planus is a long-lasting form of lichen planus that causes painful ulcers to develop, as well as burning and discomfort in the genital areas.

Occasionally, in around 2% of cases, long-term cases of erosive lichen planus can develop into certain types of cancer, such as:

  • Mouth cancer
  • Vulval cancer
  • Penile cancer

Regular monitoring: Regularly examining yourself and being aware of any changes that occur will help you to identify any problems at an early stage. Visit your GP immediately if you notice any changes that you're concerned about.

Prognosis: Most cases of lichen planus of the skin clear up on their own within 6 to 9 months. The rash rarely lasts longer than 18 months. However, oral lichen planus and lichen planus that affects the genital area may be more persistent.
