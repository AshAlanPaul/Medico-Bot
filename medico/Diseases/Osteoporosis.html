About Osteoporosis

Osteoporosis is a condition that weakens bones, making them fragile and more likely to break. It develops slowly over several years and is often only diagnosed when a minor fall or sudden impact causes a bone fracture.

The most common injuries in people with osteoporosis are:

  • Wrist fractures
  • Hip fractures
  • Fractures of the spinal bones (vertebrae)

Who's affected?

Osteoporosis affects over three million people in the UK. More than 500,000 people receive hospital treatment for fragility fractures every year as a result of osteoporosis.

Diagnosing Osteoporosis

If your doctor suspects you have osteoporosis, they can make an assessment using risk assessment tools and may refer you for a DEXA scan to measure your bone mineral density.

Osteopenia: You may be diagnosed with osteopenia if bone density tests show you have decreased bone density, but not enough to be classed as osteoporosis.

Causes of Osteoporosis

Osteoporosis causes bones to become less dense and more fragile. Some people are more at risk than others.

Bones are at their thickest and strongest in your early adult life and their density increases until your late 20s. You gradually start losing bone density from around the age of 35.

Risk Groups

Osteoporosis can affect men and women. It's more common in older people, but it can also affect younger people.

Women

Women are more at risk of developing osteoporosis than men because the hormone changes that occur in the menopause directly affect bone density.

Women are at even greater risk if they have:

  • An early menopause (before the age of 45)
  • A hysterectomy before the age of 45
  • Absent periods for more than six months

Other Risk Factors

  • Family history of osteoporosis
  • Low body mass index (BMI of 19 or less)
  • Long-term use of high-dose corticosteroids
  • Heavy drinking and smoking
  • Certain medical conditions and medications

Important: Many factors can increase your risk of osteoporosis. Speak with your doctor if you're concerned about your bone health.

Treating Osteoporosis

Treatment for osteoporosis is based on treating and preventing fractures, and using medication to strengthen bones.

Medication Options

Several types of medication are available to treat osteoporosis:

Bisphosphonates

These slow down the rate at which bone is broken down in your body, maintaining bone density and reducing fracture risk.

Calcium and Vitamin D Supplements

Calcium is important for maintaining strong bones, while vitamin D helps your body absorb calcium.

Adults need 700mg of calcium daily and may need vitamin D supplements, especially if they don't get enough sunlight exposure.

Other Treatments

  • Strontium ranelate
  • Selective oestrogen receptor modulators (SERMs)
  • Parathyroid hormone treatments
  • Hormone replacement therapy (HRT) - with caution

Note: Your doctor will discuss the treatments available and make sure the medicines are right for you based on your individual risk factors.

Preventing Osteoporosis

Your genes determine your height and skeleton strength, but lifestyle factors influence how healthy your bones are.

Regular Exercise

Adults aged 19 to 64 should do at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity every week.

Weight-bearing exercises and resistance exercises are particularly important for improving bone density.

Healthy Eating

Eating a healthy, balanced diet is recommended for everyone and can help prevent osteoporosis.

Calcium-rich foods include:

  • Leafy green vegetables
  • Dried fruit
  • Tofu
  • Yoghurt

Other Prevention Strategies

  • Quit smoking
  • Limit alcohol intake
  • Get adequate vitamin D through sunlight or supplements
  • Maintain a healthy weight

Remember: It's never too early or too late to start taking care of your bone health. Small lifestyle changes can make a big difference.

Living with Osteoporosis

If you're diagnosed with osteoporosis, there are steps you can take to reduce your chances of a fall and manage your condition effectively.

Getting Support

Your GP or nurse can answer questions about living with osteoporosis. You may also find it helpful to talk to a trained counsellor or join a support group.

Managing Pain

There are different ways to manage pain, including:

  • Medication
  • Heat and cold treatments
  • TENS (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation)
  • Relaxation techniques and massage

Daily Living

You should be able to continue working if you have osteoporosis. Maintaining physical activity and a fulfilled lifestyle is important for keeping bones healthy.
