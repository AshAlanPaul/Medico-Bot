About Carcinoid Tumours and Syndrome

A carcinoid tumour is a rare cancer of the neuroendocrine system – the body system that produces hormones.

The tumour usually grows in the bowels or appendix, but it can also be found in the stomach, pancreas, lung, breast, kidney, ovaries or testicles. It tends to grow very slowly.

Carcinoid syndrome is the collection of symptoms some people get when a carcinoid tumour – usually one that has spread to the liver – releases hormones such as serotonin into the bloodstream.

Note: Carcinoid tumours are rare and typically slow-growing. Many people may have a carcinoid tumour without experiencing carcinoid syndrome.

Signs and Symptoms

In the early stages of having a carcinoid tumour, you may not have any symptoms. You may also not have symptoms if the tumour is just in your digestive system, as any hormones it produces will be broken down by your liver.

If symptoms do develop, they tend to be fairly general and can be easily mistaken for signs of other illnesses.

Symptoms Caused by the Tumour

Symptoms will depend on where in the body the tumour develops:

  • Bowel carcinoid tumour - May cause tummy pain, blocked bowel (diarrhoea, constipation, feeling or being sick) and bleeding from the bottom
  • Lung carcinoid tumour - May cause a cough (possibly with blood), wheezing, breathlessness, chest pain and tiredness
  • Stomach carcinoid tumour - May cause pain, weight loss, tiredness and weakness

Symptoms Caused by Hormones (Carcinoid Syndrome)

Typical symptoms of carcinoid syndrome include:

  • Diarrhoea, tummy pain and loss of appetite
  • Flushing of the skin, particularly the face
  • Fast heart rate
  • Breathlessness and wheezing

Important: Some people may develop carcinoid heart disease, where the heart valves thicken and stop working properly. There is also a risk of developing a rare but serious reaction called a carcinoid crisis.

Causes and Risk Factors

It's not known exactly why carcinoid tumours develop, but it's thought that most occur by chance.

Your chances of developing a carcinoid tumour may be increased if you have:

  • A rare family syndrome called multiple endocrine neoplasia type 1 (MEN1)
  • Parents or siblings with a carcinoid tumour
  • Family history of certain other cancers
  • Conditions called neurofibromatosis or tuberous sclerosis

Remember: Having risk factors doesn't mean you will definitely develop a carcinoid tumour, and many people with carcinoid tumours have no known risk factors.

Diagnosing Carcinoid Tumours

A carcinoid tumour may be found incidentally – for example, as a surgeon is removing an appendix. In this case, the tumour will often be caught early and removed along with the appendix, causing no further problems.

Otherwise, people usually speak to their GP after they have developed symptoms. A carcinoid tumour may be diagnosed after carrying out a series of scans and tests, which may include:

  • Measuring the amount of serotonin in your urine
  • Endoscopy procedures
  • Imaging scans (CT, MRI, or PET scans)
  • Biopsy of suspicious tissue

Note: Early diagnosis can significantly improve treatment outcomes. If you experience persistent unexplained symptoms, it's important to discuss them with your doctor.

Treatment Options

If the tumour is caught early, it may be possible to completely remove it and cure the cancer altogether. Otherwise, surgeons will remove as much of the tumour as possible (debulking).

If the tumour cannot be removed but isn't growing or causing symptoms, you may not need treatment straight away – it might just be carefully monitored.

If treatment is needed, options may include:

  • Somatostatin analogues - Injections that can slow down tumour growth
  • Radiotherapy - To kill cancer cells
  • Hepatic artery embolisation - Blocking blood supply to liver tumours
  • Radiofrequency ablation - Using heat to kill cancer cells
  • Chemotherapy - To shrink tumours and control symptoms

Symptoms of carcinoid syndrome can be treated with injections of octreotide and lanreotide. You may also be given medication to widen your airways and anti-diarrhoea medication.

Treatment success: Many treatment options are available to manage carcinoid tumours and syndrome effectively, even when complete removal isn't possible.

Self-Management

There are things you can do yourself to manage some of the symptoms of carcinoid syndrome.

Generally, you should avoid triggers of flushing, such as:

  • Alcohol
  • Large meals
  • Spicy foods
  • Foods containing tyramine (aged cheese, salted or pickled meats)
  • Stress

If you have diarrhoea, it's important to keep drinking little and often to avoid dehydration.

Important: Some medications, such as SSRI antidepressants, may make symptoms worse by increasing serotonin levels. Never stop taking prescribed medication without seeking medical advice.

Outlook

If the whole tumour can be removed, this may cure the cancer and symptoms altogether. But even if surgeons cannot remove the entire tumour, it usually grows slowly and can be controlled with medication.

Overall, people with carcinoid tumours have a good life expectancy compared to many other cancers. Many people remain relatively well and lead active lives, with only occasional symptoms.

As the tumour grows or spreads, it will produce more hormones, and it may eventually be difficult to completely control symptoms with medication. You may need further surgery or other treatments.

Life expectancy is not as good for cancer that has spread to other parts of your body, because it won't usually be possible to remove all of it. However, treatment can still control your symptoms and slow down the spread of cancer.

Remember: Regular follow-up care is important for monitoring the tumour and managing symptoms effectively over time.
