About Syphilis

Syphilis is a bacterial infection. It's hard to detect and is one of the most easily caught sexually transmitted infections (STIs).

Important: Most people with syphilis don't notice any symptoms and don't know they have it. Symptoms change over time and may come and go.

Symptoms of Syphilis

Syphilis usually has 3 stages with different symptoms at each stage.

First Stage (Primary Syphilis)

Ten days to 3 months after infection, you may notice a small, painless sore or ulcer typically on:

  • Penis
  • Vagina
  • Anus or rectum
  • Tongue or lips

You might only have 1 sore, but can have more. The sore will disappear within 2 to 6 weeks.

Second Stage (Secondary Syphilis)

Symptoms begin a few weeks after the original sore disappears:

  • A non-itchy skin rash (commonly on palms or soles)
  • Tiredness and headaches
  • Swollen lymph glands
  • Fever, weight loss, hair loss, joint pains (less common)

Third Stage (Tertiary Syphilis)

This stage can begin years or decades after initial infection. 1 in 3 untreated people develop serious symptoms:

  • Stroke, dementia, loss of coordination
  • Numbness, paralysis, blindness, deafness
  • Heart disease, skin rashes

Important Warning

At the tertiary stage, syphilis can be dangerous enough to cause death. Early detection and treatment are crucial.

Testing for Syphilis

If you think you might have syphilis, get tested for free by:

  • Booking an appointment at your local sexual health service
  • Contacting your GP practice for an appointment

What Does a Syphilis Test Involve?

The only reliable way to tell if you have syphilis is to have a blood test. This is usually sent to the lab for analysis, though same-day testing may be available in some sexual health services if you have symptoms.

Treatment for Syphilis

Antibiotics will treat the syphilis infection, even during pregnancy. These are given by injection, tablets or capsules.

Treatment may be started before your test result is known. You may be given one or more doses, depending on the stage of infection.

Follow-up: You'll need to go back to your GP practice or sexual health clinic 6 and 12 weeks after starting treatment to be retested.

Important During Treatment

  • Avoid having sex until you and your partner(s) have finished treatment
  • Your partner should also get tested and treated
  • This includes oral sex and sex using a condom

Syphilis and Pregnancy

In Scotland, pregnant women are tested for syphilis as part of their antenatal checks.

If you're pregnant and have syphilis, you can pass it on to your baby (congenital syphilis), increasing the risk of:

  • Miscarriage
  • Premature birth
  • Stillbirth

How Syphilis is Passed On

You can get syphilis by having unprotected vaginal, anal or oral sex (sex without a condom). Around one third of all people who get syphilis do so through having unprotected oral sex.

Syphilis can also be passed on:

  • To an unborn baby during pregnancy
  • By injecting drugs with a needle that's been used by someone who has syphilis
  • During blood or organ transplant (extremely rare in the UK)

Increased Risk: Syphilis makes HIV easier to pass on and catch. The number of people getting syphilis in Scotland has greatly increased over the last 5 years.

You could be at higher risk if you:

  • Are a man who has sex with men
  • Have had sex overseas
  • Have had multiple sexual partners

How to Prevent Syphilis

The best way to reduce your risk of STIs is to practice safer sex. This means using a condom for vaginal, anal and oral sex.

Other STIs to Test For

If you have been diagnosed with syphilis you should get tested for all STIs including:

  • HIV
  • Chlamydia
  • Gonorrhoea

Remember: Regular STI testing is important for sexual health, especially if you have multiple partners or engage in unprotected sex.
